    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Italian Peninsula (500 BCE – 1500 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 500 BCE – 1500 CE

    Region: The Italian Peninsula — the boot-shaped landmass extending into the Mediterranean, including the Po Valley, central Apennine regions, and southern Italy; bounded by the Alps, Adriatic, Tyrrhenian, and Ionian Seas; including Sicily and Sardinia.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Etruscan inscriptions, Latin epigraphic and literary sources, Roman administrative documents, late antique codices, Ostrogothic and Lombard charters, papal registers, medieval communal records, merchant account books, and humanist manuscripts. Latin alphabet derived from Etruscan/Greek by ~700 BCE; Latin dominant through Roman period; vernacular Italian emerges in writing by 13th century; paper widespread by 14th century. Window begins with Roman Republican literacy; ends with print introduction (~1465–1500) transforming scholarly and commercial communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Roman Republic and Italic Unification (500–264 BCE)

    Early Republic: Traditional date 509 BCE; patrician-plebeian conflicts; Twelve Tables (~450 BCE); written law.

    Latin League: Roman-Latin alliance; hegemony established; dissolution 338 BCE.

    Samnite Wars: 343–290 BCE; expansion into central-southern Italy; military colonization.

    Italic Conquest: By 264 BCE peninsula under Roman control; citizenship extensions; road building.

    Religious Writing: Pontifical records; Annales Maximi; priestly calendar; augural texts.

    Legal Tradition: Twelve Tables foundation; jurisprudence begins; ius civile development.

    2. Mediterranean Expansion (264–133 BCE)

    First Punic War: 264–241 BCE; Sicily acquired; naval power; western expansion.

    Second Punic War: 218–201 BCE; Hannibal; Roman survival; Spain, Africa controlled.

    Greek Conquest: 200–146 BCE; Macedonia, Greece absorbed; Corinth destroyed; Hellenization.

    Literary Florescence: Ennius, Plautus, Terence; Greek influence; Latin literature emerges.

    Third Punic War: 149–146 BCE; Carthage destroyed; African province.

    Provincial Administration: Governors, publicani, administrative records; imperial bureaucracy seeds.

    3. Crisis and Transformation (133–27 BCE)

    Gracchi: 133–121 BCE; land reform; tribunician politics; violence in politics begins.

    Social War: 91–88 BCE; Italian allies revolt; citizenship extended.

    Marius and Sulla: Military reforms; civil war; proscriptions; senatorial restoration.

    Cicero: ~106–43 BCE; oratory, philosophy; extensive correspondence; Latin prose peak.

    Caesar: Conquest of Gaul; civil war; dictatorship; assassination 44 BCE.

    Triumvirate to Principate: Antony, Octavian; Actium 31 BCE; Republic ends.

    4. Principate (27 BCE – 180 CE)

    Augustus: 27 BCE – 14 CE; constitutional settlement; Pax Romana begins; literary patronage.

    Julio-Claudians: Tiberius to Nero; administrative consolidation; imperial bureaucracy.

    Flavians and Adoptive Emperors: Vespasian to Marcus Aurelius; imperial stability; territorial peak.

    Silver Age Literature: Seneca, Tacitus, Pliny; historical, philosophical writing.

    Administrative Documents: Military records, census, taxation; papyrus bureaucracy; archives.

    Codex Emerges: Codex form spreads; Christian adoption; parchment use increases.

    5. Third Century Crisis (180–284 CE)

    Commodus: 180–192 CE; dynasty ends; Year of Five Emperors; instability begins.

    Severan Dynasty: 193–235 CE; military emperors; citizenship extension 212 (Caracalla).

    Crisis Decades: 235–284 CE; barracks emperors; invasions; inflation; plague; territorial losses.

    Christianity Spreads: Persecution waves; martyrdom literature; church organization.

    Economic Strain: Currency debasement; trade disruption; urban decline in some areas.

    Administrative Continuity: Despite chaos, records kept; military rosters; provincial administration.

    6. Dominate and Western Collapse (284–476 CE)

    Diocletian: 284–305 CE; tetrarchy; administrative reforms; Great Persecution.

    Constantine: 306–337 CE; Christian conversion; Constantinople founded; church councils.

    Codification: Codex Theodosianus (438 CE); Justinian Codex (534 CE – though Eastern).

    Church Fathers: Jerome, Ambrose, Augustine; Latin theology; manuscript production.

    Barbarian Settlement: Visigoths, Vandals, Burgundians; foederati; Odoacer deposes Romulus 476.

    Manuscript Preservation: Monasteries preserve texts; Vivarium (Cassiodorus) exemplary.

    7. Ostrogothic Kingdom (476–568 CE)

    Theoderic: 493–526 CE; Gothic king; Ravenna capital; Roman administration continues.

    Roman Continuity: Senate functions; Latin administration; coinage; law maintained.

    Cassiodorus: ~485–585 CE; Variae (official letters); later monastic scholarship; Vivarium.

    Boethius: ~480–524 CE; Consolation of Philosophy; executed; classical learning.

    Byzantine Reconquest: Justinian's wars 535–554 CE; devastation; Gothic resistance; pyrrhic victory.

    Manuscript Culture: Scriptoria active; classical texts copied; Latin learning continuous.

    8. Lombard Italy (568–774 CE)

    Lombard Conquest: 568 CE; northern Italy occupied; gradual southward expansion.

    Fragmented Peninsula: Lombard kingdom (Pavia); Byzantine Exarchate (Ravenna); Rome (papal).

    Lombard Law: Edict of Rothari (643); written Germanic law in Latin.

    Papal Rome: Gregory I (~590–604); temporal power grows; Roman church administration.

    Monastic Scriptoria: Monte Cassino, Bobbio; manuscript copying; preservation role.

    Byzantine Decline: Exarchate falls 751; Pope turns to Franks; political realignment.

    9. Carolingian Integration (774–962 CE)

    Charlemagne: 774 conquers Lombards; Italian kingdom incorporated; crowned emperor 800.

    Carolingian Renaissance: Manuscript reform; minuscule script; educational revival; copying classical texts.

    Post-Carolingian Fragmentation: 9th century; weak central authority; local powers; Arab raids.

    Arab Raids: Sicily conquered 827–902; coastal raiding; instability.

    Italian Kingdom: Contested throne; German, local claimants; political chaos.

    Manuscript Production: Cathedral schools; monastic copying continues; Caroline minuscule spreads.

    10. Imperial-Papal Conflicts (962–1150 CE)

    Ottonian Italy: Otto I crowned 962; German-Italian kingdom renewed; imperial interventions.

    Reform Papacy: Gregory VII; Investiture Controversy (~1075–1122); Canossa.

    Norman South: Hauteville conquest of Sicily, southern Italy; Roger II (1130); separate kingdom.

    Early Communes: North Italian cities gain autonomy; Milan, Pisa, Genoa; self-government emerges.

    Concordat of Worms: 1122; compromise on investiture; church-state settlement.

    Monastic Reform: Cluny, Cistercian movements; manuscript production; reformed scriptoria.

    11. Communal Florescence (1150–1300 CE)

    City-States: Florence, Venice, Milan, Genoa; republican government; faction conflicts.

    Merchant Networks: Mediterranean trade; banking (Florence); commercial correspondence.

    Universities: Bologna (law); Salerno (medicine); Padua; scholarly manuscripts.

    Notarial Culture: Public notaries; contract documentation; extensive archives.

    Mendicant Orders: Franciscans, Dominicans; preaching; scholastic texts; urban presence.

    Dante Era: Vernacular literature; Divine Comedy (begun ~1308); Italian literary language.

    12. Crisis and Transformation (1300–1378 CE)

    Black Death: 1348; ~30–50% mortality; economic disruption; social upheaval.

    Banking Collapse: Bardi, Peruzzi fail 1340s; financial crisis; restructuring.

    Avignon Papacy: 1309–1377; papal court in France; Italian instability; Great Schism follows.

    Vernacular Literature: Petrarch, Boccaccio; Italian prose and poetry; manuscript circulation.

    Political Instability: Signorie emerge; tyrannies replace republics; Visconti Milan.

    Manuscript Production: Despite crisis, continues; university, religious, literary texts.

    13. Renaissance Humanism (1378–1450 CE)

    Great Schism: 1378–1417; competing popes; church crisis; Council of Constance.

    Humanism: Classical revival; Petrarch, Salutati, Bruni; manuscript hunting; Greek learning.

    Manuscript Discovery: Poggio Bracciolini; lost classical texts found; monastery searches.

    Territorial Consolidation: Five major states: Venice, Milan, Florence, Papal States, Naples.

    Medici Rise: Cosimo (1434); Florence dominant; patronage; banking power.

    Paper Widespread: Italian paper mills; cheaper production; manuscript volume increases.

    14. High Renaissance and Manuscript Twilight (1450–1500 CE)

    Printing Arrives: 1465 first Italian press; Venice becomes printing capital; rapid spread.

    Humanist Scholarship: Greek émigres; Platonic Academy (Florence); scholarly manuscripts.

    Artistic Peak: Leonardo, Botticelli, early Michelangelo; manuscript illumination continues.

    Italian Wars Begin: 1494 French invasion; Habsburg-Valois rivalry; political transformation.

    Print Displaces Manuscript: Scholarly, commercial, religious print; manuscript for luxury, specialized use.

    Manuscript Twilight: Handwritten production declines; print dominates public discourse.
