    1780 CE – 1810 CE — Late Colonial Crisis and Revolutionary Manuscript Culture

    Summary: The Viceroyalty of Peru from the Tupac Amaru II rebellion through Napoleon's invasion of Spain, where the accumulated tensions of Bourbon reform erupt in the largest indigenous uprising since the conquest, generating massive judicial documentation, while the 1808 imperial crisis unleashes revolutionary manuscript circulation among creole elites debating constitutional responses, binding constraint remaining the Manuscript Stage medium through which both repression and revolution are recorded, argued, and organized.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The truncated viceroyalty retains the Peruvian coast and central sierra after the 1776 creation of the Rio de la Plata viceroyalty that absorbed Upper Peru (modern Bolivia) including Potosi; the rebellion of 1780-1782 sweeps across the southern highlands from Cuzco to Lake Titicaca, terrain that advantages insurgent guerrilla movement and disadvantages royalist conventional forces; Lima remains the administrative center, connected to the crisis zone by weeks of difficult transit.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The Little Ice Age continues with no exceptional climate events; ENSO cycles produce normal variation; no climate shock correlates with the rebellion's timing, which is driven by political and fiscal triggers rather than environmental stress.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: No major seismic event disrupts the period; the 1746 Lima earthquake's reconstruction is complete; volcanic activity in the southern Andes does not significantly affect population centers; the hazard regime provides no unusual stress.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Silver production continues its long decline from 17th-century peaks but remains economically significant; mercury from Huancavelica sustains amalgamation processes; the mining economy's reduced dynamism removes one source of colonial vitality while tribute and labor extraction continue at full pressure; resource endowment is adequate but not expanding.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Mining pollution continues to accumulate at established rates; no change in waste routing or sink pressure; the physical environment absorbs loads without crisis; degradation continues gradually from earlier periods.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic disease patterns continue; the rebellion and its suppression add violent mortality, with perhaps 100,000 deaths in the 1780-1782 conflict concentrated in southern highland indigenous communities; baseline mortality returns to normal after 1782; no major epidemic coincides with the crisis.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: The rebellion disrupts agricultural production in affected zones, creating temporary food stress; royalist forces and rebels both requisition supplies; after pacification, nutritional status recovers to baseline; overall physiological vitality is not permanently impaired.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Colonial medical infrastructure proves inadequate for conflict casualties; traditional indigenous healing serves both sides; battlefield wounds and subsequent infections kill many who might otherwise survive; healing capacity is overwhelmed during the acute crisis, adequate during peacetime.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: The rebellion disrupts normal life in affected zones; some displacement and refugee movement occurs; after pacification, pairing and reproduction resume normal patterns; demographic recovery begins within a decade; the crisis is severe but not catastrophic for reproductive capacity.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: The rebellion's mortality falls disproportionately on indigenous highland populations, temporarily reversing demographic recovery; estimates suggest 100,000 deaths in a regional population of perhaps one million; the demographic shock is absorbed over the following generation; density remains low by global standards.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: The rebellion creates widows, orphans, and displaced persons in affected zones; royalist reprisals target indigenous leadership, removing community heads; the dependency load spikes during and after the conflict; extended kin networks absorb the burden over time; attrition is severe but not permanent.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: The rebellion disrupts production and trade in the southern highlands for two years; forced requisitions by both sides stress communities; the Spanish response includes economic warfare against rebel-held areas; after pacification, subsistence agriculture recovers; the floor holds despite severe stress.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: No change in energy technology; human and animal muscle remain primary; the rebellion demonstrates the limits of colonial energy mobilization against dispersed highland resistance; conversion efficiency remains at established levels.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: The rebellion is triggered partly by intensified labor coercion through the mita system and repartimiento abuses; Tupac Amaru II's program explicitly attacks forced labor and tribute; after suppression, the Crown reforms the mita and abolishes repartimiento de mercancias (1780s), moderating the coercive system without eliminating it; labor organization continues under modified colonial structures.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Mining production continues at reduced levels; the rebellion disrupts textile obrajes and agricultural haciendas in affected zones; trade routes through the southern highlands are interrupted; after pacification, production resumes at pre-war levels; surplus capacity is damaged but not destroyed; the long-term economic trend remains stagnant.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Royal treasury absorbs the cost of military mobilization against the rebellion, depleting reserves; merchant credit networks are strained; indigenous community reserves are exhausted by requisitions and tribute demands; buffering capacity is degraded, leaving less margin for future shocks.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: The rebellion interrupts the Lima-Potosi trade route, the viceroyalty's commercial spine; smuggling and informal exchange continue; after pacification, formal trade resumes; comercio libre continues to reshape Atlantic commerce; logistics infrastructure is not permanently damaged.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Silver peso remains stable as currency; the rebellion does not trigger monetary crisis; credit networks recover after pacification; no fundamental change in value storage practices; the monetary system absorbs the shock without transformation.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: The rebellion explicitly protests excessive tribute and the repartimiento system; Spanish response includes moderate fiscal reforms (abolition of corregimiento, reduction of some exactions) alongside intensified military spending; net extraction pressure may slightly decrease after reform, but fundamental colonial fiscal structure persists; redistribution continues to favor Spain and colonial elites.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: The rebellion targets Spanish and creole property in affected zones; some haciendas and obrajes are destroyed; royalist victory restores property rights; wealth concentration continues with perhaps slightly greater caution among elites; no fundamental redistribution occurs.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: War debris and unburied dead constitute temporary waste load in conflict zones; normal waste patterns resume after pacification; no lasting change in waste routing; financial system absorbs war costs through debt and deferred obligations.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: The rebellion disrupts families through death, displacement, and separation; widows and orphans require reintegration into kin networks; indigenous kinship structures prove resilient, absorbing the shock; creole family structures are unaffected outside immediate conflict zones; inheritance patterns continue unchanged.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Indigenous communities demonstrate remarkable capacity for coordinated action during the rebellion; cofradias and ayllu structures provide organizational infrastructure; after suppression, Spanish authorities attack these structures, banning indigenous political organization, traditional dress, and Quechua language in official contexts; the associational fabric is deliberately damaged by colonial repression.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Public festivals are suspended during the emergency; after pacification, religious celebrations resume with heightened surveillance; indigenous festivals face new restrictions as authorities suspect them of harboring resistance; sociability is constrained by security concerns and ethnic suspicion.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: The rebellion threatens the racial hierarchy, with indigenous leadership claiming Inca legitimacy; royalist victory reinforces colonial stratification; Spanish response eliminates the indigenous nobility (cacique class) that had mediated between communities and the state, flattening the indigenous social structure; creole-peninsular tensions continue; stratification is violently reinforced, boundaries hardened by trauma.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: The intendancy system, fully implemented by 1784, proves more effective than the corregimiento it replaced; military administration dominates the immediate post-rebellion period; judicial documentation of the rebellion generates thousands of pages of testimony, sentences, and administrative correspondence; administrative capacity is enhanced by crisis, bureaucratic procedures refined, manuscript production intensified.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: The colonial military apparatus, initially overwhelmed by the rebellion's scale, ultimately mobilizes sufficient force to defeat dispersed resistance; regularized military units garrison key points; militia reorganization improves response capacity; fortifications are reinforced; the coercive apparatus emerges from the crisis stronger, more professionalized, better documented; force advantage shifts decisively to the colonial state.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: The rebellion represents maximum discharge of accumulated grievance through violent uprising; suppression does not eliminate grievance but drives it underground; banditry and low-level resistance persist in remote areas; the apparatus contains post-war disorder through intensified surveillance and punishment; underlying tensions are managed, not resolved; the social fabric is damaged, scarred, not broken.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Jose Gabriel Condorcanqui (Tupac Amaru II) briefly galvanizes indigenous and some mestizo support through personal charisma and Inca symbolism before his capture and execution (1781); no comparable leader emerges thereafter; colonial administration relies on bureaucratic authority rather than personal leadership; the vacuum of indigenous leadership is deliberately maintained through elimination of the cacique class.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Spanish royal legitimacy survives the rebellion but is damaged; indigenous allegiance becomes instrumental compliance under coercion rather than willing acceptance; creole allegiance is complicated by fear of indigenous uprising, which paradoxically ties them to Spanish protection even as they resent peninsular privilege; legitimacy is hollowed, functional, no longer deep.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: The rebellion demonstrates indigenous capacity for collective action, terrifying colonial authorities; Spanish response deploys overwhelming will to suppress, reform, and prevent recurrence; creole elites lack independent political organization; after 1808, Napoleon's invasion of Spain creates a legitimacy crisis that will eventually mobilize creole will, but within this window collective resolve remains reactive, fragmented.

    External Relations & Posture: The viceroyalty remains subordinate to Spain; the rebellion is entirely internal; British naval pressure during European wars creates peripheral concern but no direct engagement; external posture is determined by Madrid; no independent diplomatic capacity exists.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Fear predominates after the rebellion's suppression; indigenous communities adopt protective silence; creole elites are shaken by the specter of racial warfare; peninsular administrators are anxious and vigilant; trust collapses across ethnic lines; the public mood is dark, watchful, traumatized; risk aversion intensifies among all groups.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Indigenous disaffection is suppressed but not extinguished; the rebellion's defeat produces exhaustion and apparent resignation that masks continuing resentment; creole disaffection grows through the period, fed by continued discrimination; apathy is protective strategy, not genuine acceptance; will is not spent but constrained, awaiting opportunity.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Spanish colonial authorities attack indigenous communication by banning Quechua in official contexts and restricting indigenous assembly; this drives indigenous communication further underground; Spanish and creole literate circles continue normal communication; the shared code fragments further along ethnic lines; accessibility narrows for indigenous populations, widens slightly for creoles as newspapers emerge after 1790.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: The rebellion demonstrates indigenous capacity for rapid mobilization through oral networks and runner systems; colonial authorities respond by attempting to disrupt these channels; official channels (correo system) continue functioning; after 1790, the Mercurio Peruano (1791-1795) provides Lima's first periodical, mixing print and manuscript circulation; transmission velocity unchanged, channel diversity slightly increased for creole elites.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: The rebellion generates massive judicial documentation: interrogations of prisoners, testimony of witnesses, sentences, correspondence, and administrative reports; these records are preserved in colonial archives (much survives in the Archivo General de Indias and the Archivo Nacional del Peru); the event is exhaustively documented from the Spanish perspective; indigenous perspectives are filtered through interrogation records; persistence is systematic for official records, oral indigenous memory persists in parallel.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Post-rebellion censorship intensifies; representations of Inca history and imagery are banned; indigenous political expression is prohibited; Enlightenment texts continue to circulate clandestinely among creole elites; after 1808, censorship weakens as the imperial crisis opens debate; discourse norms are restrictive during the 1780s-1800s, loosening after 1808 as constitutional questions become unavoidable.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: News of the rebellion spreads throughout Spanish America, inspiring fear among elites and hope among subordinate populations; the execution of Tupac Amaru II is staged as public spectacle to maximize deterrent effect; after 1808, manifestos, constitutional arguments, and political correspondence circulate among creole elites with unprecedented attention; amplification follows established elite networks.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Official documentation of the rebellion maintains high integrity within colonial administrative norms; rumor and oral transmission introduce distortion; after 1808, competing political claims generate confusion and contested narratives; verification capacity is overwhelmed by the volume of political argument; noise load increases as consensus fractures.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: The rebellion articulates indigenous grievances in terms of justice violated: excessive tribute, forced labor, abusive officials; Spanish response frames the uprising as treason against legitimate authority; both sides appeal to Catholic moral foundations while reaching opposite conclusions; the sense of justice is contested, fractured along ethnic and status lines; after 1808, natural rights arguments enter creole discourse, introducing new moral foundations.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: The rebellion triggers reform: abolition of the repartimiento de mercancias (forced sale of goods to indigenous communities), modification of the mita, replacement of corregidores with intendants; these reforms are codified in royal decrees extensively documented in manuscript; the Laws of the Indies are supplemented but not replaced; after 1808, constitutional debates (Spanish Cortes of Cadiz, 1810-1812) introduce new legal frameworks, circulating as manuscript copies and early printed texts.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: The rebellion's suppression demonstrates colonial judicial capacity at maximum extension: mass trials, collective punishments, public executions; Tupac Amaru II is executed by quartering (1781) after watching his family killed; subsequent leaders are hanged, exiled, or imprisoned; liability is collective, extending to communities associated with rebellion; sanction is exemplary, designed to terrorize; adjudication is rapid, severe, documented in exhaustive detail.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Post-rebellion reforms nominally improve indigenous legal protections by eliminating the most abusive practices; compliance burden shifts from corregidor extraction to more regular tributary obligations; indigenous political rights are further restricted (bans on assembly, dress, language); the net effect is mixed: some economic relief, increased cultural repression; rights remain limited, protection dependent on official enforcement.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: The rebellion itself is an attempt to redress accumulated wrongs through violence after legal channels failed; its suppression demonstrates that resistance is punished, not accommodated; impunity for pre-rebellion abuses goes unaddressed; new abuses during suppression create fresh grievances; the justice gap widens even as specific practices are reformed; unredressed wrong accumulates, feeding future conflict.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Enlightenment thought continues to penetrate creole intellectual circles through clandestine circulation of French and Spanish texts; the Society of Lovers of the Country (Sociedad de Amantes del País) publishes the Mercurio Peruano (1791-1795), promoting scientific and patriotic inquiry; after 1808, constitutional theory and political philosophy become urgent concerns; the paradigm is transitional, moving from scholasticism toward Enlightenment rationalism among elites.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: No significant technical innovation occurs; mining, agriculture, and manufacturing continue established methods; the rebellion demonstrates indigenous organizational capacity but introduces no new techniques; applied knowledge remains static throughout the period.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: The University of San Marcos continues training clergy, lawyers, and physicians; post-Jesuit educational decline continues; literacy remains confined to perhaps 10% of the population; indigenous education is neglected or actively suppressed; the knowledge stock stagnates; skill distribution remains highly uneven, perhaps becoming more so as indigenous cultural transmission is disrupted.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Indigenous oral traditions continue gradual erosion under colonial pressure; post-rebellion bans on Quechua in official contexts accelerate this process; European scholastic knowledge is increasingly superseded by Enlightenment alternatives; obsolescence affects both indigenous and colonial intellectual traditions; knowledge decay continues at established rates, accelerated for indigenous traditions.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: The rebellion invokes Inca imperial memory, with Tupac Amaru II claiming descent from the last Inca; Spanish response attacks this memory, banning Inca imagery, dress, and the Comentarios Reales of Garcilaso de la Vega; creole identity develops in tension between American patriotism and fear of indigenous mobilization; indigenous identity is driven underground but not destroyed; memory is contested, politicized, dangerous.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Colonial legitimation based on Catholic mission and royal authority is maintained but strained; the rebellion exposes the narrative's failure to convince its subjects; after 1808, constitutional liberalism offers alternative legitimation for political reform; the narrative is in transition, the old order defended but not secure, alternatives emerging.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Post-rebellion restrictions ban indigenous symbols in public display; Inca imagery disappears from legitimate circulation; creole baroque art continues in churches and elite residences; material culture becomes more explicitly Spanish in public contexts, indigenous forms retreating to private and rural settings; the visual culture is disciplined, censored, divided between public conformity and private persistence.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Colonial baroque forms retain meaning in religious contexts; indigenous symbols retain power precisely because they are forbidden; no form exhaustion is evident; meaning is contested, suppressed, but not exhausted; the symbolic vocabulary is charged with political significance.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Catholic cosmology remains officially dominant; indigenous syncretic beliefs persist despite suppression; the rebellion is sometimes interpreted in millenarian terms, with Tupac Amaru II cast as messianic restorer; after suppression, fatalism predominates among indigenous populations; Enlightenment skepticism remains confined to tiny elite circles; the outlook is traditional for most, troubled, uncertain about providence's favor.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Catholic sacraments continue marking life transitions; indigenous festivals face new restrictions; pilgrimage continues; devotional practice is intensified among some as response to crisis; the sacred is enacted regularly, providing consolation and continuity despite political trauma.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: The Archbishop of Lima and religious orders maintain authority; the Church supports colonial order against the rebellion, excommunicating Tupac Amaru II; doctrinal orthodoxy is enforced; institutional property remains substantial; the Church emerges from the crisis with authority intact, aligned with colonial power.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: No new religious movement spreads; syncretic practice continues despite official disapproval; the dominant Catholic cosmology maintains its reach, perhaps deepening as populations seek spiritual solace after trauma; no displacement is occurring; the spiritual landscape is stable.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Enlightenment skepticism reaches tiny elite circles without mass effect; for the vast majority, the sacred remains potent, meaningful, necessary; no significant disenchantment is evident; spiritual need is met within existing structures; the residue is minimal, not accumulating.
