    600 CE – 1000 CE — Middle Horizon States and Khipu Origins

    Summary: The Wari and Tiwanaku states expand across the Andean highlands and Pacific coast, establishing administrative colonies, standardized storage facilities, and redistributive economies that require systematic record-keeping beyond oral memory; probable khipu origins in this period remain fragmentary but cord bundles at Wari sites suggest external recording technology emerging to manage tribute, labor, and inventory across distances too great for face-to-face coordination; the binding constraint is administrative reach over ecological diversity, and the system holds moderate margin against climate shocks until prolonged drought and internal fragmentation collapse both polities by 1000 CE.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Andean cordillera presents extreme vertical zonation from coastal desert through highland puna to eastern montaña; Wari's Ayacucho heartland and Tiwanaku's Titicaca basin sit at 2,800–3,800 m elevation with transit corridors following river valleys and high passes; spatial friction is severe, compelling archipelago-style settlement across ecological tiers.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Highland climate regime features wet/dry seasonality with ENSO-driven interannual variability; the window opens during relatively favorable conditions but multi-decadal drought episodes intensify toward 900–1000 CE, narrowing the band both states are provisioned for.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seismic activity frequent across the subduction zone; volcanic hazard concentrated in southern highlands; drought the primary acute shock, with flood risk in coastal quebradas during strong El Niño events; infrastructure holds moderate margin until late-window drought exceeds buffering capacity.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Copper, gold, silver, obsidian available in highland zones; camelid herds on puna grasslands provide fiber, transport, and protein; marine resources along the coast; agricultural land limited to irrigated valleys and raised-field systems; endowment adequate for expansion but thinly distributed.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Low population density and dispersed settlement leave absorptive capacity effectively unlimited; metallurgical and ceramic production generate localized slag and ash deposits with no evidence of sink saturation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic intestinal parasites, respiratory infections at altitude, and localized pathogens establish a moderate baseline mortality; no epidemic disruptions recorded in the archaeological record; sanitary conditions stable.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Skeletal evidence shows adequate stature and low anemia rates during expansion phases; diverse vertical-archipelago provisioning delivers protein, carbohydrates, and micronutrients; nutritional status declines in late-window drought stress.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Patrilineal descent and ayllu-based pairing predominate; fertility adequate for population growth during favorable conditions; no evidence of selective pressures beyond endemic disease load.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population grows during Wari and Tiwanaku expansion, with nucleated centers reaching several thousand; density remains low by global standards; colonization extends the demographic frontier into new valleys.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Ayllu structures absorb orphaned and aged dependents through reciprocal obligation; dependency load manageable under favorable conditions; collapse-phase mortality and migration leave dependency question poorly attested.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Mixed agro-pastoral regime combining maize, potato, quinoa, and camelid herding across vertical tiers; coastal fishing and irrigation agriculture supplement highland production; household pooling within ayllus buffers seasonal variation; the floor sits above bare survival during expansion but tightens under drought.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and llama muscle provide the conversion regime; no waterpower or wind harnessing attested; per-capita work ceiling constrained by altitude and transport friction; efficiency adequate for monumental construction through labor mobilization.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Mit'a-style rotational labor organized through ayllu obligations; state corvée for construction, agricultural terracing, and transport; moderate coercion embedded in reciprocal redistribution ideology; colonists (mitmaqkuna) relocated to strategic locations.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Surplus generated through state-organized agriculture, storage, and textile production; Wari administrative centers show standardized storage architecture (qollqa); productive capacity supports elite consumption, ritual feasting, and monumental projects.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Qollqa storehouses hold maize, freeze-dried potatoes (chuño), dried meat (charki), and textiles; khipu-based inventory tracking probable; reserves buffer one to two poor harvests; prolonged drought exceeds storage depth.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Long-distance exchange networks move obsidian, Spondylus shell, coca, metals, and textiles along llama caravan routes; state-administered redistribution dominates over market exchange; logistics friction high but manageable through relay systems.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No monetary unit; textiles, metals, and Spondylus serve as prestige valuables and stores of value; deferred reciprocity obligations within and between ayllus carry the credit function; elite accumulation through redistribution control.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: State extracts labor tribute (mit'a) rather than goods directly; surplus flows to administrative centers for redistribution through feasting, ritual, and provisioning of labor drafts; extraction load sustainable under normal conditions.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Elite concentrations visible in mortuary goods, fine textiles, and metal objects; stratification moderate by later Inca standards; asset defense through kin networks and state affiliation rather than fortification.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Organic waste recycled into fields; metallurgical slag localized; textile production generates negligible discard; waste routing effectively closed with no accumulation pressure.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Ayllu-based reciprocity (ayni) structures mutual aid within kinship networks; trust radius extends to community and ethnic-group level; state colonization policies strain but do not break local solidarities.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Hierarchical ranking from ruling elites through administrative cadres to commoner ayllus; ethnic-group identities marked through dress, cranial modification, and mortuary practice; boundaries relatively stable during expansion.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Bilateral descent within patrilocal ayllu structures; inheritance follows lineage; marriage alliances cement inter-ayllu and elite connections; household formation embedded in community obligations.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Ayllus function as self-governing units for labor coordination, land allocation, and dispute mediation; moiety divisions organize larger communities; associational fabric robust.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Ritual feasting (tinku), music, and dance mark agricultural and calendrical cycles; chicha (maize beer) consumption central to sociability; communal labor (minka) blends work and festivity.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Reciprocity failures addressed through ayllu mediation; ritual combat (tinku) and feasting discharge intra-community tension; collapse-phase grievances poorly attested but fragmentation suggests unmetabolized strain.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Wari and Tiwanaku exercise centralized authority over core regions with variable control over colonies; power concentrated in ruling lineages governing through administrative hierarchies; parallel rather than unified polities.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Succession rules poorly attested; probable hereditary transfer within ruling lineages; no evidence of routinized mechanisms; transition points may have generated instability.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Legitimacy grounded in divine mandate, ancestral authority, and redistributive generosity; monumental architecture and ritual centers materialize elite claims; iconographic programs propagate state ideology.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Administrative centers (Pikillacta, Viracochapampa) show standardized planning and storage; probable khipu or proto-khipu record-keeping enables inventory and census; capacity reaches far but attenuates with distance from core.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Participation channeled through ayllu and ethnic-group representation; no evidence of popular assemblies; mobilization for labor drafts and military campaigns directed from above.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Wari and Tiwanaku maintain distinct spheres with overlap zones; diplomatic relations probable but poorly documented; external pressures from unconquered groups on peripheries.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Military capacity supports expansion and colony defense; weaponry includes maces, spear-throwers, slings; force concentrated in state hands but projection limited by logistics.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Internal order maintained through redistributive legitimacy and administrative oversight; collapse-phase disorder increases as central authority fragments; violence levels rise in terminal decades.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Oral messaging via runners (chasqui precursors) along established routes; velocity limited by terrain; transmission reach correlates with administrative network extent.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Khipu or proto-khipu cord records probable for inventory and census; textile tocapu patterns may encode information; persistence depends on specialist keepers; fragmentary survival to archaeology.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: State iconography standardizes religious and political messaging; local narrative traditions persist alongside; no evidence of systematic censorship beyond elite control of monumental media.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Reception concentrated at administrative and ritual centers; feasting contexts amplify state messaging; commoner attention captured through participation in collective rituals.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Khipu-based records offer high signal integrity for numerical data; oral transmission accumulates drift; verification through redundant knot structures and specialist training; noise manageable within administrative capacity.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Reciprocity (ayni) and communal obligation underwrite baseline justice; violations of exchange norms treated as moral failures; cosmic balance (reciprocity with supernatural) frames ethical order.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Formal codification absent; customary law administered through ayllu leaders and state officials; state edicts enforced at administrative centers; evolution tracks state expansion.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Disputes adjudicated by ayllu elders and kuraka (local lords); state officials handle inter-group and serious offenses; liability collective within ayllu for tributary obligations; enforcement through community sanction and state coercion.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual rights embedded in ayllu membership; compliance burden of labor tribute significant but offset by redistributive returns; predictability moderate within established relationships.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Elite impunity probable but poorly documented; collapse-phase breakdown likely increases unredressed wrongs; standing gap widens as central authority fragments.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Empirical observation of agriculture, astronomy, and metallurgy guides practical knowledge; explanatory paradigms embed natural phenomena in cosmological frameworks; no formal inquiry tradition separate from ritual specialists.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Sophisticated metallurgy (bronze alloys), textile engineering, hydraulic management, and architectural standardization; techniques transmitted through apprenticeship; reproducible methods support state projects.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Knowledge transmission through ayllu and specialist-lineage apprenticeship; literacy (if khipu counts) restricted to administrative specialists; skill distribution concentrated in craft and administrative cadres.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Collapse-phase disruption breaks transmission chains; regional fragmentation preserves local knowledge while losing imperial-scale coordination techniques; some metallurgical and architectural knowledge degrades.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Origin narratives rooted in emergence from caves, lakes, and mountains; ancestor veneration and huaca (sacred place) devotion anchor collective identity; ethnic-group memories persist beneath state overlay.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Standardized Wari polychrome ceramics, Tiwanaku stone carving, and elite textiles define visual conventions; iconography emphasizes staff-bearing deities and feline/raptor imagery; regional styles persist at peripheries.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Expansion-phase confidence expressed in monumental construction and colonization; late-window stress introduces defensive consolidation and ritual intensification; mood darkens toward collapse.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Standardized iconographic programs maintain coherence during expansion; collapse-phase fragmentation sees regional revival and imperial forms abandoned or localized; meaning erosion accompanies political dissolution.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Three-tiered cosmos (upper, middle, lower worlds) with reciprocal obligations between humans and supernatural powers; ancestor spirits and huacas mediate cosmic forces; fatalism balanced by ritual agency.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Offerings to mountains (apus), water sources, and ancestors structure daily and calendrical devotion; state rituals at monumental centers; chicha libations, textile offerings, and animal sacrifice widespread.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Priest-specialists administer state cults; oracle sites (possible Pachacamac precursors) attract pilgrimage; doctrinal authority concentrated but not rigidly codified; regional cults persist.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Wari and Tiwanaku iconography spreads through colonies, syncretizing with local traditions; staff deity complex achieves wide distribution; no complete displacement of regional cosmologies.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Collapse-phase ritual intensification suggests sacred frameworks remain charged; no evidence of disenchantment; spiritual needs addressed through continued devotional practice; residue minimal.
