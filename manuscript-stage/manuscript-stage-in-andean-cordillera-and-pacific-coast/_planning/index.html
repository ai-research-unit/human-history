    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Andean Cordillera and Pacific Coast (600 CE – 1810 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 600 CE – 1810 CE

    Region: The Andean Cordillera and Pacific Coast — the mountain chain and coastal desert from Ecuador through Peru and Bolivia to Chile and northwestern Argentina; encompasses the highland basins, intermontane valleys, and coastal oases where successive states emerged.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes khipu (knotted-string records), tocapu textile iconography, administrative architecture, and ceramic/metal artifacts. Khipu served as the primary recording medium for Wari and especially Inca administration; post-conquest colonial archives preserve manuscripts, legal documents, visitas, and chronicles (some incorporating indigenous testimony). Spanish alphabetic literacy introduced 1532; colonial manuscript culture dominant through independence (~1810–1820s). Window begins with Middle Horizon state expansion; ends as independence movements and nascent print transform public administration.

    Note on Khipu: The Andean khipu system constitutes a non-alphabetic but systematic external recording technology. Khipu preserved numerical data, narratives, and administrative information outside human memory; khipukamayuq (khipu keepers) maintained institutional records across generations. This system satisfies the Manuscript Stage definition of external record-keeping as the medium institutions run on, while differing from alphabetic scripts in structure. The window thus encompasses both indigenous cord-based recording and post-conquest alphabetic manuscript culture.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Middle Horizon States (600–1000 CE)

    Wari Empire: Highland Peru ~600–1000 CE; capital near Ayacucho; administrative centers across Andes; standardized architecture.

    Tiwanaku State: Lake Titicaca basin ~500–1000 CE; monumental center; raised-field agriculture; extensive trade networks.

    Administrative Expansion: Both states establish colonies/enclaves; standardized storage, redistribution; need for record-keeping.

    Khipu Origins: Possible khipu use in this period; evidence fragmentary; Wari sites yield cord bundles; debate on function.

    Recording Technology: Administrative complexity implies recording systems; khipu, possible textile encoding (tocapu).

    Collapse: Both states decline/collapse by ~1000 CE; drought, internal conflict debated; regional fragmentation follows.

    2. Late Intermediate Period (1000–1400 CE)

    Regional Polities: Fragmentation into competing kingdoms; Chimú (north coast), Chincha, Killke (Cusco region), others.

    Chimú Empire: Chan Chan capital; ~900–1470 CE; sophisticated administration; probable record-keeping continuity.

    Recording Continuity: Khipu likely maintained; regional variations; no imperial standardization until Inca.

    Cusco Emergence: Inca as local polity in Cusco valley; gradual consolidation; foundation narratives preserved later.

    Craft Continuity: Metallurgy, textiles, ceramics continue; elite goods production; exchange networks.

    Pre-Inca Complexity: States manage tribute, labor, storage without alphabetic writing; external recording essential.

    3. Inca Empire (1400–1532 CE)

    Tawantinsuyu: "Four Quarters"; rapid expansion under Pachacuti (~1438), successors; largest pre-Columbian American state.

    Khipu Administration: Khipukamayuq (khipu keepers) as professional corps; census data, tribute records, historical narratives.

    Khipu Structure: Knotted cords; base-10 numerical system well understood; possible narrative/phonetic encoding debated.

    Mit'a Labor: Rotational labor tax; khipu records track obligations; state projects (roads, storehouses, temples).

    Ceque System: Radial ritual geography from Cusco; recorded in khipu; social and cosmological organization.

    No Alphabetic Script: Recording through khipu, textiles (tocapu), possibly painted boards; oral narrative combined with khipu.

    4. Spanish Conquest (1532–1572 CE)

    Pizarro's Invasion: 1532; Atahualpa captured at Cajamarca; ransom, execution; Cusco taken 1533.

    Dual Recording Period: Spanish introduce alphabetic writing; khipu continue in use; colonial officials sometimes consult khipukamayuq.

    Encomienda System: Labor grants to conquistadors; indigenous tribute; early colonial records.

    Vilcabamba Resistance: Neo-Inca state 1537–1572; Manco Inca, successors; final conquest under Toledo.

    Early Chronicles: Cieza de León, Betanzos write histories; incorporate indigenous testimony; manuscript tradition begins.

    Religious Conversion: Missionary campaigns; indigenous writing trained; catechisms, confessional manuals.

    5. Toledan Reforms (1572–1650 CE)

    Viceroy Francisco de Toledo: 1569–1581; systematic administrative reforms; foundational for colonial order.

    Reducciones: Forced resettlement of indigenous population into nucleated villages; census; administrative control.

    Visitas: Inspection tours; detailed manuscript records of population, tribute, resources; legal documentation.

    Extirpation Campaigns: Anti-idolatry efforts; documentation of indigenous practices; trial records.

    Potosí Silver: Colonial economy centered on silver mining; mita labor drafts; extensive accounting records.

    Manuscript Bureaucracy: Notarial records, land titles, wills, correspondence; colonial archive formation.

    6. Mature Colonial Society (1650–1700 CE)

    Silver Economy Peak: Potosí production still significant; mercury amalgamation; colonial trade networks.

    Baroque Culture: Religious art, architecture; manuscript illumination; creole literary production.

    Indigenous Adaptation: Caciques (kuraka) navigate colonial system; some literate in Spanish; petition records.

    Religious Institutions: Convents, monasteries as manuscript centers; hagiographies, histories, theological works.

    Creole Identity: American-born Spanish elite; differentiation from peninsulares; manuscript circulation.

    University and Learning: San Marcos (Lima, 1551); colonial intellectual life; manuscript-based education.

    7. Bourbon Reforms (1700–1780 CE)

    Bourbon Dynasty: 1700; new ruling house; administrative rationalization; increased metropolitan control.

    Intendancy System: 1784 (implemented); new administrative units; professional bureaucracy; more documentation.

    Tax Reform: Efforts to increase revenue; census, tribute reassessment; resistance.

    Commercial Reform: Free trade within empire (1778); economic restructuring; commercial documentation.

    Expulsion of Jesuits: 1767; major institutional disruption; property inventories; manuscript confiscation.

    Enlightenment Currents: Scientific expeditions; natural history manuscripts; reform ideas circulate.

    8. Túpac Amaru II Rebellion (1780–1783 CE)

    José Gabriel Condorcanqui: Cacique claims Inca descent; adopts name Túpac Amaru II; rebellion November 1780.

    Great Andean Rebellion: Largest colonial uprising; spreads across southern Peru, Bolivia; tens of thousands mobilized.

    Colonial Crisis: Siege of Cusco; brutal suppression; Túpac Amaru executed May 1781; Túpac Katari continues in Bolivia.

    Aftermath Reforms: Cacique system abolished; indigenous dress restricted; intensified control.

    Documentary Record: Trial records, correspondence, proclamations; rebellion extensively documented.

    Indigenous Memory: Rebellion enters collective memory; messianic Inca traditions; documented in later sources.

    9. Post-Rebellion Order (1783–1810 CE)

    Suppression Aftermath: Harsh reprisals; indigenous leadership decimated; tightened colonial control.

    Continued Bourbon Reforms: Administrative restructuring continues; mining revivals; commercial changes.

    Napoleonic Crisis: 1808; Spanish monarchy collapses; constitutional crisis in colonies; loyalty debates.

    Creole Juntas: 1809–1810; La Paz, Quito juntas; suppressed but precedent set; independence ferment.

    Print Emerges: Limited printing presses in Lima; pamphlets, proclamations; transition from pure manuscript culture.

    Independence Threshold: Colonial order fragmenting; manuscript administration continues but print discourse grows.
