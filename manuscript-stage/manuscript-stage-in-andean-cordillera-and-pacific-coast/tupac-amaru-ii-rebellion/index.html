    1780 CE – 1783 CE — Túpac Amaru II Rebellion

    Summary: The largest indigenous uprising in colonial American history erupts across the southern Andes as José Gabriel Condorcanqui, claiming Inca descent and taking the name Túpac Amaru II, mobilizes tens of thousands against Spanish rule from November 1780; the binding constraint is the colonial state's coercive capacity tested against mass indigenous mobilization fueled by decades of extraction abuse; the system absorbs the shock through brutal suppression but at the cost of fundamental reform and intensified control; the trajectory is rupture, violent suppression, and structural reconfiguration of colonial governance.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Rebellion spreads across southern highland Peru and Upper Peru (Bolivia); rugged terrain of the altiplano and intermontane valleys shapes military operations; Spanish forces struggle with mobility in highland theater; indigenous forces exploit local terrain knowledge.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Highland dry and wet seasons structure campaigning; no anomalous climate event documented; normal seasonal patterns frame military operations across the rebellion's duration.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: No major seismic or volcanic event during the rebellion window; environmental hazards not a significant factor; exposure to warfare is the dominant threat.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Silver mining continues at Potosí though disrupted by rebellion; agricultural resources sustain combatants; fuel and material availability unchanged; endowment not directly contested.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Battlefield dead and destruction localized; no significant environmental loading beyond warfare's immediate footprint; absorptive capacity not stressed.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Warfare mortality overwhelms disease mortality during the window; endemic pathogen load unchanged but subordinate to combat and execution deaths; mortality spikes dramatically.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Provisioning disrupted in conflict zones; indigenous combatants draw on community stores; siege conditions (Cusco, La Paz) create localized famine; physiological stress acute in combat areas.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Mass mobilization of men disrupts household formation temporarily; reproductive patterns suspended during active conflict; no lasting biological impact documented.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Estimated 100,000 deaths across the rebellion (indigenous, mestizo, and Spanish); massive demographic shock concentrated in southern highlands; population density reduced in affected zones; recovery will take decades.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Mass widowing and orphaning in indigenous communities; dependency load surges; surviving communities absorb orphans and widows through traditional kinship structures; overhang significant.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Agricultural production disrupted in conflict zones; communities provision rebel and royalist forces; subsistence stressed but not collapsed; haciendas damaged or abandoned in some areas.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle remain sole power sources; no change in conversion technology; energy availability constrained by disruption of normal labor patterns.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Mita system—the proximate cause of rebellion—suspended in practice during conflict; labor organization collapses into wartime mobilization; coercion shifts from economic extraction to military service.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Silver production at Potosí disrupted; textile obrajes damaged; agricultural surplus consumed by warfare; productive capacity temporarily reduced; surplus extraction impossible during conflict.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Community stores depleted by warfare; colonial treasury funds suppression campaigns; reserves drawn down across both sides; buffering capacity exhausted in conflict zones.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Trade routes disrupted; markets suspended in conflict areas; logistics channeled to military supply; normal commercial exchange collapses temporarily.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Monetary circulation disrupted; credit networks suspended; tribute collection impossible in rebel-held areas; economic exchange reverts to immediate provisioning.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Colonial extraction apparatus paralyzed in rebel territory; Túpac Amaru abolishes tribute and mita by decree; extraction resumes with intensified rigor after suppression.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Spanish and creole property destroyed in rebel attacks; indigenous communities lose assets to reprisals; wealth concentration temporarily disrupted; asset defense collapses into warfare.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Warfare debris, unburied dead, destroyed property constitute waste load; routing by abandonment; no systematic disposal; localized environmental impact.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Indigenous solidarity mobilized for rebellion; community networks coordinate resistance; trust between indigenous population and colonial authorities shattered; intra-community bonds strengthened under pressure.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Caste boundaries weaponized; rebellion mobilizes indigenous and mestizo against Spanish and loyalist creoles; ethnic violence in both directions; stratification temporarily inverted in rebel-held areas.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Kinship networks mobilize military support; Túpac Amaru's Inca lineage claim central to legitimacy; family members (Micaela Bastidas) serve as co-leaders; inheritance disrupted by mass mortality.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Indigenous community structures (ayllu) serve as mobilization base; colonial civic institutions collapse in rebel zones; self-organization channeled into military resistance.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Normal festive life suspended; warfare consumes social energy; community gatherings become military assemblies; sociability contracted to survival and combat.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Legal petition channels exhausted before rebellion; armed uprising becomes the discharge mechanism for accumulated grievance; violence metabolizes decades of abuse; discharge catastrophic and incomplete.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Dual sovereignty during rebellion; Túpac Amaru claims Inca authority and Spanish royal loyalty simultaneously; colonial authority contested across southern Andes; power fragmented into armed camps.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Túpac Amaru's Inca lineage claim frames succession narrative; his capture and execution (May 1781) does not end rebellion; Túpac Katari continues in Upper Peru; succession through kinship and military succession.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Rebel legitimacy invokes Inca restoration, Catholic faith, and opposition to bad officials (not the king initially); colonial legitimacy defended through church and military; narrative warfare accompanies armed conflict.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Colonial administration collapses in rebel zones; Túpac Amaru issues decrees, collects forces, but lacks institutional depth; royalist administration maintains capacity in Lima and eventually overwhelms rebels.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Mass indigenous mobilization unprecedented in colonial period; tens of thousands participate actively; creole and mestizo participation mixed; mobilization capacity demonstrated then suppressed.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Rebellion internal to Spanish empire; no foreign intervention; British threat distant concern; geopolitical position unchanged though colonial vulnerability exposed.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Rebel forces armed with captured weapons, traditional arms (slings, clubs), limited firearms; royalist forces deploy militia and regular troops with artillery; coercive asymmetry favors colonial state; brutal suppression follows.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Order collapses into warfare; rebellion is the threat environment; colonial capacity to maintain order tested and strained; disorder peaks then suppressed through mass violence.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Rebel proclamations spread through runners and oral networks; colonial authorities communicate via official couriers; information velocity unchanged but volume spikes; rumor and news travel through conflict zones.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Both sides produce documents; Túpac Amaru's proclamations, correspondence preserved; colonial trial records, military dispatches extensive; rebellion becomes one of most documented events in colonial Andean history.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Rebel framing invokes Inca justice and Catholic legitimacy; colonial framing criminalizes rebellion as treason and sacrilege; competing narratives vie for allegiance; censorship impossible during active conflict.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: News of rebellion amplified across Andes through oral networks; attention maximally concentrated on conflict; Lima authorities alarmed; metropolitan Spain informed with months-long delay.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Conflicting reports, military intelligence, propaganda create noise; verification difficult in wartime; signal integrity degraded by fog of war.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Rebellion explicitly framed as response to injustice; Túpac Amaru positions himself as restorer of legitimate order against abusive officials; moral indictment of colonial system central to rebel appeal.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Túpac Amaru's decrees abolish tribute, mita, and repartimiento; competing legal frameworks; colonial law suspended in rebel zones; post-suppression reforms codify new regulations.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Colonial courts suspend normal function during rebellion; military tribunals adjudicate treason; Túpac Amaru's trial and execution (public dismemberment) exemplifies colonial judicial violence; liability assigned collectively to indigenous population.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Indigenous protective legislation rendered meaningless during conflict; compliance impossible amid warfare; post-rebellion repression eliminates remaining protections.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Colonial abuses that triggered rebellion remain unaddressed during conflict; rebel violence against Spanish and creoles creates new wrongs; impunity reigns on all sides; suppression creates new impunity for royalist atrocities.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: No intellectual activity amid warfare; Enlightenment ideas in background; rebellion not theorized in European intellectual terms by participants.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Military technique dominates; indigenous siege methods, guerrilla tactics, royalist artillery and cavalry; no technical innovation; applied knowledge is military.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Schools closed in conflict zones; knowledge transmission suspended; skill application redirected to warfare; no change in stock.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Indigenous knowledge of Inca military tradition partially activated; no significant decay during short window; post-rebellion suppression will target cultural transmission.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Inca restoration mythos fully activated; Túpac Amaru's lineage claim invokes Inca identity; rebellion enters collective memory as foundational trauma and resistance narrative; identity sharpened through conflict.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Artistic production suspended; material culture destroyed in conflict; indigenous dress, symbols become markers of rebellion; post-rebellion prohibitions target material culture.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Indigenous communities experience hope then terror; creole and Spanish populations experience fear and vengeance; collective mood polarized between liberation aspiration and existential threat; disposition combative then traumatized.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Colonial legitimacy forms exhausted; rebellion reveals hollowness of protective rhetoric; meaning concentrated in survival and group solidarity; decay of colonial symbolic order.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Andean messianic expectations activated; Inca return as cosmic restoration; Catholic framing deployed by both sides; existential stakes maximized; outlook apocalyptic.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mass, confession before battle; indigenous ritual elements possible but underdocumented; sacred practice channeled into warfare; Túpac Amaru's execution ritualized as colonial sacred violence.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Church hierarchy supports colonial state; some lower clergy sympathize with rebels; institutional authority deployed for suppression; bishops excommunicate rebels.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: No diffusion during conflict; syncretic Andean Christianity frames rebel and royalist worldviews alike; no displacement occurs.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Sacred potency maximally activated; disenchantment absent; rebellion is sacred discharge of accumulated grievance through violence; transcendence sought in Inca restoration; sacred energy fully mobilized.
