    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The North European Plain (1000 CE – 1500 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 1000 CE – 1500 CE

    Region: The North European Plain — the lowland corridor stretching from the Low Countries through northern Germany to Poland and the Baltic; including Flanders, the Rhineland, Saxony, Brandenburg, Prussia, and the Hanseatic trading zone; bounded by the North Sea, Baltic Sea, and the central European highlands.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes monastic chronicles, episcopal registers, urban charters, guild records, Hanseatic correspondence, manorial accounts, and university manuscripts. Latin dominant for ecclesiastical and scholarly use; Middle Low German for Hanseatic commerce; vernacular languages (Dutch, German, Polish) emerging in legal and literary documents. Parchment standard through 13th century; paper spreads from 14th century. Window begins with Christianization consolidating and urban growth accelerating; ends with print (Gutenberg ~1450s) transforming the region's communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Ottonian Twilight and Salian Consolidation (1000–1100 CE)

    Late Ottonians: Otto III (d. 1002); Henry II (1002–1024); imperial church system; episcopal appointments.

    Salian Dynasty: Conrad II (1024–1039); Henry III, IV; imperial authority; papal conflict begins.

    Monastic Reform: Gorze, Hirsau reforms; standardized liturgy; manuscript copying; scriptoria active.

    Imperial Church System: Bishops as administrators; cathedral schools; Ottonian Renaissance legacy.

    Investiture Controversy Begins: Gregory VII; Henry IV; Canossa 1077; conflict escalates.

    Manuscript Culture: Latin dominant; liturgical books; chronicles (Thietmar, Adam of Bremen).

    2. Investiture Settlement and Cistercian Expansion (1100–1150 CE)

    Concordat of Worms: 1122; compromise on investiture; church-state settlement.

    Cistercian Order: Bernard of Clairvaux; frontier expansion; land clearance; colonization.

    Frontier Missions: Wendish Crusade 1147; Slavic borderlands; conversion; settlement.

    Cathedral Schools: Cologne, Magdeburg, Hildesheim; education; Latin learning.

    Chronicle Writing: Annals; local histories; episcopal records.

    Agricultural Expansion: Forest clearance; marsh drainage; village foundations.

    3. Hohenstaufen Ascendancy and Urban Charters (1150–1200 CE)

    Frederick Barbarossa: 1152–1190; imperial ambitions; Italian campaigns; German consolidation.

    City Foundations: Lübeck refounded 1143/1159; Munich 1158; trade towns; market rights.

    Lübeck Law: Urban legal model; spreads to Baltic towns; standardized governance.

    Henry the Lion: Saxony, Bavaria; Slavic frontier; Brunswick; fall 1180.

    Trade Growth: Rhineland-Baltic connections; cloth, grain, fish; merchant networks.

    Charter Proliferation: Town privileges; monastic grants; land transactions; documentary culture.

    4. German Eastward Expansion (1200–1250 CE)

    Ostsiedlung Peak: German colonists; Slavic/Baltic territories; planned towns; village foundations.

    Teutonic Order: Founded 1190; Prussia from 1230; crusading state; Marienburg; administrative records.

    Colonial Towns: Riga (1201); Königsberg (1255); Magdeburg law; urban networks.

    Silesian Colonization: Mongol invasion 1241; recovery; German settlement; mining development.

    Frederick II: 1212–1250; distant emperor; princes gain autonomy; territorial fragmentation.

    Documentary Administration: Teutonic Order records; town charters; colonial grants.

    5. Interregnum and Hanseatic Origins (1250–1300 CE)

    Interregnum: 1254–1273; no effective emperor; territorial princes; local power.

    Rudolf of Habsburg: 1273–1291; minimal northern engagement; Austrian focus.

    Hanseatic Emergence: Merchant associations; Lübeck leadership; privileges negotiated.

    Baltic Trade: Novgorod, Bergen, Bruges kontors; grain, furs, fish, cloth.

    Territorial Consolidation: Brandenburg, Saxony, Bavaria; princely administration; chanceries.

    Urban Leagues: Rhenish League 1254; defensive associations; coordinated diplomacy.

    6. Early Fourteenth Century Crisis (1300–1350 CE)

    Great Famine: 1315–1317; crop failures; mortality; economic disruption.

    Black Death: 1347–1351; ~30–50% mortality; labor shortage; social upheaval.

    Flagellant Movement: Penitential processions; persecution of Jews; social panic.

    Ludwig IV and Papacy: Imperial-papal conflict; Avignon; political instability.

    Golden Bull: 1356 (just after window); electoral procedures; constitutional framework.

    Economic Contraction: Trade disruption; urban population decline; restructuring.

    7. Post-Plague Recovery and Hanseatic Zenith (1350–1400 CE)

    Labor Shortage: Wage increases; peasant mobility; landlord adjustments.

    Hanseatic Peak: Treaty of Stralsund 1370; Denmark humbled; commercial dominance.

    Kontor System: London, Bruges, Bergen, Novgorod; extraterritorial privileges; correspondence networks.

    Urban Culture: Brick Gothic architecture; town halls; guild prosperity; burgher literacy.

    Teutonic Order Peak: Prussian state; revenues; Marienburg; decline begins.

    Vernacular Writing: Middle Low German commercial; legal documents; literature.

    8. Fifteenth Century Transformations (1400–1450 CE)

    Council of Constance: 1414–1418; Great Schism resolved; Hus burned; reform debates.

    Hussite Wars: 1419–1434; Bohemian conflict; military innovation; regional impact.

    Teutonic Order Decline: Tannenberg 1410; Polish-Lithuanian victory; territorial losses.

    Territorial Consolidation: Saxony, Brandenburg, Burgundian Netherlands; princely states.

    Universities Founded: Rostock 1419; Greifswald 1456; regional learning centers.

    Paper Adoption: Cheaper than parchment; spreads from south; facilitates documentation.

    9. Print Revolution (1450–1500 CE)

    Gutenberg: Mainz; movable type; Bible ~1455; print technology spreads.

    Print Centers: Cologne, Strasbourg, Nuremberg, Basel; rapid adoption; incunabula.

    Humanist Influence: Northern humanism; classical texts; educational reform.

    Burgundian-Habsburg Politics: Charles the Bold; Maximilian I; Netherlands; diplomatic correspondence.

    Hanseatic Challenges: English, Dutch competition; kontor pressures; adaptation.

    Manuscript Twilight: Print displaces manuscript for many purposes; luxury and specialized use continues.

    10. Hanseatic Network (1000–1500 CE)

    Origins: Merchant associations; Gotland traders; 12th century; formal league by 14th.

    Kontor System: Bergen, Bruges, London, Novgorod; privileged communities; documented trade.

    Commodity Trade: Grain, timber, furs, fish, cloth, salt; Baltic-Atlantic connections.

    Commercial Correspondence: Middle Low German; merchant letters; account books; contracts.

    Legal Framework: Hanseatic recesses; arbitration; standardized practices; documentation.

    Decline Begins: 15th century; competition; political fragmentation; print era.

    11. Monastic Scriptoria and Universities (1000–1500 CE)

    Monastic Copying: Benedictine, Cistercian houses; liturgical, patristic, classical texts.

    Cathedral Schools: Cologne, Magdeburg, Hildesheim; clerical education; Latin learning.

    University Foundations: Prague 1348; Vienna 1365; Heidelberg 1386; Cologne 1388; Leipzig 1409.

    Scholastic Texts: Theology, law, medicine; manuscript production; student copying.

    Vernacular Emergence: Legal texts, devotional literature; German, Dutch, Polish.

    Print Transition: Universities early adopters; scholarly publishing; manuscript supplemented.

    12. Towns, Charters, and Urban Literacy (1000–1500 CE)

    Urban Growth: 11th–13th centuries; market towns; episcopal seats; planned foundations.

    Charter Proliferation: Town privileges; market rights; legal autonomy; documentary basis.

    Guild Records: Craft regulations; membership lists; apprenticeship documents.

    Burgher Literacy: Merchant correspondence; notarial acts; wills; legal competence.

    Town Archives: Preserved charters; council minutes; court records; administrative memory.

    Legal Standardization: Lübeck, Magdeburg law families; codified customs; written norms.
