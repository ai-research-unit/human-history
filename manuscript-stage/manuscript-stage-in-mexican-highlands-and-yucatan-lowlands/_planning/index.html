    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Mexican Highlands and Yucatan Lowlands (400 BCE – 1810 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 400 BCE – 1810 CE

    Region: The Mexican Highlands and Yucatan Lowlands — the central Mexican plateau (Valley of Mexico, Oaxaca), the Gulf Coast lowlands, and the Maya region of the Yucatan Peninsula, Chiapas, Guatemala highlands and Peten lowlands; the core zones of Mesoamerican civilization.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Maya hieroglyphic inscriptions (stelae, codices, ceramics), Zapotec script, Aztec pictographic manuscripts (codices), Spanish colonial documents, and missionary-indigenous collaborative texts. Maya script fully developed by ~300 BCE–100 CE; logosyllabic system; Aztec pictographic-calendrical system different in structure. Spanish alphabetic literacy imposed from 1519; colonial administrative manuscripts; some indigenous traditions recorded in Latin script. Window begins with mature Mesoamerican writing; ends as independence movements and nascent print transform colonial administration.

    Note on Writing Systems: Mesoamerica developed indigenous writing independently of Old World systems. Maya hieroglyphic script was logosyllabic, capable of recording full language. Aztec writing was primarily pictographic/ideographic with phonetic elements, used for historical, tribute, and calendrical records. Both systems qualified as external record-keeping media enabling institutional persistence. Post-conquest, Spanish alphabetic literacy became administrative standard, with indigenous codex traditions continuing in modified form.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Preclassic Maya (400 BCE – 250 CE)

    Maya Script Emergence: Logosyllabic writing develops by ~300 BCE; earliest inscriptions fragmentary; San Bartolo murals.

    Early Kingdoms: Kaminaljuyu (Guatemala highlands); El Mirador (Peten); monumental architecture; political hierarchy.

    Calendrical Systems: Long Count calendar; tzolk'in/haab cycles; astronomical observation; inscriptional dating.

    Epi-Olmec/Isthmian Script: Gulf Coast writing; La Mojarra stela; related but distinct tradition.

    Zapotec Script: Monte Alban; early writing in Oaxaca; conquest slabs; calendrical notation.

    Material Culture: Stucco facades; jade; cacao; long-distance trade; emerging complexity.

    2. Early Classic Maya (250–550 CE)

    Tikal: Major lowland center; Siyaj K'ahk' event 378 CE (Teotihuacan connection); dynastic records.

    Calakmul: Rival superpower; Snake Kingdom; diplomatic alliances documented.

    Teotihuacan Influence: Central Mexican metropolis; interaction/intervention in Maya politics; artistic influence.

    Diplomatic Texts: Emblem glyphs; royal visits; warfare accounts; tributary relationships.

    Copan: Southeastern Maya; elaborate hieroglyphic stairway; sculptural tradition.

    Scribal Class: Ah tz'ib (scribes); elite status; codex production (none survive from Classic).

    3. Late Classic Maya Florescence (550–800 CE)

    Monument Proliferation: Peak stela, altar, lintel production; dynastic propaganda; historical records.

    Palenque: K'inich Janaab Pakal; Temple of Inscriptions; elaborate texts; Pakal's sarcophagus.

    Regional Kingdoms: Yaxchilan, Piedras Negras, Quirigua; competitive polities; marriage alliances.

    Artistic Splendor: Painted ceramics; murals (Bonampak); calligraphic refinement.

    Warfare Documentation: Capture and sacrifice records; star-war events; political competition.

    Codex Production: Screenfold books (bark paper); none survive from Classic period; inferred from ceramics.

    4. Classic Maya Collapse (800–1000 CE)

    Political Fragmentation: Major centers abandoned; monument erection ceases; royal authority collapses.

    Depopulation: Southern lowland population decline; causes debated (drought, warfare, ecological stress).

    Terminal Classic: Puuc region (Uxmal) continues; Seibal brief revival; transitional forms.

    Migration Patterns: Population shifts to northern Yucatan, highlands; coastal trade routes.

    Script Continuity: Writing tradition persists in Postclassic, but monument production ends.

    Highland Continuity: Guatemalan highlands, Oaxaca less disrupted; continued settlement.

    5. Early Postclassic (1000–1200 CE)

    Chichen Itza: Major center; "Toltec" influence debated; militaristic iconography; sacred cenote.

    Tula: Toltec capital; Central Mexico; historical/mythological importance; warrior columns.

    Militaristic States: Shift from divine kingship toward military orders; feathered serpent cult.

    Highland-Lowland Interactions: Trade networks; artistic exchange; political connections.

    Aztec Migration: Mexica leave Aztlan (mythological); wandering period; tributary relationships.

    Mixtec Codices: Oaxacan pictographic manuscripts; genealogies; historical narratives; some survive.

    6. Late Postclassic (1200–1428 CE)

    Mayapan: Successor to Chichen Itza; confederation; falls ~1441; fragmentation follows.

    Aztec Migration Continues: Mexica reach Valley of Mexico; subordinate to Azcapotzalco; Tenochtitlan founded 1325.

    Triple Alliance Formation: 1428; Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, Tlacopan defeat Azcapotzalco; Aztec empire begins.

    Codex Traditions: Tribute lists; calendrical records; historical screenfolds; pictographic system.

    K'iche' Maya: Highland Guatemala; Popol Vuh oral tradition (later transcribed); regional power.

    Political Fragmentation (Maya): Yucatan divided among competing lineages; Xiu, Cocom rivalries.

    7. Aztec Empire (1428–1519 CE)

    Imperial Expansion: Moctezuma I, II; tribute empire; military conquests; flower wars.

    Tribute Administration: Codex Mendoza records tribute; provincial organization; pictographic tallies.

    Pictographic Codices: Tonalamatl (ritual almanacs); historical annals; tribute lists; map-like documents.

    Calendar Systems: Xiuhpohualli (365-day), tonalpohualli (260-day); calendrical manuscripts.

    Tenochtitlan: Island capital; chinampas; population ~200,000+; administrative center.

    Scribal Practice: Tlacuilo (painter-scribes); amatl bark paper; elite training; temple archives.

    8. Spanish Conquest (1519–1550 CE)

    Cortes: 1519 landing; alliance with Tlaxcalans; siege of Tenochtitlan 1521; Moctezuma death.

    Tenochtitlan Fall: August 1521; city destroyed; Aztec state collapses; epidemic mortality begins.

    Maya Conquest: Yucatan conquest prolonged; Montejo campaigns 1527–1546; resistance continues.

    Encomienda System: Labor grants; tributary extraction; indigenous records continue.

    Missionary Campaigns: Franciscans, Dominicans, Augustinians; schools; alphabetic literacy introduced.

    Dual Documentation: Spanish records; indigenous codices continue; transitional manuscripts.

    9. Colonial Consolidation (1550–1650 CE)

    Viceregal Administration: New Spain established; audiencias; tribute collection; Spanish manuscript bureaucracy.

    Nahuatl Alphabetic Texts: Indigenous languages written in Latin script; Florentine Codex; Sahagún's informants.

    Codex Traditions Adapt: Post-conquest codices (Codex Mendoza); hybrid pictographic-alphabetic documents.

    Congregación: Forced resettlement; parish records; administrative documentation.

    Legal Documents: Land titles; wills; petitions in Nahuatl and Spanish; notarial culture.

    Church Records: Baptism, marriage, death registers; extensive parish documentation.

    10. Mature Colonial Society (1650–1750 CE)

    Silver Economy: Zacatecas, Guanajuato mining; colonial wealth; administrative records.

    Religious Manuscript Production: Convent libraries; theological texts; hagiographies.

    Creole Identity: American-born Spanish; literary production; proto-national consciousness.

    Maya Chilam Balam: Books of Chilam Balam; Maya prophetic/historical texts in alphabetic script.

    Indigenous Languages Persist: Nahuatl, Maya, Zapotec continue; church uses for evangelization.

    Baroque Culture: Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz; literary florescence; manuscript circulation.

    11. Bourbon Reforms (1750–1810 CE)

    Administrative Reform: Intendancy system; increased taxation; economic restructuring.

    Enlightenment Currents: Scientific expeditions; natural history; reform ideas; manuscript reports.

    Expulsion of Jesuits: 1767; institutional disruption; library dispersal.

    Creole Grievances: Peninsular-creole tensions; economic restrictions; political marginalization.

    Print Expansion: Gazettes; limited but growing print culture; administrative modernization.

    Hidalgo Revolt: 1810; independence movement begins; colonial order challenged; manuscript twilight.

    12. Codex Traditions (400 BCE – 1810 CE)

    Pre-Columbian Codices: Screenfold books; bark paper or deer hide; Maya, Aztec, Mixtec traditions.

    Surviving Pre-Columbian Maya Codices: Dresden, Madrid, Paris codices; astronomical, ritual content.

    Aztec-Style Codices: Pre-conquest examples lost; colonial copies/continuations (Codex Borbonicus, etc.).

    Mixtec Codices: Codex Zouche-Nuttall, Codex Vindobonensis; genealogical histories; survive from Postclassic.

    Colonial Codices: Codex Mendoza; Florentine Codex; hybrid traditions; administrative/historical purposes.

    Continuity and Transformation: Indigenous scribal traditions adapt; pictographic and alphabetic merge.
