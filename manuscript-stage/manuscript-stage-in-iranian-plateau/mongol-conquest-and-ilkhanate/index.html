    1220 CE – 1335 CE — Mongol Conquest and Ilkhanate

    Summary: A long-term trend comprising initial catastrophic conquest followed by gradual stabilization and recovery under Mongol Ilkhanid rule. Chinggis Khan's invasion (1219–1221) devastates the Iranian Plateau — mass killings, urban destruction, irrigation collapse — producing demographic catastrophe and economic rupture; the subsequent Ilkhanate (formalized under Hulegu from 1256) rebuilds administrative capacity, restores trade, and eventually converts to Islam (Ghazan, 1295), while presiding over a remarkable cultural synthesis that fuses Mongol, Persian, Chinese, and Islamic elements. The binding constraint is the demographic deficit and agrarian devastation inherited from conquest, which takes generations to repair and limits surplus extraction throughout the window; the Ilkhanate's fiscal crisis recurs despite trade revival. Direction of change moves from destruction toward recovery: population slowly regrows, cities are rebuilt, agriculture partially restored, and cultural production — notably historiography (Rashid al-Din) and miniature painting — reaches new heights. The window closes with Ilkhanate disintegration (Abu Said's death, 1335), leaving successor states to inherit a partially recovered but still diminished plateau.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Iranian Plateau retains its elevated topography between Zagros, Hindu Kush, and Central Asian steppes; conquest devastates cities along caravan corridors (Merv, Nishapur, Balkh, Herat) and damages qanat irrigation systems; spatial friction increases sharply during invasion, then gradually decreases as Ilkhanate restores roads and caravanserais.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Semi-arid to arid conditions persist; no major documented climatic shift during the window; seasonal variability continues as normal; agricultural recovery constrained by water infrastructure damage rather than climate change.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seismic risk unchanged; major earthquake in 1252 damages Tabriz; destruction from conquest far exceeds natural hazard exposure; rebuilt cities use traditional materials offering limited margin against future shocks.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Mineral resources undiminished but mining disrupted; timber and fuel stocks stressed near destroyed cities where reconstruction demands materials; pastoral lands less affected than agricultural; overall endowment intact but access disrupted.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban destruction paradoxically reduces waste loads; reconstruction generates debris; absorptive capacity remains adequate relative to diminished population and production; no documented sink saturation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Conquest mortality catastrophic — contemporary sources report mass killings at Merv, Nishapur, and elsewhere, though figures are likely exaggerated; disease outbreaks follow displacement and famine; baseline mortality remains elevated through early decades, gradually normalizing; Black Death (1340s) falls just after window close.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Famine conditions during and immediately after conquest; agricultural collapse reduces food supply; pastoral production less disrupted; nutritional status severely degraded in invaded regions, recovering unevenly; vitality restored in later decades but below pre-conquest baseline.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Marriage patterns disrupted by population loss and sex ratio distortion (male combat deaths); gradual normalization under stable Ilkhanid rule; reproductive capacity constrained by nutritional stress in early decades; no structural change in mating norms.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Catastrophic population decline — estimates suggest 10–15% of pre-conquest population survives in worst-hit regions; urban populations decimated; recovery slow, taking generations; density remains below pre-conquest levels throughout the window; demographic deficit the master constraint on the system.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: War widows, orphans, and displaced persons overwhelm traditional support systems during invasion; waqf institutions partly resume function under Ilkhanate; dependency load gradually absorbed but leaves lasting social scarring.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Agricultural collapse follows qanat destruction and peasant massacre/flight; famine conditions in early decades; gradual recovery as irrigation rebuilt and peasant population returns; floor remains precariously low through mid-window, stabilizing thereafter but not reaching pre-conquest levels.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Animal and human muscle remain primary; draft animal stocks depleted by conquest, slowly rebuilt; water-power infrastructure (mills) damaged and rebuilt; no efficiency change; per-capita energy ceiling constrained by labor shortage.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Peasant labor force devastated; corvée demands for reconstruction; artisan populations reduced but survivors protected when useful to Mongol patrons; labor shortage elevates surviving workers' position; coercion levels high during invasion, normalizing under stable rule.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Production collapses during invasion; gradual recovery of agriculture, textile manufacture, and crafts; surplus capacity limited by demographic deficit; manuscript production and luxury crafts recover faster under court patronage than bulk production.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Granaries looted and destroyed; treasury reserves seized; reserves slowly rebuilt under Ilkhanate; waqf endowments pillaged but partly reconstituted; buffering capacity remains thin throughout the window, leaving little margin against crop failure.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Initial trade collapse; Pax Mongolica subsequently enables trans-Asian commerce on unprecedented scale; Tabriz becomes major emporium; caravanserai network rebuilt; east-west trade (silk, spices, textiles) flourishes; commercial networks expanding in the window's later phase.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Currency disrupted — Mongol paper money (chao) attempted under Geikhatu (1294), fails immediately; precious metal coinage resumes; credit instruments continue among merchants; debasement and fiscal instability recurrent; value storage insecure through much of the window.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Heavy extraction under Mongol tax-farming (ortaq merchants); Ghazan's reforms (1295–1304) regularize taxation and reduce predatory extraction; fiscal crisis endemic due to small tax base against large military obligations; extraction burden heavy relative to diminished productive capacity.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Pre-conquest wealth holders largely eliminated; Mongol commanders and collaborating Persian administrators acquire estates; merchant wealth grows with trade revival; waqf offers some asset protection; new distribution highly unequal but fluid.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Reduced production generates less waste; reconstruction debris the main waste stream early in window; traditional disposal patterns resume; no accumulation crisis; capacity adequate relative to diminished loads.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust networks shattered by conquest — families dispersed, communities destroyed, institutions pillaged; gradual rebuilding under Ilkhanid stability; Sufi orders provide crucial network for survival and mutual aid; trust radius slowly widening but not reaching pre-conquest levels.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: New elite structure — Mongol military aristocracy at apex, Persian administrators and merchants below, surviving population at base; ethnic hierarchy (Mongol > Turkic > Persian > others) institutionalized; religious diversity tolerated until Ghazan's conversion; boundaries hardening then softening with Islamization.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Family structures disrupted by demographic catastrophe; Islamic inheritance norms persist among Muslim population; Mongol customs influence elite; gradual normalization of kinship patterns under stable rule.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Urban institutions devastated; guilds, futuwwa brotherhoods reconstitute slowly; Sufi lodges as primary associational nodes during recovery; neighborhood structures rebuild in restored cities; associational fabric thinned but recovering.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Mongol festivals (quriltai gatherings) added to calendar; traditional festivals (Nowruz) continue; hunting as elite leisure; public spaces gradually restored; sociability recovering in tandem with urban life.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Mongol yasa law and darughachi officials impose order; mazalim courts resume under Islamized Ilkhanate; Sufi lodges absorb displaced; grievance channels thin but functioning; residual trauma from conquest era unmetabolized as standing social wound.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Ilkhanate established under Hulegu (1256) as subordinate to Great Khan in Karakorum, later increasingly autonomous; authority concentrated in Ilkhan with Mongol military commanders and Persian viziers; power centralizing under strong rulers (Hulegu, Ghazan, Öljaitü), fragmenting during succession disputes.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Succession contested — no clear primogeniture; succession crises frequent (1284, 1291, 1316, 1335); short reigns common; factionalism among Mongol nobility; power transfer a persistent destabilizer; system collapsing after Abu Said (1335).

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Chinggisid descent as primary legitimacy; post-conversion (1295), Islamic legitimation added; Persian administrative ideology (just rule, mirrors literature) absorbed; dual legitimacy sources (Mongol and Islamic) somewhat stabilizing; legitimacy eroding with fiscal crisis and succession instability.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Initially predatory extraction; Rashid al-Din and other Persian viziers rebuild bureaucratic capacity; tax surveys, record-keeping, provincial administration restored; capacity improving through Ghazan's reforms but never reaching Seljuk levels; corruption endemic; integrity compromised by fiscal desperation.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Elite politics among Mongol emirs and Persian officials; ulama regain influence after conversion; no mass participation; urban populations occasionally mobilized in factional conflicts; channels narrow but stable.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Ilkhanate controls Iran, Iraq, Azerbaijan, parts of Anatolia and Afghanistan; hostile relations with Golden Horde (north), intermittent conflict with Chagatai Khanate (east); alliance sought with Crusader states and Byzantium against Mamluks; geopolitical position strong but contested on multiple fronts.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Mongol cavalry the military core — composite bow, horse archery, siege warfare; tumen organization; Persian auxiliary forces; fortification captured or destroyed during conquest, rebuilt selectively; command concentrated in Ilkhan and noyan commanders; military capacity dominant but expensive to maintain.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Order imposed brutally during conquest; yasa law enforces discipline; banditry suppressed in core territories; Ismaili Assassin strongholds (including Alamut) destroyed (1256); internal order generally maintained but fraying with late-window fragmentation; margin adequate under strong Ilkhans.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Mongol yam (postal relay) system improves transmission velocity across the empire; courier networks connect Tabriz to Karakorum and China; caravan routes restored; information moves faster than in pre-Mongol period; access primarily administrative and commercial elite.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Administrative records maintained in Persian; historical writing flourishes — Rashid al-Din's Jami' al-tawarikh (universal history) exemplifies the window; manuscript production active in court ateliers; Chinese techniques influence book production; libraries rebuilt; persistence improving.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Court historiography celebrates Mongol rulers; after conversion, Islamic framing added; religious discourse more open before 1295 (Buddhism, Christianity, Islam, shamanism all present at court); post-conversion Sunni norms prevail; permitted band fairly wide for scholarly and historical writing.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Literate elite expanded by court patronage; Persian and Arabic scholarly networks connected to wider Mongol world; illustrated manuscripts reach new audiences; amplification through court copying and distribution.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Administrative record-keeping improves under Persian viziers; scholarly standards maintained; contradictory reports from conquest era difficult to reconcile; noise manageable; verification capacity adequate for administrative purposes.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Conquest ruptures moral order; Mongol yasa law imposed alongside Sharia; post-conversion, Islamic justice norms reasserted; justice concept includes both Mongol customary and Islamic elements; moral foundations slowly restabilizing.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Dual legal system — yasa (Mongol customary law) and Sharia; yasa governs Mongol military and political offenses; Sharia for Muslims in personal and commercial matters; qadi courts resume; Ghazan's reforms codify tax and administrative law; legal framework stabilizing after conversion.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Darughachi (Mongol governors) enforce yasa; qadi courts adjudicate Sharia matters; mazalim courts for administrative grievances; enforcement capacity adequate in urban centers; liability assigned through respective legal systems.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Property rights insecure during early decades, improving under stable rule; merchant protections facilitate trade; dhimmi status continues for non-Muslims; compliance burden heavy due to tax demands; predictability improving but never fully reliable.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Conquest atrocities unpunished — no redress for mass killing, enslavement, property seizure; powerful Mongol commanders sometimes act with impunity; ordinary justice accessible after stabilization; significant gap between wrongs committed and justice delivered; conquest-era impunity never addressed.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Intellectual activity continues despite destruction — Nasir al-Din Tusi's Maragha observatory (founded 1259) advances astronomy; philosophical and theological inquiry active; engagement with Chinese and European knowledge via Mongol networks; paradigm remains Islamic-Aristotelian with active research frontier in astronomy and mathematics.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Chinese techniques (papermaking, printing experiments, ceramic glazes) transmitted; architectural methods adapted (new decorative styles); agricultural techniques for qanat repair preserved; applied knowledge stable with some diffusion gains.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Madrasa system disrupted but reconstituted; Maragha observatory and associated library as research center; manuscript production restores and expands knowledge stock; literacy remains elite but network of educated administrators maintained; skill distribution recovering.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Manuscript losses during conquest significant — libraries burned at Merv, Nishapur, and elsewhere; some knowledge permanently lost; active copying under Ilkhanate compensates partially; philosophical tradition continues but some practical knowledge (irrigation engineering) suffers skill attrition; net knowledge stock slowly recovering but not reaching pre-conquest levels.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Iranian identity persists — Shahnameh tradition continues; trauma of conquest enters collective memory as civilizational catastrophe; Mongols gradually absorbed into Persian cultural narrative; identity wounded but not displaced; recovery narrative emerging under stable Ilkhanate.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Miniature painting reaches new heights — Ilkhanid school synthesizes Persian, Chinese, and Mongol elements; architectural innovation (Sultaniyya mausoleum); Persian poetry continues (Sa'di of Shiraz active); manuscript illustration flourishes; material culture experiencing creative synthesis despite devastation.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Traumatized during conquest; gradually recovering under stable rule; cautious optimism in later decades; underlying anxiety about Mongol unpredictability; mood lifting but never carefree; darkening again with late-window instability.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: No form exhaustion — crisis generates creative response; new synthesis of visual traditions; Persian literary forms remain vital; Sufi poetry expanding; cultural meaning actively renewed through synthesis; exhaustion absent from this window.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Islamic worldview dominant among Persian population; Mongol rulers initially practice Buddhism, shamanism, Christianity, or remain non-affiliated; Ghazan's conversion (1295) establishes Sunni Islam as state religion; fatalism reinforced by conquest experience; piety and submission to divine will predominant outlook.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Islamic ritual practice continues among Muslim population; Buddhist temples and Christian churches present under early Ilkhanate; post-1295 Islamic practice becomes official; Sufi devotion and shrine visitation intensify; ritual infrastructure rebuilt.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Ulama institutions disrupted but reconstitute; waqf endowments pillaged then restored; post-conversion clerical authority grows; Sunni orthodoxy promoted; Rashid al-Din's religious endowments fund institutions; authority consolidating in later decades.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Remarkable religious diversity under pre-conversion Ilkhanate — Buddhism at court, Nestorian Christianity, Islam, shamanism coexist; Ghazan's conversion tips balance decisively toward Islam; Sufi orders spread; Buddhism and Christianity marginalized; Islam's dominance secured by window's end.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No secularization; conquest interpreted as divine punishment in Islamic theodicy; Sufi spirituality offers consolation and meaning; sacred remains fully operative; disenchantment absent; spiritual need addressed through intensified devotion and Sufi practice.
