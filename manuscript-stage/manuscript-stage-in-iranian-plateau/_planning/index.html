    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Iranian Plateau (550 BCE – 1850 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 550 BCE – 1850 CE

    Region: The Iranian Plateau — the elevated landmass between Mesopotamia and Central Asia, including modern Iran, western Afghanistan, and adjacent regions; bounded by the Zagros Mountains, Hindu Kush, and surrounding lowlands; encompassing diverse ecological zones from desert to mountain.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Achaemenid royal inscriptions (Old Persian cuneiform), Aramaic administrative documents, Parthian parchments, Sasanian Middle Persian texts (Pahlavi), Arabic manuscripts, New Persian literature, and Qajar-era documents. Old Persian cuneiform created ~520 BCE; Aramaic as imperial lingua franca; Pahlavi for Zoroastrian and administrative texts; Arabic script adopted after Islamic conquest; New Persian literature flourishes from 9th century; paper widespread by 10th century. Window begins with Achaemenid imperial writing; ends as Qajar reforms and print technology transform public discourse (~1850s).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Achaemenid Empire (550–330 BCE)

    Cyrus the Great: ~559–530 BCE; conquers Media, Lydia, Babylon; imperial foundation; tolerance policy.

    Darius I: ~522–486 BCE; administrative consolidation; Behistun inscription; satrapies; Royal Road.

    Old Persian Cuneiform: Created ~520 BCE for royal inscriptions; limited use; prestige script.

    Aramaic Chancery: Imperial Aramaic as administrative lingua franca; extensive documentary use; Elephantine archive.

    Persepolis Tablets: Administrative records in Elamite, Aramaic; economic management; bureaucratic detail.

    Imperial Ideology: Ahuramazda worship; king of kings; universal claims; monumental architecture.

    2. Hellenistic Period (330–141 BCE)

    Alexander's Conquest: 334–323 BCE; Achaemenid defeat; Persepolis burned; rapid conquest.

    Seleucid Rule: Seleucus I and successors; Greek administration; new cities; cultural fusion.

    Greek Language: Administrative and literary use; bilingualism; Hellenization variable by region.

    Bactrian Independence: ~250 BCE; Greek kingdom; eastward orientation; eventually Indo-Greek.

    Parthian Rise: Arsaces; ~247 BCE independence; gradual expansion; displaces Seleucids.

    Religious Continuity: Zoroastrian practice continues; syncretic trends; Iranian identity persists.

    3. Parthian Empire (141 BCE – 224 CE)

    Mithridates I: ~171–138 BCE; conquers Mesopotamia; empire established; Ctesiphon capital.

    Arsacid Dynasty: Decentralized rule; feudal nobility; Greek cultural legacy; Iranian traditions.

    Parchment Documents: Avroman documents; legal texts; Greek and Parthian; documentary practice.

    Silk Road: East-West trade; Chinese contacts; commercial networks; wealth flow.

    Greek-Iranian Synthesis: Philhellenic kings; bilingual inscriptions; cultural hybridity.

    Religious Diversity: Zoroastrianism, Greek gods, local cults; tolerance; no state religion.

    4. Sasanian Empire (224–651 CE)

    Ardashir I: 224 CE overthrows Parthians; Sasanian dynasty; centralized state.

    Zoroastrian State: Fire temples; Magi priesthood; Avesta codification; religious orthodoxy.

    Pahlavi Script: Middle Persian; Book Pahlavi for religious/literary texts; Inscriptional Pahlavi.

    Administrative Texts: Royal inscriptions; land grants; legal documents; bureaucratic apparatus.

    Conflict with Rome/Byzantium: Centuries of warfare; Mesopotamian frontier; hostage exchanges.

    Khosrow I Anushirvan: ~531–579 CE; administrative reforms; academy of Gondishapur; cultural peak.

    5. Arab Conquest and Early Islam (651–820 CE)

    Islamic Conquest: 636–651 CE; Qadisiyya, Nihavand; Yazdegerd III killed; Sasanian collapse.

    Umayyad Rule: Arabic administration gradually replaces Pahlavi; conversion begins; dhimmi status.

    Abbasid Revolution: 750 CE; Iranian support (Khorasan); Baghdad founded; Persian influence at court.

    Conversion Process: Gradual Islamization; mawali system; social incentives; Zoroastrian minority persists.

    Arabic Script: Replaces Pahlavi for administration; Persian vocabulary enters Arabic.

    Translation Movement: Greek, Persian texts into Arabic; Pahlavi literature translated; cultural synthesis.

    6. Iranian Dynasties and Persian Revival (820–1040 CE)

    Tahirids, Saffarids: 9th century; eastern Iran; increasing autonomy; Persian patronage.

    Samanid Dynasty: ~819–999 CE; Bukhara capital; Persian literary renaissance; Rudaki, Ferdowsi.

    Ferdowsi's Shahnameh: ~1010 CE; national epic in New Persian; manuscript tradition; cultural landmark.

    Buyid Dynasty: ~934–1062 CE; Shi'i rulers; Baghdad control; Persian administration; manuscript patronage.

    New Persian Literature: Arabic script; Persian language; poetry, prose, science; literary explosion.

    Paper Widespread: Samarkand paper manufacture; replaces parchment; manuscript production expands.

    7. Turkic Rule and Persianate Synthesis (1040–1220 CE)

    Ghaznavid Dynasty: ~977–1186; Mahmud of Ghazni; India raids; Persian court culture.

    Seljuk Empire: 1040–1194; Turkish military; Persian administration; Nizam al-Mulk; madrasas.

    Khwarezmshah Empire: ~1077–1231; Central Asian power; Gurganj capital; pre-Mongol peak.

    Persianate Culture: Turkish-Persian synthesis; administrative Persian; Islamic learning; architecture.

    Sufi Orders: Mystical poetry; Rumi (later, but traditions develop); manuscript circulation.

    Scientific Achievement: Omar Khayyam; astronomy, mathematics; Persian scientific writing.

    8. Mongol Conquest and Ilkhanate (1220–1335 CE)

    Chinggis Khan's Invasion: 1219–1221; Khwarezm destroyed; mass destruction; population collapse.

    Ilkhanate: Hulegu; 1256 onward; Mongol rule of Iran; Tabriz capital; Buddhist then Islamic.

    Destruction and Recovery: Cities rebuilt; trade revives; Pax Mongolica facilitates exchange.

    Rashid al-Din: ~1247–1318; vizier, historian; Jami' al-tawarikh (world history); manuscript production.

    Conversion to Islam: Ghazan Khan (1295); state Islamization; Persian administration continues.

    Miniature Painting: Ilkhanid school; Chinese influence; manuscript illustration tradition.

    9. Post-Ilkhanid Fragmentation (1335–1501 CE)

    Ilkhanate Collapse: 1335; successor states; Jalairids, Muzaffarids, Kartids; political chaos.

    Timur: ~1370–1405; conquests; destruction; Samarkand capital; cultural patronage.

    Timurid Herat: 15th century; Sultan Husayn Bayqara; literary florescence; Jami; Bihzad miniatures.

    Turkoman Dynasties: Qara Qoyunlu, Aq Qoyunlu; western Iran; political competition.

    Shi'i Growth: Sufi orders; Ahl-e Haqq; messianic movements; Safavid order develops.

    Manuscript Culture: Court patronage; illustrated manuscripts; Persian literature golden age.

    10. Safavid Empire (1501–1629 CE)

    Ismail I: 1501; conquers Tabriz; declares Twelver Shi'ism state religion; forced conversion.

    Shi'i State: Clerical establishment; anti-Sunni policies; Safavid-Ottoman rivalry.

    Abbas I: 1588–1629; Isfahan capital; administrative reforms; trade development; peak power.

    Isfahan: New capital 1598; architectural splendor; Naqsh-e Jahan square; cultural florescence.

    Manuscript Production: Royal ateliers; illustrated Shahnameh copies; religious texts; administrative records.

    Armenian and European Trade: Julfa merchants; VOC, EIC contacts; silk exports; commercial growth.

    11. Late Safavid Decline (1629–1736 CE)

    Post-Abbas Weakness: Succession instability; harem politics; military decline; provincial autonomy.

    Afghan Revolt: 1709; Kandahar independent; Mahmud Hotaki; Isfahan falls 1722; Safavid collapse.

    Ottoman and Russian Pressures: Territorial losses; partition attempts; external threats.

    Nader Shah: ~1688–1747; expels Afghans; restores Iranian power; Indian campaign 1739.

    Manuscript Continuity: Despite political chaos; religious and literary production continues.

    Shi'i Consolidation: Clerical authority grows; Usuli-Akhbari debates; institutional development.

    12. Afsharid and Zand Interregnums (1736–1796 CE)

    Nader Shah: 1736–1747; Afsharid dynasty; conquests (Mughal India); assassination; chaos.

    Afghan Successors: Ahmad Shah Durrani; independent Afghanistan; eastern regions lost.

    Zand Dynasty: Karim Khan Zand; 1751–1779; Shiraz capital; relative stability; prosperity.

    Post-Zand Fragmentation: Succession wars; Qajar advance; political turmoil.

    Provincial Autonomy: Regional powers; tribal confederation; weak central authority.

    Cultural Continuity: Persian literature, manuscript production persist despite instability.

    13. Qajar Consolidation (1796–1850 CE)

    Agha Muhammad Khan: 1789–1797; unifies Iran; brutal campaigns; Tehran capital; Qajar dynasty.

    Russian Wars: 1804–13, 1826–28; territorial losses (Caucasus); Turkmenchay Treaty; humiliation.

    Reform Attempts: Crown Prince Abbas Mirza; military modernization; limited success; structural resistance.

    European Contacts: British, Russian rivalry; diplomatic missions; translation projects.

    Print Introduction: 1817 first Persian press; gradual expansion; still limited by 1850.

    Manuscript Twilight: Traditional production continues; print slowly encroaches; modernization pressures.
