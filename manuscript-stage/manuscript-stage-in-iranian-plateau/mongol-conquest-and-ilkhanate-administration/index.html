    1220 CE – 1370 CE — Mongol Conquest and Ilkhanate Administration

    Summary: The Mongol conquest devastates the Iranian Plateau through systematic massacre and destruction of cities, yet Persian manuscript culture survives and revives under Ilkhanate patronage, as the binding constraint of the Manuscript Stage proves indispensable to rulers who require written records to govern a multi-ethnic empire: Mongol warlords who arrive illiterate in Persian adopt the Persian bureaucratic apparatus whole, commission universal histories in Persian, and ultimately convert to Islam, demonstrating that administrative dependence on manuscript outlasts even catastrophic rupture.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The plateau's geography that once protected Persian civilization now channels Mongol invasion: Khorasan falls first as armies sweep through the northeastern corridors, then advance westward through the plateau to Mesopotamia. Tabriz in Azerbaijan becomes the Ilkhanate capital, positioned to control both Iranian lands and routes to Anatolia and the Levant.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Semi-arid conditions persist; the Medieval Warm Period gives way to cooler and more variable conditions as the Little Ice Age begins. Irrigation systems damaged during the conquest reduce agricultural productivity; some areas experience partial recovery, others permanent decline.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: The 1336 CE earthquake destroys parts of Tabriz. Locust plagues and droughts compound difficulties during the early conquest period. Flash floods damage rebuilt infrastructure in piedmont zones.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Mining continues where the workforce survives; Mongol demand for precious metals intensifies extraction. Deforestation around surviving cities accelerates as construction and fuel needs deplete accessible timber. Paper production recovers as scriptoriums reconstitute.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Ruined cities become repositories of debris; unburied dead in the conquest's immediate aftermath create public health hazards. Damaged qanat systems allow saline groundwater intrusion in some lowland areas. Reduced population eases pressure on waste absorption capacity.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Mass death from conquest violence exceeds epidemic mortality in the initial phase; subsequent population displacement facilitates disease spread. The Black Death (1346–1351 CE) sweeps through the Ilkhanate along trade routes, killing perhaps a third of surviving urban populations. Endemic disease baseline remains elevated throughout the period.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Agricultural disruption causes widespread famine in the conquest's aftermath; recovery is uneven across regions. Pastoral populations fare better than sedentary farmers. By mid-fourteenth century, reduced population pressure improves per-capita food availability for survivors.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Destruction of cities eliminates hospitals and disperses trained physicians; medical knowledge survives in manuscript form and reconstitutes slowly. Court physicians serve Ilkhanate rulers; popular access to trained care diminishes.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Conquest-era violence disrupts marriage patterns; massive death of males creates surplus widows; Mongol soldiers take Persian wives and concubines. Recovery-era population growth is slow, constrained by continuing instability.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population collapse is catastrophic: estimates suggest losses of 50–90 percent in some regions. Nishapur, Merv, Balkh, and other great cities are depopulated; some never recover their former scale. Partial recovery by the mid-fourteenth century is interrupted by the Black Death.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Mass casualties leave orphans, widows, and refugees; waqf institutions are damaged or destroyed; extended kin networks fragmented. The dependent population exceeds the capacity of surviving institutions to support.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Agricultural collapse follows qanat destruction and peasant massacre; famine conditions persist for years in affected areas. Nomadic pastoral economy expands into depopulated zones. Recovery is gradual and incomplete; some irrigation systems are never rebuilt.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Labor scarcity intensifies as population collapses; water mills fall into disrepair; animal power substitutes where available. The energy base contracts with population decline.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Mongol conquest enslaves skilled artisans and transports them to imperial centers; forced labor rebuilds damaged infrastructure. Surviving peasants are bound more tightly to the land; labor scarcity eventually improves bargaining position of workers.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Urban craft production collapses in destroyed cities; some centers (Tabriz, Sultaniyya) are rebuilt and become new production sites. Textile, metalwork, and manuscript production revive under Ilkhanate patronage. Agricultural surplus is reduced but sufficient for diminished population.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Mongol conquest strips accumulated wealth; treasuries, granaries, and endowments are plundered. Recovery-era accumulation is slow and interrupted by political instability and Black Death losses.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: The Pax Mongolica opens overland routes from China to the Mediterranean; Ilkhanate position athwart these routes generates commercial revenue. Tabriz becomes a major entrepot. Maritime trade through the Persian Gulf continues. Manuscript trade connects to broader Eurasian networks.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Mongol experiment with paper currency (1294 CE) fails disastrously; precious metal coinage is restored. Credit relationships rebuild slowly; waqf system reconstitutes to provide stable wealth storage.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Mongol taxation is initially predatory, treating sedentary population as resource to be exploited; reform under Ghazan Khan (r. 1295–1304) attempts more sustainable extraction. Agricultural taxes, commercial levies, and tribute fund the Ilkhanate treasury.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Conquest expropriates Persian landholding elites; Mongol nobles receive grants of land and revenue. New wealth concentrations form around Ilkhanate court. Waqf endowments provide some protection against confiscation.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Reduced production generates less waste; ruined cities accumulate debris that is gradually cleared or built over. Agricultural residues feed diminished livestock herds.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Conquest disrupts family structures through death, enslavement, and displacement; survivors rebuild kin networks. Islamic inheritance law continues to govern property transmission. Mongol-Persian intermarriage produces blended elite families.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Guilds and Sufi brotherhoods, damaged by conquest, reconstitute as organizing structures in recovering cities. Mutual aid networks prove essential for survival; futuwwa organizations revive.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Mongol court culture introduces new entertainments—wrestling, horse racing, hunting—alongside Persian poetry and music. Urban sociability recovers in rebuilt cities.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Mongol military elite sits atop the hierarchy; Persian administrators occupy essential but subordinate positions; merchants benefit from expanded trade; peasants and artisans bear the heaviest burdens. Buddhist, Christian, and Jewish populations briefly enjoy Mongol toleration before Islamic restoration.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: The Persian divan system survives conquest and becomes indispensable to Ilkhanate governance. Scribes trained in insha serve Mongol rulers who cannot read Persian; bureaucratic practice and documentary archives reconstitute. Rashid al-Din's administration demonstrates Persian manuscript culture's essential function: tax records, diplomatic correspondence, historical chronicles flow from the reconstituted chancellery.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Mongol cavalry remains the premier military force; composite bow technology dominates battlefield tactics. Siege warfare capabilities allow reduction of fortified cities. Gunpowder weapons appear but do not yet transform warfare. The military balance favors steppe forces against sedentary armies.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Dynastic fragmentation after 1335 CE ends centralized Ilkhanate rule; successor states compete violently. Banditry flourishes in weakly governed zones. Civil disorder persists until Timurid conquest.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Mongol khans command through military prowess and hereditary charisma; Persian viziers exercise administrative authority. Ghazan Khan's Islamic conversion (1295 CE) marks a pivot toward legitimacy in Persian-Islamic terms.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Initial Mongol rule rests on naked conquest; legitimacy develops through Islamic conversion, patronage of religious and scholarly institutions, and provision of order. Popular allegiance remains contingent on protection from violence.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Ilkhanate political will focuses on territorial consolidation, conflict with Mamluks and Golden Horde, and internal succession struggles. Persian populations lack capacity for collective political action; survival rather than resistance dominates.

    External Relations & Posture: Ilkhanate engages in persistent warfare with Mamluks of Egypt and Golden Horde to the north; diplomatic relations extend to European powers (abortive Crusade alliance), Byzantium, and Yuan China. Trade diplomacy accompanies military competition.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Trauma pervades collective memory; survivors and their descendants carry psychological burden of mass death. Fear of renewed violence persists. Trust is contracted to immediate kin and community; suspicion of outsiders deepens.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Exhaustion pervades post-conquest society; political fragmentation after 1335 CE reflects spent will at the center. Local populations withdraw into survival strategies.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Persian remains the administrative and literary language; Mongol is used for military and court communication; Arabic serves religious scholarship. Uighur script initially serves Mongol records before Persian-Arabic takes over. Multilingual court reflects diverse empire; translation flourishes.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: The yam (relay post) system provides rapid communication across Mongol domains; couriers carry official correspondence at unprecedented speed. Caravan routes connect to broader Eurasian information networks.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Conquest destroys libraries and archives; manuscript losses are catastrophic in cities like Nishapur and Merv. Reconstitution begins under Ilkhanate patronage; Rashid al-Din's scriptoria at Tabriz produce thousands of manuscripts. The Jami al-Tawarikh (Compendium of Chronicles) represents a monumental effort to record world history in manuscript form.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Mongol rulers initially tolerate religious diversity; Islamic restoration under Ghazan narrows permitted discourse. Historical chronicles serve dynastic legitimation; critical accounts are suppressed.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Persian poetry continues to circulate; Rumi (d. 1273 CE) and Sadi (d. 1291 CE) produce work that achieves wide audience. Illustrated manuscripts reach new heights of visual sophistication. Court patronage amplifies selected works.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Loss of earlier manuscripts creates gaps in the record; reconstruction efforts sometimes introduce errors. Official histories require critical reading for dynastic bias.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Islamic ethics remain the foundation for Persian populations; Mongol yasa (law code) governs some matters. Conquest violence shocks moral sensibilities; survivors struggle to reconcile catastrophe with divine justice.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Sharia courts continue to function for Muslim populations; Mongol yasa operates in parallel for some criminal and military matters. Ghazan's reforms restore Islamic legal primacy. Madrasa-trained jurists preserve and develop legal scholarship.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Multiple legal systems create jurisdictional complexity; qadis handle Muslim affairs, Mongol tribunals address military and administrative cases. Punishments remain severe for both systems.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Initial Mongol rule offers little protection for conquered populations; reform under Ghazan attempts to limit extortion and establish predictable taxation. Dhimmi status is complicated by Mongol toleration of non-Muslim religions.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Conquest atrocities go entirely unpunished; power determines outcomes. Post-conquest judicial restoration gradually establishes more regular administration of justice, though elite impunity persists.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Scholarly activity continues despite disruption; the Maragha observatory (founded 1259 CE) advances astronomical research. Nasir al-Din Tusi produces major works in mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy. Historical scholarship flourishes under Ilkhanate patronage.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Irrigation engineering attempts to restore damaged qanat systems. Architectural technique develops in construction of new buildings at Tabriz and Sultaniyya. Manuscript illumination and bookbinding reach new levels of refinement.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Madrasa system rebuilds in recovering cities; Tusi endows institutions for religious and rational sciences. Manuscript copying reconstitutes scholarly libraries. Literacy remains restricted but manuscript culture demonstrates remarkable resilience.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Conquest-era manuscript losses are irretrievable; some texts survive only in fragments or through later copies. Oral transmission within craft guilds preserves practical knowledge. The knowledge stock contracts but does not collapse.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: The Shahnameh continues to provide foundational identity for Persian-speaking populations; its narrative of Iranian kingship offers implicit critique of foreign rule. Islamic sacred history and Sufi hagiography provide additional layers of meaning.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Mongol rulers initially legitimate through conquest right and Chinggisid descent; Islamic conversion enables traditional Islamic legitimation. Persian historical chronicles (Jami al-Tawarikh, Tarikh-i Wassaf) provide Ilkhanate with dynastic mythology.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Manuscript illustration achieves unprecedented sophistication; the Jami al-Tawarikh manuscripts combine Persian, Chinese, and Central Asian visual traditions. Architecture at Sultaniyya and Tabriz displays Mongol-Persian synthesis. Metalwork, ceramics, and textiles continue Persian traditions with new influences.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Some classical Persian literary forms continue without significant innovation; panegyric poetry serves new patrons with familiar conventions. The trauma of conquest resists adequate artistic expression; later literature struggles to represent catastrophe.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Islamic cosmology struggles to encompass conquest catastrophe; theodicy becomes urgent. Sufi mysticism offers escape from worldly suffering; Rumi's poetry speaks to spiritual seeking amid political chaos. Fatalism deepens.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Islamic ritual observance continues; Sufi practices (dhikr, shrine visitation) provide participatory spirituality. Mongol shamanic practices exist alongside and gradually yield to Islam among rulers.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Ulama institutions rebuild; waqf endowments reconstitute to support religious education. Sufi orders expand, providing social organization and spiritual guidance. Shi'i communities survive and maintain distinct identity.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Mongol conversion to Islam marks decisive turn; Buddhism and Christianity, briefly favored, recede. Sufi expansion into popular religion accelerates. Zoroastrian communities survive in reduced circumstances.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Conquest trauma generates spiritual questioning; some survivors struggle with faith in the face of catastrophe. Sufi mysticism absorbs and redirects spiritual crisis. The sacred ultimately reconsolidates within Islamic framework.
