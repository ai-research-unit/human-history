    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Aegean Archipelago (2000 BCE – 1200 BCE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 2000 BCE – 1200 BCE

    Region: The Aegean Archipelago — the islands and coastlands of the Aegean Sea, including Crete, the Cycladic islands, and the Greek mainland (Peloponnese, Attica, Boeotia); the maritime zone linking Europe, Anatolia, and the Eastern Mediterranean.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes palatial complexes (Knossos, Phaistos, Mycenae, Pylos), clay tablet archives with Linear A and Linear B scripts, frescoes, seal impressions, and artifacts. Linear A remains undeciphered; Linear B deciphered as early Greek. Tablets preserve administrative records. The window ends with the Late Bronze Age collapse (~1200 BCE) and the loss of palatial literacy.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Middle Minoan Palatial Emergence (2000–1700 BCE)

    First Palaces: Knossos, Phaistos, Malia constructed ~2000–1900 BCE; monumental architecture; central courts; storage magazines.

    Linear A Emergence: Hieroglyphic script (~2000 BCE) evolves into Linear A (~1800 BCE); syllabic script; administrative records.

    Administrative Function: Clay tablets record economic transactions; seals authenticate; palace redistributes goods.

    Craft Specialization: Palatial workshops; pottery (Kamares ware); metalworking; seal carving.

    Maritime Networks: Trade with Egypt, Levant, Anatolia; Minoan merchants; imported goods (tin, copper, ivory).

    Social Hierarchy: Palace elite; scribes; craftsmen; agricultural producers; centralized authority.

    2. Late Minoan Florescence (1700–1450 BCE)

    New Palaces: Rebuilt after destructions ~1700 BCE; larger, more elaborate; Knossos dominates.

    Thera Eruption: ~1628 BCE (debated); volcanic destruction of Akrotiri; tsunami damage to Crete; regional disruption.

    Minoan Thalassocracy: Naval dominance; colonies and trading posts across Aegean; Cycladic influence.

    Art and Religion: Frescoes; bull-leaping; goddess figures; peak sanctuaries; ritualized palace life.

    Linear A Archives: Administrative tablets; libation inscriptions; language undeciphered (non-Greek).

    Peak Complexity: Maximum territorial extent; sophisticated palace economy; scribal bureaucracy.

    3. Mycenaean Dominance (1450–1200 BCE)

    Mycenaean Takeover: ~1450 BCE mainland Greeks control Knossos; Linear B replaces Linear A.

    Linear B Archives: Clay tablets at Knossos, Pylos, Mycenae, Thebes, Tiryns; administrative records; early Greek language.

    Palatial Economy: Centralized redistribution; landholding records; craft inventories; workforce management; religious offerings.

    Mainland Centers: Mycenae, Tiryns, Pylos, Thebes; cyclopean walls; tholos tombs; warrior elite.

    International Trade: Mycenaean pottery in Egypt, Levant, Cyprus, Italy; imported goods; diplomatic contacts.

    Writing Function: Strictly administrative; no literature preserved; scribes attached to palaces.

    4. Late Bronze Age Collapse (1200 BCE)

    Destruction Wave: Palaces destroyed ~1200–1180 BCE; Pylos burned with archive intact; Mycenae sacked.

    Multiple Causes: Sea Peoples, internal conflict, systems collapse, drought, earthquake — debated.

    Linear B Lost: With palaces destroyed, writing ceases; no preservation of scribal tradition.

    Dark Age Transition: Population decline; loss of literacy; simpler material culture; oral tradition resumes.

    Manuscript Stage Interrupted: The administrative writing system does not survive; Greek world returns to orality until Phoenician alphabet adopted (~800 BCE).
