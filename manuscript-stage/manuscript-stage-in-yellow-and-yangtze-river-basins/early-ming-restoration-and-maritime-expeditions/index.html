    1368 CE – 1500 CE — Early Ming Restoration and Maritime Expeditions

    Summary: Native Chinese dynasty restored under Zhu Yuanzhang eliminates Mongol rule and reconstructs centralized imperial administration on Neo-Confucian principles; the binding constraint is order — rebuilding state capacity, resettling depopulated lands, and reestablishing examination orthodoxy after decades of war and plague; system margin expands as population recovers and agriculture stabilizes through the 15th century; direction of change moves from post-war reconstruction toward institutional consolidation, punctuated by the Yongle emperor's assertive expansion and the abrupt termination of maritime expeditions.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Two river basins remain the productive core; Nanjing serves as initial capital (to 1421), Beijing thereafter; Grand Canal restored and improved for grain transport; northern frontier fortified against Mongol resurgence; coastline monitored against piracy.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Monsoon pattern continues; 15th century relatively stable climatically compared to 14th-century disruptions; droughts and floods occur regionally but within tolerable range.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Yellow River flooding continues as chronic hazard; state invests heavily in hydraulic maintenance; southern levee systems maintained; earthquake exposure remains but no catastrophic events recorded in core regions.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Agricultural land gradually reclaimed after Yuan-era depopulation; forests under pressure for construction and fuel; coal and iron extraction continues; no fundamental change in endowment base.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Agricultural and urban waste absorbed within traditional capacities; population recovery increases load but sink capacity remains adequate; no systemic saturation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Post-plague population recovery indicates reduced epidemic pressure; endemic diseases persist but no major epidemics recorded; mortality baseline normalizes after 14th-century catastrophe.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Nutritional status improves as agriculture recovers and stabilizes; land-to-labor ratio favorable in early Ming due to population loss; physiological vitality recovering throughout window.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Marriage patterns resume Confucian norms — early marriage, patrilocal residence, emphasis on male heirs; state encourages population recovery through tax incentives and land grants.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population recovers from estimated 60–70 million at Ming founding toward 100+ million by 1500; sustained growth indicates positive replacement; density increases but remains below late Song levels.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: War generation's widows and orphans gradually absorbed; clan and community networks reconstituted; dependency load manageable as productive population expands.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Rice in the south, wheat and millet in the north; land reclamation and resettlement improve food security; subsistence margin widens through the 15th century; household provisioning stabilizes.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle primary; water mills widespread; coal heating in the north; no change in prime movers; work ceiling stable at traditional agrarian levels.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Household registration (lijia) system organizes labor and tax obligations; corvee for public works; artisan households registered for state service; hereditary military households (weisuo); coercion moderate, embedded in administrative structure.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Agricultural output recovers with population; silk, ceramics, and cotton textile production expand; surplus capacity grows, supporting state extraction and commercial activity.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Granary system (changpingcang, yicang) reconstructed; state maintains strategic reserves; buffering capacity adequate for normal fluctuations; system tested but not overwhelmed during window.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Grand Canal primary artery for north-south grain shipment; regional markets recover; maritime trade initially suppressed (haijin policy) then briefly expanded under Zheng He (1405–1433), then restricted again; internal exchange robust, external trade policy-constrained.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Paper currency (baochao) issued but loses value; copper cash primary medium; silver increasingly used in larger transactions; credit through pawnshops and merchant networks; value storage shifts from failed paper toward metal.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Land tax (assessed in grain) and labor service primary extractions; grain tribute system feeds Beijing; extraction moderate relative to productive base; redistribution prioritizes military, court, and infrastructure.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Land redistributed through military colonies and civilian resettlement; gentry families accumulate through examination success and land purchase; concentration increasing but moderated by early Ming policies; tax evasion by elites begins.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Traditional waste recycling in agriculture; urban waste managed locally; no routing crisis; load-to-sink ratio comfortable.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust rebuilds as order returns; clan organizations reconstitute; village mutual-aid networks (baojia concepts) structured by state; trust radius expands with stability.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Four-tier ethnic hierarchy abolished; gentry-commoner distinction primary axis; examination success primary status marker; merchant status low in ideology but rising in practice; ethnic minorities (Miao, etc.) subordinated at margins.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilineal descent reinforced; clan organizations rebuild; ancestral halls constructed; property inheritance follows Confucian norms — equal division among sons; widow chastity idealized.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Guilds (hang) organize trades in cities; temple associations coordinate community activities; charitable granaries supplement state system; gentry take local leadership roles; associational fabric thickens.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Festival calendar observed; drama and storytelling continue; teahouses and wine shops as sociability venues; gentry cultivate poetry, painting, and gardens; leisure normalized as prosperity returns.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Petition system to magistrates functions; examination provides mobility channel; religious practice channels discontent; local mediation handles most disputes; grievance load cleared adequately in early-mid Ming.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Highly centralized autocracy; Hongwu emperor abolishes prime ministership (1380), concentrating power; Grand Secretariat evolves as de facto cabinet; eunuch influence begins under Yongle; power concentrated but succession instabilities emerge.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Hereditary succession; contested under Yongle (usurpation from nephew, 1402); subsequent transfers relatively stable through 1500; institutionalized but vulnerable to palace intrigue.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Mandate of Heaven restored to native dynasty; Neo-Confucian orthodoxy (Zhu Xi) officially sanctioned; examination system as legitimacy mechanism; narrative of restoration from barbarian rule.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Centralized bureaucracy rebuilt; six ministries, censorate, provincial administration; examination recruitment resumed and regularized; documentation extensive (Ming Shilu); capacity high, though early emperors' autocratic interventions create vulnerabilities.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Examination system reopened, providing gentry participation channel; remonstrance accepted within limits; no popular participation; mobilization confined to examination aspirants and bureaucratic factions.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Mongol threat persists on northern frontier; Great Wall construction and repair; tributary system reestablished with Korea, Vietnam, Ryukyu, Japan; Zheng He voyages project power across Indian Ocean (1405–1433) but discontinued; maritime policy turns inward.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Hereditary military households (weisuo) provide army; Beijing as military capital; Great Wall garrison system; gunpowder weapons standard; guard units (jinyi wei) for internal security; military capacity adequate for defense, less for sustained offense.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Order restored and maintained; banditry suppressed; religious heterodoxy monitored; no major rebellions through 1500; internal threat environment low; enforcement capacity exceeds disorder load.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Courier post system rebuilt; official dispatches travel efficiently; commercial information flows along trade routes and Grand Canal; printing accelerates text dissemination; velocity adequate for administrative needs.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Extensive state documentation — Ming Shilu (Veritable Records), population registers, land surveys, examination records; Grand Secretariat archives; provincial and county archives; woodblock printing preserves texts; persistence high.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: State controls examination curriculum (Four Books, Eight-Legged Essay); literary inquisition occasionally prosecutes heterodox writing; Confucian discourse norms enforced; vernacular literature circulates with less scrutiny; permitted band defined by orthodoxy.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Literate gentry primary audience for serious texts; examination materials widely consumed; drama and fiction reach broader audiences through performance and storytelling; religious texts copied and circulated.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Standardized examination materials ensure canonical consistency; archival practices maintain signal integrity; printing reduces copying errors; noise load manageable within orthodox framework.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Confucian morality official basis — filial piety, loyalty, ritual propriety; popular sense of justice aligns broadly with these norms; moral consensus restored after Yuan-era disruption.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Da Ming Lü (Ming Code) promulgated 1397; comprehensive criminal code with detailed regulations; substatutes (lüli) accumulate through precedent; legal evolution through accretion.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: County magistrates primary adjudicators; prefectural and provincial review; capital cases reviewed in Beijing; punishment swift and often harsh (Hongwu era especially); liability assigned through confession-based process.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Hereditary status binds some households (military, artisan); commoners have defined legal standing; compliance burden moderate — registration, tax, corvee; predictability restored after Yuan irregularities.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Imperial relatives and high officials enjoy some impunity; eunuch abuses begin; local elites can deflect cases; some judicial gap but system functional; backlog and wrongful outcomes within tolerable limits.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Zhu Xi Neo-Confucianism established as state orthodoxy; Four Books as canonical core; inquiry constrained by examination framework; independent philosophical speculation limited until Wang Yangming (after 1500); paradigm stable and conservative.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Agricultural, hydraulic, and craft techniques maintained and transmitted; Yongle Encyclopedia (Yongle Dadian) compiles existing knowledge; navigation and shipbuilding peak with Zheng He; gunpowder weaponry refined; technique robust but not rapidly advancing.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: State schools and private academies train examination candidates; literacy spreading through gentry and merchant families; knowledge stock preserved through printing; skill distribution concentrated in educated elite but broader than Yuan period.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Examination orthodoxy freezes interpretive innovation; some practical knowledge from Yuan maritime connections lost as policy turns inward; accumulated stock preserved but not significantly expanded; decay rate low.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Restoration narrative central — native dynasty reclaiming civilization from barbarian rule; Tang-Song cultural heritage invoked as standard; Han Chinese identity reinforced against Mongol other; founding struggle remembered in popular legend.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Blue-and-white porcelain perfected and exported; court painting academy active; lacquerware, silk weaving reach high standards; Forbidden City (completed 1420) defines imperial architecture; Ming style emerges as distinctive synthesis.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Mood shifts from post-war reconstruction urgency to consolidation and stability; Confucian propriety valued; risk-averse disposition settles in after Zheng He voyages discontinued; confidence in restored order.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Examination essay (baguwen) becomes increasingly formulaic; literary forms from Tang-Song continue without innovation; vernacular fiction and drama develop as vital alternatives; official forms rigidify while popular forms innovate.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Neo-Confucian cosmology official — ordered universe, human perfectibility through moral cultivation, this-worldly focus; Buddhist and Daoist cosmologies coexist at popular level; millenarian hopes fade as order returns.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: State rituals (ancestral temple, altars of heaven and earth) reinstituted; Confucian family rituals promoted; Buddhist and Daoist temple worship continues; popular religion blends traditions; festival calendar observed.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Buddhist and Daoist clergy registered and controlled; monastery lands limited; Confucian orthodoxy dominates education; religious institutions subordinated to state supervision; doctrinal authority centralized in examination curriculum.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Popular religion continues syncretic blending; state religion stabilizes; no major new diffusions; Tibetan Buddhism loses court patronage; Islamic communities maintain practice in northwest; boundaries stable.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No secular drift; Neo-Confucian moral cultivation provides this-worldly sacred framework; popular devotion continues; transcendence needs met within established traditions; spiritual void not evident.
