    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins (1200 BCE – 1900 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 1200 BCE – 1900 CE

    Region: The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins — the two great river systems of East Asia, from the loess plateau and North China Plain to the Yangzi delta and southern rice lands; the core zones of Chinese civilization from Shang through Qing dynasties.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Shang oracle bones, Zhou bronze inscriptions, bamboo and silk manuscripts, paper documents, Buddhist sutras, official archives, and examination essays. Chinese script emerges by ~1200 BCE (Shang oracle bones); logographic system evolving but structurally continuous. Bamboo/wood strips dominant medium through Han; silk for special texts; paper invented ~100 CE, widespread by Tang. Woodblock printing from Tang (868 CE Diamond Sutra), movable type from Song, but manuscript remains primary administrative medium. Window begins with mature oracle bone script; ends as Western printing technology and telegraph transform Qing communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Shang (1200–1046 BCE)

    Anyang Capital: Yin ruins; royal center; palatial architecture; bronze workshops.

    Oracle Bone Divination: Pyromancy; royal questions to ancestors; diviners; archival storage.

    Oracle Bone Script: ~4,500 characters known; logographic; ancestor of modern Chinese.

    Royal Administration: King; military campaigns; tribute; agricultural coordination.

    Bronze Ritual Vessels: Clan insignia; inscription beginnings; ancestral worship.

    Zhou Conquest: ~1046 BCE; Battle of Muye; Shang overthrown; continuity of script.

    2. Western Zhou (1046–771 BCE)

    Zhou Dynasty: Mandate of Heaven concept; feudal enfeoffment; ritual system.

    Bronze Inscriptions: Longer texts; land grants; appointments; legal records.

    Feudal Order: Fengjian system; regional lords; Zhou king as ritual center.

    Ritual Texts: Beginnings of classical canon; Book of Documents; Book of Poetry.

    Zongfa System: Patrilineal descent; ancestral temples; clan organization.

    Western Zhou Fall: 771 BCE; Quanrong invasion; capital moves east to Luoyang.

    3. Spring and Autumn Period (771–476 BCE)

    Eastern Zhou: Weakened royal house; hegemonic lords (ba); interstate competition.

    Hegemonic States: Qi, Jin, Chu, Qin; alliance diplomacy; ritual legitimacy.

    Confucius: ~551–479 BCE; ethical teaching; disciples; textual transmission.

    Bamboo Manuscripts: Writing on bamboo strips; silk for special texts; brush and ink.

    Classical Learning: Editing of canonical texts; transmission of ritual knowledge.

    Interstate System: Covenants recorded; diplomatic archives; scribal class.

    4. Warring States (476–221 BCE)

    Competing Kingdoms: Seven major states; total warfare; territorial consolidation.

    Hundred Schools: Confucians, Mohists, Daoists, Legalists; philosophical debates.

    Legalist Theory: Shang Yang, Han Fei; state power; law codes; bureaucratic method.

    Script Variants: Regional variations; Chu scripts; pre-unification diversity.

    Qin Rise: Western state; military power; Legalist reforms; eventual conquest.

    Literary Florescence: Mencius, Zhuangzi, Xunzi; bamboo manuscripts proliferate.

    5. Qin Unification (221–206 BCE)

    First Emperor: Qin Shi Huang; conquest completed 221 BCE; centralized empire.

    Script Standardization: Small seal script (xiaozhuan); Li Si's reforms; uniformity.

    Administrative System: Commanderies and counties; standardized measures; roads.

    Book Burning: 213 BCE; Legalist suppression; Confucian texts targeted; survival underground.

    Terracotta Army: Imperial tomb; massive state mobilization; documentary records.

    Collapse: Qin falls 206 BCE; rebel coalitions; Liu Bang victorious.

    6. Western Han (206 BCE – 9 CE)

    Han Empire: Liu Bang (Gaozu); centralized bureaucracy; Confucian synthesis.

    Confucian Canon: Five Classics established; imperial academy; examination beginnings.

    Sima Qian: Historical Records (Shiji); historiographical model; comprehensive history.

    Bureaucratic Archives: Population registers; tax records; legal documents; silk, bamboo.

    Expansion: Central Asia; Silk Road; diplomatic correspondence; tributary system.

    Wang Mang: Usurpation 9 CE; Xin dynasty; classical revival; failure.

    7. Eastern Han and Paper (9–220 CE)

    Han Restoration: Guangwu Emperor; Eastern Han; Luoyang capital.

    Paper Invention: Cai Lun ~105 CE; paper from bark, rags; gradual adoption.

    Buddhist Introduction: First translations; Luoyang communities; new textual traditions.

    Confucian Classics: Stone classics carved; textual standardization; scholarly culture.

    Yellow Turban Rebellion: 184 CE; Daoist millenarianism; Han collapse begins.

    Three Kingdoms: 220 CE; Wei, Shu, Wu; regional division; literary culture continues.

    8. Period of Disunion (220–589 CE)

    Three Kingdoms: Cao Wei, Shu Han, Eastern Wu; military competition; literati culture.

    Jin Dynasty: Brief reunification; collapse; northern invasions; southern flight.

    Sixteen Kingdoms: Non-Chinese dynasties in north; cultural mixing; Buddhism spreads.

    Southern Dynasties: Jiankang (Nanjing); refugee aristocracy; literary culture; Buddhism.

    Northern Dynasties: Xianbei rulers; sinicization; Buddhist cave temples; sutra copying.

    Buddhist Manuscripts: Translation projects; monastic scriptoria; paper adoption.

    9. Sui-Tang Reunification (589–755 CE)

    Sui Dynasty: 589 reunification; Grand Canal; brief dynasty; Tang succession.

    Tang Empire: Li family; cosmopolitan capital; territorial expansion; cultural peak.

    Examination System: Jinshi degree; literary examination; bureaucratic recruitment.

    Tang Poetry: Li Bai, Du Fu; poetic manuscripts; literary prestige.

    Buddhist Florescence: Chan, Pure Land; sutra copying; woodblock printing begins.

    Administrative Archives: Land-equalization records; census; legal codes; Tang Code.

    10. Late Tang and Five Dynasties (755–960 CE)

    An Lushan Rebellion: 755–763; devastating civil war; Tang never recovers.

    Regional Militarism: Jiedushi (military governors); autonomous regions; tax collection.

    Woodblock Printing: Diamond Sutra 868 CE (earliest dated); Buddhist texts; calendars.

    Huang Chao Rebellion: 874–884; massive destruction; Tang collapse accelerates.

    Five Dynasties: 907–960; northern succession; Ten Kingdoms in south; fragmentation.

    Manuscript-Print Transition: Printing spreads; manuscripts still predominant.

    11. Northern Song (960–1127 CE)

    Song Reunification: Taizu; centralized civil government; military subordination.

    Examination Culture: Expanded recruitment; printing facilitates study; social mobility.

    Neo-Confucian Revival: Cheng Yi, Cheng Hao; philosophy; textual scholarship.

    Printing Expansion: Commercial publishing; examination texts; medical, agricultural.

    Wang Anshi Reforms: 1069–1076; state activism; factional conflict; bureaucratic records.

    Jurchen Invasion: 1127; Kaifeng falls; court flees south; Northern Song ends.

    12. Southern Song (1127–1279 CE)

    Southern Retreat: Hangzhou capital; reduced territory; defensive strategy.

    Commercial Publishing: Fujian, Hangzhou centers; private academies; book trade.

    Zhu Xi: 1130–1200; Neo-Confucian synthesis; Four Books; examination orthodoxy.

    Maritime Trade: South China Sea; ceramic exports; commercial documentation.

    Mongol Conquest: 1279; Song resistance ends; Yuan dynasty established.

    Print Culture: Widespread printing; manuscript for calligraphy, personal texts.

    13. Yuan Dynasty (1279–1368 CE)

    Mongol Rule: Kublai Khan; multi-ethnic administration; Pax Mongolica.

    Multi-Script Administration: Chinese, Mongolian, Persian, Tibetan; translation bureaus.

    Status System: Mongol, Semu, Hanren, Nanren hierarchy; examination suspended.

    Marco Polo Era: Eurasian connections; trade; cultural exchange.

    Drama and Fiction: Yuan zaju; vernacular literature; printing continues.

    Yuan Decline: 1340s; rebellions; Red Turbans; Ming rises.

    14. Early Ming (1368–1500 CE)

    Ming Founding: Zhu Yuanzhang; restoration; neo-Confucian orthodoxy; Nanjing capital.

    Yongle Era: Beijing capital; Forbidden City; encyclopedia projects; centralization.

    Zheng He Voyages: 1405–1433; maritime expeditions; tributary diplomacy; discontinuation.

    Examination Orthodoxy: Eight-legged essay; Four Books emphasis; bureaucratic selection.

    Grand Secretariat: Administrative evolution; cabinet government; document flow.

    Ming Shilu: Veritable Records; comprehensive court documentation; archival practice.

    15. Late Ming (1500–1644 CE)

    Silver Economy: Global trade; Spanish American silver; monetization; commercial growth.

    Factional Politics: Donglin; eunuch power; court conflicts; bureaucratic dysfunction.

    Wang Yangming: 1472–1529; mind philosophy; popular lectures; intellectual ferment.

    Popular Publishing: Novels; vernacular texts; examination aids; commercial printing.

    Manchu Rise: Nurhaci; Later Jin; military pressure; Ming collapse.

    Li Zicheng: 1644; rebel capture of Beijing; Chongzhen suicide; Qing entrance.

    16. Qing Consolidation (1644–1800 CE)

    Manchu Conquest: Dorgon; Beijing secured; southern campaigns; resistance suppressed.

    Kangxi Era: 1661–1722; consolidation; expansion; literary projects; Sacred Edict.

    Qianlong Era: 1736–1795; territorial peak; Four Treasuries project; archival organization.

    Evidential Scholarship: Kaozheng; textual criticism; philology; manuscript scholarship.

    Banner System: Manchu military organization; documentary records; registration.

    Population Growth: Doubling; New World crops; commercial agriculture.

    17. Qing Crisis (1800–1900 CE)

    Opium Wars: 1839–1842, 1856–1860; British victory; treaty ports; unequal treaties.

    Taiping Rebellion: 1850–1864; massive civil war; regional militarization.

    Self-Strengthening: 1860s–1890s; modernization; arsenals; telegraph; printing presses.

    Examination Reform: 1898; abolition 1905 (just after window); system collapse.

    Print Transition: Western printing technology; newspapers; lithography; manuscript decline.

    1898 Reform: Hundred Days; conservative reaction; institutional crisis.

    18. Chinese Script and Scholarly Culture (1200 BCE – 1900 CE)

    Script Evolution: Oracle bone → bronze inscription → seal → clerical → regular script.

    Writing Materials: Bone, bronze, bamboo, silk, paper; brush technology.

    Examination System: Sui origins; Tang expansion; Song maturation; Ming-Qing orthodoxy.

    Literati Culture: Scholar-officials; calligraphy; poetry; painting; social prestige.

    Textual Transmission: Manuscript copying; printing supplement; canonical authority.

    Imperial Archives: Veritable Records; Grand Secretariat; provincial archives.
