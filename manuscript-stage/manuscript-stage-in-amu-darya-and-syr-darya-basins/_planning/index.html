    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Amu Darya and Syr Darya Basins (400 CE – 1900 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 400 CE – 1900 CE

    Region: The Amu Darya and Syr Darya Basins — the two great rivers of Central Asia (Oxus and Jaxartes), draining from the Pamir and Tian Shan into the Aral Sea; the historical Transoxiana (Mawarannahr) and Khwarezm, including the oasis cities of Samarkand, Bukhara, Khiva, Merv, and the surrounding steppe margins.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Sogdian commercial letters and religious texts, Arabic geographical and historical works, Persian literature and chronicles, waqf endowment documents, Timurid royal manuscripts and library collections, Chagatai Turkic texts, and Russian administrative records. Paper manufacture in Samarkand from 8th century; continuous manuscript tradition until Russian print infrastructure (~1860s–1880s). Window begins with Sogdian documentary evidence; ends as Russian colonial administration establishes print-based institutions.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Sogdian Commercial Networks (400–650 CE)

    Sogdian Trading Diaspora: Sogdian merchants dominate Silk Road trade from Samarkand; colonies from China to Byzantium; commercial correspondence in Sogdian script.

    Ancient Letters: Discovered at Dunhuang; ~314 CE; earliest Sogdian documents; commercial/personal correspondence; demonstrate network extent.

    Sogdian Script: Derived from Aramaic; ancestor of Uyghur, Mongolian scripts; used for commercial, religious, literary texts.

    Religious Pluralism: Zoroastrianism native; Buddhism, Manichaeism, Nestorian Christianity all present; texts in Sogdian.

    Political Fragmentation: City-states (Samarkand, Bukhara, Panjikent) under Hephthalite then Western Turkic overlordship; local autonomy.

    Material Culture: Panjikent excavations; wall paintings; wealthy merchant class; sophisticated urban culture.

    2. Arab Conquest (650–750 CE)

    Qutayba ibn Muslim: Umayyad general; conquers Transoxiana 705–715 CE; Bukhara 709, Samarkand 712; resistance and revolts.

    Battle of Talas: 751 CE; Abbasid-Tang clash; Arab victory; Chinese papermakers captured; paper manufacture begins.

    Conversion Process: Gradual Islamization; jizya incentives; Arab garrison cities; indigenous resistance; 9th century largely Muslim.

    Arabic Script Adoption: Arabic becomes administrative, religious language; local languages adopt Arabic script; bilingual transition.

    Destruction and Continuity: Conquest violence; libraries burned; but administrative infrastructure survives; Persian scribal tradition.

    Early Islamic Architecture: First mosques; adapting Sogdian building traditions; Bukhara congregational mosque.

    3. Abbasid Integration (750–900 CE)

    Caliphal Province: Khurasan province includes Transoxiana; governors in Merv, then local amirs; tax farming.

    Samarkand Paper: Chinese technique; local production from 8th century; exports to Islamic world; replaces papyrus, parchment.

    Tahirid and Saffarid Governors: Persian military families; increasing autonomy; cultural patronage; Persian language revival.

    Persian Literary Revival: Arabic remains scholarly language; Persian re-emerges for poetry, administration; New Persian in Arabic script.

    Islamic Scholarship: Translation movement; hadith collection (Bukhari from Bukhara); Hanafi jurisprudence dominant.

    Urban Development: Samarkand, Bukhara prosper; irrigation expansion; trade continues; caravanserais.

    4. Samanid Renaissance (900–999 CE)

    Samanid Dynasty: 819–999 CE; Bukhara capital; Persian-speaking Muslim dynasty; model for successor states.

    Bukhara Cultural Capital: Royal library; scholars, poets patronized; intellectual center of eastern Islamic world.

    Rudaki: "Father of Persian poetry" (~858–941); court poet; established Persian literary conventions.

    Ferdowsi: Shahnameh begun under Samanids, completed later; Persian national epic; manuscript tradition.

    Islamic Learning: Hadith scholarship; Bukhari's collection canonical; Hanafi jurisprudence; early madrasas.

    Administration: Persian diwans; sophisticated bureaucracy; waqf endowments; manuscript-based record keeping.

    5. Turkic Dynasties (999–1220 CE)

    Qarakhanid Conquest: 999 CE; Turkic dynasty; conversion to Islam prior; adopt Persian administrative tradition.

    Seljuk Expansion: 1040s; Transoxiana under Seljuk influence; Nishapur, then broader empire; Persian viziers.

    Khwarezmshah Empire: ~1097–1220 CE; initially Seljuk vassals; powerful empire; Gurganj capital; final pre-Mongol peak.

    Persianate Synthesis: Turkic rulers, Persian administration, Arabic scholarship; trilingual elite culture.

    Madrasa Development: Qarakhanid patronage; Bukhara madrasas; institutionalized Islamic learning; manuscript libraries.

    Scientific Achievement: al-Biruni (973–1048); mathematical, astronomical, geographical works; wrote in Arabic.

    6. Mongol Conquest and Chagatai Khanate (1220–1370 CE)

    Chinggis Khan's Invasion: 1219–1221; destruction of Bukhara, Samarkand, Urgench; massive population loss.

    Chagatai Khanate: Chinggis's son; Central Asian inheritance; division into western and eastern khanates.

    Recovery Period: Cities slowly rebuild; trade routes restore; Mongol Pax facilitates exchange; religious tolerance.

    Mongol Administration: Yasa law; census; postal system (yam); Persian scribes employed; dual script traditions.

    Religious Transformation: Initial tolerance; gradual Islamization of Mongol elite; Tarmashirin (1331) officially Muslim.

    Manuscript Survival: Some libraries destroyed; others survive; Persian traditions continue; patronage resumes.

    7. Timurid Empire (1370–1507 CE)

    Timur (Tamerlane): 1370–1405; Samarkand capital; vast conquests; destruction and construction; cultural patronage.

    Samarkand Rebuilding: Registan complex; Bibi-Khanym mosque; Gur-e-Amir mausoleum; architectural splendor.

    Royal Libraries: Herat, Samarkand; elaborate manuscript production; illustrated manuscripts; calligraphy arts.

    Ulugh Beg: 1394–1449; astronomer-prince; Samarkand observatory; astronomical tables; madrasa founder.

    Chagatai Literature: Turkic literary language develops; Ali-Shir Nava'i (1441–1501); bilingual Persian-Chagatai culture.

    Herat School: Painting, calligraphy; Bihzad; illuminated manuscripts; artistic peak.

    8. Uzbek Khanates (1507–1740 CE)

    Shaybanid Conquest: Muhammad Shaybani; 1500–1510; Uzbek Turkic dynasty; defeats last Timurids.

    Three Khanates Emerge: Bukhara (Shaybanids, then Ashtarkhanids); Khiva; later Kokand; fragmented political landscape.

    Bukhara Emirate: Cultural continuity; madrasa network; manuscript copying; conservative religious establishment.

    Madrasa System: Extensive network; Mir-i Arab (1535); traditional curriculum; scribal training continues.

    Economic Decline: Trade shifts to sea routes; reduced prosperity; but manuscript culture persists.

    Religious Scholarship: Sufi orders influential (Naqshbandiyya); religious texts; legal literature; hadith study.

    9. Khanate Decline and Russian Conquest (1740–1900 CE)

    Nadir Shah's Invasion: 1740; Iranian ruler; briefly conquers Bukhara, Khiva; disruption and recovery.

    Three Khanates Reconstitute: Bukhara emirate; Khiva khanate; Kokand khanate; endemic warfare; slave trade.

    Russian Advance: Fort lines southward; Tashkent 1865; Samarkand 1868; Khiva 1873; Bukhara protectorate 1868.

    Colonial Administration: Russian Turkestan; print introduced; newspapers; schools; administrative modernization.

    Manuscript Twilight: Traditional scribal culture continues into late 19th century; print gradually dominant.

    Jadidist Reform: Late 19th century; modernizing intellectuals; new method schools; newspapers; transition to print culture.
