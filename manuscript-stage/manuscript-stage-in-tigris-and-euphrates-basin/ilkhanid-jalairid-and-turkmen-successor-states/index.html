    1258 CE – 1534 CE — Ilkhanid, Jalairid, and Turkmen Successor States

    Summary: A nearly three-century trend in the Tigris-Euphrates basin following the Mongol sack of Baghdad (1258), during which successive regimes — Ilkhanid governors, Jalairid sultans, Tamerlane's devastation (1401), and competing Qara Qoyunlu and Aq Qoyunlu Turkmen confederations — attempt partial reconstruction atop a shattered agrarian base; the binding constraint is the collapsed irrigation infrastructure and depopulated countryside, which cap recovery far below pre-1258 levels; margin against shock remains minimal, and Tamerlane's second sack resets the cycle, leaving the region a contested frontier until Ottoman conquest in 1534.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The alluvial plain persists as the spatial frame, but political gravity shifts: Baghdad reduced, Tabriz and later Herat become Ilkhanid and Timurid centers; the Tigris-Euphrates basin now a provincial periphery; Zagros passes and steppe corridors remain open to mounted armies.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Semi-arid conditions continue; some evidence of cooler, drier episodes in the 14th–15th centuries (Little Ice Age onset debated); rainfall variability strains marginal agriculture.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Flood hazard persists but less consequential as fewer canals remain to breach; seismic risk unchanged; the principal hazard is now human — invasion and sack — not environmental.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Fertile alluvium still present but underutilized; date palms and reeds accessible; bitumen seeps and clay unchanged; wood remains scarce; endowment intact, exploitation collapsed.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Reduced population and production lower waste loads; salinized soils persist but are not further stressed; absorptive slack adequate given diminished activity.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Black Death reaches Iraq in 1347–1349, killing perhaps one-third of surviving population; plague recurs episodically through 15th century; endemic malaria continues; mortality baseline elevated.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Nutritional stress chronic in disrupted agricultural zones; urban populations dependent on unreliable supply chains; famine conditions during sieges (1258, 1401); physiological vitality diminished.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Islamic marriage norms persist; high mortality elevates remarriage and polygyny among survivors; fertility potential adequate but realized reproduction constrained by insecurity.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population collapses after 1258; partial recovery under Jalairids; second collapse after 1401; Iraq remains underpopulated relative to pre-Mongol baseline throughout the window; density low, renewal slow.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Wars and plagues generate orphans, widows, and displaced; waqf institutions attenuated; tribal structures absorb some dependents; overhang exceeds diminished absorptive capacity.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Irrigated agriculture contracts drastically; pastoral nomadism expands into former farmland; date cultivation persists in the south; subsistence precarious, provisioning floor lowered and unstable.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle remain sole converters; watermills fewer as canals decay; no technological advance; per-capita work ceiling static at reduced level.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Peasant flight and enslavement by invading armies disrupt labor supply; sharecropping continues where agriculture survives; tribal levies mobilize pastoral labor; coercion high, compensation minimal.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Urban crafts (textiles, metalwork) survive at reduced scale in Baghdad and Basra; surplus capacity a fraction of pre-1258 levels; recovery partial under Jalairids, reset by Tamerlane.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Granaries and treasuries repeatedly plundered; waqf endowments confiscated or neglected; institutional buffering collapsed; reserves negligible; no margin against next shock.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Overland trade routes disrupted by warfare; Persian Gulf commerce continues via Hormuz and Basra; transit trade benefits Ilkhanid Tabriz more than Iraqi cities; infrastructure decayed.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Mongol paper money (chao) attempted and failed; silver coinage unstable; merchant credit networks attenuated; value storage insecure; purchasing power erratic.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Mongol taxation (qubchur, tamgha) extractive and irregular; Jalairid and Turkmen exactions continue; iqta' grants to military; extraction heavy on shrunken base.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Wealth concentrates in nomadic-military elite; urban merchants and ulama impoverished; land values collapsed; waqf less effective as shield; inequality extreme but total wealth low.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Reduced production generates less waste; routing unchanged; sink pressure negligible.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Repeated invasions and plagues shatter community bonds; survivors cluster in fortified quarters or tribal groups; trust radius contracted to kin and immediate neighbors.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Mongol and Turkic military elites dominate; Persian administrative culture persists; Arab tribal identity strengthens in countryside; ethnic stratification rigid; boundaries hardening.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Islamic inheritance law continues; extended kin networks vital for survival; endogamy within tribal and status groups intensifies; kinship structures load-bearing under institutional collapse.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Guilds attenuated; Sufi lodges provide refuge and mutual aid; tribal councils (majlis) govern pastoral groups; associational fabric thin in cities, denser in tribal zones.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Public sociability contracted; religious festivals and Sufi gatherings persist; storytelling and poetry in reduced court settings; leisure minimal for most.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Formal channels (mazalim, qadi courts) weakened; tribal arbitration absorbs some disputes; unmetabolized grievance high; periodic violence between factions.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Ilkhanid governors rule from Tabriz (1258–1335); Jalairid sultans center on Baghdad (1335–1432); Qara Qoyunlu (1374–1468) and Aq Qoyunlu (1378–1508) Turkmen confederations contest control; authority fragmented, unstable.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Succession by force or factional agreement; no routinized mechanism; civil wars recurrent within each dynasty; transfer a constant flashpoint.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Ilkhanids convert to Islam (1295), claiming Islamic legitimacy; Jalairids invoke Mongol and Islamic heritage; Turkmen rely on tribal charisma and Sufi affiliations; legitimacy thin, contested.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Persian vizierial tradition persists but capacity degraded; record-keeping sporadic; corruption endemic; institutional integrity minimal.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Tribal councils and military elites participate; urban populations passive; Sufi shaykhs occasionally mobilize followers; channels narrow and informal.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Iraq contested between Ilkhanate and Mamluk Egypt, then between Turkmen, Timurids, and rising Safavids; frontier zone; no stable external alliances.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Mongol and Turkic cavalry dominant; composite bow and saber standard; siege warfare conventional; gunpowder artillery emergent by late 15th century; command fragmented among tribal chiefs.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Tribal raiding, banditry, and factional warfare endemic; urban disorder during succession struggles; enforcement capacity far below threat level; order precarious.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Mounted couriers and caravan travelers; pilgrim routes to shrines; velocity unchanged; reach contracted with political fragmentation.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Arabic and Persian manuscripts copied in surviving madrasas and Sufi lodges; Jalairid patronage supports some manuscript production; libraries vulnerable to sack; persistence precarious.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Court patronage shapes chronicle writing; sectarian sensitivities (Sunni-Shi'i) constrain discourse; permitted band narrow, varying with regime.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Friday sermons, Sufi gatherings, and market storytellers amplify narratives; literacy remains confined to ulama and scribes; oral channels dominant.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Chronicle tradition attenuated; contradictory accounts of events multiply; verification capacity reduced; noise load elevated.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Islamic justice norms persist as ideal; Mongol yasa code overlays briefly; gap between ideal and practice wide; moral consensus frayed.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Sharia remains formal law; application sporadic under weak regimes; Mongol customary law (yasa) fades after Ilkhanid conversion; regulatory evolution minimal.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Qadi courts function where political authority stable; tribal arbitration handles disputes in pastoral zones; enforcement weak; throughput far below need.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Property rights insecure under repeated confiscations; dhimmi communities diminished but retain nominal autonomy; compliance unpredictable.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Military elites act with near-total impunity; land seizures, extortion, and violence unredressed; backlog and impunity accumulate beyond any clearing capacity.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Intellectual activity shifts to Tabriz, Shiraz, and later Herat under Timurid patronage; Baghdad a backwater; Ilkhanid-era astronomy (Maragha observatory) not based in Iraq; local inquiry dormant.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Irrigation engineering knowledge decays as practitioners disperse; medical and astronomical technique maintained in court circles elsewhere; local technique atrophied.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Madrasas reduced; ulama emigrate to safer centers; literacy contracting; knowledge stock depleted by library losses; skill distribution narrowing.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Irrigation expertise lost through practitioner death and displacement; manuscript losses in 1258 and 1401 sacks irreversible; decay outpacing preservation.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Trauma of 1258 and 1401 sacks enters collective memory as civilizational catastrophe; pre-Mongol Baghdad idealized; Shi'i sacred geography (Karbala, Najaf) anchors identity for that community.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Manuscript illumination persists under Jalairid patronage; architectural activity limited; ceramics and metalwork decline; material culture impoverished relative to pre-1258.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Chronicles convey resignation, piety, and nostalgia; apocalyptic expectations surface; collective mood somber, anxious, darkening after each devastation.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Older courtly forms hollowed; caliphal ceremonial extinct; poetic and artistic renewal occurs in Timurid Herat, not Iraq; local forms exhausted, meaning decayed.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Sunni and Shi'i cosmologies persist; Sufi metaphysics offer solace; fatalism deepens amid recurrent disaster; piety intense as worldly order collapses.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Five daily prayers, fasting, pilgrimage continue; Shi'i shrine visitation (ziyara) to Najaf and Karbala intensifies; Sufi dhikr gatherings proliferate; devotional infrastructure load-bearing.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Ulama networks attenuated but surviving; Sufi orders (Qadiriyya, Kubrawiyya) provide institutional continuity; sectarian boundaries (Sunni-Shi'i) sharpening under Turkmen rivalries.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Shi'ism consolidates around shrine cities; Sufi orders spread; Christian and Jewish communities diminished but present; Safavid Shi'i expansion looms at window's end.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No secularizing drift; sacred framework absorbs trauma and channels grief; spiritual intensity high; disenchantment absent, the sacred fully engaged with existential crisis.
