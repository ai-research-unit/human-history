    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Tigris and Euphrates Basin (3200 BCE – 1850 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 3200 BCE – 1850 CE

    Region: The Tigris and Euphrates Basin — the alluvial plain between the two rivers from the Persian Gulf to the Armenian highlands, including ancient Sumer, Akkad, Babylonia, and Assyria; modern Iraq and portions of Syria, Turkey, and Iran; the cradle of cuneiform civilization.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes cuneiform tablets (Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, Assyrian), clay archives, Aramaic documents, Syriac manuscripts, Arabic chronicles, and Ottoman administrative records. Cuneiform emerges ~3200 BCE as the world's earliest known writing; clay tablets dominant medium; Aramaic alphabetic script spreads from 8th century BCE; Arabic replaces older scripts after Islamic conquest. Papyrus, parchment, and paper successively supplement clay. Window begins with Uruk proto-cuneiform; ends as Tanzimat reforms and print transform Ottoman Iraq.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Uruk Expansion (3200–2900 BCE)

    Proto-Cuneiform: ~3200 BCE; Uruk IV–III tablets; pictographic/logographic; administrative tokens → script.

    Temple Economy: Inanna precinct; redistributive system; accounting tablets; beveled-rim bowls.

    Uruk Dominance: Southern Mesopotamia; Uruk influence zones; Uruk Expansion phenomenon.

    Numerical Systems: Sexagesimal (base-60); standardized measures; accounting complexity.

    Material Culture: Cylinder seals; monumental architecture; urbanization; social stratification.

    Writing Function: Primarily economic—livestock, grain, labor; not yet narrative.

    2. Early Dynastic Sumer (2900–2350 BCE)

    City-States: Ur, Uruk, Lagash, Umma, Nippur; competitive polities; EN, LUGAL titles.

    Cuneiform Matures: Logosyllabic system; Sumerian language recorded; phonetic complements.

    Royal Inscriptions: Votive statues; building accounts; military campaigns; legitimization.

    Archives: Temple and palace; Fara texts; administrative, lexical, literary tablets.

    Lagash-Umma Conflict: Boundary disputes; earliest documented warfare; Stele of Vultures.

    Scribal Schools: Edubba tradition begins; copying, memorization; standardized curriculum.

    3. Akkadian Empire (2350–2150 BCE)

    Sargon of Akkad: ~2334–2279 BCE; first empire; Sumer to Mediterranean; military conquest.

    Akkadian Language: Semitic language; administrative use; bilingual texts; Sumerian persists.

    Administrative Unification: Standardized weights, measures; royal messengers; provincial governors.

    Naram-Sin: "King of the Four Quarters"; divine kingship; Stele of Naram-Sin.

    Archive Evidence: Girsu tablets; economic administration; correspondence.

    Gutian Collapse: ~2150 BCE; empire fragments; highland incursions; decentralization.

    4. Third Dynasty of Ur (2150–2000 BCE)

    Ur-Nammu: Founded dynasty ~2112 BCE; law code (earliest known); temple construction.

    Shulgi: 2094–2047 BCE; administrative reforms; standardization; divine kingship.

    Bureaucratic State: Bala taxation system; messenger service; detailed archives; economic control.

    Archive Proliferation: Tens of thousands of tablets; Drehem, Puzrish-Dagan; administrative records.

    Sumerian Renaissance: Literary texts; hymns; Sumerian language peak; scribal training.

    Collapse: Amorite, Elamite pressure; Ibbi-Sin; Ur falls ~2004 BCE; fragmentation.

    5. Old Babylonian Period (2000–1600 BCE)

    Amorite Dynasties: Isin, Larsa, Babylon; Amorite rulers adopt Mesopotamian culture.

    Hammurabi: 1792–1750 BCE; Babylon dominant; law code; administrative correspondence.

    Legal Codification: Code of Hammurabi; case law; contracts; judicial records.

    Mathematical Texts: Plimpton 322; algebraic problems; astronomy basics; scribal education.

    Literary Florescence: Epic of Gilgamesh standardized; wisdom literature; hymns; myths.

    Hittite Raid: ~1595 BCE; Babylon sacked; Kassite takeover follows.

    6. Kassite Babylon (1600–1155 BCE)

    Kassite Dynasty: ~1595–1155 BCE; non-native rulers; cultural continuity; stable period.

    International Diplomacy: Amarna letters; Egypt, Hatti, Mitanni correspondence; cuneiform diplomacy.

    Kudurru Stones: Boundary stones; land grants; divine symbols; legal records.

    Scribal Tradition: Canonical text series; cataloging; library organization; scholarly culture.

    Middle Babylonian Language: Akkadian standard; diplomatic lingua franca.

    Elamite Invasion: 1155 BCE; Shutruk-Nahhunte; Marduk statue taken; dynasty ends.

    7. Post-Kassite Fragmentation (1155–911 BCE)

    Second Dynasty of Isin: Nebuchadnezzar I (~1125–1104 BCE); Marduk statue recovered; brief revival.

    Political Instability: Weak dynasties; Aramean, Chaldean pressure; territorial fragmentation.

    Aramean Infiltration: Nomadic groups; settlements; Aramaic language spreads.

    Assyrian Pressure: Middle Assyrian resurgence; Tiglath-Pileser I; campaigns into Babylonia.

    Cultural Persistence: Scribal schools continue; temple worship; traditional learning.

    Transitional Era: Cuneiform maintained; Aramaic alphabetic script emerges as alternative.

    8. Neo-Assyrian Empire (911–612 BCE)

    Imperial Expansion: Ashurnasirpal II, Shalmaneser III, Tiglath-Pileser III, Sargon II, Sennacherib.

    Ashurbanipal's Library: Nineveh; ~30,000 tablets; literary, scholarly, administrative; preservation.

    Administrative System: Provincial governors; tribute records; correspondence archives; deportations.

    Aramaic Adoption: Aramaic alongside cuneiform; alphabetic convenience; administrative bilingualism.

    Royal Annals: Military campaigns; propaganda; palace reliefs with inscriptions.

    Fall of Nineveh: 612 BCE; Babylonian-Median coalition; empire collapses.

    9. Neo-Babylonian Period (612–539 BCE)

    Chaldean Dynasty: Nabopolassar, Nebuchadnezzar II (605–562 BCE); Babylon dominant.

    Babylon Rebuilt: Ishtar Gate; Processional Way; ziggurat; administrative center.

    Temple Archives: Eanna at Uruk; detailed economic records; priestly administration.

    Judean Exile: 586 BCE; Jerusalem destroyed; deportees in Babylon; cultural encounter.

    Nabonidus: 556–539 BCE; antiquarian interests; Sin worship controversy; absent kingship.

    Persian Conquest: Cyrus II; 539 BCE; Babylon falls; Achaemenid period begins.

    10. Achaemenid Mesopotamia (539–331 BCE)

    Cyrus Cylinder: Persian propaganda; Marduk's choice; restoration rhetoric.

    Aramaic Administration: Imperial Aramaic lingua franca; cuneiform continues for traditional texts.

    Babylon as Capital: One of imperial capitals; Persian kings crowned; economic activity.

    Cuneiform Persistence: Astronomical diaries; temple records; scribal continuity.

    Babylonian Revolts: Rebellions suppressed; Xerxes; reduced status over time.

    Alexander's Arrival: 331 BCE; Gaugamela; Babylon falls; Alexander dies there 323 BCE.

    11. Seleucid Mesopotamia (331–141 BCE)

    Alexander and Successors: Seleucus I; Seleucia on Tigris founded; Greek administration.

    Hellenistic Synthesis: Greek-Babylonian cultural mixing; Berossus; astronomical texts continue.

    Cuneiform Twilight: Last cuneiform texts ~1st century CE; astronomical tablets; scribal decline.

    Seleucia: New capital; Greek polis; Babylon declines; population shift.

    Parthian Pressure: Arsacid expansion; Seleucid retreat; Mesopotamia contested.

    Cultural Layering: Greek administration, Babylonian temples, Aramaic daily life.

    12. Parthian Period (141 BCE – 224 CE)

    Arsacid Rule: Parthian empire; Ctesiphon capital; decentralized governance.

    Aramaic Dominance: Aramaic administrative language; Greek continues in some contexts.

    Cuneiform Ends: Last dated cuneiform tablet 75 CE; astronomical tradition fades.

    Trade Networks: Silk Road; Palmyra connections; caravan commerce; Charax Spasinu.

    Jewish Communities: Babylonian Jews; proto-Talmudic development; exilarch leadership.

    Roman Conflicts: Carrhae 53 BCE; Trajan's invasion 116 CE; frontier zone.

    13. Sasanian Iraq (224–651 CE)

    Sasanian Empire: Ardashir I; Zoroastrian state; Ctesiphon capital; centralized administration.

    Religious Diversity: Zoroastrianism official; Nestorian Christianity; Jewish academies (Sura, Pumbedita).

    Talmudic Era: Babylonian Talmud compiled; rabbinic scholarship; Aramaic texts.

    Syriac Christianity: Nestorian church; monasteries; Syriac manuscripts; theological schools.

    Agricultural Development: Irrigation maintenance; Nahrawan canal; economic prosperity.

    Byzantine Wars: Centuries of conflict; Khosrow II's conquests; exhaustion before Islamic conquest.

    14. Arab Conquest and Umayyads (651–750 CE)

    Islamic Conquest: Qadisiyya 636; Ctesiphon falls; Sasanian collapse; Arab rule.

    Garrison Cities: Basra, Kufa founded; Arab settlement; administrative centers.

    Arabization: Arabic administrative language; conversion gradual; dhimmi status for others.

    Umayyad Period: Damascus-centered caliphate; Iraq as province; opposition center.

    Syriac Continuity: Christian communities; Syriac scholarship; translation activity.

    Abbasid Revolution: 750 CE; overthrow of Umayyads; Iraqi-centered movement.

    15. Abbasid Golden Age (750–945 CE)

    Baghdad Founded: 762 CE; al-Mansur; Round City; new capital; imperial center.

    Translation Movement: Bayt al-Hikma; Greek, Persian, Syriac texts into Arabic; Hunayn ibn Ishaq.

    Islamic Scholarship: Al-Khwarizmi (algebra); paper adoption; hadith collection; jurisprudence.

    Paper Revolution: Paper mills; replaces papyrus, parchment; manuscript proliferation.

    Economic Peak: Trade networks; banking; agricultural prosperity; urban growth.

    Caliphal Decline: Turkish guards; provincial autonomy; Samarra interlude (836–892).

    16. Buyid, Seljuk, and Mongol Catastrophe (945–1258 CE)

    Buyid Emirate: 945–1055; Shi'i rulers; caliph as figurehead; Iranian influence.

    Seljuk Dominance: 1055; Sunni restoration; Turkish military elite; madrasas established.

    Nizamiyya: Baghdad madrasa; al-Ghazali; Islamic learning; manuscript production.

    Crusader Era: Indirectly affected; Saladin's rise; regional realignment.

    Mongol Invasion: Hulagu; 1258; Baghdad sacked; caliph killed; catastrophic destruction.

    Cultural Losses: Libraries burned; irrigation damaged; population collapse; trauma.

    17. Post-Mongol Successor States (1258–1534 CE)

    Ilkhanate: Mongol rule; gradual conversion to Islam; partial recovery; Tabriz focus.

    Jalairid Dynasty: 1335–1432; Baghdad-centered; cultural revival; manuscript patronage.

    Tamerlane: 1401; Baghdad sacked again; devastation; slow recovery.

    Qara Qoyunlu and Aq Qoyunlu: Turkmen confederations; competing control; instability.

    Safavid-Ottoman Rivalry: Iraq contested; border zone; sectarian dimension.

    Manuscript Culture: Arabic, Persian texts; religious scholarship; reduced but continuing.

    18. Ottoman Iraq (1534–1850 CE)

    Ottoman Conquest: Suleiman I; 1534; Baghdad taken; eyalet of Baghdad established.

    Provincial Governance: Pashas; Mamluk governors (18th century); tribal relations; loose control.

    Shi'i Shrines: Najaf, Karbala; pilgrimage economy; clerical establishment; manuscript production.

    Economic Decline: Irrigation neglect; trade route shifts; population low; tribal predominance.

    Tanzimat Prelude: 19th century reforms; Midhat Pasha (1869–1872); modernization attempts.

    Manuscript Twilight: Print slowly arrives; traditional manuscript copying continues.

    19. Cuneiform Tradition and Its Successors (3200 BCE – 1850 CE)

    Proto-Cuneiform: ~3200 BCE; pictographic; administrative tokens; Uruk innovation.

    Classic Cuneiform: Logosyllabic; Sumerian, Akkadian, Hittite; international script.

    Scribal Training: Edubba schools; lexical lists; copying exercises; professional class.

    Aramaic Transition: 8th century BCE onward; alphabetic convenience; administrative adoption.

    Syriac and Arabic: Christian Syriac manuscripts; Islamic Arabic dominance; paper facilitates.

    Script Succession: Cuneiform → Aramaic → Syriac/Arabic; continuous documentation tradition.

    20. Babylon Across Five Millennia (3200 BCE – 1850 CE)

    Early Settlement: Akkadian period; modest city; Hammurabi's capital; first prominence.

    Kassite Era: Stable center; international recognition; religious significance.

    Neo-Babylonian Glory: Nebuchadnezzar II; Ishtar Gate; ziggurat; imperial capital.

    Hellenistic Decline: Seleucid neglect; Seleucia rival; population shrinks.

    Symbolic Persistence: Biblical resonance; Tower of Babel; Alexander's death site; scholarly memory.

    Ottoman Hillul: Village on ruins; archaeological discovery 19th century; legacy.
