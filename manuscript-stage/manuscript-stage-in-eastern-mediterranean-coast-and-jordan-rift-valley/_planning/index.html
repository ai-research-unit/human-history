    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Eastern Mediterranean Coast and Jordan Rift Valley (2000 BCE – 1850 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 2000 BCE – 1850 CE

    Region: The Eastern Mediterranean Coast and Jordan Rift Valley — the Levantine littoral from Ugarit to Gaza, the inland valleys and highlands from the Anti-Lebanon to the Negev, and the Jordan Rift from the Sea of Galilee to the Dead Sea; the crossroads between Mesopotamia, Egypt, Anatolia, and the Mediterranean world.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes cuneiform tablets (Ugarit, Amarna), Proto-Sinaitic and Proto-Canaanite inscriptions, Phoenician alphabetic texts, Hebrew Bible manuscripts (Dead Sea Scrolls), Aramaic documents, Greek and Latin inscriptions, Syriac and Arabic manuscripts, Crusader charters, Mamluk and Ottoman administrative records. The region is the origin of the Phoenician alphabet (~1050 BCE), ancestor of Greek, Latin, Arabic, and Hebrew scripts. Window begins with Middle Bronze Age city-states using cuneiform; ends as Ottoman print reforms and Egyptian modernization transform public administration.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Middle Bronze Age Canaanite City-States (2000–1550 BCE)

    Urban Centers: Hazor, Megiddo, Gezer, Shechem, Jericho; fortified cities; palatial architecture; regional polities.

    Cuneiform Correspondence: Diplomatic and commercial letters; scribal training in Akkadian; Mesopotamian influence.

    Egyptian Contacts: Execration texts; trade relations; Middle Kingdom influence wanes, returns with New Kingdom.

    Proto-Sinaitic Script: ~1800 BCE Sinai inscriptions; earliest alphabetic experiments; Canaanite origin.

    Material Culture: Distinctive pottery; bronze weapons; international exchange; wealth accumulation.

    Social Organization: City-based kingdoms; temple establishments; merchant classes; agricultural hinterlands.

    2. Late Bronze Age (1550–1200 BCE)

    Egyptian Hegemony: New Kingdom control; garrison towns; vassal rulers; tribute extraction.

    Amarna Letters: ~1350 BCE diplomatic archive; Canaanite rulers write to Pharaoh; cuneiform in Akkadian.

    Ugarit: Major port city; cuneiform alphabetic script; mythological texts (Baal cycle); cosmopolitan culture.

    Alphabetic Crystallization: Proto-Canaanite evolves toward linear alphabet; simplification; wider accessibility.

    International System: Egypt, Hatti, Assyria, Babylon interact; treaties; trade networks; diplomatic marriages.

    Late Bronze Collapse Prelude: 13th century pressures; Sea Peoples movements begin; system stress.

    3. Bronze Age Collapse (1200–1000 BCE)

    Sea Peoples: Philistines settle coastal plain; new material culture; disruption of old order.

    Egyptian Withdrawal: Ramesses III repels Sea Peoples but Egypt contracts; loss of Levantine control.

    Israelite Emergence: Highland settlement expansion; tribal confederation; early Hebrew identity.

    Phoenician Continuity: Coastal cities (Tyre, Sidon, Byblos) survive; alphabetic tradition continues.

    Political Fragmentation: Old city-states weakened; new ethnic/political configurations emerge.

    Alphabetic Spread: Linear alphabet stabilizes; Phoenician script emerges as standard; consonantal system.

    4. Iron Age Kingdoms (1000–720 BCE)

    United Monarchy Tradition: David, Solomon (~1000–930 BCE); Jerusalem capital; extent debated.

    Divided Kingdoms: Israel (north) and Judah (south) after ~930 BCE; Omride dynasty; Samaria built.

    Phoenician Expansion: Maritime trade; Carthage founded (~814 BCE); alphabet spreads to Greece.

    Alphabetic Literacy: Royal inscriptions (Mesha Stele, Tel Dan); administrative records; wider literacy than cuneiform.

    Israelite Religion: Temple cult; prophetic tradition; early biblical texts composed.

    Assyrian Threat: Tiglath-Pileser III onward; tribute demands; territorial absorption begins.

    5. Assyrian and Babylonian Domination (720–586 BCE)

    Assyrian Conquest: Israel falls 722 BCE; deportation; Judah vassal; Sennacherib's siege 701 BCE.

    Aramaic Lingua Franca: Imperial administration uses Aramaic; alphabetic script spreads; bilingualism.

    Judean Survival: Hezekiah, Josiah reforms; Deuteronomic movement; temple centralization.

    Babylonian Conquest: Nebuchadnezzar; Jerusalem destroyed 586 BCE; elite deportation to Babylon.

    Exile Literature: Prophetic texts; Deuteronomistic history edited; theological reflection.

    Script Transition: Aramaic square script begins replacing paleo-Hebrew; manuscript tradition.

    6. Persian Period (586–332 BCE)

    Achaemenid Empire: Cyrus conquers Babylon 539 BCE; Jews permitted return; Yehud province.

    Temple Rebuilt: Second Temple completed ~516 BCE; priestly authority; community reorganization.

    Torah Codification: Pentateuch reaches near-final form; Ezra tradition; scriptural authority.

    Aramaic Administration: Official documents in Aramaic; Elephantine papyri show diaspora practice.

    Phoenician Continuity: Sidon, Tyre prosper under Persia; coinage; Mediterranean trade.

    Provincial Life: Limited autonomy; Persian garrison; modest population; agricultural base.

    7. Hellenistic Transformation (332–167 BCE)

    Alexander's Conquest: 332 BCE; Gaza siege; end of Persian rule; Greek era begins.

    Ptolemaic Rule: Egypt controls Palestine ~301–200 BCE; Alexandria influence; Greek immigration.

    Seleucid Rule: After 200 BCE; Antiochus III; Greek cities (poleis); gymnasium culture.

    Septuagint: Greek translation of Torah; Alexandria; Jewish Hellenization.

    Hellenization Tensions: Greek vs. Jewish identities; temple politics; high priest conflicts.

    Antiochus IV Epiphanes: Temple desecration 167 BCE; traditional cult suppressed; rebellion triggers.

    8. Hasmonean Kingdom (167–63 BCE)

    Maccabean Revolt: Judas Maccabeus; guerrilla war; temple rededication 164 BCE (Hanukkah).

    Independence: Simon achieves autonomy ~142 BCE; Hasmonean dynasty; expanding territory.

    Territorial Expansion: John Hyrcanus, Alexander Jannaeus; forcible conversions; coast and Transjordan.

    Sectarian Diversity: Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes (Qumran); Dead Sea Scrolls community.

    Manuscript Production: Biblical manuscripts copied; sectarian texts; scriptural interpretation.

    Civil Conflict: Fratricidal wars; Roman intervention invited; Pompey's arrival.

    9. Roman Rule and Jewish Revolts (63 BCE – 135 CE)

    Pompey's Conquest: 63 BCE; temple entered; Judea becomes Roman client/province.

    Herodian Era: Herod the Great ~37–4 BCE; temple renovation; Roman clientage; Hellenistic building.

    Jesus Movement: Early 1st century CE; crucifixion ~30 CE; early Christian community; New Testament texts.

    First Jewish Revolt: 66–73 CE; Jerusalem destroyed 70 CE; Masada; Josephus writes histories.

    Rabbinic Emergence: Yavneh academy; Mishnah compilation begins; oral tradition codified.

    Bar Kokhba Revolt: 132–135 CE; final revolt; massive casualties; Judea renamed Syria Palaestina.

    10. Roman-Byzantine Palestine (135–638 CE)

    Post-Bar Kokhba: Jewish population reduced; Galilee center; Talmud (Jerusalem) compiled ~400 CE.

    Christian Ascendancy: Constantine; pilgrimage begins; church construction; Holy Land concept.

    Church Building: Church of Holy Sepulchre (335 CE); Bethlehem, Nazareth sites; monastic foundations.

    Samaritan Revolts: 5th–6th centuries; suppression; population decline.

    Persian Invasion: 614 CE; Jerusalem captured; churches destroyed; brief occupation.

    Byzantine Recovery: Heraclius; 630 CE; brief restoration before Arab conquest.

    11. Early Islamic Period (638–1099 CE)

    Arab Conquest: Umar; Jerusalem surrenders 638 CE; Pact of Umar; dhimmi status for Christians, Jews.

    Umayyad Damascus: Caliphate capital nearby; Dome of the Rock (691 CE); Al-Aqsa Mosque.

    Abbasid Shift: Capital to Baghdad 762 CE; Palestine provincial; Ramla administrative center.

    Christian Communities: Greek Orthodox, Melkite, Syriac churches continue; pilgrimage maintained.

    Fatimid Period: 969–1099 CE; Shi'i caliphate from Cairo; al-Hakim's destructions; instability.

    Manuscript Culture: Arabic scholarship; copying traditions; trilingual (Arabic, Greek, Syriac) texts.

    12. Crusader States (1099–1291 CE)

    First Crusade: Jerusalem captured 1099 CE; massacre; Latin Kingdom established; Frankish settlement.

    Kingdom Organization: Feudal structure; military orders (Templars, Hospitallers); castle building.

    Intercultural Exchange: Translation; medical knowledge; material culture; Arabic-Latin contacts.

    Muslim Counteroffensive: Zengi, Nur ad-Din; Saladin; Hattin 1187; Jerusalem lost.

    Later Crusades: Third Crusade; coastal strip retained; Frederick II's negotiated return; final losses.

    Fall of Acre: 1291; Mamluk conquest; end of Crusader presence; manuscript archives dispersed.

    13. Mamluk Sultanate (1291–1516 CE)

    Cairo-Based Rule: Mamluk sultans; Palestine as province; strategic importance reduced.

    Pilgrimage Management: Christian and Muslim pilgrimage continues; Franciscan custody; tolerated minorities.

    Economic Decline: Trade shifts; plague (Black Death 1348); population decline; urban contraction.

    Jerusalem Restored: Modest rebuilding; madrasas founded; Sufi lodges; scholarly visits.

    Manuscript Continuity: Arabic copying continues; Christian and Jewish communities maintain traditions.

    Ottoman Threat: Rising Ottoman power; Mamluk decline; conquest imminent.

    14. Early Ottoman Period (1516–1700 CE)

    Selim I's Conquest: 1516–17; Mamluk defeat; Palestine incorporated into Ottoman Empire.

    Administrative Integration: Sanjak of Jerusalem, Damascus province; timar system; provincial stability.

    Jerusalem Restoration: Suleiman the Magnificent; walls rebuilt 1535–42; religious sites maintained.

    Millet System: Religious communities self-governing; Greek Orthodox, Armenian, Jewish quarters.

    Christian Missions: Franciscan, Greek Orthodox competition; European powers seek protectorates.

    Regional Stability: Relatively peaceful period; modest economic recovery; manuscript copying.

    15. Late Ottoman Decentralization (1700–1831 CE)

    Zahir al-Umar: Galilee strongman ~1730–1775; Acre rebuilt; cotton trade; local autonomy.

    Ahmad Pasha al-Jazzar: Acre ruler 1775–1804; defeats Napoleon 1799; regional power.

    Napoleon's Invasion: 1798–99; Egypt, Palestine campaign; Jaffa, Acre siege; withdrawal.

    Local Autonomy: Weak central control; local notables; tribal powers; insecurity.

    Population Stability: Low population; modest urban centers; agriculture; pilgrimage continues.

    Reform Pressures: Ottoman reform attempts begin; Mahmud II centralizing; Tanzimat anticipated.

    16. Egyptian Occupation and Tanzimat Beginnings (1831–1850 CE)

    Muhammad Ali's Invasion: Egyptian forces 1831–1840; Ibrahim Pasha; modernizing administration.

    Reforms: Conscription; taxation; equality rhetoric; missionary activity permitted; European influence.

    Ottoman Restoration: Great powers intervene; Egypt withdraws 1840; Ottoman control restored.

    Tanzimat Reforms: 1839 Gülhane edict; legal reforms; land registration begins; modernization.

    Missionary Printing: Protestant missions; printing presses established; Arabic print emerges.

    Manuscript Twilight: Administrative records increasingly printed; traditional copying declines.
