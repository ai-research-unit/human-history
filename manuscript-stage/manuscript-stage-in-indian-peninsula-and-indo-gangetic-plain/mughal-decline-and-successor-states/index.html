    1707 CE – 1765 CE — Mughal Decline and Successor States

    Summary: A half-century of imperial dissolution following Aurangzeb's death, during which effective sovereignty fragments among successor states while the Mughal emperor becomes a ceremonial figurehead; binding constraint is the collapsed fiscal-military nexus as provincial governors, Marathas, and Afghans vie for revenue and territory; margin against external shock eliminated by 1739 when Nadir Shah sacks Delhi and by 1757 when Ahmad Shah Durrani repeats the devastation; trajectory moves from contested succession through regional consolidation to European commercial penetration culminating in the Company's Bengal diwani (1765).

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Subcontinent remains arena; Gangetic heartland fought over; Deccan under Maratha expansion; Bengal's riverine terrain favors local powers and European coastal positions; mountain passes admit Afghan invasions; spatial friction now works against any single hegemon.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Monsoon regime unchanged; regional droughts continue to disrupt revenue and provisioning; 1769–70 Bengal famine (just beyond window) prefigured by earlier local scarcities; volatility strains weak successor administrations.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Floods, droughts, localized famines; hazard exposure unchanged but weakened state capacity reduces buffering; armies and populations more vulnerable than under consolidated Mughal rule.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Endowment intact; Bengal's fertility and textile production attract European interest; Deccan's revenue base contested; forests, minerals remain but extraction disrupted by warfare; resources redistributed among competing powers.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban warfare damages infrastructure; some cities (Delhi) devastated by invasions; waste absorption capacity unchanged physically but social disruption reduces maintenance; localized degradation around war-damaged cities.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic diseases persist; warfare spreads disease along marching routes; famines and sieges produce mortality spikes; no new epidemics but baseline mortality elevated in war zones.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Agricultural disruption degrades nutrition in contested areas; Bengal remains productive; Awadh, Hyderabad relatively stable; nutritional vitality tracks regional security; uneven across fragmented polity.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Patterns unchanged; war casualties skew sex ratios locally; elite households continue polygyny; no shift in reproductive biology; population growth slowed by warfare mortality.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population stagnant or slightly declining in war-affected regions; Bengal, Awadh maintain density; Delhi and Gangetic plain depopulated by invasions and warfare; age structure stressed by male casualties.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: War widows, orphans, displaced persons accumulate; charitable institutions (Sufi, Sikh, Hindu) absorb some; state incapable of systematic relief; dependency overhang significant in worst-affected areas.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Peasant agriculture continues; revenue demands contested among multiple claimants; subsistence squeezed in war zones; Bengal relatively prosperous; floor adequate where order maintained, precarious elsewhere.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: No change in energy regime; human and animal muscle; agricultural surplus still generated but diverted to military competition rather than accumulation; per-capita work ceiling unchanged.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Peasant labor under multiple revenue claimants; weavers in Bengal increasingly tied to European factors; artisan production continues; coercion shifts from Mughal center to regional powers and Company buyers.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Bengal textiles, Awadh agriculture, Deccan trade continue; overall production maintained in stable regions; surplus contested rather than centrally accumulated; productive capacity intact but not coordinated.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Imperial treasury exhausted; regional states accumulate locally; Nadir Shah and Ahmad Shah Durrani carry off Delhi's remaining treasure; buffering capacity fragmented and inadequate against major shocks.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Internal trade disrupted by warfare and toll extraction; coastal trade flourishes under European protection; Bengal-Europe trade expands; logistics fragmented; Surat declines, Calcutta rises.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Mughal rupee loses uniformity; regional currencies proliferate; shroff networks adapt; European bills of exchange enter; capital flows toward Company-controlled areas; credit system resilient but fragmenting.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Multiple claimants extract from same peasantry; Marathas levy chauth and sardeshmukhi; Awadh and Hyderabad nawabs collect tribute; extraction intensifies, redistribution collapses into warlord competition.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Concentration shifts from Delhi to regional courts and European trading posts; nobles diversify holdings; merchant wealth seeks Company protection; asset defense via mobility, diversification, and alignment with rising powers.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Craft production waste unchanged; war debris and unburied dead in conflict zones; routing informal; sink capacity adequate physically but social disruption prevents maintenance; localized degradation.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust radius contracts amid political uncertainty; caste and kin networks remain primary; inter-community trust damaged by succession wars; regional identities (Maratha, Sikh, Jat) strengthen; mutual aid localizes.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Maratha, Rajput, Afghan military identities compete; regional nawabi courts create local elites; caste hierarchy intact; Hindu-Muslim relations variable by region; European presence adds new status gradations.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patterns unchanged; elite families hedge across factions; marriage alliances realign to new power centers; inheritance customs continue; joint family absorbs war losses.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Caste panchayats, guilds, religious associations persist; Sikh Khalsa emerges as militant brotherhood; Maratha Brahmin administrative networks expand; self-organization fills state vacuum.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Regional courts maintain culture; Lucknow, Hyderabad develop distinctive styles; festivals continue; sociability retreats from war zones to stable courts; nawabi culture flourishes in Awadh.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Petitioning fragmented among multiple courts; religious mediation continues; armed resistance (Maratha, Sikh, Jat) becomes grievance channel; discharge uneven; some grievances accumulate as standing instability.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Mughal emperor nominal sovereign; real power with Marathas (Peshwa at Pune), Nizam of Hyderabad, Nawab of Awadh, Nawab of Bengal; multiple competing centers; authority fragmenting.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Mughal succession wars 1707–19 produce eleven emperors in twelve years; regional dynasties more stable; Peshwa succession orderly; transfer mechanisms vary by polity; Delhi succession a flashpoint.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Mughal farman still legitimates; regional rulers claim autonomy under nominal Mughal sovereignty; Marathas invoke Hindu revival; Sikhs invoke Guru's legacy; multiple competing legitimacy narratives.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Mughal center collapses; regional administrations variable; Awadh and Hyderabad maintain Persian bureaucracy; Maratha administration efficient; Bengal increasingly under Company influence; capacity fragmenting.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Maratha confederacy involves multiple sardars; Sikh confederacy (misl system) disperses authority; elite participation in regional courts; mass mobilization via Maratha light cavalry, Sikh armed community; participation broadens locally.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Afghan invasions (Nadir Shah 1739, Ahmad Shah 1747–61) devastate north; Marathas push to Delhi then destroyed at Panipat (1761); Europeans expand coastal influence; external pressures fragment subcontinent.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Multiple armies; Maratha light cavalry dominant; Afghan heavy cavalry defeats Marathas; Mughal artillery captured by invaders; European-trained sepoy armies emerging; force dispersed among competing powers.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Widespread banditry; warfare endemic; Thuggee active; no single authority maintains order; threat environment high; disorder normalized in contested zones; stable areas under strong regional rulers.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Imperial postal system fragments; regional systems emerge; akhbarat continue but report to multiple courts; velocity unchanged technically but coordination lost; Europeans develop own networks.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Regional courts maintain records; Maratha Peshwa archives extensive; Hyderabad, Awadh continue Persian documentation; manuscript production continues in stable courts; some loss in war-damaged areas.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: No central framing; regional courts control local discourse; multiple narratives compete; Persian remains administrative language; censorship localized; discourse space more varied than under centralized empire.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Elite audiences fragment by region; oral transmission continues; regional literatures (Urdu poetry in Lucknow, Marathi chronicles) flourish; attention localizes; amplification via regional networks.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Multiple conflicting reports from fragmented polity; signal integrity declines; noise increases as coordinating authority collapses; verification difficult across political boundaries.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Regional variation; Maratha courts invoke dharma; Muslim states continue Sharia; sense of justice fragments along political lines; shared moral foundation weakened but not collapsed.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Fatawa-i-Alamgiri continues in Muslim states; Maratha customary law; Sikh codes emerging; no new comprehensive codification; legal evolution localized; regulatory fragmentation.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Qazis in Muslim areas; panchayats and Brahmin judges in Hindu areas; enforcement depends on local power; liability assigned per regional custom; adjudication functions but inconsistently across boundaries.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Variable by region; Hindu subjects better protected under Marathas; Muslims under nawabs; compliance burden depends on local extraction intensity; predictability within regions, chaos across them.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Invaders unpunished; warlords beyond reach; backlog in disrupted courts; impunity widespread in contested zones; redress available in stable regions; overall gap between justice owed and delivered widening.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Persian-Islamic learning continues in Awadh, Hyderabad; Sanskrit scholarship in Maratha areas; no paradigm shift; traditional inquiry maintained in stable courts; investigation disrupted in war zones.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Architecture, textile production, agriculture continue; techniques transmitted via guilds; no new technical methods; European technical influence beginning in coastal areas; military technology borrowed.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Madrasas, pathshalas continue where patronage survives; Lucknow emerges as learning center; literacy stable among traditional elites; knowledge stock maintained in stable regions, threatened in war zones.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Some manuscript collections destroyed (Delhi sack); institutional continuity broken in worst-affected areas; traditional learning maintained in Awadh, Hyderabad, Pune; decay localized rather than systemic.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Maratha identity invokes Shivaji's legacy; Sikh memory of martyred Gurus; Mughal nostalgia among Muslim elites; regional identities crystallize; composite Mughal culture fragments into distinct streams.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Lucknow, Hyderabad develop distinctive styles; Pahari painting flourishes in hills; Deccan painting continues; architecture less monumental; manuscript production continues in nawabi courts; regionalization of artistic expression.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Anxiety in war zones; relative confidence in stable courts; nostalgia for Mughal order; pragmatic adaptation to new powers; mood variable by region; resilience in population accustomed to instability.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Mughal court forms continue but lose power as center collapses; regional adaptations renew some forms (Lucknawi refinement); syncretic symbols contested; meaning decay in former imperial symbols, renewal in regional ones.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Plural cosmologies; Sunni, Shia, Hindu, Sikh coexist; fatalism amid political chaos; devotional traditions provide solace; existential outlook shaped by regional dominance; no unified cosmological shift.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mosque, temple, gurdwara practice continues; pilgrimage disrupted by warfare in some areas; Muharram processions elaborate in Lucknow; devotional intensity high as political order collapses; ritual provides stability amid chaos.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Ulema influential in Muslim courts; Brahmin authority in Maratha domains; Sikh Khalsa consolidates; temple and mosque institutions persist; doctrinal authority localized to regional powers.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Syncretic traditions continue at popular level; political boundaries harden religious identities; Sikh identity sharpens; no displacement but hardening of boundaries between traditions.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No secularization; sacred remains potent; political chaos interpreted through religious frames; spiritual need met within traditions; no disenchantment; sacred energy channeled into regional and communal identities.
