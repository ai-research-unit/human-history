    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain (300 BCE – 1850 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 300 BCE – 1850 CE

    Region: The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain — the South Asian subcontinent from the Himalayan foothills to Cape Comorin, including the Indus and Gangetic river systems, the Deccan plateau, and the coastal plains; bounded by the Hindu Kush, Himalayas, and ocean.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Ashokan edicts (Brahmi and Kharosthi), Sanskrit and Prakrit manuscripts on palm leaf, birch bark, and later paper, copper plate grants, temple and cave inscriptions, Persian and Arabic administrative documents, regional language manuscripts. Brahmi script develops by ~300 BCE; regional scripts diverge; Sanskrit codification; vernacular literatures emerge; Persian becomes administrative language under sultanates/Mughals; paper widespread by 13th century. Window begins with Mauryan epigraphy; ends as British colonial print transforms public sphere (~1835 English Education Act, vernacular press growth).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Mauryan Empire (300–185 BCE)

    Chandragupta Maurya: ~321–297 BCE; defeats Nanda dynasty; builds empire; Arthashastra tradition.

    Ashoka: ~268–232 BCE; Kalinga war; Buddhist conversion; dhamma policy; pillar and rock edicts.

    Brahmi Script: Developed by ~300 BCE; earliest widely attested Indian script; ancestor of South/Southeast Asian scripts.

    Kharosthi Script: Northwestern script; Aramaic-derived; Buddhist texts; fades by 3rd century CE.

    Ashokan Edicts: Major inscriptions across subcontinent; administrative communication; earliest substantial Indian texts.

    Imperial Administration: Maurya bureaucracy; spies, officials, roads; writing enables centralized control.

    2. Post-Mauryan Kingdoms (185 BCE – 100 CE)

    Shunga Dynasty: 185–73 BCE; Brahmanical revival; Bharhut, Sanchi stupas; Sanskrit resurges.

    Satavahana Dynasty: Deccan; ~1st century BCE–3rd century CE; Prakrit inscriptions; Buddhist patronage.

    Indo-Greeks: Bactrian Greek kingdoms; bilingual coinage; Greek-Brahmi scripts; Menander.

    Indo-Scythians/Parthians: Central Asian rulers; continued coinage tradition; cultural synthesis.

    Buddhist and Jain Texts: Pali canon written down (~1st century BCE); Jain manuscripts; monastic copying.

    Commercial Writing: Trade expansion; guild records; donative inscriptions; growing literacy.

    3. Kushan Empire (100–320 CE)

    Kushan Dynasty: Central Asian origin; Kanishka peak ~127–150 CE; northwest India/Central Asia.

    Gandhara Art: Greco-Buddhist sculpture; Buddhist narrative art; inscriptions.

    Mahayana Buddhism: New scriptures; Sanskrit preferred; Nagarjuna, philosophical texts.

    Silk Road Connections: Trade networks; Buddhist missionaries; manuscript transmission to China.

    Mathura School: Hindu and Jain art; inscriptions; religious manuscript traditions.

    Multiple Scripts: Kharosthi declining; Brahmi variants; Greek influence; Sanskrit emerging.

    4. Gupta Empire (320–550 CE)

    Imperial Consolidation: Samudragupta, Chandragupta II; North Indian empire; classical age.

    Sanskrit Literature: Kalidasa; drama, poetry; manuscript copying; royal patronage.

    Nalanda University: Buddhist monastic university; major manuscript center; international scholars.

    Scientific Treatises: Aryabhata (astronomy, mathematics); Varahamihira; Charaka, Sushruta (medicine).

    Temple Inscriptions: Copper plates; land grants; religious foundations; administrative records.

    Gupta Script: Standardized Brahmi derivative; ancestor of Nagari and regional scripts.

    5. Post-Gupta Fragmentation (550–750 CE)

    Harsha: 606–647 CE; North Indian empire; Xuanzang visits; Buddhist patronage; Harshacharita biography.

    Chalukyas of Badami: Deccan power; Aihole inscription; temple architecture; Sanskrit and Kannada.

    Pallavas: Tamil Nadu; Mahabalipuram; Grantha script; Sanskrit-Tamil synthesis.

    Bhakti Emergence: Devotional poetry; Alvars, Nayanars; Tamil bhakti; vernacular religiosity.

    Regional Scripts: Script families diverge; Telugu-Kannada, Tamil, Devanagari lines develop.

    Land Grant System: Copper plates proliferate; Brahmanical land ownership; temple endowments.

    6. Tripartite Struggle (750–1000 CE)

    Pratihara Dynasty: North-central India; Kannauj; resist Arab expansion; Sanskrit patronage.

    Rashtrakutas: Deccan; military power; Ellora caves; Kannada-Sanskrit literature.

    Pala Dynasty: Bengal-Bihar; Buddhist patronage; Vikramashila, Nalanda; tantric texts.

    Temple-State Synthesis: Major temple complexes; administrative centers; inscriptional records.

    Tantric Literature: Hindu and Buddhist tantras; esoteric manuscripts; ritual texts.

    Regional Scripts Mature: Nagari, Telugu-Kannada, Bengali, Oriya scripts stabilize.

    7. Chola Imperium and Turkish Incursions (1000–1206 CE)

    Chola Empire: Rajaraja I, Rajendra I; Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia; naval power; temple administration.

    Brihadishvara Temple: Thanjavur; massive inscriptional record; land, ritual, personnel documentation.

    Ghaznavid Raids: Mahmud of Ghazni; 997–1030; temple wealth plundered; Buddhist centers destroyed.

    Muhammad of Ghor: 1175–1206; defeats Prithviraj III 1192; Delhi conquered; sultanate foundation.

    Destruction of Nalanda: ~1193; monastic libraries destroyed; Buddhist manuscript tradition disrupted.

    Regional Resilience: South India largely unaffected; literary production continues; Tamil, Telugu literature.

    8. Delhi Sultanate (1206–1398 CE)

    Mamluk, Khalji, Tughlaq Dynasties: Turkish-Afghan rulers; Delhi capital; expanding sultanate.

    Persian Administration: Persian as court/administrative language; Islamic legal texts; chronicle writing.

    Islamic Learning: Madrasas; Sufi lodges; Arabic and Persian manuscripts; translation.

    Regional Sultanates Emerge: Bengal, Malwa, Gujarat, Bahmani; decentralization begins.

    Hindu-Muslim Interactions: Vernacular bhakti; Kabir, Sufis; syncretic traditions; manuscript diversity.

    Paper Introduction: Paper manufacture spreads; replaces palm leaf in north; manuscript production expands.

    9. Sultanate Fragmentation (1398–1526 CE)

    Timur's Invasion: 1398; Delhi sacked; Sultanate weakened; regional fragmentation accelerates.

    Bahmani Sultanate: Deccan; breaks into five sultanates 1490s; Deccan Persian culture.

    Vijayanagara Empire: Hindu kingdom; Hampi capital; Telugu, Kannada literature; temple patronage.

    Bengal, Gujarat, Malwa: Independent sultanates; regional manuscript cultures; vernacular growth.

    Vernacular Literature: Bengali, Gujarati, Marathi, Braj Bhasha; devotional poetry; manuscript proliferation.

    Sikhism Emerges: Guru Nanak (~1469–1539); Adi Granth tradition begins; Gurmukhi script.

    10. Mughal Foundation (1526–1605 CE)

    Babur: 1526; Panipat; Baburnama memoir; Central Asian literary tradition.

    Humayun/Sher Shah Interlude: Afghan interruption; administrative innovations; restored Mughal rule.

    Akbar: 1556–1605; empire consolidates; mansabdari system; Din-i-Ilahi; tolerance.

    Ain-i-Akbari: Abu'l Fazl; administrative gazetteer; detailed documentation of empire.

    Imperial Manuscripts: Illustrated histories; royal library; Persian translation projects.

    Religious Synthesis: Translation of Sanskrit texts; Akbar's eclecticism; multi-script administration.

    11. High Mughal Empire (1605–1707 CE)

    Jahangir: 1605–27; Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri memoir; naturalist painting; wine and culture.

    Shah Jahan: 1628–58; Taj Mahal; Shahjahannama; architectural inscriptions; court splendor.

    Aurangzeb: 1658–1707; Deccan conquest; religious conservatism; fatawa compilation; overextension.

    Miniature Painting: Mughal school; manuscript illustration; court ateliers; artistic peak.

    Historical Chronicles: Extensive Persian histories; administrative documentation; manuscript bureaucracy.

    Deccan Campaigns: Long wars; administrative strain; Maratha resistance; imperial overstretch.

    12. Mughal Decline and Successor States (1707–1765 CE)

    Post-Aurangzeb Crisis: Succession wars; weak emperors; provincial autonomy; invasions.

    Marathas: Shivaji's legacy; Peshwa administration; Marathi documents; territorial expansion.

    Nizam of Hyderabad: Deccan autonomy; Persian administration continues; court culture.

    Nawabs of Bengal, Awadh: Regional successor states; revenue administration; manuscript culture.

    Nadir Shah/Ahmad Shah Durrani: Invasions; Delhi sacked 1739, 1757; imperial collapse.

    Regional Manuscript Cultures: Bengal, Awadh, Mysore; vernacular and Persian; diverse traditions.

    13. Company Expansion (1765–1818 CE)

    Battle of Plassey: 1757; Clive; Bengal under Company influence; revenue control.

    Diwani Grant: 1765; Company revenue authority; Persian records; administrative continuity.

    Warren Hastings: 1773–85; Orientalist scholarship; Sanskrit, Persian studies; translation projects.

    Revenue Records: Land surveys; settlement records; Persian and English documentation.

    Third Anglo-Maratha War: 1817–18; Maratha defeat; Company dominance; subsidiary system.

    Persian Continues: Administrative language until 1830s; gradual English encroachment.

    14. Consolidation and Administrative Transition (1818–1835 CE)

    Subsidiary Alliances: Princely states under British supervision; dual administration.

    Legal Codification: Regulation provinces; law codes; written records; court documentation.

    Vernacular Revival: Ram Mohan Roy; Bengali prose; reformist literature; social critique.

    Print Expands: Missionary presses; vernacular newspapers; Calcutta intellectual life.

    Persian Declining: English increasingly used; 1835 English replaces Persian officially.

    Manuscript Culture Persists: Traditional education; religious texts; manuscript copying continues.

    15. English Education and Manuscript Twilight (1835–1850 CE)

    Macaulay's Minute: 1835; English education policy; English language in administration.

    English Education Act: English medium schools; Western curriculum; new elite formation.

    Vernacular Press Growth: Newspapers, journals, books in regional languages; print dominates.

    Traditional Learning Marginal: Sanskrit pathshalas, madrasas decline; colonial education ascendant.

    Manuscript Culture Recedes: Print for administration, public discourse; manuscripts for religion, tradition.

    Railway and Telegraph: 1850s; communications revolution; print distribution expands; manuscript era ends.
