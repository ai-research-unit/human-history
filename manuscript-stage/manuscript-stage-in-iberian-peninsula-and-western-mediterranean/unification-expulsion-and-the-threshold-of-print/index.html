    1479 CE – 1500 CE — Unification, Expulsion, and the Threshold of Print

    Summary: The union of the Crowns of Castile and Aragon under Ferdinand and Isabella creates a dual monarchy that completes the Reconquista with the conquest of Granada (1492), expels the Jewish population, and sponsors Columbus's Atlantic crossing, all while print presses multiply across Iberian cities; the binding constraint is the Manuscript Stage, as administrative unification, inquisitorial procedure, expulsion orders, and royal propaganda still rely on handwritten registers and chancery documents even as printed books begin to circulate in parallel, marking the threshold where manuscript dominance yields to the print era.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The union joins Castile's vast Meseta and Atlantic coastline with Aragon's Mediterranean seaboard and its Italian dependencies, creating a composite realm stretching from Galicia to Sicily; the Pyrenees seal the northern frontier while the Strait of Gibraltar links Iberia to North Africa, a corridor the conquest of Granada now closes to Islamic counter-thrust.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Mediterranean and semi-arid conditions prevail in the south, Atlantic moisture in the northwest, and a harsh continental regime on the central plateau; variability is moderate, with no catastrophic drought during the window, permitting agrarian recovery after the fourteenth-century crisis.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seismic risk is low across most of the peninsula; flood hazard affects the major river valleys; coastal exposure to storm and piracy requires fortification of ports and patrols against Barbary raiders; no major natural catastrophe marks the window.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Iron from Basque forges, merino wool from Castilian flocks, Mediterranean grain, olive oil, and wine compose the agrarian surplus; silver from reopened Andalusian mines and gold imports from Guinea supplement the precious-metal stock; timber remains adequate for shipbuilding along the Cantabrian coast.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban concentrations in Seville, Barcelona, Toledo, and Valladolid strain local sanitation, but overall population density remains below pre-plague levels; rural sinks absorb agrarian runoff without visible saturation; the physical residue is light, with no major degradation recorded.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Plague recurs in localized outbreaks through the 1480s and 1490s but does not approach the lethality of 1348; endemic typhus, smallpox, and dysentery persist in crowded urban quarters; baseline mortality is elevated by poverty but not by pandemic crisis.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Grain harvests stabilize after the mid-century recovery; provisioning improves for middling and upper strata, while urban poor and displaced conversos face precarious access; stature and vitality recover slowly from the fourteenth-century nadir.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Hospitals attached to religious houses serve the indigent; court physicians attend the monarchs and grandees; folk healers circulate remedies in villages; medical faculties at Salamanca and Valladolid train a thin stratum of learned practitioners.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Marriage remains early among peasants, later among urban artisans; noble lineages negotiate unions for dynastic consolidation; fertility recovers as subsistence stabilizes, though infant mortality remains high across all strata.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population climbs from the post-plague trough, perhaps reaching five to six million in Castile and one million in Aragon by 1500; the expulsion of Jews (1492) removes perhaps 100,000 to 200,000 persons; urban density grows in Seville, which profits from Atlantic trade.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: The dependent load of orphans, widows, and aged is absorbed by kin networks, confraternities, and church charity; no demographic crisis overwhelms the productive cohort during the window; the residue is contained.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Grain, oil, and wine secure the subsistence floor for most households; periodic scarcity in interior Castile is offset by coastal imports; household pooling and communal gleaning rights buffer marginal producers.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle drive agriculture and transport; water mills grind grain and full cloth; charcoal fuels iron forges; no new energy source enters during the window; per-capita work capacity remains pre-industrial.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Free peasant tenancy predominates in Castile; serfdom lingers in Aragon's Catalan hinterland; urban guilds regulate craft labor; African slaves appear in Seville's docks and Andalusian plantations; the converso artisan class faces social exclusion after 1492.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Merino wool exports to Flanders generate Castile's principal surplus; Catalan textiles and Valencian ceramics supply Mediterranean markets; agrarian yields recover; royal revenue rises, funding the Granada campaign and the Atlantic venture.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Royal treasuries accumulate through the hermandad tax, crusade indulgences, and confiscated Jewish property; granaries buffer harvest shortfalls; noble houses hold landed reserves; credit from Genoese and converso bankers finances extraordinary expenditures.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: The Mesta's transhumance routes move wool to northern ports; Mediterranean galleys link Barcelona to Naples and Sicily; Atlantic shipping expands from Seville and Cadiz; internal tolls and poor roads slow overland commerce; Genoese merchants dominate high finance.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Castilian maravedi and Aragonese florin circulate alongside Genoese and Florentine coin; bills of exchange facilitate long-distance trade; converso bankers provide short-term credit; debasement is moderate; the monetary regime is stable.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: The Crown extracts alcabalas, tercias reales, and crusade subsidies; the Inquisition confiscates converso property; noble seigneurial rents persist; church tithes flow to cathedrals and monasteries; fiscal room expands with the Granada conquest.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Grandees and military orders hold vast latifundia in Andalusia; urban oligarchies control municipal office; converso merchants face expropriation; wealth concentration increases as expelled Jews forfeit property; asset defense relies on lineage, entail, and royal favor.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Urban refuse accumulates in streets and rivers; tanning and dyeing effluent pollutes watercourses; no systematic waste management exists; material residue is localized; financial mispricing from speculative ventures remains minor.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilineal inheritance prevails; mayorazgo (entail) protects noble estates; dowry and lineage purity (limpieza de sangre) govern marriage; converso families face genealogical scrutiny; extended kin networks provide mutual aid.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Confraternities, guilds, and religious brotherhoods knit urban society; rural communes manage commons and settle disputes; charity hospitals and alms distributions sustain the indigent; the fabric thickens as the economy recovers.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Festivals mark the liturgical calendar; bullfights and jousts entertain urban crowds; pilgrimages to Santiago de Compostela bind communities; taverns and plazas host daily sociability; Carnival inversions release tension before Lent.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: The three estates persist: clergy, nobility, and commoners; within the nobility, grandees outrank caballeros; hidalgos cling to tax exemptions; conversos form a suspect intermediate stratum; moriscos (baptized Moors) occupy the lowest rung after 1492; boundaries harden around purity of blood.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: The Catholic Monarchs centralize administration through the Royal Council, corregidores in towns, and the Santa Hermandad (rural police); the Inquisition operates as a parallel bureaucracy with its own records and prisons; chanceries in Valladolid and Granada register royal decrees; manuscript remains the medium of record.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: The Granada campaign mobilizes feudal levies, urban militias, and the military orders; artillery (lombards) breaches Moorish walls; the standing guard (Guardas Reales) protects the monarchs; the Santa Hermandad polices the countryside; Aragon maintains its own institutions; coercive capacity suffices for conquest and internal control.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: The expulsion of Jews and repression of conversos generate grievance among the dispossessed; rural banditry persists in frontier zones; noble factionalism is suppressed but not eliminated; the residue is contained by coercion and surveillance; the apparatus is expanding, not decaying.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Ferdinand and Isabella command joint authority, their partnership projecting unity; Cardinal Cisneros wields spiritual and administrative power; Columbus embodies the adventurer-visionary; the monarchs' personal presence legitimizes policy.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: The conquest of Granada and the Atlantic enterprise boost royal legitimacy; the Inquisition enforces religious conformity, deepening allegiance among Old Christians while alienating conversos; the dual monarchy commands broad assent.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: The monarchs display relentless will in completing the Reconquista, expelling religious minorities, and sponsoring exploration; succession remains anxious after the death of Prince Juan (1497); the ruling partnership coheres; collective resolve is high.

    External Relations & Posture: Dynastic marriages project Iberian power into Habsburg, Tudor, and Portuguese orbits; rivalry with France over Italy intensifies; Portugal accepts the Treaty of Tordesillas (1494), dividing Atlantic claims; the posture is expansive, oriented toward Europe and the Atlantic.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Triumphalism marks the conquest of Granada and the Atlantic discovery; religious fervor and purity anxiety grip the populace; trust contracts around lineage and Old Christian status; conversos live in fear of denunciation; the mood is confident but edged with suspicion.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Converso disaffection smolders under inquisitorial pressure; exiled Jews carry grievance into diaspora; morisco communities submit outwardly but harbor resentment; among Old Christians, no significant disaffection surfaces; the residue is suppressed rather than resolved.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Castilian consolidates as the administrative vernacular; Latin remains the language of diplomacy, theology, and learned exchange; Catalan, Portuguese, and Basque persist regionally; Arabic fades after Granada's fall; literacy remains confined to clergy, administrators, and urban elites.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Royal couriers and post relays speed official dispatches; merchant correspondence travels with trade caravans and ships; oral proclamation reaches the illiterate majority; information velocity improves with administrative centralization but remains slow by later standards.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Chancery registers, notarial archives, and monastic scriptoria preserve manuscript records; the Inquisition maintains its own archive of denunciations and trials; libraries hold illuminated codices; print arrives but does not yet dominate record-keeping; manuscript remains the authoritative medium for administration and law.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: The Inquisition censors heretical and Jewish texts; royal propaganda frames the monarchs as defenders of the faith; converso voices are silenced or driven underground; the permitted band narrows around Catholic orthodoxy; print permits wider circulation but also wider surveillance.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Sermons, public proclamations, and festival pageants amplify royal and ecclesiastical messages; printed pamphlets begin to reach urban audiences; attention concentrates on religious conformity and dynastic glory; oral networks remain the primary amplification channel for most of the population.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Inquisitorial procedure generates mountains of denunciation, much of it unreliable; rumor and slander circulate freely; the archive accumulates faster than it can be verified; noise increases with surveillance intensity; the residue of corrupted and contradictory record accrues.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Catholic doctrine underwrites the moral order; purity of blood becomes a quasi-religious criterion; the expulsion of Jews and repression of conversos are framed as acts of purification; a harsh, exclusionary justice prevails.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: The Leyes de Toro (1505, prepared in this window) consolidate Castilian civil law; the Inquisition operates under its own procedural rules; fueros persist in Aragon; royal ordinances regulate trade and guilds; codification advances under centralized monarchy.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Royal courts (audiencias) adjudicate civil and criminal matters; the Inquisition tries heresy and apostasy, imposing confiscation, imprisonment, and burning; sanction is severe and public; the auto-da-fe ritualizes punishment.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Old Christians enjoy legal protections; conversos face limpieza de sangre statutes barring them from office and honor; Jews lose all rights upon expulsion; moriscos retain minimal protections under surrender terms; compliance burden falls heaviest on suspect minorities.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Inquisitorial denunciation often lacks due process; confiscated property is rarely restored; appeals are slow and expensive; the powerful enjoy informal impunity; the gap between justice owed and delivered widens for the marginalized; the residue accrues.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Scholasticism persists at Salamanca and Alcala (founded 1499); humanist currents arrive from Italy; Nebrija's Gramatica Castellana (1492) codifies vernacular grammar; geographical knowledge expands with Atlantic exploration; no paradigm shift occurs, but horizons widen.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Navigation improves with the compass, astrolabe, and portolan charts; printing presses multiply (Valencia 1472, Barcelona, Seville, Salamanca by 1480); artillery and siege craft advance during the Granada campaign; shipbuilding techniques enable Atlantic caravels.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Cathedral schools and university faculties train clergy and lawyers; Cisneros promotes biblical scholarship; literacy remains low among commoners; the knowledge stock grows with printed books but distribution remains narrow.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Arabic scientific texts become inaccessible as Arabic literacy fades; Jewish learning disperses with the diaspora; manuscript copying errors persist; superseded astronomical tables remain in use alongside newer Alfonsine tables; the residue of lost and obsolete knowledge accumulates.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: The Reconquista narrative reaches its triumphant conclusion with Granada; the Catholic Monarchs are celebrated as restorers of Visigothic unity; purity of blood becomes a founding myth; Jewish and Islamic contributions are erased from official memory.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Divine favor, crusading mission, and dynastic destiny legitimate the monarchs' rule; the expulsion of Jews is framed as spiritual hygiene; the Atlantic enterprise is cast as providential expansion of Christendom; ideology enforces conformity.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Late Gothic (Isabelline) architecture adorns royal foundations (San Juan de los Reyes, Toledo); Flemish altarpieces and tapestries fill cathedrals; illuminated manuscripts persist alongside early printed books; mudejar craftsmen continue decorative work; the visual surface blends Gothic exuberance with residual Islamic ornament.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: The convivencia ideal collapses with expulsion; multicultural coexistence becomes untenable as a public narrative; courtly love poetry persists but loses contact with troubadour roots; the forms of tolerance are exhausted; the residue of hollowed pluralism lingers beneath triumphalist uniformity.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Catholic cosmology frames the universe as a divine order threatened by heresy and unbelief; the Atlantic discovery raises eschatological speculation about the approaching conversion of all peoples; fatalism and piety coexist; doubt is dangerous.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mass, confession, and pilgrimage structure religious life; Marian devotion intensifies; flagellant processions mark Holy Week; conversos practice crypto-Judaism at mortal risk; moriscos maintain hidden Islamic observance; public ritual enforces orthodoxy.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: The Spanish Inquisition (established 1478) consolidates doctrinal authority under royal patronage; bishoprics and monastic orders accumulate wealth; Cisneros reforms the Franciscans and founds Alcala; the Church is powerful, centralized, and orthodox.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Catholicism displaces Judaism and Islam by force; converso and morisco communities practice outward conformity; no syncretism is tolerated; the reach of orthodoxy extends to every corner of the realm; spiritual pluralism ends.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No significant disenchantment surfaces; the sacred is enforced rather than eroded; doubt and skepticism are driven underground; the residue of repressed heterodoxy accumulates in silence, awaiting later eruption; the spiritual order appears intact but its foundations rest on coercion.
