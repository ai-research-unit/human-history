    1450 CE – 1500 CE — Catholic Monarchs and Manuscript Twilight

    Summary: Long-term trend capturing the final half-century of the manuscript stage in Iberia as Ferdinand and Isabella forge dynastic union, complete the Reconquista with Granada's fall in 1492, expel Jews and Muslims, establish the Inquisition to enforce religious uniformity, and witness print technology arrive in the 1470s–80s to displace manuscript as the medium of public discourse. Binding constraint is the crown's drive for confessional and administrative uniformity, achieved through documentary apparatus and coercive conversion; the system expands territorially (Columbus, Canaries) while expelling minority populations that had sustained commercial and intellectual networks. Margin against internal dissent tightens as Inquisition apparatus thickens; direction is toward centralized monarchical power, religious homogeneity, and technological transition from hand-copied codex to movable type.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Peninsula bounded by Pyrenees, Atlantic, and Mediterranean; Meseta plateau dominates interior; Granada's mountain fastness surrenders 1492; Canaries and Atlantic islands integrated; road networks remain medieval; maritime access opens Atlantic horizons.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Mediterranean and semi-arid interior climates persist; moderate interannual variability; no major secular drift; agricultural rhythms stable after mid-century recovery from Black Death disruptions.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Periodic droughts affect Castilian interior; coastal storms manageable; earthquake risk low; no catastrophic shocks in the window; system holds adequate margin against environmental hazard.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Merino wool dominates exports; timber from Cantabrian north; iron from Basque country; silver and gold from remaining mines modest; Atlantic fisheries; stock adequate for domestic needs and growing trade.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban waste from growing cities (Seville, Toledo, Valencia) accumulates in streets and rivers; tanning and textile effluent localized; absorptive capacity not yet strained at aggregate scale.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic malaria in southern wetlands; plague recurrences less severe than 1348 peak; urban mortality elevated but population recovering; direction is toward demographic growth.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Cereal, olive, and vine staples support adequate caloric intake for most; meat consumption stratified by class; nutritional status improving as agriculture recovers from mid-fourteenth-century collapse.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Church-regulated marriage universal; age at first marriage stable; fertility unconstrained physiologically; pairing norms unchanged.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population recovering toward pre-plague levels; Castile perhaps 4–5 million by 1500; urban growth in Seville, Toledo; replacement rates positive; expulsions (1492) remove 150,000–200,000 Jews, reducing population temporarily.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Widow and orphan rates from Granada campaigns and plague recurrences manageable; ecclesiastical and lay charity absorbs modest dependent load; no acute overhang crisis.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Cereal production meets basic needs; regional deficits buffered by internal trade; household pooling through extended family; subsistence floor stable and improving.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle dominate; water mills for grain; wind power coastal; wood and charcoal for fuel; per-capita work ceiling unchanged; no technological breakthrough in energy conversion.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Free peasantry predominates in Castile; serfdom remnants in Aragon; urban guilds regulate craft labor; Mudejar and Morisco labor in agriculture; wage labor expanding in towns; coercion moderate.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Merino wool for Flemish and Italian markets; olive oil, wine exports; silk from Granada; productive capacity rising as Reconquista lands incorporated; surplus flows to crown and nobility.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Granaries in major towns; treasury reserves modest but growing through conquest booty, taxation, and ecclesiastical contributions to crusade; buffer capacity improving.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Seville emerges as Atlantic hub; Burgos controls wool trade; Mediterranean trade through Barcelona and Valencia; Mesta shepherds move flocks across Castile; logistics adequate, improving with crown consolidation.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Maravedi and real coinage; Jewish and converso bankers provide credit; Genoese financiers enter; expulsion of Jews disrupts credit networks temporarily; capital formation proceeds.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Crown taxation (alcabala sales tax) intensifies; ecclesiastical tithes; conquest revenues from Granada; Santa Hermandad funds; extraction load rising but productivity keeps pace.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Nobility and church hold vast estates; converso merchants accumulate urban wealth; Inquisition confiscations redistribute converso property; inequality high; noble entail (mayorazgo) defends aristocratic holdings.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Urban slaughterhouses, tanneries, and textile workshops discharge into rivers and streets; waste dispersed by low population density; no critical routing crisis.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust radius contracting for conversos and religious minorities; Old Christian–converso suspicion deepens; Inquisition prosecutions fracture social confidence; mutual aid persists within confessional bounds.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Limpieza de sangre (blood purity) ideology hardens boundaries; Old Christian status privileged; converso identity stigmatized; Mudejar and Morisco populations subordinated; stratification by religion and lineage intensifying.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilineal inheritance; mayorazgo concentrates noble estates; dowry and marriage alliances shape elite kinship; converso families intermarry cautiously; kinship norms stable.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Urban cofradias (confraternities) and guilds active; rural councils (concejos) coordinate commons; associational fabric thick but increasingly monitored by crown and Inquisition.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Festivals, bullfights, religious processions mark communal calendar; converso participation scrutinized for Judaizing signs; sociability continues under surveillance.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Inquisition absorbs denunciations, channeling grievance into prosecution; communal violence (pogroms) suppressed after 1391–1412 wave; residual anti-converso tension persists but is formalized into judicial apparatus.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Isabella of Castile and Ferdinand of Aragon marry 1469; joint rule from 1479; Castile-Aragon personal union; crown authority centralizing; cortes subordinated; power concentrating rapidly in monarchs.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Dynastic marriage secures union; succession crisis looms as heirs die (Prince Juan 1497); transfer mechanisms hereditary but vulnerable to biological accident; transfer routinized in principle, fragile in practice.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Reconquista completion frames monarchs as defenders of faith; Granada conquest and expulsions justify religious uniformity; legitimacy narrative intensifying around crusading Catholicism.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Royal councils (Consejo Real) professionalize; corregidores extend crown authority into towns; Inquisition provides parallel bureaucracy; record-keeping intensive; administrative reach expanding.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Cortes consulted on taxation but weakening; noble factions suppressed; urban autonomy curtailed; popular voice channeling through petitions; participation narrowing as crown consolidates.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Granada war (1482–92) concludes Reconquista; Italian campaigns begin; marriage alliances with Habsburgs, Portugal, England; Columbus voyage 1492 opens Atlantic; Spain emerges as European power.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Standing forces minimal but Granada campaign mobilizes tens of thousands; artillery decisive in siege warfare; Santa Hermandad rural police; Inquisition as enforcement arm; force capacity expanding.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Banditry suppressed by Hermandad; noble violence curbed; converso unrest absorbed by Inquisition; Granada Moriscos quiescent initially; internal order improving under crown monopoly.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Royal messengers, church networks, merchant correspondence; news travels days to weeks across peninsula; print arrives (Valencia 1474, Barcelona 1478) accelerating transmission; velocity increasing at window's end.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Inquisition archives systematically record testimony; royal chancery produces massive documentation; university libraries; manuscript copying continues alongside early print; persistence intensifying through documentary apparatus.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Inquisition monitors religious discourse; Index of prohibited books begins to form; converso speech self-censored; permissible band narrowing around orthodox Catholicism.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Print pamphlets and religious texts reach literate minority; sermons amplify to illiterate majority; Inquisition autos-da-fe spectacles command mass attention; amplification increasingly controlled by church-crown nexus.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Inquisition testimony introduces denunciation noise; verification through torture and cross-examination; signal integrity compromised by coerced confession; informational noise rising within judicial channel.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Divine sanction for religious uniformity underpins justice; heresy as ultimate crime; blood purity as moral category; sense of justice increasingly confessionalized.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Ordenanzas Reales de Castilla (1484) codify Castilian law; Siete Partidas remain authoritative; Inquisitorial procedure formalized; expulsion edicts (1492) create new statutory framework; law intensively evolving.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Royal audiencias and chancillerias handle civil/criminal cases; Inquisition tribunals parallel royal courts for heresy; confiscation and execution for convicted heretics; enforcement capacity high and expanding.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Converso rights precarious; accusation alone triggers Inquisition process; property confiscation follows conviction; compliance burden heavy for suspect populations; predictability low for those outside Old Christian norm.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: False denunciations yield convictions; torture-extracted confessions unreliable; noble impunity curtailed but ecclesiastical immunity persists; redress deficit significant for Inquisition victims.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Scholastic theology dominant; humanist currents arrive from Italy (Nebrija returns from Bologna); Columbus synthesis of geography and eschatology; paradigm stable with humanist inflection at margins.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Printing technology transferred from Germany; artillery engineering advances; navigation techniques improving (compass, charts); cartography active; technical method diffusing.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Salamanca, Alcala universities train clergy and lawyers; literacy 5–10% among males; Nebrija's Castilian grammar (1492) codifies vernacular; knowledge stock expanding through print; skill distribution narrow but widening.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Arabic and Hebrew scholarly traditions disrupted by expulsions; manuscript copying skills beginning to atrophy as print dominates; some knowledge loss as minority intellectual communities disperse.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Reconquista completion as national triumph; Santiago Matamoros imagery; Catholic Monarchs as restorers of Visigothic unity; converso presence as stain on collective body; foundational identity coalescing around crusading Christianity.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Isabelline Gothic in architecture (San Juan de los Reyes); Flemish painting influence; illuminated manuscripts continue alongside early printed books; material culture transitional between medieval and Renaissance idioms.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Triumphalism over Granada conquest; millennial expectation around 1492; anxiety over converso infiltration; mood oscillates between confidence and suspicion; disposition is vigilant, expansionist, intolerant.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Manuscript illumination declining as print commodifies text; courtly romance genres persist but vernacular chronicle ascends; some form exhaustion in late medieval conventions; renewal through humanist and print modes beginning.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Catholic Christianity frames cosmos; divine providence directs history; 1492 as providential year (Granada, Columbus, expulsion); fatalism subordinated to active crusading piety; outlook intensely theocentric.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mass, confession, feast days structure religious life; converso practice scrutinized for Judaizing; Marian devotion strong; flagellant and penitential movements active; sacred practice intensely monitored.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Spanish church under crown patronage (patronato real); Inquisition enforces orthodoxy; mendicant orders (Franciscans, Dominicans) influential; doctrinal authority consolidated and enforced; institutional power peaking.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Judaism expelled 1492; Islam expelled from Granada 1502; crypto-Judaism survives covertly; Morisco Islam persists under conversion; displacement and forced uniformity dominant direction.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No significant secularizing drift; sacred framework saturates public life; existential void absent at aggregate level; spiritual need fully anchored in triumphant Catholicism; disenchantment negligible in this window.
