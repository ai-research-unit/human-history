    200 BCE – 19 BCE — Roman Conquest and Iberian Incorporation

    Summary: Long-term trend spanning Rome's transformation of the Iberian Peninsula from a contested periphery into consolidated provinces — beginning with the expulsion of Carthage during the Second Punic War, proceeding through two centuries of episodic indigenous resistance and brutal suppression, and closing with Augustus's Cantabrian campaigns that complete the pacification. The binding constraint is military logistics against dispersed highland populations; Roman capacity eventually overwhelms but extraction precedes integration, draining bullion and labor toward the Mediterranean core while indigenous polities, scripts, and languages progressively contract.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Mountainous interior (Meseta, Pyrenees, Cantabrian range) fragments the peninsula into defensible micro-regions; river valleys (Ebro, Guadalquivir, Tagus) and coastal plains offer transit corridors Roman armies exploit, but highland zones impose persistent friction, delaying pacification for nearly two centuries.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Mediterranean south with winter-rain cereal regime; Atlantic northwest wetter and cooler; interior Meseta semi-arid with harsh winters and summer drought; no attested secular shift across the window, though interannual variability stresses armies and harvests during prolonged campaigns.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seismic risk moderate; flooding episodic along major rivers; wildfire in summer-dry zones; exposure affects siege logistics and supply lines but no catastrophic event documented as binding on the conquest itself.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Exceptional metal wealth — silver at Cartagena, gold in northwest, copper and tin — draws Roman interest and finances the state; timber, pasture, and grain complement the extractive economy; endowment large relative to indigenous exploitation capacity, inviting intensified Roman extraction.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Mining effluent and smelting slag accumulate at extraction sites; localized stream contamination attested archaeologically near silver workings; landscape-scale sink capacity remains high relative to discharge, no systemic saturation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic malaria in coastal wetlands; gastrointestinal disease around camps; no major epidemic recorded during the conquest period; mortality concentrated in combat and siege attrition rather than disease.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Indigenous populations exhibit robust skeletal profiles; Roman soldiers provisioned through supply trains; nutritional stress localized during sieges (Numantia's famine 133 BCE) but not systemic across the window.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Indigenous kinship patterns persist; Roman military marriage restrictions limit settler families early; intermarriage increases toward window's close as veteran colonies settle.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Indigenous population in low millions; conquest-era attrition — massacres, enslavement, deportation — creates demographic voids in some regions (Celtiberian plateau, Lusitania); replacement lags but does not collapse renewal capacity overall.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: War-widows, orphans, and enslaved survivors accumulate in disrupted communities; dependency load absorbed unevenly, heavier in zones of prolonged resistance; no evidence of system-wide dependency crisis.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Indigenous agro-pastoral base — grain, sheep, cattle — supports dispersed settlement; Roman requisitions and scorched-earth tactics depress provisioning locally during campaigns; post-conquest villa settlement stabilizes the floor.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle dominant; water mills appear in mining contexts; Roman engineering intensifies metal extraction but energy conversion remains preindustrial throughout.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Mass enslavement central to conquest — tens of thousands taken at Numantia, in Lusitania, in the northwest; mine labor heavily coerced; corvee and tribute exact indigenous labor; wage labor marginal.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Mining output (silver, gold) constitutes principal surplus; agricultural surplus modest but sufficient for local garrisons; surplus flows outward to Rome rather than accumulating locally.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Granaries at military camps; indigenous hillfort storage disrupted by conquest; provincial reserves thin until Augustan integration builds infrastructure.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Via Augusta and other roads under construction; Mediterranean ports (Tarraco, Gades) link Iberia to Rome; indigenous exchange networks contract as Roman commercial structures overlay them.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Roman coinage penetrates; indigenous minting (Iberian script denarii) ceases by mid-1st century BCE; credit relations remain tied to military supply contracts and elite Roman investors.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Tribute, mining rents, and war indemnities flow to Rome; publicani tax-farmers and military governors extract; redistribution within Iberia minimal — extraction dominant.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Indigenous elites co-opted or dispossessed; Roman settlers and colonists acquire land; concentration in Roman hands accelerates toward window's close; asset defense via Roman legal instruments replaces indigenous custom.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Mine tailings dumped near workings; slag heaps accumulate; military camp refuse localized; routing largely dispersal into landscape with no systemic constraint yet binding.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Indigenous kin-based trust networks disrupted by conquest; Roman clientela relations overlay but do not yet replace local solidarities; trust radius contracts in war zones, stabilizes in pacified areas.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Indigenous tribal identities (Celtiberi, Lusitani, Turdetani) persist but weaken; Roman citizen-subject hierarchy imposed; Latinization creates new status pathways; boundaries permeable for collaborating elites.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Indigenous bilateral and patrilineal systems coexist; Roman inheritance law applies in colonies; veteran settlement introduces Roman family norms; hybridization underway by window's close.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Indigenous oppida councils dissolve or transform; Roman municipia and coloniae provide new associational frameworks; guilds (collegia) appear in towns; civic fabric thickening in Romanized zones.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Indigenous festivals persist in rural areas; Roman amphitheaters and baths under construction in colonies; sociability increasingly structured by Roman urban forms toward end of window.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Indigenous revolt (Sertorian War, Cantabrian resistance) absorbs grievance through violence; suppression forecloses armed channels; Roman legal petition opens but access limited; residual grievance high in recently conquered zones.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Roman Senate and provincial governors hold sovereign authority; indigenous chieftains co-opted as clients or eliminated; power centralizing under Roman command, fragmenting indigenous polities.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Roman magistracies rotate; indigenous succession disrupted by conquest; client-king arrangements unstable; Augustan settlement regularizes provincial administration by 19 BCE.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Roman conquest justified as civilizing mission and revenge for Carthaginian alliance; indigenous legitimacy collapses with military defeat; Augustan propaganda celebrates pacification.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Roman administration rudimentary early (publicani, military governors); corruption and extortion endemic (Verres-style abuses); Augustan reforms begin regularization; capacity building throughout window.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Indigenous assemblies suppressed; Roman citizenship restricted; participation limited to collaborating elites; mass mobilization only through revolt, which is crushed.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Iberia shifts from contested frontier to secure province; Carthage eliminated; internal Iberian polities no longer independent actors; Rome's sole external interlocutor.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Roman legions (up to four deployed) constitute dominant force; indigenous warriors outmatched in pitched battle but effective in guerrilla resistance; fortification and siege technology Roman monopoly.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Banditry endemic in unconquered highlands; armed resistance episodic; pacification incomplete until 19 BCE; disorder concentrated in Cantabrian-Asturian zone late in window.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Roman military couriers and road network accelerate transmission; indigenous oral and inscriptional channels persist but diminish; information flows to Rome faster than internally among indigenous groups.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Latin inscriptions and administrative records multiply; indigenous scripts (Iberian, Celtiberian) decline, largely extinct by 1st century BCE; Roman archives begin; persistence shifting to Latin.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Roman framing dominates written record; indigenous voices preserved only through Roman ethnography; censorship not formalized but indigenous discourse marginalized by script loss.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Roman senatorial and popular audiences receive news of victories and triumphs; indigenous reception fragmented; amplification favors Roman narrative.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Military dispatches reliable; ethnographic knowledge patchy; noise from distance and language barriers; verification limited but improving as administration consolidates.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Indigenous notions of honor, vengeance, and reciprocity persist; Roman ius introduces contractual and property norms; tensions between systems unresolved in transition zones.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Roman law applies in colonies; indigenous custom tolerated in peregrini communities; no unified code; lex provinciae frameworks emerging.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Roman governors hold judicial authority; indigenous dispute resolution persists informally; enforcement via military coercion; liability increasingly framed in Roman terms.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Roman citizens protected; peregrini subject to governor's discretion; compliance burden heavy (tribute, corvee); predictability low until Augustan regularization.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Governor abuses widespread; indigenous victims lack standing; impunity high for Roman officials; redress available only through senatorial prosecution, rarely pursued.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Greek and Roman geographical and ethnographic inquiry (Polybius, Posidonius, Strabo) frames Iberia; indigenous cosmologies undocumented; knowledge production Roman-centered.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Roman engineering (roads, aqueducts, mines) transfers to Iberia; indigenous metallurgy absorbed; technical method increasingly Roman.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Latin literacy spreads among elites in colonies; indigenous literacy (Iberian script) contracts; skill distribution narrow, concentrated in Roman urban centers.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Indigenous scripts and associated knowledge systems decaying; oral traditions persist but transmission weakening; Roman knowledge stock growing but not yet deep in Iberia.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Indigenous origin myths and heroic narratives (Viriathus, Numantine resistance) persist orally; Roman foundational narrative (Aeneas, Romulus) introduced via elite education; hybrid identities forming.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Indigenous sculpture (warrior statues), fibulae, pottery styles persist but Romanization visible in architecture, coinage iconography, and urban planning; material culture transitioning.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Defeated populations exhibit resignation in pacified zones, defiance in highlands; Roman settlers confident; mood stabilizing toward end of window as resistance ends.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Indigenous ritual forms and symbols losing coherence as social context collapses; Roman forms not yet fully internalized; liminal cultural moment with old meanings decaying, new ones not yet anchored.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Indigenous polytheisms (Celtic and Iberian deities) persist; Roman pantheon introduced via colonies; interpretatio Romana syncretizes some deities; existential outlook shifting under conquest trauma.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Indigenous sanctuaries (sacred groves, hilltop shrines) continue; Roman temples constructed in colonies; votive practices blend traditions; ritual infrastructure transitioning.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Indigenous priesthoods undocumented but likely declining; Roman collegial priesthoods established in colonies; no unified doctrinal authority; institutional landscape fragmented.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Roman cults spreading via military and settlers; indigenous cults contracting but not eliminated; syncretism (e.g., Mars-indigenous war god) emerging; displacement partial.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Conquest disrupts indigenous sacred geography; meaning-loss among defeated populations; Roman ritual order not yet fully compensatory; spiritual residue accumulates in transition, partially discharged through syncretic adaptation.
