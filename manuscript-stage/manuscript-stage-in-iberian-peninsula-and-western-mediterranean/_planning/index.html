    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean (200 BCE – 1500 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 200 BCE – 1500 CE

    Region: The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean — the landmass comprising modern Spain, Portugal, Andorra, and Gibraltar; bounded by the Pyrenees, Atlantic, and Mediterranean; including the Balearic Islands and interactions with North Africa and the western Mediterranean basin.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Iberian and Celtiberian inscriptions, Roman epigraphic and literary sources, Visigothic legal codes and ecclesiastical documents, Arabic manuscripts and inscriptions from al-Andalus, Latin chronicles and charters from Christian kingdoms, Hebrew and Arabic translations, and late medieval vernacular texts. Latin literacy arrives with Roman conquest; Arabic manuscript culture flourishes under Muslim rule; Romance vernaculars develop written form by 10th–12th centuries. Window begins with Roman provincial integration; ends with print introduction (1470s–1500) and completion of Reconquista (1492).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Roman Conquest (200–19 BCE)

    Second Punic War: 218–201 BCE; Rome enters Iberia against Carthage; Scipio Africanus; Carthaginian expulsion.

    Provincial Organization: Hispania Citerior and Ulterior; later Tarraconensis, Baetica, Lusitania; Roman administration.

    Indigenous Resistance: Celtiberian wars; Numantia siege 133 BCE; Lusitanian resistance (Viriathus); Sertorian War.

    Romanization: Latin spreads; municipal organization; road network; indigenous scripts (Iberian, Celtiberian) fade.

    Mining Economy: Gold, silver, copper; slave labor; extractive economy; wealth flows to Rome.

    Cantabrian Wars: 29–19 BCE; final conquest under Augustus; peninsula fully incorporated.

    2. Roman Hispania (19 BCE – 409 CE)

    Pax Romana: Provincial peace; urban growth; Mérida, Tarragona, Córdoba as major centers.

    Latin Literacy: Inscriptions, literature; Seneca, Lucan, Martial, Quintilian from Hispania; provincial education.

    Christianity Arrives: 1st–3rd centuries; martyrdoms; Council of Elvira (~305 CE); episcopal organization.

    Late Imperial Crisis: 3rd century disruptions; Diocletian's reforms; barbarian pressures; economic strains.

    Villa Economy: Rural estates; agricultural production; aristocratic landholding; continuity into late antiquity.

    Manuscript Culture: Codex replaces scroll; parchment use; Christian and classical texts copied.

    3. Germanic Kingdoms (409–589 CE)

    Barbarian Invasions: 409 CE; Suevi, Vandals, Alans cross Pyrenees; territorial settlement.

    Visigothic Kingdom: Visigoths from Gaul; Toulouse kingdom; after 507, Toledo becomes center.

    Suevic Kingdom: Northwest (Galicia); converted to Catholicism; absorbed by Visigoths 585.

    Arian-Catholic Tension: Visigothic elite Arian; Hispanic-Roman population Catholic; religious division.

    Continuity and Change: Roman administrative structures partially survive; law codes; Latin persists.

    Episcopal Authority: Bishops as community leaders; ecclesiastical councils; manuscript copying in churches.

    4. Catholic Visigothic Kingdom (589–711 CE)

    Third Council of Toledo: 589; Reccared converts to Catholicism; religious unification.

    Liber Iudiciorum: Law code (654); comprehensive legislation; Latin legal tradition continues.

    Isidore of Seville: ~560–636; Etymologiae encyclopedia; scholarly compilation; manuscript authority.

    Toledo Councils: Ecclesiastical-royal governance; canon law; church-state integration.

    Jewish Community: Significant population; increasing restrictions; forced conversions under later kings.

    Late Visigothic Crisis: Succession disputes; noble factions; weakened kingdom on eve of conquest.

    5. Islamic Conquest and Umayyad Emirate (711–929 CE)

    Tariq ibn Ziyad: 711; crosses strait; Battle of Guadalete; Roderic killed; rapid conquest.

    Umayyad Emirate: Abd al-Rahman I (756); independent emirate; Córdoba capital; Damascus refugee.

    Al-Andalus: Muslim Iberia; Arabic administration; jizya taxation; Christian and Jewish dhimmis.

    Christian Resistance: Asturias; Pelayo (~722 Covadonga); small northern kingdoms survive.

    Arabization: Arabic becomes prestige language; bilingualism; Mozarabic Christians adopt Arabic culture.

    Manuscript Traditions: Arabic scholarly culture develops; Mozarabic Latin continues in north; Hebrew texts.

    6. Caliphate of Córdoba (929–1031 CE)

    Abd al-Rahman III: 929; declares caliphate; political peak; Mediterranean power.

    Córdoba Splendor: Largest European city; libraries; Great Mosque expansion; Madinat al-Zahra palace.

    Scholarly Culture: Arabic manuscripts; philosophy; medicine; astronomy; translation from Greek.

    Multicultural Society: Muslim majority; Christian Mozarabs; Jews (Hasday ibn Shaprut); cultural interaction.

    Al-Mansur: Late 10th century; military campaigns; Santiago sacked 997; political instability follows.

    Caliphal Collapse: 1009–1031; civil wars (fitna); caliphate dissolves into taifa kingdoms.

    7. Taifa Kingdoms and Almoravid Intervention (1031–1147 CE)

    Taifa Fragmentation: ~30 successor kingdoms; Seville, Granada, Zaragoza, Toledo; competing courts.

    Cultural Continuity: Taifa courts patronize arts; poetry; architecture; scholarly competition.

    Toledo Falls: 1085; Alfonso VI of Castile; major Christian advance; symbolic significance.

    Almoravid Intervention: 1086; Berber dynasty from Sahara; Sagrajas victory; reunifies al-Andalus.

    Cid Campeador: Rodrigo Díaz; frontier warfare; Valencia captured 1094; legendary figure.

    Translation Movement Begins: Toledo under Christian rule; Arabic-Latin translation; intellectual transfer.

    8. Almohad Empire and Reconquista Acceleration (1147–1250 CE)

    Almohad Conquest: 1147–1172; Berber reformers; replace Almoravids; stricter Islam.

    Las Navas de Tolosa: 1212; decisive Christian coalition victory; Almohad power broken.

    Major Conquests: Córdoba 1236; Valencia 1238; Seville 1248; Faro 1249; Christian expansion.

    Granada Remains: Nasrid kingdom survives; tributary; last Muslim state until 1492.

    Military Orders: Calatrava, Santiago, Alcántara; frontier defense and settlement.

    Mudéjar Population: Muslims under Christian rule; agricultural labor; distinct legal status.

    9. Christian Kingdoms Consolidation (1250–1350 CE)

    Crown of Castile: Largest kingdom; Atlantic and Mediterranean coasts; wool trade develops.

    Crown of Aragon: Catalonia, Aragon, Valencia; Mediterranean expansion; Sicily, Sardinia.

    Portugal: Independent kingdom; Atlantic orientation; Reconquista complete by 1250s.

    Toledo Translation School: Arabic-Latin; philosophical, scientific texts; Gerard of Cremona; European transmission.

    Alfonso X the Wise: 1252–84; legal codification (Siete Partidas); Castilian prose; astronomy tables.

    Vernacular Literature: Castilian, Catalan, Portuguese emerge as written languages; chronicles, poetry.

    10. Crisis, Recovery, and Converso Society (1350–1450 CE)

    Black Death: 1348–50; severe mortality; economic disruption; labor shortages.

    Trastámara Dynasty: 1369; civil war; new Castilian royal house; centralizing tendencies.

    1391 Pogroms: Anti-Jewish violence; forced conversions; converso population emerges.

    Converso Integration: Converted Jews; social advancement; suspicions; "Old Christian" anxieties.

    Castilian Expansion: Canary Islands; Atlantic ventures; preparation for overseas enterprise.

    Manuscript Production: Continues; vernacular chronicles; religious texts; university copying.

    11. Catholic Monarchs and Manuscript Twilight (1450–1500 CE)

    Isabella and Ferdinand: Marriage 1469; joint rule from 1479; Castile-Aragon union.

    Granada War: 1482–92; final conquest; Reconquista complete; Muslim and Jewish expulsions.

    Spanish Inquisition: 1478; targets conversos; religious uniformity; documentary apparatus.

    1492: Granada, Columbus, Jewish expulsion; transformative year.

    Print Arrives: 1470s in Valencia, Barcelona; 1480s widespread; Nebrija's grammar 1492.

    Manuscript Twilight: Print displaces manuscript for public discourse; religious and administrative shift.
