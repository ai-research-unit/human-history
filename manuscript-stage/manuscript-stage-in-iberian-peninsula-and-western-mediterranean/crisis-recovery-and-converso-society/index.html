    1350 CE – 1450 CE — Crisis, Recovery, and Converso Society

    Summary: A long-term trend across Christian Iberia encompassing the Black Death shock, dynastic upheaval, anti-Jewish violence, and the emergence of converso society; the binding constraint shifts from territorial expansion to demographic recovery, social reordering, and management of religious-ethnic tensions within the three Christian kingdoms; Castile experiences civil war and Trastámara dynastic replacement (1369), followed by centralizing efforts; severe mortality from plague (1348–50 and recurrences) disrupts labor supply and economic production; pogroms of 1391 trigger mass conversions creating the converso population whose integration and suspected crypto-Judaism generate lasting social friction; margin against shock is depleted by plague and civil conflict; direction of change moves toward demographic recovery, intensified religious uniformity pressure, Atlantic commercial expansion, and preparation for the overseas ventures that follow.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Iberian landmass remains divided between the arid Meseta interior, the Mediterranean littoral of Aragon, and Portugal's Atlantic coast; internal transit friction persists across the central plateau; maritime zones gain relative advantage as Mediterranean and Atlantic trade routes become primary arteries; Granada remains isolated in the southeast as the last Muslim polity.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The Little Ice Age intensifies after 1300 with cooler, wetter conditions and increased climate volatility; growing seasons shorten in upland zones; harvest variability increases across the Meseta; the system is provisioned for warmer medieval norms and the cooling drift narrows margins against agricultural failure.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Drought and harvest failure remain primary hazards, now compounded by plague recurrence; famine years correlate with cold spells; coastal storms affect Mediterranean and Atlantic shipping; the window sees repeated shocks (plague waves, famines) that test and often exceed buffering capacity.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Merino wool production continues as Castile's primary export commodity despite labor shortages; iron and timber resources remain adequate; fishing grounds off Atlantic Portugal support growing maritime activity; the resource base is not depleted but extraction capacity is constrained by demographic losses.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Population decline from plague reduces pressure on agricultural land and urban waste absorption; abandoned villages and fallow fields characterize the post-plague landscape; sink capacity exceeds diminished loads; no environmental stress from waste accumulation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: The Black Death strikes Iberia in 1348–50 with catastrophic mortality (estimated 30–50% in affected areas); recurrent plague waves (1362, 1371, 1383, 1396, and continuing) prevent demographic recovery; mortality baseline is severely elevated throughout the window; the population sits far below pre-plague levels with no clear return to former numbers.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Paradoxically, survivors experience improved per-capita nutrition as land-to-labor ratios shift favorably; meat consumption increases; physiological vitality among survivors is adequate or improving even as aggregate population remains depressed; this creates a characteristic post-plague pattern of individual prosperity amid collective demographic deficit.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Marriage rates increase and ages at first marriage decline as widowed individuals remarry and young people gain earlier access to land and resources; reproductive rates rise in response to mortality but lag behind deaths; household formation patterns accelerate but cannot immediately replace losses.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population remains 30–50% below 1340 levels throughout most of the window; slow recovery begins after mid-century but recurrent plague waves interrupt growth; density decreases sharply with village abandonment and urban contraction; age structure is disrupted with gaps in cohorts struck by plague; recovery is incomplete by window's close.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Plague creates waves of orphans and widows; the productive cohort is depleted but so is the elderly population struck by disease; dependency ratios fluctuate with each plague wave; ecclesiastical and confraternal charity absorbs orphans; the overhang is manageable given overall population decline.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Subsistence improves for survivors as depopulation releases land pressure; abandoned villages provide opportunity for consolidation; grain prices fall relative to wages; the surviving population sits more comfortably above the carrying-capacity edge despite aggregate economic disruption.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Labor scarcity increases the relative value of animal power and water mills; pastoral economy expands at the expense of labor-intensive cultivation; per-capita energy access improves for survivors; the overall energy-capture regime remains agrarian with modest intensification through substitution of animal for human labor.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Labor scarcity empowers surviving workers; wages rise; seigneurial attempts to reimpose labor obligations meet resistance; Mudéjar agricultural labor becomes more valuable; coercion levels fluctuate as lords attempt to bind scarce labor while workers exploit their improved bargaining position.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Aggregate production falls with population; wool output declines but recovers as pastoral economy requires less labor than cultivation; manufacturing output contracts in plague-struck urban centers; surplus per capita may rise but total surplus available for extraction declines.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Treasury reserves depleted by Castilian civil war (1366–69) and recurring plague disruption; granary stocks adequate in normal years but the frequency of crisis years exhausts buffers; commercial reserves contracted by trade disruption; buffering capacity is strained throughout the window.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Trade disruption accompanies plague waves; Mediterranean commerce recovers through Aragonese networks; Atlantic trade through Portugal and Castilian ports (Seville, Santander) develops; the Canary Islands come under Castilian influence (1402); logistics infrastructure damaged by depopulation recovers slowly; market integration remains limited.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Currency debasement accompanies fiscal crisis; Jewish and converso merchants and financiers provide credit networks; Italian banking presence continues; debt levels rise as crowns borrow against future revenue; capital formation is disrupted but not destroyed; converso commercial networks emerge as a distinctive feature.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Royal taxation attempts to maintain pre-plague revenue from contracted economic base; fiscal pressure increases on reduced population; the Church maintains tithe collection; seigneurial extraction faces peasant resistance; the load relative to productive capacity increases even as absolute extraction may decline.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Land redistribution through plague mortality allows some social mobility; noble estates consolidate abandoned holdings; converso families accumulate commercial wealth; Jewish assets are vulnerable to confiscation and violence; distribution shifts but stratification persists; asset defense for religious minorities becomes precarious after 1391.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Reduced economic activity generates less waste; abandoned properties and uncultivated land absorb what is produced; urban contraction reduces concentrated effluent; waste routing poses no systemic constraint.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: The 1391 pogroms shatter trust between Christian and Jewish communities; converso status creates new suspicion as "Old Christians" question the sincerity of conversion; intra-Christian trust persists through confraternities and guilds; overall the radius of trust contracts sharply, with religious boundaries hardening into barriers.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Converso identity emerges as a new social category: legally Christian but genealogically Jewish, subject to suspicion and discrimination; limpieza de sangre (blood purity) concepts develop; "Old Christian" identity hardens in response; noble, clerical, and commoner estates persist; boundaries between religious groups become impassable in new ways even as conversion erases formal distinction.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Plague disrupts kin networks through mass mortality; inheritance flows to more distant relatives or collapses entirely; noble mayorazgo (entail) strengthens to preserve weakened lineages; converso families maintain marriage networks within their community; kinship reconstruction is a multi-generational process.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Guilds and confraternities reorganize after plague losses; converso exclusion from some guilds begins; municipal governance continues but with depleted personnel; associational fabric is damaged but regenerating with new exclusionary features based on lineage.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Religious festivals continue with altered character after plague; bullfighting expands as noble entertainment; pilgrimage to Santiago persists but at reduced scale; sociability is shadowed by plague fear and religious tension; the atmosphere is darker than the preceding period.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: The 1391 pogroms represent catastrophic failure of grievance channels as economic frustration and religious hostility explode into mass violence; subsequent decades see attempts to restore order but anti-converso sentiment lacks adequate discharge mechanisms; significant grievance residue accumulates as social instability.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Castile undergoes Trastámara dynastic replacement (1369) after civil war; royal authority attempts centralization against noble resistance; Aragon faces succession crisis (Compromise of Caspe, 1412) bringing Castilian Trastámara line to Aragonese throne; Portugal consolidates under Avis dynasty (1385); power is contested but trending toward royal consolidation across all three kingdoms.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Violent succession characterizes the period: Pedro I killed (1369), ending legitimate Burgundian line in Castile; Aragonese interregnum resolved by compromise (1412); Portuguese succession war ends at Aljubarrota (1385); transfer mechanisms function but through crisis rather than routine procedure.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Trastámara legitimacy is contested given usurpation origins; compensatory grants to nobility and church purchase support; religious rhetoric intensifies as legitimation strategy; anti-Jewish measures serve political purposes; the narrative is functional but requiring continuous reinforcement.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Royal administration expands despite crisis; Castilian audiencias (appellate courts) develop; Portuguese bureaucracy consolidates; Aragonese pactism constrains royal administration but maintains function; corruption persists but institutional integrity is adequate; capacity is recovering and expanding by window's end.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Cortes continue to meet in all kingdoms; noble factions and urban leagues contest royal policy; converso populations lack formal representation despite growing numbers; peasant revolts (Irmandiño risings in Galicia foreshadow post-window conflicts); mobilization channels exist but strained by social tensions.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Castile and Portugal compete over Atlantic spheres; Castile intervenes in French wars; Aragon expands Mediterranean influence (Naples); Granada continues as tributary; England and France court Iberian alliances; the external position is strengthening despite internal disruption.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Military capacity is disrupted by plague and civil war; noble private forces remain significant; military orders continue frontier functions; naval capacity develops in Portugal and Aragon; gunpowder weapons begin appearing; command remains distributed between crown and nobility with crown capacity slowly increasing.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: The 1391 pogroms represent major internal disorder; banditry increases in depopulated zones; noble violence persists despite royal efforts; anti-converso riots recur sporadically; internal order is strained throughout, with enforcement capacity struggling to match the threat environment.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Manuscript networks continue; plague disrupts copyist communities; royal chanceries maintain documentary production; mercantile correspondence expands with Catalan and Portuguese Atlantic trade; transmission velocity remains limited by animal-pace messengers; information reaches elites within weeks across the peninsula.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Monastic and cathedral scriptoria suffer plague mortality but recover; royal archives expand administrative documentation; vernacular chronicles proliferate; Inquisition record-keeping (anticipating formal establishment) develops; parchment and early paper records persist in institutional collections; some institutional memory is lost to plague mortality.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Anti-Jewish and anti-converso rhetoric circulates with increasing intensity; church authorities frame acceptable discourse around religious orthodoxy; suspicion of crypto-Judaism becomes expressible concern; the permitted band narrows around religious conformity while expanding in vernacular literature.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Vernacular readership expands; sermons amplify religious messages including anti-Jewish content; chronicle literature reaches noble and urban audiences; attention increasingly focuses on religious identity questions; Ferrand Martínez's preaching amplifies anti-Jewish sentiment preceding 1391.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Genealogical claims become subject to dispute as converso families attempt to obscure Jewish ancestry; false documentation emerges; rumor and suspicion proliferate around crypto-Judaism; signal integrity degrades in matters of religious identity; verification mechanisms are inadequate for the noise load generated by conversion and suspicion.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Christian moral teaching intensifies emphasis on religious conformity; the sense of justice increasingly incorporates bloodline considerations; treatment of conversos creates tension between formal equality (as Christians) and social suspicion; moral foundations are stable but application is contested.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Siete Partidas continues as Castilian legal foundation; anti-converso regulations begin appearing in guild and municipal statutes; Jewish legal autonomy is progressively restricted; formal law evolves toward religious uniformity though implementation varies; regulatory burden on religious minorities increases.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Royal courts continue function; ecclesiastical courts handle religious matters but formal Inquisition does not yet exist (established 1478); enforcement of anti-Jewish violence is inconsistent, with perpetrators often unpunished; liability increasingly attaches to religious status; adjudication capacity is strained by social tensions.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Conversos hold formal Christian rights but face practical discrimination; remaining Jews face increasing restrictions; Old Christian privileges develop informally; fuero protections persist for commoners; compliance burden differentiates sharply by religious and genealogical status.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: The 1391 pogrom perpetrators largely escape punishment; subsequent anti-converso violence often goes unredressed; royal protection for religious minorities fails repeatedly; impunity for violence against Jews and conversos is substantial; the gap between justice owed and delivered is severe for these populations.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Scholastic synthesis continues as the dominant paradigm; university production at Salamanca, Valladolid maintains theological and legal training; translation activity declines as Arabic-language scholarship attrites in Christian territories; the discovery frontier has shifted northward to Paris and Oxford; Iberian intellectual life is derivative rather than innovative.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Navigational techniques develop in Portuguese Atlantic exploration; cartographic knowledge expands; agricultural methods adapt to post-plague labor scarcity; medical knowledge is tested and found wanting against plague; technical method continues without major innovation but with practical adaptation.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Universities recover from plague disruption; clerical literacy persists; converso families often emphasize education as social strategy; vernacular literacy slowly expands; knowledge stock is maintained but growth slows; skill distribution narrows temporarily with plague mortality then slowly recovers.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Arabic-language scholarly tradition effectively ends in Christian Iberia; Hebrew learning persists in diminished Jewish communities and among some conversos; Latin remains dominant; skill transmission is disrupted by plague mortality; some vernacular and technical knowledge is lost; decay outpaces accumulation in several domains during crisis years.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Reconquista narrative persists but shifts emphasis from territorial recovery (largely complete) to religious purification; converso ambiguity troubles clean Christian identity categories; "Old Christian" identity crystallizes in opposition to converts; foundational memory increasingly emphasizes genealogical purity; the bedrock is shifting from triumphalist expansion to anxious self-definition.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Gothic architecture continues in cathedral construction (Seville cathedral begun 1402); Mudéjar decorative traditions persist; vernacular literature flourishes (Libro de Buen Amor, romancero ballads); international Gothic painting emerges; material culture reflects crisis and adaptation rather than confident expansion.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Plague and pogrom darken the collective mood; death consciousness intensifies (danse macabre themes); religious anxiety mixes with economic frustration; scapegoating behavior targets vulnerable minorities; confidence is diminished from the preceding period though recovery sentiment emerges by window's end; the behavioral disposition is more cautious and suspicious.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Earlier convivencia cultural forms are actively destroyed rather than merely exhausted; synagogues convert to churches; Jewish cultural production ceases in converted communities; converso cultural position is ambiguous; some medieval forms hollow (chivalric literature becomes nostalgic) while vernacular innovation continues; meaning decay concentrates in disrupted religious coexistence.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Plague reinforces sense of divine judgment and human sinfulness; memento mori themes proliferate; purgatory theology emphasizes posthumous purification; fatalism increases relative to human agency; piety intensifies as protective response; doubt remains suppressed but existential anxiety is elevated.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mass, sacraments, and saint veneration intensify as plague response; flagellant movements appear briefly; confraternal devotion expands; pilgrimage continues; converso populations participate in Christian ritual with varying degrees of internalization; crypto-Jewish practice (if present) is concealed; devotional infrastructure is robust but anxious.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Church institutional power strengthens during crisis as populations seek spiritual protection; mendicant orders expand influence; Western Schism (1378–1417) creates papal authority confusion but Iberian kingdoms navigate it; episcopal authority is stable; doctrinal orthodoxy is increasingly enforced though formal Inquisition awaits the following period.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Mass conversion of Jews (1391 onward) expands Christianity's formal reach but creates new internal tension around sincerity; Mudéjar populations face increased conversion pressure; Jewish religious practice contracts to remaining communities and possible crypto-Jewish survival; the dominant cosmology expands through coerced rather than persuaded conversion, generating its own instability.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No secularizing drift; if anything the opposite occurs as crisis intensifies religiosity; the sacred canopy is reinforced by plague fear; converso populations may experience private disenchantment but cannot express it; spiritual need is addressed through intensified orthodox practice; disenchantment residue is minimal or concealed, with the characteristic feature being religious anxiety rather than doubt.
