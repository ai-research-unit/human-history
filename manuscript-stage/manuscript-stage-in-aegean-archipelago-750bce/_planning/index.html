    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Aegean Archipelago (750 BCE – 1830 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 750 BCE – 1830 CE

    Region: The Aegean Archipelago — the islands and coastlands of the Aegean Sea, including the Greek mainland, the Cyclades, Crete, the Dodecanese, and the Anatolian coast; the core of the Greek-speaking world from antiquity through the Byzantine and Ottoman periods.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes archaic inscriptions, classical literature and epigraphy, Hellenistic papyri, Roman provincial records, Byzantine manuscripts and administrative documents, and Ottoman registers. Greek alphabet adopted ~800–750 BCE from Phoenician; continuous manuscript tradition until Greek press established (~1820s). Window ends with Greek independence and emergence of a print-based public sphere.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Alphabetic Adoption (750–600 BCE)

    Phoenician Alphabet Adaptation: Greeks adopt consonantal Phoenician script ~800–750 BCE; add vowels; earliest inscriptions on pottery, stone.

    Dipylon Inscription: ~740 BCE; one of earliest Greek alphabetic texts; poetic meter on oinochoe.

    Homer Transcribed: Iliad and Odyssey fixed in writing; oral traditions preserved in manuscript form.

    Laws on Stone: Early law codes inscribed (Dreros, Gortyn); public display; writing as political tool.

    Regional Script Variants: Different poleis develop local alphabets; eventual standardization.

    Expanding Literacy: Symposium culture; pottery inscriptions; dedicatory texts; writing spreads beyond elite.

    2. Archaic Florescence (600–480 BCE)

    Polis Consolidation: City-states as political units; constitutions; citizenship; public life.

    Colonization: Greek settlements across Mediterranean and Black Sea; written records accompany expansion.

    Lyric Poetry: Sappho, Alcaeus, Pindar; written composition; performance and text intertwined.

    Ionian Science: Thales, Anaximander, Heraclitus; prose writing; philosophical inquiry.

    Tyranny and Democracy: Political experimentation; laws and decrees; public inscriptions.

    Coinage and Trade: Monetary economy; commercial records; Mediterranean networks.

    3. Classical Athens (480–323 BCE)

    Persian Wars: Salamis, Plataea; collective Greek identity; Herodotus documents.

    Athenian Democracy: Mass participation; literacy for citizens; public records; ostraka.

    Tragedy and Comedy: Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides, Aristophanes; performed and transcribed texts.

    Historical Writing: Thucydides; documentary method; archives consulted.

    Philosophy: Socrates, Plato, Aristotle; Academy, Lyceum; written dialogues and treatises.

    Rhetoric and Oratory: Demosthenes, Isocrates; speeches preserved in writing; education in letters.

    4. Hellenistic Kingdoms (323–31 BCE)

    Alexander's Successors: Ptolemaic Egypt, Seleucid Asia, Antigonid Macedon; Greek administration.

    Library of Alexandria: Scholarly collection; textual editing; Pinakes catalog; Callimachus.

    Koine Greek: Common Greek spreads; administrative and literary lingua franca.

    Papyrus Trade: Egyptian papyrus dominant; enables manuscript production; private letters, documents.

    Scholarship: Textual criticism; commentaries; scientific treatises (Euclid, Archimedes).

    Hellenization: Greek culture and writing spread across Near East; bilingual societies.

    5. Roman Achaea (31 BCE – 330 CE)

    Provincial Status: Greece under Roman rule; Achaea province; continued Greek cultural life.

    Second Sophistic: Greek rhetorical revival; Plutarch, Lucian, Pausanias; literary tourism.

    Christian Emergence: Paul's letters; Greek New Testament; early church in Greek cities.

    Philosophical Schools: Stoicism, Epicureanism, Neoplatonism; written transmission.

    Manuscript Production: Parchment supplements papyrus; codex form emerges.

    Bilingual Elite: Greek and Latin; Roman administration; Greek cultural prestige.

    6. Late Antiquity and Byzantine Emergence (330–640 CE)

    Constantinople Founded: 330 CE; New Rome; Greek-speaking Eastern Empire.

    Christian Empire: Council of Nicaea; church fathers (Basil, Gregory, Chrysostom); theological writing.

    Manuscript Preservation: Classical texts copied; Christian and pagan libraries; endangered heritage.

    Imperial Administration: Greek bureaucracy; extensive documentation; chancery practice.

    Justinian Codification: Legal compilation in Latin; Greek paraphrases; administrative law.

    Architectural and Artistic Records: Hagia Sophia; mosaic inscriptions; monumental text.

    7. Arab Threat and Iconoclasm (640–843 CE)

    Islamic Expansion: Loss of Levant, Egypt, North Africa; territorial contraction.

    Iconoclasm: 726–843 CE; image controversy; theological writing; council records.

    Administrative Adaptation: Theme system; militarized provinces; documentary needs.

    Manuscript Survival: Fewer manuscripts survive from this period; copying continues but reduced.

    Cultural Continuity: Greek remains imperial language; church, court, bureaucracy.

    Arab-Byzantine Exchange: Translation movements; shared intellectual currents.

    8. Macedonian Renaissance (843–1056 CE)

    Iconoclasm Ends: 843 CE; image veneration restored; cultural confidence.

    Encyclopedism: Constantine VII Porphyrogennetos; De Administrando Imperio; De Ceremoniis; imperial compilations.

    Photios: Bibliotheca; summaries of classical texts; scholarly revival.

    Manuscript Copying: Minuscule script replaces uncial; faster copying; more manuscripts survive.

    Hagiography: Saints' lives; Symeon Metaphrastes; widespread genre.

    Administrative Complexity: Extensive documentation; seals; correspondence.

    9. Komnenian-Angelid Period (1056–1204 CE)

    Komnenian Restoration: Military recovery; Anna Komnena's Alexiad; historical writing.

    Crusades Begin: Latin contacts; translations; cultural exchange; tension and cooperation.

    Constantinople's Wealth: Imperial capital; manuscript production center; libraries.

    Religious Schism: 1054 formalized; polemical writing; Orthodox identity.

    Provincial Documentation: Praktika; tax records; monastic archives.

    Intellectual Life: Michael Psellos; philosophy and rhetoric; classical learning.

    10. Latin Fragmentation and Palaiologan Revival (1204–1453 CE)

    Fourth Crusade: 1204; Constantinople sacked; Latin Empire; fragmented successor states.

    Nicaean Exile: Greek Empire in exile; manuscript tradition continues.

    Palaiologan Renaissance: After 1261 reconquest; Plethon, Bessarion; classical revival.

    Scholarly Emigration: Greek scholars to Italy; bring manuscripts; Renaissance stimulus.

    Ottoman Advance: Gradual conquest; Thessalonica (1430); Greek lands shrink.

    Manuscript Preservation: Texts carried west; Byzantine tradition transplanted.

    11. Ottoman Conquest and Early Tourkokratia (1453–1669 CE)

    Fall of Constantinople: 1453; Ottoman rule; Greek Patriarchate continues under millet system.

    Millet System: Greeks as religious community; Patriarch as ethnarch; church administration.

    Phanariot Emergence: Greek elite in Phanar district; Ottoman administration; dragomans.

    Cretan War: 1645–1669; Venice vs. Ottomans; Crete falls; refugee scholars.

    Manuscript Culture Persists: Church, monastic, and private libraries; copying continues.

    Greek Printing Abroad: Aldus Manutius (Venice); Greek texts printed; but local access limited.

    12. Ottoman Integration and Commercial Networks (1669–1774 CE)

    Post-Cretan Stability: Ottoman consolidation; Greeks integrated into imperial economy.

    Merchant Networks: Greek traders in Mediterranean, Black Sea; commercial correspondence.

    Phanariot Administration: Greeks as Danube principality governors; diplomatic roles.

    Education: Church schools; manuscript-based learning; limited print access.

    Religious Life: Monasteries (Athos, Patmos); manuscript copying; liturgical production.

    Nascent Revival: Vernacular writing; folk traditions; intellectual stirring.

    13. Enlightenment Currents (1774–1821 CE)

    Rigas Feraios: Revolutionary thought; printed pamphlets; martyrdom (1798).

    Adamantios Korais: Paris-based scholar; linguistic purification; edited texts; print advocacy.

    Philiki Etaireia: Secret society (1814); revolutionary network; correspondence.

    Diaspora Intellectuals: Vienna, Paris, Odessa; access to print; Enlightenment ideas.

    Greek Press Emerges: Printing in Greek diaspora; newspapers; revolutionary literature.

    Manuscript Twilight: Print increasingly available; manuscript production declines.

    14. Greek War of Independence (1821–1830 CE)

    Revolution Begins: 1821; Peloponnese, islands; guerrilla war against Ottomans.

    Navarino: 1827; allied naval victory; Ottoman defeat; great power intervention.

    Independence: 1830; Kingdom of Greece; new state institutions.

    Print-Based Public Sphere: Newspapers, constitutions, laws printed; national education.

    Manuscript Stage Ends: State administration, public discourse now run on printed record.
