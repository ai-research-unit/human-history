    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Ethiopian Highlands (350 CE – 1945 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 350 CE – 1945 CE

    Region: The Ethiopian Highlands — the mountainous plateau of northeastern Africa, including the northern highlands (Tigray, Eritrea), the central highlands (Amhara, Shewa), and extending to the southern regions; bounded by the Red Sea coast, the Rift Valley, and the lowland peripheries.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Aksumite inscriptions in Ge'ez, Greek, and South Arabian; Christian manuscripts from the 4th century onward; royal chronicles (particularly from Solomonic period); hagiographies; land charters; church archives. Ge'ez script (Ethiopic) develops from South Arabian consonantal script into syllabary by ~350 CE. Continuous manuscript tradition until print introduced (~1860s–1900s, remaining limited until post-WWII). Window begins with Aksumite adoption of Christianity and literary Ge'ez; ends with Italian occupation's end and post-war modernization.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Aksumite Christian Kingdom (350–640 CE)

    Ezana's Conversion: ~340 CE; Aksumite king adopts Christianity; Frumentius tradition; church establishment.

    Ge'ez Script Development: South Arabian consonantal script adapted; vowel notation added; syllabary system by 4th century.

    Bible Translation: Ge'ez translation of Scriptures; literary tradition established; liturgical use.

    Nine Saints: Late 5th/6th century; Syrian monks; monastic foundations; further translations.

    Red Sea Trade: Adulis port; Indian Ocean commerce; ivory, gold, incense; Mediterranean connections.

    Aksumite Apogee: 4th–6th centuries; monumental stelae; coinage; South Arabian campaigns; powerful kingdom.

    2. Islamic Expansion and Highland Isolation (640–1137 CE)

    Arab Conquest: Muslim control of Red Sea coast; Adulis declines; trade routes shift.

    Trade Disruption: Indian Ocean commerce redirected; Aksum's commercial basis erodes.

    Aksum's Decline: Capital shifts southward; political fragmentation; "dark centuries" with sparse records.

    Christian Survival: Church maintains continuity; manuscript copying continues; monasteries as refuges.

    Obscure Period: Limited archaeological and textual evidence; royal genealogies uncertain; oral tradition fills gaps.

    Contact with Egypt: Coptic church connection maintained; patriarch appointment from Alexandria.

    3. Zagwe Dynasty (1137–1270 CE)

    Zagwe Rulers: Non-Solomonic dynasty; Agew origin; legitimacy contested by later traditions.

    Lalibela: King Lalibela (late 12th/early 13th c.); rock-hewn churches at Roha (Lalibela); pilgrimage center.

    Rock-Hewn Architecture: Eleven churches carved from bedrock; architectural innovation; devotional labor.

    Monastic Expansion: Monastery foundations continue; manuscript production; hagiography.

    Church-State Relations: Royal patronage; ecclesiastical authority; Solomonic tradition preserved by church.

    Literary Continuity: Ge'ez remains liturgical/literary language; Bible, hagiography, hymns copied.

    4. Early Solomonic Restoration (1270–1529 CE)

    Yekuno Amlak: 1270; overthrows Zagwe; claims Solomonic descent; dynastic legitimation.

    Solomonic Ideology: Kebra Nagast (Glory of Kings) compiled/finalized ~14th century; Solomon-Sheba mythology.

    Royal Chronicles: Amharic enters royal records; chronicle tradition begins; historical documentation.

    Territorial Expansion: Amda Seyon (1314–44), Zara Yaqob (1434–68); campaigns against Muslim sultanates.

    Monastic Networks: Monasteries as literacy centers; Ewostatewos, Tekle Haymanot traditions; land grants.

    Land Charters: Rist tenure documents; ecclesiastical endowments; administrative records.

    5. Muslim Jihad and Restoration (1529–1636 CE)

    Ahmad ibn Ibrahim (Gragn): 1529–43; Adal jihad; devastating highland invasion; churches destroyed.

    Portuguese Intervention: 1541; Cristóvão da Gama; firearms; Ahmad killed 1543; Muslim defeat.

    Emperor Galawdewos: Restoration; rebuilding; continuing pressures; killed 1559.

    Jesuit Interlude: 1557–1632; Jesuit missionaries; Emperor Susenyos converts (1622); civil conflict.

    Expulsion: 1632–36; Fasilides expels Jesuits; Orthodox restoration; foreign contact limited.

    Oromo Migrations: 16th century onward; pastoral expansion into highlands; demographic shift; ongoing conflict.

    6. Gondar Period (1636–1769 CE)

    Fixed Capital: Fasilides founds Gondar 1636; first permanent capital; palace complex (Fasil Ghebbi).

    Palace Architecture: Stone castles; European and Indian influences; royal compound.

    Manuscript Production Peak: Royal scriptorium; illuminated manuscripts; religious and historical texts.

    Church Painting: Distinctive Gondarene style; manuscript illumination; church murals.

    Royal Patronage: Emperors sponsor churches, literature; Iyasu I, Bakaffa, Iyasu II.

    Theological Controversies: Christological debates (Sost Lidet, Qibat, Tewahedo); factionalism.

    7. Zemene Mesafint (1769–1855 CE)

    Era of Princes: Royal power collapses; regional lords (Ras) control emperors as puppets.

    Ras Mikael Sehul: 1769; deposes and kills emperor; warlord dominance begins.

    Provincial Autonomy: Tigray, Gojjam, Shewa, Begemder compete; shifting alliances; endemic warfare.

    Weak Emperors: Nominal rulers in Gondar; real power with provincial Ras; fragmented polity.

    Church Continuity: Monasteries maintain literary tradition; copying continues; ecclesiastical authority.

    Manuscript Culture Persists: Despite political chaos, manuscript production, copying, church education continue.

    8. Reunification and Modern State (1855–1945 CE)

    Tewodros II: 1855–68; reunification begins; modernization attempts; British expedition 1868; suicide.

    Yohannes IV: 1872–89; Tigrayan emperor; defends against Egypt, Mahdists, Italy; killed at Metemma.

    Menelik II: 1889–1913; Shewan expansion; Adwa victory 1896; modern capital Addis Ababa; railways.

    Haile Selassie: Regent 1916, emperor 1930–74; 1931 constitution; League of Nations; modernization.

    Italian Invasion: 1935–36; fascist conquest; occupation 1936–41; manuscript destruction and looting.

    Liberation: 1941; British-Ethiopian forces; emperor restored; post-war modernization; print begins displacing manuscript.
