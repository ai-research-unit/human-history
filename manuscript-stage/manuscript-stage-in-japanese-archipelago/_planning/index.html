    Planning Document — Manuscript Stage in The Japanese Archipelago (700 CE – 1650 CE)

    Age Definition

    Manuscript Stage: External records kept outside the body become the medium public life runs on — accounts, holdings, and obligations, then laws, letters, and argument, written rather than remembered. Persistence becomes systematic: a record outlasts its maker, can be consulted by someone never told, and accumulates beyond any living memory. The substrate begins as the administrative store of early states and deepens across the stage — as signs detach from the things they pictured into a recombinable script able to set down anything sayable, as literacy spreads beyond the specialist class toward the public, and as cheap, abundant paper carries records through everyday public life. Dominant until mass print carries the public sphere.

    Window: 700 CE – 1650 CE

    Region: The Japanese Archipelago — the island chain off the East Asian coast, including Honshu, Kyushu, Shikoku, and Hokkaido; bounded by the Sea of Japan, Pacific Ocean, and neighboring waters; the core zone of Japanese civilization from Yamato to Tokugawa eras.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological and textual record includes Nara-period administrative documents, Heian court literature, Buddhist sutras and monastic records, medieval estate records (shoen), warrior house documents, Zen temple archives, and early Edo-period governance records. Chinese script adopted ~5th–6th centuries; kana syllabaries develop 9th century; paper manufacture well established; woodblock printing exists but manuscript dominant until commercial publishing expands (~1590s–1650s). Window begins with Taika/Ritsuryo state codification; ends as Edo-period commercial printing and Tokugawa administration transform public communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Nara State (700–794 CE)

    Taika Reforms: ~645 CE; Chinese-inspired administrative reorganization; mature by early 8th century.

    Ritsuryo Codes: Taiho Code (701); Yoro Code (718); Chinese-style penal and administrative law.

    Nara Capital: 710–784 CE; permanent capital; grid plan; central administration.

    Buddhist Establishment: State-sponsored temples; Todaiji (Great Buddha); Six Nara Schools; sutra copying.

    Administrative Documents: Population registers; tax records; provincial reports; extensive bureaucracy.

    Chinese Script: Kanji dominant; manyogana (phonetic kanji use) develops; Kojiki, Nihon Shoki compiled.

    2. Early Heian Court (794–1000 CE)

    Capital Move: 794 CE; Heian-kyo (Kyoto); imperial seat until 1868.

    Fujiwara Regency: Sekkan politics; regents dominate court; marriage politics.

    Kana Development: Hiragana, katakana emerge 9th century; vernacular writing enabled.

    Court Literature: Tale of Genji (~1000 CE, Murasaki Shikibu); Pillow Book; poetry collections.

    Esoteric Buddhism: Tendai, Shingon; monastic centers; manuscript sutra production.

    Paper Manufacture: Well-established; domestic production; supports manuscript culture.

    3. Late Heian (1000–1185 CE)

    Insei System: Retired emperors rule; complex court politics; imperial competition.

    Shoen Estates: Private landholding expands; tax-exempt manors; estate documents.

    Provincial Disorder: Warrior bands; frontier conflict; Taira, Minamoto rise.

    Taira Hegemony: Taira no Kiyomori; court dominance; Itsukushima patronage.

    Genpei War: 1180–1185; Minamoto vs. Taira; decisive Minamoto victory.

    Documentary Practice: Estate records proliferate; warrior house documents emerge.

    4. Kamakura Shogunate (1185–1333 CE)

    Minamoto Yoritomo: First shogun; Kamakura bakufu; dual governance with court.

    Hojo Regency: Hojo clan as shogunal regents; effective power; legal codification.

    Jokyu Disturbance: 1221; retired emperor's failed revolt; bakufu strengthened.

    Mongol Invasions: 1274, 1281; kamikaze typhoons; defensive success; financial strain.

    Goseibai Shikimoku: 1232; warrior house laws; legal precedent; documentary adjudication.

    Pure Land Buddhism: Honen, Shinran; popular Buddhism; vernacular teachings; manuscript spread.

    5. Northern and Southern Courts (1333–1392 CE)

    Kemmu Restoration: 1333–1336; Emperor Go-Daigo; direct imperial rule; failure.

    Ashikaga Takauji: Defects from Go-Daigo; establishes shogunate; Northern Court emperor.

    Court Schism: Northern (Kyoto, Ashikaga-backed) vs. Southern (Yoshino); competing legitimacy.

    Nanbokucho Period: Civil war; regional autonomy; disruption; eventual reunification 1392.

    Documentary Confusion: Competing court systems; legitimacy disputes; manuscript proliferation.

    Warrior Culture: Bushi values; gunki monogatari (war tales); Taiheiki compiled.

    6. Muromachi Stability (1392–1467 CE)

    Court Reunification: Southern Court submits 1392; Ashikaga legitimacy secured.

    Shogunal Authority: Yoshimitsu peak; Kitayama culture; waning under successors.

    Gozan Zen: Five Mountains system; Zen monasteries; Chinese learning; printing centers.

    Ming Trade: Tally trade; Chinese imports; commercial expansion; Sakai merchants.

    Noh Drama: Zeami; aristocratic patronage; performance texts.

    Paper Trade: Domestic paper widely available; commercial manuscript copying.

    7. Sengoku Period (1467–1568 CE)

    Onin War: 1467–1477; Kyoto devastated; shogunal authority collapses; provincial autonomy.

    Sengoku Daimyo: Regional warlords; independent domains; castle towns; local governance.

    Bunkoku-ho: Provincial law codes; daimyo legislation; documentary administration.

    Economic Growth: Agricultural expansion; commercial networks; market development.

    Christian Mission: Francis Xavier 1549; Jesuit missionaries; European contacts; printing press (limited).

    Castle Architecture: Mountain to plain castles; administrative centers; document archives.

    8. Unification Wars (1568–1600 CE)

    Oda Nobunaga: ~1534–1582; conquers central Japan; temple destruction; assassination.

    Toyotomi Hideyoshi: ~1537–1598; completes unification; land surveys (taiko kenchi); Korea invasions.

    Korea Invasions: 1592, 1597; failed conquest; cultural plunder; Korean captives.

    Cadastral Surveys: Systematic land registration; documentary basis for taxation.

    Momoyama Culture: Grand castles; screen painting; tea ceremony; manuscripts and early print.

    Jesuit Press: ~1590s; movable type; limited production; Catholic texts.

    9. Tokugawa Establishment (1600–1615 CE)

    Sekigahara: 1600; decisive battle; Tokugawa Ieyasu victory; eastern coalition triumphs.

    Shogunate Founded: 1603; Edo bakufu; Ieyasu first Tokugawa shogun.

    Fudai/Tozama Division: Inside/outside lords; domain redistribution; political settlement.

    Osaka Sieges: 1614–1615; Toyotomi remnants destroyed; Tokugawa hegemony complete.

    Laws for Warrior Houses: Buke shohatto (1615); shogunal control; documentary governance.

    Capital Development: Edo grows; administrative hub; sankin-kotai system.

    10. Early Edo Order (1615–1650 CE)

    Bakufu Consolidation: Hidetada, Iemitsu; system stabilization; domain supervision.

    Sankin-kotai: Alternate attendance; daimyo control; Edo economy; road development.

    Sakoku: Closed country edicts ~1633–1641; limited foreign contact; Nagasaki Dutch/Chinese.

    Commercial Publishing: Kyoto, Osaka, Edo; woodblock printing expands; Sagabon luxury editions.

    Manuscript Twilight: Commercial print supplements manuscript; popular literature; broadening literacy.

    Temple Registration: Buddhist temple certification; population documentation; administrative literacy.
