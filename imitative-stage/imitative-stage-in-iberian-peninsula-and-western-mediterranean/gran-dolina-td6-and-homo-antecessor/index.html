    900,000 BCE – 780,000 BCE — Gran Dolina TD6 and Homo antecessor

    Summary: Gran Dolina level TD6 in the Sierra de Atapuerca preserves the type specimens of Homo antecessor — a hominin species combining archaic and modern facial features, with cranial capacity around 1000 cc — alongside abundant Mode 1 tools and faunal remains. The defining feature of this assemblage is evidence of cannibalism: cut-marks and percussion damage on hominin bones indistinguishable from those on animal prey, indicating that these hominins processed and consumed their own kind. The binding constraint remains the imitative substrate — all knowledge held in living heads, passed by demonstration, lost at death — which here extends even to the treatment of the dead as food, a practice transmitted without moral codification or prohibition.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Gran Dolina is a cave site in the Sierra de Atapuerca, a limestone massif in the northern Meseta of Iberia. The cave system sits at a junction of ecological zones — the Duero basin to the south, the Cantabrian foothills to the north — providing access to diverse habitats within walking range. The Pyrenees still blocked easy passage from Europe; Iberian populations remained semi-isolated, connected to the wider hominin world only through the Gibraltar corridor or difficult mountain routes.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The period 900–780 ka spans the transition from the Early to Middle Pleistocene, including the major climatic shift around 900 ka when glacial-interglacial cycles intensified. Gran Dolina TD6 accumulated during an interglacial phase — warm, relatively moist — supporting mixed woodland and open grassland. Seasonal variation was marked, with cold winters requiring shelter and fire or fat reserves to survive.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: The cave provided shelter from weather and predators. Seismic risk was low. The principal hazards were climatic deterioration during glacial advances and competition with large carnivores — hyenas, large cats, wolves — that also used cave sites.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: The surrounding landscape supported abundant large fauna — deer, horse, bison, rhinoceros — as evidenced by the faunal assemblage at TD6. Flint and quartzite were available in local river gravels. Water came from springs and streams. The cave itself was a resource — defensible, dry, preserving heat.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Bone and lithic refuse accumulated on the cave floor, later buried by sediment. Population density was far too low to stress any environmental sink. The waste simply preserved, becoming the archaeological record.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Interglacial warmth supported higher pathogen and parasite loads than glacial conditions. Mortality was high — from infection, injury, predation, and violence. The age profile of the TD6 hominin remains skews young, suggesting high juvenile and young-adult mortality.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Homo antecessor was robust, medium-statured, with heavy brow ridges and large teeth. Cranial capacity averaged around 1000 cc — smaller than later Homo but larger than Homo erectus sensu stricto. The cut-marked bones show access to meat, including marrow; nutrition was adequate to support growth and reproduction, at least in favorable seasons.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: No direct evidence of healing practice. The presence of some older individuals among the remains suggests that some injured or ill individuals survived long enough to be supported, but this may reflect tolerance rather than active care. Without stored medical knowledge, recovery depended on rest and natural healing.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Reproductive biology followed the hominin pattern — extended juvenile dependency, slow maturation, pair-bonding within groups. The genetic evidence (from later Atapuerca sites) suggests small, inbred populations with limited gene flow — consistent with isolated bands occasionally meeting for mating.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population density was extremely low. The TD6 assemblage represents accumulation over centuries or millennia, not a single event. Replacement rates were marginal; the population was vulnerable to local extinction from climatic shift, disease, or simple demographic stochasticity.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: The age profile suggests most individuals died before reaching old age. Dependency consisted mainly of immatures. The loss of adults erased whatever knowledge they carried; there was no mechanism to preserve expertise beyond the living.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Subsistence combined hunting, scavenging, and gathering. The TD6 fauna shows primary access to carcasses — not just scavenging from carnivore kills but active hunting or early arrival at death sites. The hominin bones with processing marks indicate that human bodies were also part of the subsistence economy, whether from violence, opportunistic consumption of the dead, or deliberate killing.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Energy capture was muscular — human locomotion and hand-held tools. Fire use is attested at some Early Pleistocene sites but remains uncertain at TD6. If fire was available, it would have extended the edible range of foods and provided warmth and protection.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Division of labor followed age and sex gradients. Cooperative hunting, if practiced, required coordination by imitation and joint attention. Coercion within groups was interpersonal, not institutionalized.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Mode 1 tools — cores and flakes — were the only manufactured goods. Production was expedient: make, use, discard. No surplus accumulated beyond what bodies stored as fat.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: No material storage. The fat and marrow from prey, including the cannibalized dead, served as the immediate reserve. Buffering depended on group mobility and the ability to relocate when local resources failed.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: No evidence of exchange beyond immediate sharing. Raw materials were procured locally. Movement was on foot, carrying only what the body could bear.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No material medium of value. Reciprocity was maintained in living memory and erased by death or dispersal.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No surplus existed to extract. Distribution was immediate and governed by face-to-face negotiation and kin obligation.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: No durable assets to concentrate or defend. Inequality was situational and dissolved with each consumption event.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Lithic debitage, bone fragments, and butchery refuse accumulated on the cave floor. Disposal was abandonment; the cave's sedimentary regime buried and preserved the discard.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Bands were organized around kin — mothers with offspring, associated males. The genetic evidence from later Atapuerca (Sima de los Huesos) suggests small, inbred populations; TD6 likely followed a similar pattern. Inheritance was biological only; there was no property to pass.

    Association & Mutual Aid: The band was the unit of mutual aid — food sharing, collective vigilance, assistance in childcare. The cannibalism evidence complicates this picture: mutual aid coexisted with the processing of group members as food, whether after death or through violence.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Play and grooming would have reinforced bonds within the group. These activities left no archaeological trace but are inferred from primate baselines.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Status differences were informal — dominance in specific contexts, deference to skilled foragers. There was no fixed hierarchy, no inherited rank.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No administration. Decision-making was collective and immediate.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Coercion was interpersonal. The same tools used for butchery could be used for violence. The cannibalism evidence suggests that lethal violence within or between groups was not taboo — or rather, that no taboo against it had been codified and transmitted.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Social friction was discharged through fission, avoidance, or violence. The small scale of groups prevented grievance from accumulating into organized conflict; it either resolved, the group split, or someone died.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Leadership was situational. Authority dissolved when the situation passed. There was no office, no stable role of leader.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Allegiance was personal, owed to kin and familiar band members. There was no formal authority to legitimate.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Collective action was coordinated by imitation and joint attention. Resolve formed around immediate goals — a hunt, a defense, a move — and dissolved when the action ended.

    External Relations & Posture: Relations with other bands, if encountered, were probably cautious — avoidance, occasional violence, possible interbreeding. The cannibalism may have included intergroup violence, with the eaten being enemies rather than kin.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Emotional life included fear, anger, affection, and grief, expressed through gesture and vocalization. The processing of human bodies as food suggests either emotional compartmentalization — treating dead or enemy humans as prey — or practices so normalized that they required no special emotional framing.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Loss and exhaustion would have produced grief and apathy. Without ritual or narrative to process loss, emotional recovery depended on time and the demands of survival.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Communication relied on gesture, vocalization, and demonstration. There was no speech in the full linguistic sense — no syntax, no arbitrary symbols, no recursive structure. Meaning was conveyed by showing, not telling. Mutual legibility was limited to those who had learned together.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Information traveled at the speed of walking and the range of hearing. There was no way to send a message ahead or store one for later retrieval.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: There was no record. The Mode 1 toolkit was simple enough to reinvent, but any refinement — a better knapping angle, a preferred raw material source — died with its discoverer unless demonstrated and copied in time.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: There was no discourse to frame or censor. Behavioral norms — who could approach whom, how to signal intent — were learned by observation and enforced by reaction.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Attention was face-to-face. What was not witnessed was not known. There was no amplification, no way to reach beyond the immediate audience.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Each transmission of technique or knowledge introduced drift. Without correction mechanisms, the signal degraded generationally. The stability of Mode 1 technology across hundreds of thousands of years may reflect this constraint — not stasis by choice but the floor below which accumulated noise erased improvement.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Moral intuitions — fairness, resentment of cheating, protection of kin — are inferred from primate baselines. The cannibalism evidence suggests that these intuitions did not extend to prohibiting the consumption of human flesh, at least not in all circumstances. There was no codified ethic, only felt reactions to behavior.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: There was no law. Norms were behavioral, learned by observation, enforced by immediate reaction. The absence of a taboo against cannibalism — or its presence but non-enforcement — illustrates the limits of uncodified norms.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Disputes were resolved by dominance, negotiation, or fission. There was no third-party judgment, no formal sanction beyond immediate retaliation.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: There were no rights in the formal sense. Protection came from kin, allies, and one's own capacity for violence. Compliance was to immediate social pressure.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Wrongs were redressed immediately or not at all. There was no accumulated grievance beyond living memory, no persistent record of transgression. The eaten left no descendants to demand justice; the eaters faced no judgment beyond their contemporaries.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: There was no inquiry in the reflective sense. Understanding was procedural — knowing where to find game, how to knap a flake, which plants were edible. There was no theory, no why, only how.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Mode 1 technology — simple cores and flakes — was the sole technical method. The TD6 assemblage shows competent knapping but no elaboration beyond the Early Pleistocene baseline. Technique was learned by observation and imitation, without verbal instruction.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Education was demonstration. The knowledge stock was what living adults could show. Skill distribution depended on who was present during teaching and who survived to practice. There was no cumulative improvement across generations, only maintenance of the baseline.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Knowledge decayed with each death. There was no obsolescence — no replacement of old by new — only loss. Whatever improvements arose were tested against the filter of transmission and mostly failed to persist.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: There was no mythos that survives. Identity was immediate — recognition of kin, familiarity with band members. There was no transmitted narrative of origin or history.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: There was no narrative to legitimate any practice. Cannibalism required no justification because there was no authority to question it, no code to violate, no story to explain or excuse.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Material culture consisted of Mode 1 tools — functional, undecorated, varying by raw material and immediate need. There was no art, no ornament, no symbolic marking. The tools were purely utilitarian.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: There was no accumulated form to exhaust. Style did not exist because meaning was not stored. Each tool was made for immediate use, not for display or identity.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: There is no evidence of cosmology. The world was encountered, not explained. Existence was immediate, without speculation about its nature or purpose.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: There is no evidence of ritual in the TD6 assemblage. The processing of human bodies shows no special treatment distinguishing them from animal prey — no careful placement, no association with unusual objects, no pattern suggesting ceremony.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: There were no institutions and no doctrine. The spiritual dimension, as far as evidence shows, was not yet occupied.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: There was nothing to diffuse. Each group's orientation to the world was self-generated and died with it.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: There was no enchantment to lose. The sacred had not yet emerged as a category. The dead were food, not ancestors; the world was resource, not cosmos. The void that later disenchantment would leave had not yet been filled by what it would empty.
