    350,000 BCE – 200,000 BCE — Late Acheulean to Mousterian Transition

    Summary: A gradual technological shift across the Iberian Peninsula during which the Acheulean handaxe tradition gave way to prepared-core flaking techniques, culminating in the Mousterian industries associated with Neanderthals. The single most causal factor is the emergence and spread of the Levallois method — a technique for predetermining flake shape before detachment — which offered greater efficiency in raw material use and more standardized tool blanks, but required more complex mental planning and more precise motor control, both of which had to be transmitted through imitation alone.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Iberian Peninsula remained bounded by the Pyrenees and surrounded by sea, with the Gibraltar Strait narrowing to fourteen kilometers at its closest point. Internal movement followed major river valleys — the Ebro, Tagus, Guadiana, and Guadalquivir — while the Meseta central plateau imposed its own barriers of elevation and aridity. The Cantabrian coast offered Atlantic-facing habitats distinct from the Mediterranean littoral. This geography channeled population movement and created semi-isolated regional pockets where technological variants could develop independently.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The window spans multiple glacial-interglacial cycles, including Marine Isotope Stages 10 through 7. Glacial phases brought steppe-tundra conditions to northern Iberia and expanded arid zones in the interior, while interglacials restored Mediterranean woodland and deciduous forest. The southern coast and Gibraltar corridor remained relatively buffered, serving as refugia during cold phases. This climatic oscillation drove repeated expansion and contraction of habitable zones, fragmenting and reuniting populations.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: No evidence of catastrophic volcanic or seismic events affecting Iberian populations during this window. Drought and cold posed the primary hazards, forcing movement to better-watered or warmer refugia. Coastal populations faced rising and falling sea levels, which periodically exposed and drowned coastal plains.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Flint remained the preferred raw material where available, with outcrops in the Ebro valley, parts of the Meseta, and the Mediterranean coast. Quartzite and quartz were used where flint was scarce. Large herbivore populations — horses, aurochs, red deer, and during cold phases, reindeer — provided the primary protein source. Wood for spears and fuel was available in most contexts.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Population densities remained low enough that no cumulative environmental impact is detectable. Occupation sites show refuse accumulation, but abandonment and natural decay cleared the record between occupations.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Skeletal evidence is sparse for this transitional period. General Middle Pleistocene mortality patterns apply: high infant mortality, significant attrition from trauma and infection, and few individuals surviving past forty. Endemic disease load was likely lower than in later agricultural populations, but episodic epidemic exposure from animal contact remained possible.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: The populations of this window — late Homo heidelbergensis transitioning toward Neanderthal morphology — were robustly built, with heavy musculature and large brains (1,200-1,400 cc). Dental wear indicates mixed diets with substantial meat consumption. Nutritional stress episodes are recorded in enamel hypoplasia, but overall physiological condition appears adequate for survival and reproduction.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Continuity with earlier periods: injured individuals sometimes survived, implying tolerance or active care. No specialized healing practices can be inferred beyond wound tending and rest. The transition period shows no change in this capacity.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Pronounced sexual dimorphism continued. Genetic evidence from later Neanderthal populations shows low diversity, suggesting small effective population sizes and possibly inbreeding, a pattern likely extending back into this transitional period. Pair bonding and family structure remain invisible in the archaeological record.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population density across Iberia was low — perhaps a few thousand individuals in the entire peninsula at any time, organized in bands of fifteen to thirty. The glacial-interglacial oscillations drove cycles of population expansion into newly habitable areas during warm phases and contraction to refugia during cold phases. The southern coast and Gibraltar corridor maintained population continuity through cold intervals.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: The demographic profile skewed young, with few elderly. The productive cohort carried the burden of dependent young and incapacitated injured; the elderly were rare enough that their dependency was minimal. Overall, the population replaced itself, but with little margin for sustained growth.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Hunting of large and medium game remained the subsistence base. Site assemblages show processing of horses, deer, aurochs, and rhinoceros. Plant foods were certainly consumed but leave minimal archaeological trace. Coastal sites (e.g., Gibraltar area) show early shellfish exploitation. The provisioning floor was set by hunting success, which was seasonal and variable.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle remained the sole motive power. Fire use was established, providing warmth, protection, and cooking capacity. The energetic budget centered on the hunt: the caloric investment in pursuit and kill had to be recovered in meat returns. The Levallois technique may have improved this balance by producing more usable cutting edge per unit of raw material transported.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Cooperative hunting required coordination, achieved through imitation and situational leadership rather than formal organization. Division of labor by sex and age is inferred from sexual dimorphism and the demands of child-rearing but cannot be directly observed. Coercion, if any, was personal and immediate, not institutionalized.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Production was limited to stone tools, wooden spears, and perishable equipment. The Levallois technique represented a production innovation: by preparing a core in advance, the knapper could produce multiple flakes of predictable shape, reducing waste and transport cost. No surplus accumulation beyond immediate consumption; meat spoiled quickly without preservation technology.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: No storage capacity. The body's fat reserves and the ability to move to better-provisioned areas were the only buffers against scarcity. Hunting success in autumn, when animals were fattest, provided a seasonal peak that had to carry populations through winter.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: No evidence of inter-group exchange. Raw material sourcing shows transport distances of tens of kilometers, consistent with band-range mobility rather than trade. Each group was self-sufficient within its territory.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Absent. Reciprocity was immediate and personal, held in memory and discharged within the group. No medium of deferred exchange existed.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Redistribution occurred through sharing of kills. Ethnographic analogy suggests strong norms of sharing, enforced by immediate social pressure. No extraction by dominant individuals beyond what sharing norms permitted.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: No material wealth beyond personal tools. Tools were made, used, and discarded; no accumulation or defense of holdings is evident.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Lithic debris accumulated at knapping sites; butchery waste at processing locations. Abandonment cleared these sites, and natural processes buried or dispersed the debris. No managed waste disposal.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Bands were kin-based, with genetic evidence from later Neanderthal sites showing close relatedness within groups. Inheritance of territory and technique passed through demonstration within family lines. Marriage or mating patterns are unknown but presumably involved some exchange between groups to maintain genetic diversity, however limited.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Mutual aid within the band was essential for survival. Hunting, child-rearing, and care of the injured required cooperation. The band itself was the unit of association; no broader structures existed.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: No dedicated leisure is observable. Sociability was embedded in daily activity — the hunt, the hearth, the camp. Play among juveniles likely served learning functions, but leaves no trace.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: No evidence of ranked status. Leadership was situational — the skilled hunter, the experienced elder — not institutionalized. Sexual dimorphism implies role differentiation but not hierarchy. The band was small enough that all members knew each other personally.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Absent. No administration; no institutions beyond the band itself.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Weapons were hunting tools: wooden spears, stone-tipped spears toward the end of the window, handaxes, and flake tools. Interpersonal violence occurred — cranial trauma is attested in skeletal remains — but no organized coercive apparatus existed. Conflict with other groups was probably rare given low population density and large territories.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Grievance was resolved immediately through dominance, submission, or departure. The small scale of the group meant that persistent conflict was unsustainable; resolution or fission followed quickly. No accumulated grievance; no disorder beyond the interpersonal.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Unknowable directly. The continuation of band life over this long window implies sufficient internal cohesion and trust to maintain cooperation. The demands of imitative learning and cooperative hunting required tolerance and patience.

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Leadership was personal and situational — the skilled hunter led the hunt, the experienced knapper demonstrated technique. No permanent leadership; authority was earned and lost with demonstrated competence.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Allegiance was to the band, held through face-to-face bonds. No abstract legitimacy; the group simply was, and membership was identity.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Collective action for hunting required coordinated will, achieved through shared attention and imitation rather than command. The adoption and spread of Levallois technique across the peninsula implies a diffuse collective will to learn and improve, though this was not articulated or directed.

    External Relations & Posture: Other bands were distant and rarely encountered. Relations were probably cautious — neither hostile nor allied, simply other. Mating exchanges may have occurred during chance encounters, but no sustained inter-group relations are evident.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: No evidence. The long continuity of the transition implies sustained engagement with the demands of survival. Exhaustion and despair were individual, not collective, and left no mark.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Communication remained gestural, postural, and vocal but pre-linguistic. The Levallois technique required transmission of a more complex operational sequence than Acheulean handaxe manufacture — the core had to be prepared in multiple stages before the final flake was struck — and this increased the informational demands on imitative learning. Whether this pressure contributed to the evolution of enhanced communication is debated but cannot be resolved from the evidence.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Face-to-face and immediate. Information traveled at walking pace and could not be sent ahead. The spread of Levallois technique across the peninsula took thousands of years, consistent with slow diffusion through population contact rather than rapid transmission.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: No external record. The technique itself was the only "storage" — embodied in the knapper's skill and the tools produced. When practitioners died, their knowledge died unless already transmitted by imitation.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Absent. What could be shown could be learned; nothing could be forbidden except by preventing demonstration.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Attention was local and immediate. The learner watched the master; no amplification beyond the circle of observers was possible.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Each imitative transmission introduced error. The Levallois technique's greater complexity may have increased transmission fidelity — failure to execute the sequence correctly produced unusable results, creating selection pressure for accurate learning — but drift remained inevitable. Regional variants in technique reflect accumulated transmission differences.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Norms were enacted in practice, not articulated. Sharing of meat, care of the injured, and treatment of the dead implied standards of conduct, but these were felt obligations, not stated rules.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No codification. Custom was behavior, not text or doctrine.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Disputes were resolved immediately by the parties involved. No third-party adjudication; no formal sanction beyond social pressure and personal retaliation.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Absent. Individuals had no rights beyond what group membership and personal relationships secured.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Wrongs were either avenged or absorbed. Memory of grievance was personal and died with the aggrieved. No backlog accumulated.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: No formal inquiry. The world was navigated through embedded practical knowledge, not theorized. Understanding of stone fracture mechanics, animal behavior, and landscape was profound but implicit, never abstracted into theory.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: The central intellectual development of the window is the emergence of prepared-core techniques, culminating in the Levallois method. This required a mental template of the desired flake, a multi-stage reduction sequence to prepare the core, and precise motor control to execute the final strike. The technique represented a genuine advance in cognitive planning — holding a future state in mind through multiple intermediate steps — though it remained within the bounds of imitative transmission. Hafting of stone points to wooden shafts also emerged during this window, requiring adhesives (birch pitch) and binding, adding further complexity to the technical repertoire.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Learning remained imitative. The knowledge stock was the sum of living practitioners; skill distribution was uneven, with some individuals more adept than others, but no formal differentiation. The Levallois technique may have been more demanding to learn, narrowing the pool of fully competent practitioners.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Acheulean handaxe manufacture did not disappear instantly; it persisted alongside prepared-core techniques for millennia before fading. This gradual obsolescence reflects the slow pace of transmission-by-imitation: no one declared the old technique obsolete; it simply produced fewer practitioners as learners chose to copy the newer method. Knowledge decayed continuously through transmission error and practitioner death.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: No evidence of narrative identity. The band was identity; membership was belonging. No origin stories could survive the generation that held them.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Absent. The order required no justification because it was not conceived as contingent or alterable.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: No clear evidence of symbolic art or ornament during this window. Material culture was functional: tools for hunting and processing, hearths for warmth and cooking. Some handaxes show symmetry beyond strict functional requirement, but whether this reflects aesthetic intention or simply skilled manufacture remains debated. The transition to Levallois involved a change in tool morphology — from large bifacial handaxes to smaller, thinner flakes — which represents a material-culture shift even if not a symbolic one.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Forms could not exhaust because they were not fixed. The gradual replacement of Acheulean by Mousterian was not a rejection of exhausted forms but a functional displacement: the newer technique worked better, and learners chose to copy it.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Unknowable. The continuation of body-disposal practices (suggested by occasional deliberate burials in later Mousterian contexts) implies some orientation toward death, but the content of any cosmology is beyond recovery.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: No clear evidence of ritual during this transitional window. The Sima de los Huesos practice, if it continued, left no comparable accumulation. Occasional possible burials in later Mousterian sites (Sima de las Palomas, Kebara) suggest the practice persisted or emerged independently, but remains contested.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Absent. No institution; no doctrine; no authority.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Not applicable at this scale. If spiritual orientations existed, they were local to each band and could not spread except through population contact and imitation.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Not applicable. The sacred, if present, was not differentiated from daily life and could not be lost.
