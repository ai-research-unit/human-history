    Planning — Imitative in The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean

    Binding Context

    • Age: Imitative — The substrate of public life before language: everything a group knows held in living heads and passed only by showing and copying, hand guiding hand — dominant by default until speech becomes the primary medium.
    • Region: The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean
    • Window: 1,400,000 BCE – 42,000 BCE
    • Target article count: 7–11 (soft target; attestation and significance are hard constraints)

    Shared Evidence Base

    Overview of Hominin Occupation in Iberia and the Western Mediterranean

    The Iberian Peninsula represents one of the earliest and most continuous records of hominin occupation in western Europe. The region served as a refugium during glacial periods due to its southern latitude and varied topography, and as a gateway for dispersals between Africa and Europe.

    Key Archaeological Sites and Evidence

    Earliest Occupation (1.4–1.0 Ma) - Sima del Elefante, Atapuerca (Spain): Level TE9 dated to ~1.2 Ma, yielding Homo sp. mandible fragment and Mode 1 (Oldowan-like) lithics. Source: Carbonell et al. 2008, Nature 452:465–469. - Barranco León and Fuente Nueva 3, Orce (Spain): Dated ~1.3–1.4 Ma by paleomagnetic and biostratigraphic methods. Disputed hominin presence; clear Oldowan lithics with large fauna. Source: Toro-Moyano et al. 2013, Journal of Human Evolution 65:1–9. - The 1.4 Ma date for earliest Iberian occupation is contested; secure dates cluster around 1.2 Ma, with earlier claims (Orce) subject to ongoing debate about taphonomy and association.

    Early Pleistocene Occupation (~1.2–0.8 Ma) - Gran Dolina TD6, Atapuerca: ~800–900 ka, Homo antecessor remains (>80 fragments, min. 6 individuals). Mode 1 lithics. Evidence of cannibalism (cutmarks, percussion marks consistent with defleshing). Source: Bermúdez de Castro et al. 1997, Science 276:1392–1395; Fernández-Jalvo et al. 1999, JHE 37:591–622. - Mode 1 (core-and-flake) technology persists throughout; no handaxes at these levels.

    Middle Pleistocene Mode 2 Transition (~600–500 ka) - Galería, Atapuerca: ~400–300 ka, bifacial handaxes (Acheulean/Mode 2) appear alongside faunal accumulations interpreted as hominin-processed. Source: Ollé et al. 2013, Quaternary International 295:138–150. - Cueva Negra del Estrecho del Río Quípar (Murcia): ~780 ka claimed, but controversial; more secure Acheulean sites date to ~500–400 ka. - Aridos 1 and 2, Madrid: ~350–300 ka, elephant butchery sites with handaxes. - The transition from Mode 1 to Mode 2 in Iberia is gradual and asynchronous, appearing later than in East Africa (~1.76 Ma) and broadly contemporaneous with northern Europe (~600–500 ka in Britain, France).

    Sima de los Huesos Population (~430 ka) - Sima de los Huesos, Atapuerca: >28 individuals, representing a single population accumulation, likely funerary or natural-trap. Morphology ancestral to Neanderthals. Handaxe (Excalibur) found in deposit — deliberate deposition debated. Source: Arsuaga et al. 2014, Science 344:1358–1363. - This is the largest Middle Pleistocene hominin assemblage worldwide; provides unique window into population biology: high juvenile mortality, evidence of pathology (cranial trauma healed ante-mortem), and possible earliest evidence of intentional body disposal.

    Late Middle Pleistocene (~300–130 ka) - Early Neanderthal populations established across Iberia. Sites include Bolomor (Valencia), Cova del Bolomor levels with evidence of fire use, small-game hunting. - Levallois (Mode 3) prepared-core technology appears ~300 ka, gradually replacing earlier Acheulean. - Evidence of systematic fire use becomes consistent by ~300–200 ka.

    Last Interglacial and Early Last Glacial (~130–70 ka) - Neanderthal florescence in Iberia; dense site distribution. Key sites: El Sidrón (Asturias), Abric Romaní (Catalonia), Gibraltar (Gorham's and Vanguard Caves). - Mousterian technology (Mode 3) standard; regional variants (Quina, Ferrassie, denticulate Mousterian). - Evidence of plant processing, fire, pigment use (ochre at Cueva de los Aviones, ~115–120 ka — source: Hoffmann et al. 2018, Science 359:912–915, though date contested).

    Late Neanderthal Persistence (~70–42 ka) - Iberia as final Neanderthal refugium; populations persist here longer than elsewhere in Europe. - Terminal Mousterian at sites like Gorham's Cave (Gibraltar) dated to ~42–40 ka (though some claim younger dates, these are contested). - No clear evidence of interaction with modern humans within this window (pre-42 ka). - Population density appears low; genetic evidence (from later aDNA) suggests small, isolated groups.

    Environmental Context

    Glacial-Interglacial Cyclicity - Pleistocene climate dominated by ~100 ka glacial cycles (after Mid-Pleistocene Transition ~1.2 Ma); earlier 41 ka cycles also affected the region. - During glacials, Iberia served as southern refugium; coastlines shifted (sea level drops of 100+ m exposed continental shelves). - Mediterranean zones retained relatively moderate conditions; interior plateaus (Meseta) experienced continental extremes.

    Fauna - Early Pleistocene: Mammuthus meridionalis, Hippopotamus, giant hyena, saber-toothed cats. - Middle Pleistocene: transition to Mammuthus trogontherii, Equus, Bos, large cervids. - Late Pleistocene: Mammuthus primigenius, Coelodonta (woolly rhino), red deer, ibex, aurochs. - Mediterranean coastal zones retained distinct fauna (monk seal, marine resources exploited from ~150 ka).

    Key Themes for Article Selection

    1. Initial colonization/earliest presence (~1.4–1.2 Ma) — contested but significant.
    2. Mode 1 technological persistence — long duration of Oldowan-like industries.
    3. Homo antecessor and Gran Dolina TD6 (~850 ka) — unique taxon, cannibalism evidence.
    4. Mode 2 (Acheulean) adoption (~500–400 ka) — technological transition.
    5. Sima de los Huesos population (~430 ka) — demographic/biological window.
    6. Mode 3 (Levallois/Mousterian) emergence (~300–200 ka) — technological shift.
    7. Neanderthal establishment and florescence (~130–70 ka) — demographic expansion.
    8. Late Neanderthal persistence (~70–42 ka) — refugium, isolation, eventual disappearance.

    Given the evidence concentration, a set of 8–9 articles is defensible. The earliest colonization is too thinly attested for a standalone article; it can be incorporated into a broader "Initial Occupation" snapshot. The Sima de los Huesos deposit and the TD6 cannibalism assemblage each warrant distinct articles due to their exceptional nature.

    Coverage and Grain Check

    • Coverage: The window 1,400,000–42,000 BCE spans ~1.36 million years. Articles are distributed across the major attested phases with no large unexplained gaps. The earliest 200,000 years (1.4–1.2 Ma) are thin in evidence and subsumed under Article 1 with appropriate caveats about the Orce controversy.
    • Grain: All articles operate at a multi-tens-of-thousands to hundred-thousands of years scale, appropriate for this deep-time window. None is at century scale; none at multi-million year scale.
    • No overlaps: Temporal boundaries are sequential with minor acceptable overlaps where evidence clusters (e.g., TD6 within the Mode 1 persistence period is a distinct demographic/biological focus distinct from the technological snapshot).
