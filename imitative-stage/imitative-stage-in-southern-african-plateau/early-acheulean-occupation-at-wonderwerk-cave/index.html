    1,400,000 BCE – 1,000,000 BCE — Early Acheulean Occupation at Wonderwerk Cave

    Summary: Wonderwerk Cave in the Northern Cape preserves a deep Acheulean sequence spanning several hundred thousand years, with contested evidence of early fire use around 1.0 Ma representing one of the oldest potential controlled-fire contexts globally. The binding constraint is the cave's fixed interior space limiting group size and activity range. Margin against environmental shock is moderate given the cave's stable shelter and proximity to water sources. The system holds in technological and demographic stasis across most variables, with fire use — if confirmed — representing the single notable directional shift.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Wonderwerk Cave lies in dolomite hills on the interior plateau edge at approximately 1400m elevation; the deep cave chamber extends over 140m providing exceptional shelter, with surrounding semi-arid Kalahari thornveld and seasonal drainage systems imposing moderate transit friction across open terrain.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Semi-arid conditions prevail with seasonal summer rainfall; glacial-interglacial oscillations produce multi-millennial shifts between wetter and drier phases, with the cave interior buffering daily and seasonal temperature extremes regardless of external regime.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Minimal seismic or volcanic exposure; primary hazards are periodic drought restricting surface water and vegetation, with the cave itself providing substantial margin against exposure-related mortality.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Dolomite provides raw knapping material of variable quality; better-quality quartzite and other raw materials require transport from sources several kilometers distant; the surrounding savanna-woodland supports diverse fauna and plant resources seasonally.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Open plateau landscape holds effectively unlimited absorptive capacity for minimal organic waste and lithic debris; cave deposits accumulate slowly without saturating any sink.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Sparse population density minimizes epidemic exposure; endemic parasites and wound infections impose a modest background mortality load; cave habitation may reduce certain exposure-related illnesses while concentrating others.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Homo erectus populations exhibit robust skeletal builds and large body size consistent with adequate nutritional intake; seasonal variation in food availability produces periodic stress but no evidence of chronic malnutrition in the skeletal record.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Extended juvenile dependency typical of Homo erectus constrains birth spacing; mating patterns are unattested but presumed to involve some form of pair-bonding or multi-male grouping; selection favors cooperative provisioning capacity.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population density remains extremely low across the plateau; small mobile bands likely number in the low tens of individuals; replacement rates appear sufficient to maintain occupation continuity over hundreds of millennia.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: High juvenile mortality and limited post-reproductive survival minimize dependency overhang; the productive cohort absorbs modest dependent loads without evident strain.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Mixed foraging economy combining hunting of large and medium fauna with plant collection; the cave's proximity to seasonal water sources and game trails provides a reliable provisioning base above bare subsistence.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle provides all mechanical work; fire use — if the contested evidence around 1.0 Ma is accepted — represents a transformative addition to the energy portfolio, extending processing capacity and digestibility; this remains the primary disputed variable.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Task differentiation is minimal and informal; cooperation in hunting and butchery is evidenced by large-mammal bone accumulations; no coercive extraction structures exist beyond situational dominance within small groups.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Acheulean handaxe production represents the primary manufactured output; surplus above immediate consumption is negligible and not storable; production matches immediate needs without accumulation.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: No durable storage technology exists; the cave itself serves as a spatial anchor but not a larder; buffering against resource failure relies on mobility and landscape knowledge rather than stockpiling.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Raw material transport over distances of several kilometers indicates logistical planning; no evidence of inter-group exchange or trade networks; distribution occurs within the co-resident band.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No medium of deferred exchange exists; reciprocal sharing within bands operates on immediate or near-term obligation without formalized debt or credit.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No coordinating authority extracts surplus; communal sharing of kills distributes resources within the band according to proximity, kinship, and situational negotiation.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: No durable wealth exists; tool ownership is individual and ephemeral; no defensive strategies against appropriation are needed or evidenced.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Knapping debris and food waste accumulate in occupation layers; volume is trivial relative to cave capacity and imposes no routing constraint; ash deposits — if present — represent fire-related residue absorbed into sediments.
