    1,200,000 BCE – 900,000 BCE — Lantian and Early Homo erectus in the Wei River Valley

    Summary: A snapshot of early Homo erectus occupation along the loess plateau margins of the Wei River Valley, centered on the Gongwangling cranium dated to approximately 1.15 Ma. The primary constraint is the narrow habitable zone where loess meets riverine resources. Populations hold moderate margin against seasonal stress but remain exposed to climatic oscillation. The system is stable with no directional drift.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Wei River valley forms a corridor through the eastern Qinling foothills where loess deposits meet alluvial floodplains; movement follows the river axis with lateral access constrained by plateau escarpments.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Temperate continental conditions prevail with warm summers, cold winters, and monsoonal rainfall concentrated in summer months; interannual variability is moderate with occasional drought years.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seasonal flooding along the Wei River poses the principal hazard; loess terrain subject to gullying and slope failure; seismic exposure present but infrequent acute events.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Locally abundant quartzite and quartz cobbles in river gravels; animal biomass concentrated along riparian corridors; plant foods seasonally available on plateau margins.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Effectively unlimited absorptive capacity across an open landscape; negligible organic residue from small foraging populations disperses without accumulation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic parasites and zoonotic exposure from prey species impose a low but steady mortality load; no epidemic regime recoverable; baseline attrition stable.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: The Gongwangling cranium indicates robust Homo erectus morphology with thick cranial vault and strong musculature attachments; nutritional status sufficient to support large body size.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Physiological fertility follows typical Homo erectus parameters; pair-bonding patterns unrecoverable but presumed stable given prolonged juvenile dependency.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population density extremely low across a large foraging range; replacement ratios hover near unity; no directional demographic pressure evident.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Mortality likely concentrated in juveniles and aged; dependency load absorbed within small bands without evidence of accumulated surplus or deficit.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Mixed foraging economy exploiting riverine fauna, terrestrial game, and seasonal plant resources; associated fauna include Equus and cervids indicating grassland-forest mosaic exploitation.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle provides all motive power; fire use unattested at Lantian sites; energetic return constrained by pursuit hunting and opportunistic scavenging.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Cooperative foraging within small kin-based bands; task division likely by age and sex; no coercion structures recoverable beyond immediate social pressure.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Simple flake tools produced from local raw materials; no surplus accumulation; production meets immediate consumption needs without reserve.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: No storage technology; buffering achieved through body fat and mobility across the seasonal resource gradient rather than accumulated reserves.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Raw material sourcing appears local to river gravels; no evidence of long-distance transport; exchange if present limited to immediate band range.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No material medium of deferred exchange; reciprocity within bands operates through immediate sharing and remembered obligation.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Large kills shared within the band by immediate distribution; no extraction by any authority above the foraging group.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: No durable wealth accumulation; positional advantage limited to access to prime foraging locations; no asset defense recoverable.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Knapping debris and food refuse dispersed across activity areas; volume negligible relative to landscape absorptive capacity.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Status hierarchy minimal and based on individual capability rather than hereditary position; group identity tied to band membership and shared foraging territory.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Kinship structures unrecoverable but presumed bilateral with flexible residence; no material inheritance beyond tool knowledge transmitted by demonstration.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: The band itself constitutes the sole organizational unit; no nested associations or institutions beyond immediate kin groupings.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Non-foraging time spent in camp; play likely important for juvenile skill development; sociability expressed through grooming and shared rest.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Interpersonal conflict resolved through avoidance, fission, or immediate physical contest; no formal mediation; unresolved tension discharged through group mobility.
