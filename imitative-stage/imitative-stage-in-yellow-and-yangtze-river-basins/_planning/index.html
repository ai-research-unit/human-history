    Planning: Imitative in The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins — 2,100,000 BCE – 50,000 BCE

    Age Constraint (FIXED)

    Imitative: The substrate of public life before language: everything a group knows held in living heads and passed only by showing and copying, hand guiding hand — dominant by default until speech becomes the primary medium.

    This constraint governs every article: knowledge transmission is strictly imitative (gestural, demonstrative), not linguistic. The Informational domain must reflect this — no symbolic encoding, no speech-based transmission. All cultural learning proceeds through observation and emulation.

    Shared Evidence Base

    Nihewan Basin (~2.1–1.0 Ma)

    Location: Hebei Province, northern China Dating: Oldest sites at ~2.1–1.7 Ma (paleomagnetic); Xiaochangliang ~1.36 Ma; Majuangou ~1.66 Ma; Donggutuo ~1.1 Ma Evidence: Oldowan-like core-and-flake industries; no handaxes; small tools on local quartz and chert; no hominin fossils yet recovered Significance: Earliest evidence of hominin presence in East Asia north of the tropics; challenges simple out-of-Africa dispersal models Sources: Zhu et al. 2018; Ao et al. 2013 Contested Points: Some early dates questioned; hominin identity unknown (no fossils)

    Yuanmou (~1.7 Ma)

    Location: Yunnan Province, southwestern China Dating: ~1.7 Ma (paleomagnetic, contested); some argue younger (~0.6–0.7 Ma) Evidence: Two Homo erectus incisors; associated artifacts; mammalian fauna Significance: Among earliest hominin fossils in China if older dates accepted Sources: Zhu et al. 2008; Hyodo et al. 2002 Contested Points: Dating highly contested between 1.7 Ma and 0.6 Ma

    Lantian (~1.15–0.65 Ma)

    Location: Shaanxi Province, Wei River Valley Dating: Gongwangling cranium ~1.15 Ma; Chenjiawo mandible ~0.65 Ma (paleomagnetic) Evidence: Homo erectus cranium (Gongwangling) with thick vault and supraorbital torus; mandible (Chenjiawo); associated fauna and simple flake tools Significance: Key early Homo erectus from loess plateau margin Sources: An & Ho 1989; Zhu et al. 2015 Contested Points: Gongwangling dating debated; some propose younger ages

    Bose Basin (~0.8 Ma)

    Location: Guangxi Province, southern China Dating: ~0.8 Ma (association with Australasian tektites) Evidence: Large cutting tools (LCTs) including picks and handaxe-like forms; distinct from typical East Asian "chopper-chopping tool" tradition Significance: Challenges "Movius Line" concept; shows Acheulean-like technology in East Asia Sources: Yamei et al. 2000; Wang et al. 2014 Contested Points: Whether true Acheulean or convergent technology; tektite association dating

    Zhoukoudian Locality 1 (~0.77–0.4 Ma)

    Location: Near Beijing, northern China Dating: ~770–400 ka (cosmogenic, U-series, paleomagnetic) Evidence: Multiple Homo erectus individuals (Peking Man); thick deposits with burnt bone and sediment suggesting fire use; faunal remains; simple flake tools; cave occupation Significance: Most extensive East Asian Homo erectus sample; fire use evidence (contested); long occupation sequence Sources: Shen et al. 2009; Goldberg et al. 2001 Contested Points: Fire use debated (some argue natural burning); original fossils lost in WWII

    Hexian (~0.4–0.3 Ma)

    Location: Anhui Province, eastern China Dating: ~412 ka (U-series on animal teeth) Evidence: Homo erectus calvaria with some progressive features; associated fauna Significance: Late Homo erectus showing possible transitional morphology Sources: Wu & Poirier 1995; Grün et al. 1998 Contested Points: Taxonomic assignment debated (late erectus vs. archaic sapiens)

    Dali (~0.26–0.2 Ma)

    Location: Shaanxi Province, northern China Dating: ~260–200 ka (U-series, contested) Evidence: Nearly complete cranium with mosaic features; large brain size (~1120 cc); archaic vault but more modern face Significance: Key specimen for understanding East Asian archaic Homo sapiens; possible Denisovan affinity suggested Sources: Wu 1981; Athreya & Wu 2017 Contested Points: Dating imprecise; taxonomic assignment varies (H. heidelbergensis, archaic H. sapiens, Denisovan)

    Maba (~0.13–0.12 Ma)

    Location: Guangdong Province, southern China Dating: ~130–120 ka (U-series) Evidence: Partial cranium with archaic features; cave context; associated fauna Significance: Archaic Homo sapiens in southern China during Last Interglacial Sources: Wu & Poirier 1995; Shen et al. 2002 Contested Points: Fragmentary; taxonomic assignment uncertain

    Tianyuan Cave (~40 ka)

    Location: Near Beijing, northern China Dating: ~40 ka (AMS radiocarbon) Evidence: Early modern human skeleton; DNA indicates modern human ancestry with some archaic admixture Significance: Among earliest securely dated modern humans in northern China Sources: Shang et al. 2007; Fu et al. 2013 Contested Points: Represents arrival of modern humans, but earlier presence possible

    Paleoenvironment

    Yellow River Basin: Loess plateau and alluvial plains; seasonal monsoon climate; temperate deciduous forest and grassland mosaic Yangtze River Basin: More subtropical; mixed evergreen-deciduous forest; wetter conditions Climate Oscillations: Glacial-interglacial cycles with monsoon intensification during interglacials; colder/drier glacials Sources: An et al. 2001; Sun et al. 2006

    Faunal Context

    Early Pleistocene (Nihewan fauna): Equus sanmeniensis, Proboscidipparion, Gazella; temperate grassland indicators Middle Pleistocene (Zhoukoudian): Crocuta, Ursus, Cervus, Sus; mixed forest-grassland Late Middle Pleistocene: Gradual modernization; appearance of extant species Sources: Tong 2007; Norton & Gao 2008
