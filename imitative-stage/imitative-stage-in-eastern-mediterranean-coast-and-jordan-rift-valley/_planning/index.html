    Planning: Imitative in The Eastern Mediterranean Coast and Jordan Rift Valley — 1,500,000 BCE – 50,000 BCE

    Age Constraint (FIXED)

    Imitative: The substrate of public life before language: everything a group knows held in living heads and passed only by showing and copying, hand guiding hand — dominant by default until speech becomes the primary medium.

    This constraint governs every article: knowledge transmission is strictly imitative (gestural, demonstrative), not linguistic. The Informational domain must reflect this — no symbolic encoding, no speech-based transmission. All cultural learning proceeds through observation and emulation.

    Shared Evidence Base

    'Ubeidiya (1.5–1.2 Ma)

    Location: Southern Jordan Valley, ~3 km south of Lake Kinneret outlet, Israel Dating: 1.5–1.2 Ma (paleomagnetic, biostratigraphy) Evidence: >10,000 artifacts including handaxes, spheroids, choppers; African fauna (Hippopotamus, Kolpochoerus, Megantereon); fluvio-lacustrine deposits Hominin Association: No hominin fossils; attributed to Homo erectus/ergaster based on technology and African affinity Sources: Bar-Yosef & Goren-Inbar 1993; Belmaker 2010 Contested Points: Exact dating remains debated; some propose extension to 1.6 Ma

    Early Acheulean Sites (1.2–0.8 Ma)

    Key Sites: Evron Quarry, Latamne (Syria), various Jordan Valley localities Evidence: Handaxe-dominated assemblages; large flake blanks; minimal Levallois Pattern: Northward spread from 'Ubeidiya; open-air lakeside and riverside occupations Sources: Goren-Inbar 1995; Hours 1981 Contested Points: Limited dating control at many sites

    Gesher Benot Ya'aqov (GBY) (~780 ka)

    Location: Northern Jordan Valley, Lake Hula basin, Israel Dating: ~780 ka (magnetostratigraphy, 40Ar/39Ar) Evidence: Refined Acheulean (handaxes, cleavers); waterlogged deposits preserving wood, seeds, and plant remains; burnt flint, wood, and seeds documenting fire use; basalt bifaces; fish, nuts, and fruit exploitation Significance: Earliest systematic fire use outside Africa; exceptional organic preservation Sources: Goren-Inbar et al. 2004; Alperson-Afil & Goren-Inbar 2010 Contested Points: Whether fire use is habitual or opportunistic (consensus now favors habitual)

    Qesem Cave (420–200 ka)

    Location: Central Israel, western foothills Dating: 420–200 ka (U-Th, ESR-U) Evidence: Acheulo-Yabrudian industry; systematic blade production; habitual fire use in a central hearth; intensive marrow extraction; possibly hafted tools; teeth possibly attributed to early H. sapiens (contested) Significance: Transitional complex bridging Acheulean and Middle Paleolithic; earliest routine fire use for cooking in the region Sources: Barkai et al. 2017; Gopher et al. 2010; Shahack-Gross et al. 2014 Contested Points: Hominin attribution (some teeth suggested as H. sapiens, debated); relationship to Yabrudian/Amudian industries

    Tabun Cave (>400 ka – 45 ka)

    Location: Mount Carmel, Israel Dating: Deep sequence from >400 ka to ~45 ka (Units XI through I) Evidence: Complete Acheulean-to-Mousterian transition; Tabun C1 Neanderthal skeleton (~120 ka, contested); Tabun C2 mandible; long Mousterian sequence Significance: Type site for Levantine Middle Paleolithic chronology Sources: Jelinek 1982; Mercier et al. 1995; Grün & Stringer 2000 Contested Points: Dating of Tabun C1 (ranges from 170 ka to 90 ka in different studies); taxonomic assignment of C1

    Misliya Cave (~185 ka)

    Location: Mount Carmel, Israel Dating: ~185 ka (U-Th, combined ESR-U) Evidence: Partial maxilla (Misliya-1) with anatomically modern human features; Upper Levallois-Mousterian industry Significance: Earliest H. sapiens fossil outside Africa; pushes out-of-Africa dispersal back significantly Sources: Hershkovitz et al. 2018 Contested Points: Single specimen; some question definitive H. sapiens attribution

    Skhul and Qafzeh Caves (~130–90 ka)

    Locations: Skhul (Mount Carmel); Qafzeh (near Nazareth), Israel Dating: Skhul ~130–100 ka; Qafzeh ~120–90 ka (ESR, TL) Evidence: Multiple anatomically modern human burials (Skhul: ~10 individuals; Qafzeh: ~27 individuals); grave goods (Qafzeh ochre, Skhul mandible with boar tusk); Mousterian industry; intentional burial postures Significance: Earliest definitive intentional burials; early H. sapiens expansion during Last Interglacial Sources: Vandermeersch 1981; Bar-Yosef & Vandermeersch 1993; Grün et al. 2005 Contested Points: Whether expansion was sustained or terminated; relationship to later populations

    Neanderthal Sites (90–50 ka)

    Key Sites: Amud Cave, Kebara Cave, Tabun (upper units), Dederiyeh (Syria) Dating: Amud 1 ~53 ka; Kebara 2 (KMH2) ~60 ka Evidence: Neanderthal skeletal remains; Kebara 2 is most complete Neanderthal torso known; hyoid bone indicating vocal tract anatomy; Mousterian industry throughout; some burials Pattern: Neanderthal reoccupation following H. sapiens withdrawal; continuous Mousterian technology Sources: Arensburg et al. 1989; Hovers et al. 1995; Bar-Yosef 1998 Contested Points: Whether Neanderthals and H. sapiens ever overlapped in the Levant; directionality of population movements

    Paleoenvironment

    Jordan Rift Valley: Tectonic graben; lakes at various levels; north-south corridor connecting Africa to Eurasia Climate Oscillations: Glacial-interglacial cycles (MIS 5e, MIS 4, MIS 3); Last Interglacial (~130–115 ka) brought warm, wet conditions favorable to H. sapiens expansion; MIS 4 cooling associated with Neanderthal return Vegetation: Mediterranean woodland on coast and highlands; steppe in interior; variable with climate Sources: Bar-Yosef 1998; Frumkin et al. 2011

    Faunal Context

    Lower Pleistocene: African affinity fauna (Hippopotamus, Kolpochoerus) indicating open corridor Middle–Upper Pleistocene: Palearctic fauna; fallow deer (Dama), aurochs, gazelle, red deer Megafauna: Extinct by late Middle Pleistocene; Elephas present at GBY Sources: Belmaker 2010; Marder et al. 2011
