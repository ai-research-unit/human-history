    Planning Document: Imitative in The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain

    Context

    • Region: The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain
    • Age: Imitative — The substrate of public life before language: everything a group knows held in living heads and passed only by showing and copying, hand guiding hand — dominant by default until speech becomes the primary medium.
    • Window: 1,500,000 BCE – 55,000 BCE
    • Target Article Count: 7–11 (soft target; attestation and significance are hard constraints)

    Shared Evidence Base

    I. CHRONOLOGICAL AND DATING FRAMEWORK

    Absolute Dating Methods Employed: - Cosmogenic nuclide burial dating (²⁶Al/¹⁰Be): Attirampakkam Early Acheulean (1.51 ± 0.07 Ma; Pappu et al. 2011) - Post-infrared infrared stimulated luminescence (pIR-IRSL): Attirampakkam MP transition (385 ± 64 ka; Pappu et al. 2018) - Optically stimulated luminescence (OSL): Jwalapuram (77 ± 6 ka; Petraglia et al. 2007) - Electron spin resonance (ESR): Isampur (730 ± 100 ka; Paddayya et al. 2002) - Uranium-thorium (²³⁰Th/²³⁴U): Chirki-Nevasa (>350–400 ka; Karve-Corvinus) - Paleomagnetic correlation and faunal association: Didwana (~800 ka)

    Era Suffixes: All dates in this window are BCE; no CE dates.

    II. KEY ARCHAEOLOGICAL SITES

    A. South India

    Attirampakkam (Tamil Nadu) - Location: Kortallayar River basin, ~60 km NW of Chennai - Discovery: Robert Bruce Foote, 1863; systematic excavation by S. Pappu - Dating: 1.51 ± 0.07 Ma (earliest Acheulean); 385 ± 64 ka (MP transition); 172 ± 41 ka (late MP) - Significance: Type site for "Madrasian" industry; earliest confirmed Acheulean outside Africa; earliest documented MP transition in South Asia - Tool types: Large handaxes and cleavers (early); Levallois flakes, blades, small scrapers (late) - Sources: Pappu et al. 2011, Science 331:1596–1599; Pappu et al. 2018, Nature 554:97–101

    Jwalapuram (Andhra Pradesh) - Location: Kurnool District, Jurreru River valley - Dating: 80–38 ka; pre-Toba levels ~77 ± 6 ka; post-Toba levels ~74 ka - Significance: Middle Paleolithic occupation surface sealed by Toba ash; critical for human survival debates - Tool types: Scrapers, Levallois technology - Sources: Petraglia et al. 2007, Science 317:114–116

    B. Karnataka Plateau

    Hunsgi-Baichbal Valleys / Isampur - Location: Northern Karnataka, Deccan Plateau - Discovery: K. Paddayya and colleagues, 1970s–2000s - Dating: ESR 730 ± 100 ka; ²³⁰Th/²³⁴U 174 ka to >350 ka - Significance: >200 Acheulean sites; Isampur is a quarry-cum-workshop preserving all stages of tool manufacture; >15,000 artifacts - Raw material: Siliceous limestone - Environmental context: Perennial seep springs enabled continuous occupation; seasonal mobility inferred - Sources: Paddayya et al. 2002, Current Science 83(5)

    C. Central India — Narmada Valley

    Hathnora / Narmada Valley (Madhya Pradesh) - Location: Central Narmada Valley near Hathnora village - Discovery: 1982 (calvarium); 2005–2010 (postcranial) - Fossil evidence: Partial calvarium, 2 clavicles, partial rib, 6 femora, 3 humeri, 2 sacra - Dating: ~250–300 ka (some estimates 500 ka based on fauna) — CONTESTED - Taxonomic status: Variously attributed to Homo erectus, archaic H. sapiens, H. heidelbergensis; best classified as Homo sp. (indeterminate) - Associated fauna: Stegodon namadicus, Palaeoloxodon namadicus, Sus namadicus - Associated archaeology: Late Acheulean tool kit - Sources: Sonakia 1984; Patnaik et al. 2009, Journal of Human Evolution

    Bhimbetka Rock Shelters (Madhya Pradesh) - Location: Raisen District, ~45 km SE of Bhopal - Dating: Lower Paleolithic (Acheulean) >100,000 years; continuous to historic - Significance: >750 rock shelters; UNESCO World Heritage Site (2003) - Tool types: Handaxes, cleavers, scrapers

    Adamgarh Hills (Madhya Pradesh) - Location: Narmadapuram District, northern Narmada bank - Discovery: Manoranjan Ghosh, 1922 - Dating: Lower Paleolithic through Neolithic - Significance: 18 rock shelters with continuous occupation

    D. Son Valley (North-Central India)

    Patpara / Middle Son Valley (Madhya Pradesh) - Location: NE Madhya Pradesh, between Kaimur Range and Baghelkhand Plateau - Dating: Late Acheulean 140–120 ka; Middle Paleolithic <103 ka - Stratigraphy: Sihawal Fm (oldest) → Patpara Fm → Baghor Fm → Khetaunhi Fm - Significance: World's youngest Acheulean (140–85 ka); mosaic of Acheulean and MP technologies - Tool types: Refined small bifaces, Levallois cores and flakes, scrapers - Sources: Haslam et al. 2011, Quaternary Research

    Dhaba (Middle Son Valley) - Dating: 80,000-year stratigraphic record - Significance: Evidence for human continuity across 74 ka Toba eruption - Sources: Clarkson et al. 2020, Nature Communications

    E. Western India — Rajasthan

    Didwana / Singi Talav (Rajasthan) - Location: Didwana-Kuchaman District - Discovery: S.N. Rajaguru and V.N. Misra, 1978–79 - Dating: Singi Talav deposits ~800 ka; Lower Paleolithic handaxes ~400–700 ka; Middle Paleolithic >150 ka; latest Acheulean ~85 ka - Climate record: Prolonged humid phase ~140–25 ka - Significance: "India's Olduvai Gorge"; complete Paleolithic sequence - Tool count: >300 Acheulean bifaces - Sources: Misra & Rajaguru; Singhvi et al.

    F. Northwestern Subcontinent — Siwalik Region

    Soan Valley / Soanian Industry (Pakistan/Northern India) - Location: Potwar Plateau, Siwalik Hills (Pakistan, Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Nepal) - Discovery: Helmut de Terra and T.T. Paterson, 1930s (Yale-Cambridge Expedition) - Dating: 500 ka – 125 ka (Soanian proper); earlier pebble tool claims contested - Significance: Type site for pebble-tool industry distinct from bifacial Acheulean; coeval parallel tradition - Tool types: Choppers, discoids, scrapers, cores, flakes from quartzite pebbles/cobbles - Raw material: Waterworn quartzite from Himalayan-derived outwash - Sources: de Terra & Paterson 1939; Dennell et al. 2008, Journal of Human Evolution

    Riwat (Pakistan) - Dating: Claimed ~2.0–2.5 Ma — HIGHLY CONTESTED - Status: Artifacts may not be in primary context; dating under active research - Sources: Dennell et al. 1988, Antiquity

    III. HOMININ PRESENCE

    Hominin Evidence Type Location Date (ka) Confidence
    Homo erectus or related Stone tools (Acheulean) Attirampakkam ~1500 HIGH
    Homo sp. (erectus/heidelbergensis/archaic sapiens) Fossil (cranium + postcranial) Narmada Valley 250–500 MODERATE (taxonomy debated)
    Unknown hominin Soanian pebble tools Siwalik region 500–125 HIGH (artifacts), LOW (maker identity)
    Unknown hominin Late Acheulean tools Son Valley 140–85 HIGH
    Unknown hominin (possibly H. sapiens) Middle Paleolithic tools Jwalapuram, Dhaba 80–55 HIGH (artifacts), CONTESTED (maker identity)

    Critical Gap: No human fossils from the Indian subcontinent securely dated between 55,000 BCE and 200,000 BCE; Middle Paleolithic makers cannot be identified to species with current evidence.

    IV. TECHNOLOGICAL PHASES

    Phase 1: Early Acheulean (~1,500,000–800,000 BCE) - Large Flake Acheulean (LFA) tradition - Handaxes typically >20 cm, cleavers made on large flakes - Hard hammer percussion - Distribution: South India (Attirampakkam), central Narmada, Deccan

    Phase 2: Middle/Late Acheulean (~800,000–100,000 BCE) - Refined handaxes and cleavers, increasing flake tool component - Soft hammer percussion emerges - Regional variation: Peninsular (classic LFA) vs. Siwalik (Soanian pebble tradition) - Persistence: Son Valley retains Acheulean until ~85,000 BCE — world's youngest

    Phase 3: Acheulean-to-Middle Paleolithic Transition (~385,000–170,000 BCE) - Documented at Attirampakkam (Pappu et al. 2018) - Key markers: - Declining biface frequency (from dominant to <10%) - Shrinking handaxe dimensions (mean length drops by ~40%) - Emergence of Levallois prepared-core technology - Increasing blade production - Shift to smaller, more portable tool kits - Contemporaneous with transitions in Africa (MSA) and Europe

    Phase 4: Middle Paleolithic (~170,000–55,000 BCE) - Levallois cores: preferential, recurrent, unidirectional, bidirectional - Points, scrapers, blades - "Nevasan" regional variant: fine-grained siliceous materials (agate, jasper, chalcedony) - Continuity through Toba (74 ka) documented at Jwalapuram, Dhaba

    V. CLIMATE AND ENVIRONMENT

    Pleistocene Glacial-Interglacial Framework (relevant Marine Isotope Stages):

    MIS Approximate Date Climate State Indian Conditions
    37–19 1,500,000–800,000 BCE Multiple glacial cycles Variable; monsoonal fluctuations
    12–11 478,000–374,000 BCE Glacial→Interglacial Transition
    6 191,000–130,000 BCE Glacial Cold, dry; reduced vegetation
    5e 130,000–115,000 BCE Last Interglacial Warm, wet; enhanced monsoon
    5d–5a 115,000–71,000 BCE Variable Fluctuating monsoon
    4 71,000–57,000 BCE Glacial Cold, dry; Toba eruption (74 ka)

    Key Environmental Points:

    1. Indian Summer Monsoon: Primary climate driver; strength varied with orbital forcing and North Atlantic conditions; enhanced monsoon correlates with expanded vegetation and lake levels.

    2. Humid Thar Phase (~140,000–25,000 BCE): Rajasthan (now desert) was a well-watered savanna with perennial lakes and rivers; rich fauna supported hominin occupation (Didwana-Singi Talav evidence).

    3. Toba Supereruption (74 ka / ~74,000 BCE):

    4. Volcanic Explosivity Index 8 (super-colossal)
    5. ~2,800 km³ dense rock equivalent ejected
    6. Ash deposited across India: >10 cm in Son Valley, >5 cm in Jwalapuram
    7. Proposed "volcanic winter" lasting 6–10 years; cooling ~3–5°C for 1,000 years
    8. Archaeological evidence: Middle Paleolithic tools above and below ash at Jwalapuram and Dhaba indicate population continuity — no occupational hiatus detected

    9. Contested point: Whether Toba caused population bottleneck remains debated; Indian archaeological record argues against catastrophic die-off

    10. Vegetation: C4 grasslands dominated during 75,000–25,000 BCE; C3 forests more extensive during interglacials.

    Megafauna: - Palaeoloxodon namadicus (Asian straight-tusked elephant): body mass ~22 tonnes - Stegodon namadicus (stegodon) - Hexaprotodon sp. (hippopotamus) - Equus namadicus (equid) - Extinction timing: Most survived until late Pleistocene/early Holocene (~30,000 years after modern human arrival); low extinction rate compared to other continents — possibly due to long hominin-megafauna coexistence.

    VI. CONTESTED POINTS AND UNCERTAINTIES

    1. Earliest Hominin Presence:
    2. Riwat (Pakistan) 2+ Ma claims remain unverified; artifacts may be geofacts or redeposited
    3. Pre-Acheulean/Oldowan presence NOT confirmed for Indian Peninsula

    4. Consensus: Earliest confirmed = 1.51 Ma at Attirampakkam

    5. Hathnora Hominin Identity:

    6. Assigned to H. erectus, H. heidelbergensis, or archaic H. sapiens by different researchers
    7. Current best practice: Classify as Homo sp. (indeterminate)

    8. Dating uncertain: 250–500 ka range

    9. Middle Paleolithic Makers:

    10. Were pre-Toba (>74 ka) Middle Paleolithic tools made by Homo sapiens or archaic hominins?
    11. African MSA similarity suggests possible H. sapiens (southern dispersal hypothesis)
    12. No fossils confirm species identity

    13. Contested: Could represent convergent evolution or cultural transmission

    14. Timing of Modern Human Arrival:

    15. Genetic evidence (mtDNA): ~70,000–55,000 BCE
    16. Archaeological claims: As early as 130,000–70,000 BCE based on MP technology

    17. No human fossils older than ~30,000 BCE found in India

    18. Soanian-Acheulean Relationship:

    19. Parallel coeval traditions, not sequential
    20. Different raw material economies (quartzite pebbles vs. larger blanks)

    21. Possibly different hominin populations or functional/environmental adaptations

    22. Toba Impact:

    23. "Volcanic winter" hypothesis vs. evidence for continuity
    24. Archaeological record suggests minimal disruption; genetic bottleneck remains debated
