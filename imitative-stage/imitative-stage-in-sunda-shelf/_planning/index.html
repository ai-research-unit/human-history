    Planning: Imitative in The Sunda Shelf — 1,600,000 BCE – 60,000 BCE

    Age Constraint (FIXED)

    Imitative: The substrate of public life before language: everything a group knows held in living heads and passed only by showing and copying, hand guiding hand — dominant by default until speech becomes the primary medium.

    This constraint governs every article: knowledge transmission is strictly imitative (gestural, demonstrative), not linguistic. The Informational domain must reflect this — no symbolic encoding, no speech-based transmission. All cultural learning proceeds through observation and emulation.

    Shared Evidence Base

    Sangiran (~1.6–0.9 Ma)

    Location: Sangiran dome, Central Java, Indonesia Dating: Oldest fossils ~1.6 Ma; sequence extends to ~0.9 Ma (40Ar/39Ar, paleomagnetic) Evidence: Over 100 Homo erectus specimens including crania (S17, Sangiran 2), mandibles, teeth; Pucangan and Kabuh Formation stratigraphy; associated Jetis and Trinil fauna Significance: Richest early Homo erectus site outside Africa; documents earliest Sunda colonization Sources: Swisher et al. 1994; Larick et al. 2001; Huffman 2001 Contested Points: Dating of earliest specimens debated (1.8 Ma claims not widely accepted); taxonomy (H. erectus vs. H. ergaster)

    Mojokerto (~1.5–1.4 Ma)

    Location: Perning, East Java, Indonesia Dating: ~1.49 Ma (40Ar/39Ar on pumice) Evidence: Juvenile Homo erectus calvaria; youngest definitively dated early Asian hominin Significance: Confirms early Pleistocene presence of hominins on Java Sources: Swisher et al. 1994; Morwood et al. 2003 Contested Points: Exact provenance of 1936 discovery; age estimates range from 1.0–1.8 Ma

    Trinil (~0.9–0.7 Ma)

    Location: Solo River, East Java, Indonesia Dating: ~0.9–0.7 Ma (biostratigraphy, revised chronology) Evidence: Original Pithecanthropus erectus calotte and femur (Dubois 1891); Trinil H.K. (Hauptknochenschicht) fauna; fluvial deposits Significance: Type locality for "Java Man"; foundational site for Asian Homo erectus studies Sources: Dubois 1894; de Vos et al. 1994; Joordens et al. 2015 Contested Points: Dating revised downward from original estimates; femur possibly more recent

    Sambungmacan (~200–100 ka)

    Location: Solo River basin, Java, Indonesia Dating: ~200–100 ka (uncertain; biostratigraphy) Evidence: Three Homo erectus crania (Sm 1, 3, 4); mosaic of archaic and derived features Significance: Documents late persistence of Homo erectus morphology Sources: Baba et al. 2003; Widianto & Zeitoun 2003 Contested Points: Dating imprecise; relationship to Ngandong hominins debated

    Ngandong (~120–50 ka)

    Location: Solo River terraces near Ngandong, Java, Indonesia Dating: Currently estimated ~117–108 ka (U-series, ESR) Evidence: 12–14 Homo erectus calvaria and 2 tibiae from terrace deposits; archaic morphology; no associated artifacts at original excavation Significance: Latest known Homo erectus population globally; extinction timing debates Sources: Swisher et al. 1996 (now revised); Rizal et al. 2020; Ciochon 2009 Contested Points: Dating highly contested (original 27–53 ka dates now rejected; current dates ~108–117 ka more accepted but still debated)

    Pacitanian Industry

    Location: Southern Java coast, Pacitan region Dating: Middle Pleistocene (precise dates lacking) Evidence: Large flake tools, choppers, handaxe-like forms; surface and stratified collections Significance: Characteristic Sunda stone tool tradition; different from African Acheulean Sources: Movius 1948; Bartstra 1985 Contested Points: Whether true industry or palimpsest; comparison to Acheulean debated; dating uncertain

    Tam Pa Ling (~70–46 ka)

    Location: Annamite Mountains, Laos Dating: ~70–46 ka (U-series, luminescence) Evidence: Partial Homo sapiens cranium and mandible; fully modern morphology Significance: Earliest securely dated modern human in mainland Southeast Asia Sources: Demeter et al. 2012, 2015 Contested Points: Dating at older end of range less secure

    Callao Cave (~67 ka, with older disputed levels)

    Location: Luzon, Philippines Dating: Homo luzonensis at ~67–50 ka; modern human metatarsal at ~67 ka Evidence: Small-bodied hominin (Homo luzonensis) distinct from erectus and sapiens; modern human bone Significance: Documents complex hominin diversity in the region Sources: Mijares et al. 2010; Détroit et al. 2019 Contested Points: Taxonomy of Homo luzonensis; route of arrival (Sunda connection vs. oceanic crossing)

    Toba Supervolcanic Eruption (~74 ka)

    Location: Sumatra, Indonesia Dating: ~74 ka (40Ar/39Ar) Evidence: Massive tephra deposits across South and Southeast Asia; Young Toba Tuff Significance: Potential population bottleneck; environmental devastation; debated impact on hominins Sources: Ambrose 1998; Williams et al. 2009 Contested Points: Severity of impact on hominin populations debated; some argue minimal effect

    Paleoenvironment

    Sunda Shelf: Exposed during glacial lowstands, connecting Java, Sumatra, Borneo to mainland; tropical rainforest and savanna mosaic; major rivers draining to South China Sea Sea Level Oscillations: Glacial cycles repeatedly exposed/submerged shelf; MIS 5e (130–115 ka) high stand fragmented landmass; MIS 4-2 lowstands reconnected islands Climate: Tropical; wetter interglacials, drier glacials; volcanic activity frequent Sources: Voris 2000; Bird et al. 2005; Sathiamurthy & Voris 2006

    Faunal Context

    Stegodon-Elephas Fauna: Characteristic Pleistocene megafauna; Stegodon, Elephas, Rhinoceros, Tapirus, Pongo Trinil Fauna: Middle Pleistocene assemblage; Axis, Bibos, Hippopotamus in addition to above Ngandong Fauna: Late Pleistocene; includes modern Asiatic fauna elements Sources: van den Bergh et al. 2001; Louys et al. 2007
