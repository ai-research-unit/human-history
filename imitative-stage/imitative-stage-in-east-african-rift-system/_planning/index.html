    Planning: Imitative in The East African Rift System — 3,300,000 BCE – 150,000 BCE

    Age Constraint (FIXED)

    Imitative: The substrate of public life before language: everything a group knows held in living heads and passed only by showing and copying, hand guiding hand — dominant by default until speech becomes the primary medium.

    This constraint governs every article: knowledge transmission is strictly imitative (gestural, demonstrative), not linguistic. The Informational domain must reflect this — no symbolic encoding, no speech-based transmission. All cultural learning proceeds through observation and emulation.

    Shared Evidence Base

    Lomekwian (3.3 Ma)

    Key Site: Lomekwi 3, West Turkana, Kenya Dating: 3.3 Ma (paleomagnetic, radiometric) Evidence: ~150 artifacts including cores, flakes, and anvils; passive hammer and bipolar techniques; no associated hominin remains but predates Homo Sources: Harmand et al. 2015 (Nature); Lewis & Harmand 2016 Contested Points: Maker identity uncertain; some debate over intentionality vs. natural fracture (largely resolved in favor of intentional manufacture)

    Early Oldowan (2.6–2.0 Ma)

    Key Sites: Gona (OGS-6, OGS-7, EG-10, EG-12), Ethiopia; Lokalalei 2C, Kenya; Fejej, Ethiopia Dating: Gona oldest at 2.58–2.55 Ma (40Ar/39Ar); Lokalalei 2.34 Ma Evidence: Core-and-flake assemblages; unifacial and bifacial cores; simple flaking with minimal retouch; associated fauna suggesting scavenging/early access to carcasses Hominin Association: Australopithecus garhi (Bouri, 2.5 Ma) proposed but not confirmed; Homo habilis appears ~2.3–2.1 Ma Sources: Semaw et al. 1997, 2003; Roche et al. 1999; de la Torre 2011 Contested Points: Exact maker identity; degree of planning vs. opportunistic reduction

    Olduvai Gorge Beds I–II (2.0–1.7 Ma)

    Key Sites: DK, FLK Zinj, FLK North, HWK East Dating: Bed I: 2.0–1.79 Ma; Bed II: 1.79–1.15 Ma (40Ar/39Ar, magnetostratigraphy) Evidence: Classic Oldowan choppers, polyhedrons, discoids; FLK Zinj "living floor" with Paranthropus boisei (OH 5) and Homo habilis (OH 7, OH 24); cutmarked bones; lakeside occupation pattern Sources: Leakey 1971; Blumenschine et al. 2003; Domínguez-Rodrigo et al. 2010 Contested Points: "Living floor" interpretation debated; passive scavenging vs. early hunting; competition between Paranthropus and Homo

    Homo ergaster/erectus (1.9–1.0 Ma)

    Key Specimens: KNM-WT 15000 (Nariokotome Boy, 1.53 Ma), KNM-ER 3733, KNM-ER 3883, KNM-ER 1808 Morphology: Tall stature (1.6–1.85 m adult), reduced sexual dimorphism, modern body proportions, cranial capacity 850–1100 cc, reduced dentition Evidence: Nariokotome near-complete skeleton; pathological specimens (1808 hypervitaminosis A); extended postcranial record Sources: Walker & Leakey 1993; Antón 2003; Spoor et al. 2007 Contested Points: ergaster vs. erectus taxonomy; timing of dispersal out of Africa

    Acheulean Emergence (1.76–1.4 Ma)

    Key Sites: Kokiselei 4 (West Turkana, 1.76 Ma); Konso (Ethiopia, 1.75 Ma); Olduvai Bed II (1.5 Ma) Dating: Oldest securely dated at 1.76 Ma (Lepre et al. 2011) Evidence: Large cutting tools (handaxes, cleavers, picks); bifacial shaping; larger blanks than Oldowan; initially co-occurs with Oldowan Maker: Homo ergaster/erectus Sources: Lepre et al. 2011; Beyene et al. 2013; Diez-Martín et al. 2015 Contested Points: Abrupt invention vs. gradual emergence; relationship to developed Oldowan

    Classic Acheulean (1.5 Ma – 500 ka)

    Key Sites: Olduvai Bed IV; Olorgesailie (lower members); Kariandusi; Kilombe; Isenya; Melka Kunture Evidence: Standardized handaxe forms; symmetry and refinement increase over time; large site accumulations (Olorgesailie); fauna includes Elephas recki, equids, bovids Characteristics: Remarkable technological stasis over ~1 Ma; minimal regional variation; "mental template" debate Sources: Isaac 1977; Potts 1989; Shipton 2018 Contested Points: Cognitive implications of symmetry; functional interpretations; why such long stasis

    Middle Pleistocene Hominins (600–300 ka)

    Key Specimens: Bodo cranium (Ethiopia, ~600 ka), Omo II (195 ka), Lake Ndutu (Tanzania), Broken Hill/Kabwe (Zambia, ~300 ka — outside Rift but comparative) Morphology: Cranial capacity 1200–1400 cc; Homo heidelbergensis/rhodesiensis grade; some archaic features retained Evidence: Bodo shows cutmarks (defleshing/scalping?); associated Late Acheulean Sources: Rightmire 1996; Stringer 2016; Grün et al. 2020 Contested Points: Species-level taxonomy; relationship to H. sapiens lineage; behavioral modernity markers

    Olorgesailie Transition (~320–295 ka)

    Key Site: Olorgesailie, Kenya (Member 1 through Oltulelei Formation) Dating: MSA transition dated to 320–305 ka (Potts et al. 2018) Evidence: Abrupt replacement of Acheulean by MSA points and pigments; associated with faunal turnover; environmental instability documented; obsidian transport from distant sources (25–50 km) Sources: Potts et al. 2018; Brooks et al. 2018 Contested Points: Causal mechanisms; rate of transition; presence during gap interval

    Early Homo sapiens (315–160 ka)

    Key Sites: Jebel Irhoud (Morocco, ~315 ka — outside Rift but earliest H. sapiens); Omo Kibish (Ethiopia, ~195 ka); Herto (Ethiopia, ~160–155 ka) Evidence: Omo I: anatomically modern cranium; Omo II: more archaic features (variation within population); Herto: H. sapiens idaltu with defleshed crania indicating mortuary practice Dating: Omo Kibish redated to 195 ± 5 ka (McDougall et al. 2005); Herto 160–155 ka Sources: McDougall et al. 2005; White et al. 2003; Hublin et al. 2017 Contested Points: Single vs. multiple origin centers; pace of anatomical transition; behavioral markers

    Middle Stone Age (320–150 ka within window)

    Key Sites: Olorgesailie, Baringo (Kapthurin Formation), Gademotta, Aduma, Porc-Epic Evidence: Prepared-core technology (Levallois, discoidal); hafted points; pigment use; long-distance raw material transport; smaller, more standardized tools than Acheulean Regional Variants: Different point styles across Rift sites; obsidian exploitation varies by proximity to sources Sources: McBrearty & Brooks 2000; Tryon & Faith 2013; Blinkhorn & Grove 2018 Contested Points: Behavioral modernity package; symbolic behavior evidence; connection to H. sapiens emergence

    Paleoenvironment

    Turkana Basin: Fluctuating lake levels; alternating wet/dry phases; open woodland to grassland mosaic Ethiopian Rift: Volcanic activity; lake systems (Awash drainage); varied elevation creates ecological diversity Tanzanian Rift: Olduvai paleolake; seasonal variation; Serengeti-type savanna developing Climate Drivers: Orbital forcing; African monsoon variability; East African climate particularly sensitive to precession cycles Sources: Potts 1998; Trauth et al. 2010; Maslin et al. 2014

    Faunal Context

    Lower Pleistocene: Elephas recki, Megantereon, Dinofelis, Theropithecus oswaldi, large bovids, equids Middle Pleistocene: Gradual turnover; megafauna decline; modern African fauna emerging Upper Pleistocene: Essentially modern fauna; human competition/predation effects debated Sources: Bobe & Behrensmeyer 2004; Faith 2014
