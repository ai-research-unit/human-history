    Planning Document — Broadcast Stage in The East African Rift, Congo Basin and Southern African Plateau (1945 CE – 2012 CE)

    Age Definition

    Broadcast Stage: One source reaches a multitude in the same instant: radio then television radiate to countless receivers who take it in together, wherever they are. A population can be addressed as one audience and held in step in real time, and public information life comes to run on the broadcast signal. Dominant until the digital substrate takes over.

    Window: 1945 CE – 2012 CE

    Region: The East African Rift, Congo Basin and Southern African Plateau — sub-Saharan Africa from Kenya to South Africa; including the Great Lakes region, Congo, Zimbabwe, and the diverse states from colonial transition through independence and post-Cold War transformation.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes colonial broadcasting archives, state broadcasting records, international shortwave recordings, and media studies. Colonial radio services from 1930s; independence-era state broadcasting; SABC under apartheid; Radio Rwanda; FM liberalization 1990s; mobile and digital from 2000s. Window begins with post-WWII colonial consolidation; ends as mobile phones and internet reshape communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Colonial and Independence (1945–1965 CE)

    Colonial Broadcasting: British, Belgian, Portuguese services; limited reach.

    Independence Wave: Ghana 1957; Kenya 1963; Tanzania 1961; Uganda 1962.

    State Broadcasting: New nations establish radio; television limited.

    Congo Crisis: 1960–1965; Radio Congo; UN intervention; instability.

    South Africa: SABC consolidated; apartheid broadcasting begins.

    Shortwave: BBC, VOA reach across region; Cold War context.

    2. Cold War Era (1965–1980 CE)

    One-Party States: Tanzania ujamaa; Kenya; Zaire Mobutu.

    Apartheid SABC: Bantu Radio; separate services; propaganda.

    Liberation Movements: Radio Freedom (ANC); SWAPO broadcasting; exile stations.

    Cold War Alignment: Soviet, Western influence; propaganda wars.

    Television Introduction: Urban centers; limited penetration.

    Rural Radio: Agricultural extension; development communication.

    3. Crises and Conflicts (1980–1994 CE)

    Zimbabwe Independence: 1980; ZBC; nation-building broadcasting.

    Apartheid Endgame: State of emergency; banned broadcasts; international pressure.

    Rwandan Genocide: 1994; RTLM hate radio; incitement role.

    DRC Transition: Mobutu decline; Zaire to DRC; fragmentation.

    South African Transition: Unbanning 1990; negotiations; election 1994.

    AIDS Crisis: Broadcasting response; health communication.

    4. Democratic Transitions (1994–2005 CE)

    South Africa Transformation: SABC restructuring; community radio.

    FM Liberalization: Private stations; commercial broadcasting.

    Kenya Pluralism: Multi-party era; private broadcasters.

    DRC War: 1996–2003; Radio Okapi (UN); peace broadcasting.

    Rwanda Recovery: State control; reconciliation broadcasting.

    Satellite Television: DStv; pan-African reach; urban audiences.

    5. Digital Beginnings (2005–2012 CE)

    Mobile Phones: Rapid penetration; SMS; mobile radio.

    Digital Migration: Planning; digital terrestrial preparation.

    Internet Growth: Urban access; cyber cafes; social media begins.

    Chinese Investment: Infrastructure; media engagement.

    Al Jazeera, CNN: International news channels; satellite reach.

    Community Radio: Expansion; local language services.

    6. South African Broadcasting (1945–2012 CE)

    SABC Origins: 1936; Afrikaner nationalism; English-Afrikaans.

    Bantu Radio: Separate ethnic services; divide and rule.

    Total Strategy: 1980s; propaganda intensification.

    Transformation: Post-1994; public broadcaster; political pressures.

    Commercial Sector: e.tv; MultiChoice; DStv dominance.

    Community Radio: Post-apartheid growth; local voices.

    7. Radio in Conflict (1945–2012 CE)

    Colonial Counterinsurgency: Kenya Emergency; Mau Mau; propaganda.

    Liberation Radios: ANC Radio Freedom; exile broadcasting.

    Rwanda RTLM: 1994; genocide incitement; "hate radio."

    Congo Conflicts: Armed group stations; UN Radio Okapi.

    Peace Radio: Post-conflict reconciliation; Studio Ijambo.

    International Standards: Post-Rwanda reflection; media responsibility.

    8. Vernacular Broadcasting (1945–2012 CE)

    Colonial Policy: Vernacular services; ethnic segmentation.

    Swahili: East Africa lingua franca; national broadcasting.

    South Africa: 11 official languages; linguistic diversity.

    Ethnic Politics: Broadcasting reinforcing or transcending division.

    FM Revolution: Local language stations; community radio.

    National Unity: Language policy debates; broadcasting role.
