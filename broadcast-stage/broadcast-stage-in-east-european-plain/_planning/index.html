    Planning Document — Broadcast Stage in The East European Plain (1930 CE – 2012 CE)

    Age Definition

    Broadcast Stage: One source reaches a multitude in the same instant: radio then television radiate to countless receivers who take it in together, wherever they are. A population can be addressed as one audience and held in step in real time, and public information life comes to run on the broadcast signal. Dominant until the digital substrate takes over.

    Window: 1930 CE – 2012 CE

    Region: The East European Plain — Russia and the former Soviet Union; from Stalinist consolidation through Cold War, collapse, and post-Soviet transformation.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes Soviet broadcasting archives, party documents, samizdat, foreign radio transcripts, and post-Soviet media records. Soviet radio centralized 1920s; television from 1930s; mass television 1960s; glasnost liberalization; post-Soviet privatization; Putin-era reconsolidation. Window begins with mature Soviet radio; ends as internet and social media challenge state broadcasting.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Stalinist Radio and WWII (1930–1945 CE)

    Radio Consolidation: All-Union Radio Committee; centralized control.

    Collectivization Coverage: Propaganda; dekulakization; famine concealment.

    Terror Period: 1936–1938; show trials; silence and propaganda.

    Great Patriotic War: 1941–1945; Levitan; wartime mobilization.

    Siege of Leningrad: Radio survival symbol; metronome broadcast.

    Victory: 1945; radio announcement; national trauma and triumph.

    2. Postwar and Television Dawn (1945–1964 CE)

    Late Stalinism: Cold War; anti-cosmopolitan campaign; control.

    Stalin's Death: 1953; radio announcement; uncertainty.

    Khrushchev Thaw: Limited liberalization; de-Stalinization.

    Television Introduction: Moscow TV 1938; expansion 1950s–1960s.

    Space Age: Sputnik 1957; Gagarin 1961; broadcast triumphs.

    Cuban Crisis: 1962; limited information; radio diplomacy.

    3. Brezhnev Era (1964–1985 CE)

    Stagnation: Economic slowdown; ideological exhaustion.

    Mass Television: Near-universal coverage; Vremya; centralized news.

    Normalized Propaganda: Ritualized; disbelieved but unavoidable.

    Foreign Radio: BBC, VOA, Radio Liberty; jamming; audience.

    Afghanistan War: 1979–1989; limited coverage; soldier testimony.

    Samizdat: Underground information; tape recordings; circulation.

    4. Glasnost and Collapse (1985–1991 CE)

    Gorbachev: Reform; perestroika; glasnost openness.

    Chernobyl: 1986; initial coverup; then unprecedented disclosure.

    Media Liberalization: Vzglyad; open debate; historical revelations.

    Nationalist Movements: Baltic; Ukraine; republics assert.

    August Coup: 1991; failed; media role; Yeltsin on tank.

    Soviet Dissolution: December 1991; broadcasting fragmentation.

    5. Post-Soviet Chaos (1991–2000 CE)

    Privatization: Media oligarchs; Berezovsky; Gusinsky; NTV.

    Media Pluralism: Independent journalism; investigative reporting.

    Chechen Wars: Coverage; NTV criticism; government anger.

    Economic Collapse: 1998 crisis; social disruption.

    Yeltsin Era: Erratic; media battles; election manipulation.

    Putin Succession: 1999; new direction anticipated.

    6. Putin Consolidation (2000–2012 CE)

    NTV Takeover: 2000–2001; Gazprom; state control restored.

    Vertical of Power: Centralized authority; managed democracy.

    Television Control: State channels dominant; opposition marginalized.

    Beslan: 2004; restricted coverage; security state.

    Internet Growth: RuNet; social media; new space.

    Protests 2011–2012: Bolotnaya; online mobilization; broadcast silence.

    7. Soviet Propaganda Model (1930–2012 CE)

    Agitprop Origins: Lenin; mobilization; education.

    Total Control: No alternative sources; single narrative.

    Ritualization: Brezhnev era; disbelieved but performative.

    Glasnost Break: Brief pluralism; historical importance.

    Putin Restoration: Updated control; not total but dominant.

    Legacy: Media-state relationship; audience cynicism.

    8. Foreign Radio (1930–2012 CE)

    Early Broadcasting: Limited shortwave reach; war years.

    Cold War Services: BBC Russian; VOA; Radio Liberty.

    Jamming: Expensive; partially effective; audience nonetheless.

    Glasnost Ending: Jamming stopped 1988; services continued.

    Post-Soviet Role: Reduced; internet transition; RFE/RL continues.

    Putin Era: Renewed tension; foreign agent laws; restrictions.

    9. Nationalities Broadcasting (1930–2012 CE)

    Soviet Structure: Republic radio; controlled diversity.

    Languages: Ukrainian, Baltic, Central Asian services.

    National Identity: Broadcasting as identity maintenance.

    Independence: 1991; national broadcasters emerge.

    Post-Soviet: Divergent paths; Baltic integration; Russian influence.

    Ukraine: Contested space; Russian-Ukrainian tensions.

    10. Wired Radio (1930–2012 CE)

    Radioточка: Wired speakers; apartment systems.

    State Control: Cannot be switched off; single channel.

    Mass Reach: Universal coverage; low cost; control.

    Persistence: Continued into 1990s; gradual decline.

    Television Transition: Supplemented then replaced wired radio.

    Infrastructure Legacy: Broadcast as state utility.
