    Planning Document — Broadcast Stage in The North American Eastern Woodlands and Mississippi Basin (1930 CE – 2005 CE)

    Age Definition

    Broadcast Stage: One source reaches a multitude in the same instant: radio then television radiate to countless receivers who take it in together, wherever they are. A population can be addressed as one audience and held in step in real time, and public information life comes to run on the broadcast signal. Dominant until the digital substrate takes over.

    Window: 1930 CE – 2005 CE

    Region: The North American Eastern Woodlands and Mississippi Basin — The United States and Canada from the Atlantic seaboard through the Great Lakes and Mississippi Valley; the commercial broadcasting model, network television, and cable/satellite transition.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes FCC archives, network records, broadcast journalism studies, and media scholarship. Commercial radio 1920s; network consolidation 1930s; television post-WWII; cable 1970s-1980s; satellite 1990s; digital transition 2000s. Window begins with mature radio networks; ends as internet and digital media reshape broadcasting.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Radio Golden Age (1930–1941 CE)

    Network Consolidation: NBC, CBS dominance; affiliate structure.

    Depression Programming: Entertainment escape; news; drama; comedy.

    FDR Fireside Chats: Presidential radio; direct communication.

    Advertising Model: Sponsor system; commercial broadcasting.

    News Development: War in Europe; Edward R. Murrow; foreign correspondents.

    Regulatory Framework: FCC 1934; spectrum allocation; licensing.

    2. World War II (1941–1948 CE)

    War Coverage: News dominance; correspondents; home front.

    Propaganda Role: War information; bond drives; morale.

    Technical Development: Electronics; radar; television pause.

    Postwar Planning: Television licenses; network preparation.

    Canadian Broadcasting: CBC wartime role; national unity.

    Talent Migration: Radio to television anticipation.

    3. Television Dawn (1948–1960 CE)

    Rapid Adoption: Set sales explosion; household penetration.

    Network Television: NBC, CBS, ABC; primetime dominance.

    McCarthyism: Army-McCarthy hearings; See It Now; Murrow.

    Quiz Show Scandals: 1958–1959; credibility crisis; regulation.

    Golden Age Drama: Live drama; Playhouse 90; creative peak.

    Suburban Culture: Television and postwar lifestyle.

    4. Social Transformation (1960–1968 CE)

    Kennedy-Nixon Debates: 1960; television impact; visual politics.

    Civil Rights Coverage: Birmingham; March on Washington; Selma.

    Assassination Broadcasting: JFK 1963; MLK, RFK 1968.

    Vietnam Coverage: Living room war; escalation; credibility.

    Cultural Programming: Beatles; youth culture; generational shift.

    Color Television: Mass adoption; visual transformation.

    5. Vietnam Era (1968–1980 CE)

    War Coverage: Tet; body counts; antiwar movement.

    Watergate: 1973–1974; hearings; Nixon resignation.

    Trust Decline: Media credibility; institutional questioning.

    Regulatory Debates: Fairness doctrine; cable beginnings.

    Miniseries: Roots; Holocaust; event television.

    News Evolution: Anchor era; Cronkite; Rather; Brokaw.

    6. Cable Revolution (1980–1991 CE)

    Cable Expansion: CATV growth; multiple channels; niche programming.

    CNN Launch: 1980; 24-hour news; Ted Turner.

    MTV: 1981; music television; youth programming.

    Reagan Era: Deregulation; media consolidation allowed.

    Gulf War: 1991; CNN moment; live war coverage.

    Network Decline: Cable competition; audience fragmentation begins.

    7. Fragmentation Era (1991–2001 CE)

    Multi-Channel Universe: Hundreds of channels; niche audiences.

    Media Consolidation: Telecommunications Act 1996; mergers.

    Fox Network: Fourth network; conservative news.

    Internet Emergence: Dial-up; news websites; competition anticipated.

    Clinton Coverage: Lewinsky scandal; 24-hour news saturation.

    Digital Preparation: HDTV standards; switchover planning.

    8. Post-9/11 (2001–2005 CE)

    September 11: Continuous coverage; national unity moment.

    War on Terror: Afghanistan; Iraq; embedded journalists.

    Political Polarization: Fox vs. CNN/MSNBC; partisan news.

    Digital Transition: Switchover deadlines; converter boxes.

    Internet Growth: Broadband; streaming; YouTube 2005.

    Media Trust: Declining confidence; partisan sources.

    9. Network System (1930–2005 CE)

    Structure: National networks; local affiliates; clearance.

    Programming: Primetime; daytime; news; sports.

    Competition: Three-network oligopoly; Fox fourth; cable erosion.

    Business Model: Advertising revenue; ratings currency.

    News Divisions: Prestige operations; anchor stars; declining profits.

    Decline: Cable, satellite, internet fragment audience.

    10. FCC Regulation (1930–2005 CE)

    Licensing: Spectrum allocation; station licensing; renewals.

    Fairness Doctrine: 1949–1987; balanced coverage requirement.

    Deregulation: Reagan era; ownership limits relaxed.

    Telecommunications Act: 1996; consolidation enabled.

    Digital Mandate: Switchover requirements; transition planning.

    Content Regulation: Indecency; violence; children's programming.

    11. Advertising Model (1930–2005 CE)

    Sponsorship: Single-sponsor shows; network sales.

    Ratings: Nielsen; audience measurement; advertising rates.

    30-Second Spot: Standard unit; scatter market; upfronts.

    Cable Advertising: Targeted audiences; lower rates; growth.

    Digital Challenges: DVR; ad-skipping; measurement problems.

    Political Advertising: Campaign spending; negative ads.

    12. Public Broadcasting (1930–2005 CE)

    Educational Origins: Noncommercial licenses; educational television.

    PBS Creation: 1970; national system; local stations.

    NPR: 1970; radio network; Morning Edition; All Things Considered.

    Funding Battles: Federal support; political pressure; underwriting.

    Programming: Children's; documentary; cultural; news.

    Digital Challenges: Multiple channels; online presence.

    13. Canadian Broadcasting (1930–2005 CE)

    CBC Creation: 1936; public broadcaster; national unity.

    Bilingual Service: English and French networks.

    Cultural Protection: Canadian content requirements; CRTC.

    Private Competition: CTV; Global; commercial networks.

    US Spillover: Border reception; cable imports; cultural debates.

    Digital Transition: Canadian standards; switchover planning.

    14. Broadcasting and Identity (1930–2005 CE)

    Shared Experience: Mass audience; national events; unity.

    Cultural Influence: American culture; global export; soft power.

    Regional Variation: Local stations; regional identity; national integration.

    Diversity Representation: Civil rights; minority programming; evolution.

    Fragmentation Impact: Niche audiences; shared experience decline.

    Canadian Identity: Cultural protection; US influence; distinctiveness.
