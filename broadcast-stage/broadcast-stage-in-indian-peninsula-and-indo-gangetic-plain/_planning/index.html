    Planning Document — Broadcast Stage in The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain (1960 CE – 2015 CE)

    Age Definition

    Broadcast Stage: One source reaches a multitude in the same instant: radio then television radiate to countless receivers who take it in together, wherever they are. A population can be addressed as one audience and held in step in real time, and public information life comes to run on the broadcast signal. Dominant until the digital substrate takes over.

    Window: 1960 CE – 2015 CE

    Region: The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain — India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, and Nepal; from state broadcasting monopolies through liberalization, satellite revolution, and digital transition.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes All India Radio archives, Doordarshan records, government policy documents, media studies, and broadcasting legislation. AIR established 1936; Doordarshan television 1959; SITE satellite experiment 1975-1976; Emergency media control 1975-1977; color television 1982; liberalization 1991; satellite/cable revolution 1990s; 24-hour news channels 2000s. Window begins with television expansion; ends as digital and internet reshape broadcasting.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Early Television and State Broadcasting (1960–1975 CE)

    Doordarshan Origins: Television experimental 1959; Delhi service; limited reach.

    AIR Dominance: All India Radio; national network; regional services.

    Development Communication: Agricultural programs; family planning; education.

    Nehruvian Vision: Broadcasting as development tool; nation-building.

    Limited Penetration: Urban centers; community viewing; high costs.

    Pakistan PTV: 1964 launch; separate development; state control.

    2. Emergency and SITE (1975–1984 CE)

    Emergency: 1975–1977; press censorship; radio/TV as government voice.

    SITE Experiment: 1975–1976; NASA satellite; 2400 villages; education.

    Post-Emergency: Press freedom restored; broadcasting remains state.

    INSAT Launch: 1983; Indian satellite; expanded coverage potential.

    Asian Games 1982: Color television introduced; mass set purchases.

    State Monopoly: No private broadcasting; Doordarshan sole provider.

    3. Color Television Era (1984–1991 CE)

    Rapid Expansion: Transmitter network; national coverage achieved.

    Ramayan Serial: 1987–1988; massive audiences; national phenomenon.

    Mahabharat Serial: 1988–1990; continued epic broadcasting success.

    Gulf War: 1991; CNN availability; appetite for satellite news.

    Economic Crisis: 1991; reforms begin; media liberalization anticipated.

    Regional Services: Doordarshan regional channels; language diversity.

    4. Liberalization and Satellite (1991–2000 CE)

    Economic Reforms: 1991; liberalization; foreign investment.

    Zee TV Launch: 1992; first private Hindi channel; cable distribution.

    Star TV Entry: Hong Kong-based; pan-Asian reach; foreign content.

    Cable Explosion: Informal networks; rapid urban penetration.

    News Channels: NDTV produces for Star; journalism evolution.

    Regulatory Chaos: Cable TV Act 1995; enforcement challenges.

    5. News Proliferation (2000–2008 CE)

    24-Hour News: Aaj Tak 2000; NDTV 24x7 2003; competitive landscape.

    Regional News: Telugu, Tamil, Marathi channels; vernacular expansion.

    Political Coverage: Election broadcasting; sting operations; activism.

    Entertainment Expansion: Reality shows; game shows; format imports.

    DTH Launch: Dish TV 2003; direct-to-home; rural potential.

    Media Conglomerates: Corporate ownership; cross-media holdings.

    6. Digital Transition (2008–2015 CE)

    Mobile TV: 3G licensing 2010; mobile video consumption.

    IPL Cricket: 2008 launch; broadcasting rights; sports transformation.

    Social Media: Facebook, Twitter adoption; second-screen viewing.

    News Debates: Aggressive formats; political polarization on screen.

    Digitization Mandate: Cable digitization phases; addressable systems.

    Convergence: Online streaming begins; YouTube penetration.

    7. All India Radio (1960–2015 CE)

    Network Scale: Hundreds of stations; near-universal coverage.

    Language Services: Major and minor languages; tribal services.

    Vividh Bharati: Commercial service; film songs; advertising.

    Rural Reach: Farm broadcasts; community listening; development.

    FM Revolution: Private FM 2000s; urban entertainment; limited news.

    Digital Radio: DRM trials; limited adoption; internet streaming.

    8. Pakistan and Bangladesh (1960–2015 CE)

    PTV Pakistan: State broadcaster; Urdu dominance; limited private.

    Bangladesh BTV: 1964 as Pakistan; post-1971 independence; state control.

    Military Periods: Broadcast control; propaganda; censorship.

    Private Entry: Pakistan 2000s; Geo TV; competitive news.

    Cross-Border: Indian channels watched; cultural influence debates.

    Sri Lanka: SLRC; ethnic conflict coverage; Tamil services.

    9. Cinema-Television Symbiosis (1960–2015 CE)

    Film Songs: Radio primary music source; Binaca Geetmala countdown.

    Chitrahaar: Television film song program; weekly event.

    Star System: Film stars to television; crossover audiences.

    Film Broadcasting: Sunday Hindi film; family viewing tradition.

    Bollywood Channels: Dedicated movie channels; archive exploitation.

    Reality Shows: Film industry tie-ins; talent discovery.
