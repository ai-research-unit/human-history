    Planning Document — Broadcast Stage in The West African Sahel and Niger Basin (1955 CE – 2018 CE)

    Age Definition

    Broadcast Stage: One source reaches a multitude in the same instant: radio then television radiate to countless receivers who take it in together, wherever they are. A population can be addressed as one audience and held in step in real time, and public information life comes to run on the broadcast signal. Dominant until the digital substrate takes over.

    Window: 1955 CE – 2018 CE

    Region: The West African Sahel and Niger Basin — Francophone West Africa (Senegal, Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso, Côte d'Ivoire) and Anglophone Ghana and Nigeria; from late colonial broadcasting through independence, one-party states, democratic transitions, and mobile revolution.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes colonial broadcasting archives, national radio records, ORTF/RFI archives, media studies, and development communication research. Colonial radio services; independence-era state broadcasting; one-party control; structural adjustment; democratic transitions 1990s; FM liberalization; mobile penetration 2000s-2010s. Window begins with pre-independence consolidation; ends as mobile phones and internet reshape communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Colonial and Independence (1955–1970 CE)

    Colonial Radio: French and British services; limited reach; colonial languages.

    Independence Wave: Ghana 1957; Nigeria 1960; Senegal, Mali, others 1960.

    State Broadcasting: New nations establish radio; nation-building mission.

    French Influence: ORTF training; equipment; programming ties.

    Nigerian Scale: Federal structure; regional services; NTA television.

    Limited Television: Urban only; radio primary medium.

    2. One-Party Era (1970–1990 CE)

    Single-Party Control: State monopoly; propaganda; censorship.

    Development Communication: Rural radio; agricultural extension; health.

    Military Coups: Nigeria cycles; radio as announcement medium.

    External Services: BBC, VOA, RFI; alternative information.

    Structural Adjustment: 1980s; economic crisis; broadcasting budget cuts.

    Television Expansion: Limited; urban centers; state control.

    3. Democratic Transitions (1990–2005 CE)

    Multi-Party Politics: National conferences; elections; media debates.

    Private FM: Licensing opens; commercial stations; urban focus.

    Press Freedom: Gradual expansion; journalist struggles; limited independence.

    International Support: Development aid; media training; NGO stations.

    Nigeria Transition: 1999; fourth republic; media expansion.

    Regional Conflicts: Sierra Leone, Liberia; radio in war.

    4. Mobile Revolution (2005–2018 CE)

    Mobile Penetration: Rapid growth; SMS; mobile money.

    FM Proliferation: Hundreds of stations; local languages; community radio.

    Internet Beginnings: Urban access; social media emergence.

    Sahel Conflicts: Mali 2012; jihadist threat; information warfare.

    Digital Transition: Planning begins; limited implementation.

    Nollywood: Nigerian video; television distribution; continental reach.

    5. Nigerian Broadcasting (1955–2018 CE)

    Federal Structure: Regional services; NTA national; state stations.

    Commercial Radio: Licensed 1990s; urban growth; advertising.

    Television: NTA; AIT; Channels; competitive market.

    Nollywood Connection: Video industry; television programming.

    Regional Influence: Nigerian content across West Africa.

    Digital Transition: DSO planning; partial implementation.

    6. Francophone Broadcasting (1955–2018 CE)

    ORTF Legacy: French training; equipment; programming models.

    RFI Reach: Radio France Internationale; French news; influence.

    Language Policy: French official; local languages gradual.

    OCORA/CFI: French broadcasting cooperation; technical assistance.

    Television: Limited reach; state monopolies; Canal+ Afrique.

    Cultural Ties: Francophonie; broadcasting integration.

    7. Rural Radio (1955–2018 CE)

    Agricultural Extension: Farm broadcasts; weather; markets.

    Health Communication: Family planning; disease prevention; campaigns.

    Literacy Programs: Educational radio; adult education.

    Community Radio: Local languages; participatory; development.

    Rural Development: Radio as development tool; donor support.

    Mobile Displacement: Phone overtaking radio for information.

    8. Music and Culture (1955–2018 CE)

    Highlife: Ghanaian; Nigerian; radio popularization.

    Afrobeat: Fela Kuti; political music; radio resistance.

    Griot Tradition: Mande; radio adaptation; cultural preservation.

    Regional Styles: Mbalax; jùjú; chimurenga; broadcast spread.

    Music Industry: Radio promotional role; recording links.

    Identity Formation: Music broadcasting reinforcing cultural identities.
