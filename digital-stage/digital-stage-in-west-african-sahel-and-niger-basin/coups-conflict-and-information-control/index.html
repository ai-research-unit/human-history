    2022 CE – 2026 CE — Coups, Conflict, and Information Control

    Summary: A cascade of military coups reshapes the Sahelian information environment as Niger (2023) joins Mali and Burkina Faso under junta rule, French forces withdraw and Russian/Wagner influence advances, while Nigeria's 2023 election and subsequent economic reforms under Tinubu demonstrate the parallel trajectory of the region's democratic anchor — the binding constraint being the contest between military regimes seeking information control and the decentralized digital infrastructure that enables both resistance and manipulation.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Sahel Alliance (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger) consolidates as a landlocked bloc dependent on coastal access through Nigeria, Benin, and Togo; ECOWAS sanctions and threatened intervention reshape transit routes; Nigerian infrastructure remains the regional backbone with Lagos data centers serving continental traffic.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Sahel drought conditions intensify displacement pressures; climate-conflict nexus drives population movements toward connected urban areas; flooding in Nigeria (2022) damages infrastructure; energy transition pressures begin to affect fossil fuel-dependent economies.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Jihadist violence persists across the Sahel despite (or enabled by) Wagner involvement; northeast Nigeria remains insecure; coups create immediate political instability while promising eventual order; infrastructure in conflict zones remains degraded or destroyed.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Niger's uranium resources gain strategic importance amid Russia-Europe tensions; Nigerian oil revenues decline with global energy transition concerns; gold mining in Mali and Burkina Faso funds junta operations; mineral extraction increasingly digitally coordinated.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: E-waste volumes grow with accelerating device turnover; environmental governance weakens under military rule; artisanal mining expands environmental damage in gold-producing areas; urban air quality deteriorates with generator dependence.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: COVID-19 recedes as a primary concern by 2023; endemic diseases persist; conflict-related mortality continues in Sahel zones; healthcare systems under pressure from displacement and governance breakdown.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Food insecurity intensifies in Sahel due to conflict, climate, and sanctions; Nigerian fuel subsidy removal (2023) raises food costs; humanitarian response increasingly coordinated through digital platforms.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Telemedicine expands in Nigeria while Sahel healthcare collapses in conflict zones; diaspora remittances fund private healthcare access; traditional healing persists alongside limited formal care.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Cross-border family connections disrupted by sanctions and border closures; digital communication maintains ties despite physical separation; migration patterns reshape family formation.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Nigeria approaches 230 million by 2026; youth bulge intensifies pressure for economic opportunity; Sahel displacement creates refugee concentrations; urban density continues to rise in connected cities.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Conflict creates orphan and widow populations requiring support; economic disruption increases dependency burdens; diaspora remittances partially offset but cannot replace functional governance.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Naira devaluation and fuel subsidy removal (2023) severely impact Nigerian purchasing power; Sahel populations face sanctions-related shortages; mobile money becomes essential for basic transactions; informal economy adapts to scarcity.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Nigerian electricity crisis continues despite reforms; solar adoption accelerates for off-grid power; Sahel energy infrastructure degrades; data center power costs rise; diesel generator dependence persists.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Nigerian tech sector continues growth despite economic challenges; brain drain accelerates as skilled workers emigrate; gig economy expands; Sahel labor markets disrupted by conflict and sanctions.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Nigerian fintech continues to attract investment though at reduced pace; economic uncertainty dampens startup formation; Sahel productive capacity contracts under military rule; artisanal mining fills economic vacuum.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Mobile money adoption accelerates in Nigeria following PSB expansion; savings erode with inflation; foreign reserves under pressure; household buffering capacity stretched thin.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: ECOWAS sanctions on Sahel states disrupt regional trade; Nigeria-Niger border remains tense after coup; platform commerce adapts to currency instability; cryptocurrency usage grows as store of value and transfer mechanism.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Naira volatility drives dollarization and crypto adoption; multiple exchange rates create arbitrage opportunities; fintech lending tightens; remittance flows remain critical for household survival.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Tinubu reforms attempt to capture informal economy revenue; tax digitization expands; junta revenues from mining and Russian support; aid flows disrupted by political conditions.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Economic volatility widens inequality; asset holders seek foreign currency and offshore protection; middle class shrinks with purchasing power erosion; wealth concentrates in those with dollar access.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Environmental regulation weakens under economic pressure and military rule; mining waste accumulates; e-waste processing remains informal; carbon accounting absent.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Borders divide families as Sahel Alliance separates from ECOWAS; digital communication maintains cross-border kin networks; diaspora connections intensify as emigration accelerates; remittance obligations strain those abroad.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Digital mutual aid networks activate during economic crises; online communities provide information and support; civil society organizations face pressure in coup states; professional networks maintain cross-border connections.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Social media remains primary leisure space; streaming entertainment expands; gaming grows with improved devices; festival and social gatherings disrupted by economic constraints and security concerns.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Military-civilian divide sharpens in coup states; ethnic tensions instrumentalized by political actors; generational divide persists between digital natives and elders; tech elite maintain status despite economic headwinds.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Nigerian 2023 election demonstrates contested but functional democracy; Tinubu government pursues reforms amid legitimacy questions; junta administrations vary in capacity from Mali's relative stability to Burkina Faso's continued violence; ECOWAS credibility damaged by coup wave and failed intervention.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Wagner presence in Mali expands to Burkina Faso; Niger coup tests ECOWAS intervention capacity (threat not executed); Nigerian military faces jihadist threat while avoiding coup temptation; security force social media monitoring continues.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: 2023 Nigerian election contentious but violence limited; Sahel violence continues with Wagner unable to suppress insurgency; banditry in northwest Nigeria intensifies; state capacity erosion creates governance vacuums filled by armed groups and local strongmen.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Junta leaders (Goita, Traore, Tiani) cultivate social media personas as anti-colonial champions; Tinubu struggles to establish authority amid legitimacy questions; Senegal's Faye represents generational change with 2024 victory; Obi maintains digital following despite electoral loss.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Coup governments claim popular support while suppressing dissent; Nigerian democracy retains legitimacy despite electoral controversies; regional institutions (ECOWAS, AU) lose credibility; external actors (France, Russia) compete for narrative dominance.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Sahel Alliance consolidates as bloc against ECOWAS; Nigerian government pursues painful reforms despite political cost; youth energy channels into migration rather than domestic activism; collective action capacity dispersed.

    External Relations & Posture: France expelled from Sahel; Russian influence expands through Wagner and information operations; US maintains presence but reduced footprint; ECOWAS sanctions create regional tensions; Sahel Alliance announces ECOWAS exit (2024).

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Anti-French sentiment peaks with military takeovers; Russian flags appear at pro-coup rallies; Nigerian public sentiment mixes frustration with economic hardship and hope for reform; youth disillusionment deepens across the region.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Post-reform exhaustion in Nigeria; resignation to military rule in Sahel absent viable alternatives; emigration as exit rather than voice; activist burnout following failed movements; hope concentrated in diaspora success.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Nigeria internet penetration reaches ~55% by 2026; francophone Sahel stagnates at 20-30%; language fragmentation persists; offline populations remain majority in rural Sahel; smartphone costs limit adoption among poor.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: 4G consolidates as dominant standard; 5G expands slowly in Nigerian cities; submarine cable capacity grows; Sahel connectivity depends on satellite and limited fiber; WhatsApp, Facebook, TikTok dominate engagement.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Platform archives document coups and protests; content moderation removes some material; state attempts to control narrative face distributed documentation; oral circulation supplements digital record.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Internet shutdowns accompany Niger coup (2023); RFI/France 24 remain suspended in Mali and Burkina Faso; Russian state media and affiliated accounts amplify pro-junta narratives; Nigerian government maintains Twitter presence after ban lifted (2022).

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Pro-coup content achieves viral reach in Sahel; Russian information operations exploit anti-French sentiment; Nigerian political discourse fragments across platforms; influencer economy matures; attention increasingly contested.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Disinformation reaches unprecedented levels around coups and elections; fact-checking capacity overwhelmed; coordinated inauthentic behavior identified but incompletely countered; verification becomes individual responsibility.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Sovereignty claims justify coups; anti-colonial framing resonates deeply; economic justice demands accompany Nigerian reforms; generational justice claims persist; jihadist violence challenges moral frameworks.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Military decrees replace constitutional order in coup states; emergency measures expand executive power; Nigerian reforms attempt regulatory modernization; digital regulation fragmented and contested.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Military tribunals in coup states; civilian courts pressured; platform moderation as de facto adjudication; international criminal jurisdiction irrelevant in practice; local justice mechanisms stressed.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Press freedom contracts in Sahel; journalist arrests increase; Nigerian media faces pressure but maintains plurality; digital rights contested; privacy eroded by surveillance expansion.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Wagner atrocities in Mali documented but unpunished; jihadist violence continues without accountability; electoral grievances unresolved; justice deficit accumulates; international mechanisms ineffective.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Nigerian universities continue producing graduates despite funding challenges; conflict disrupts Sahel academic institutions; diaspora expertise circulates through remote connections; AI and data science skills develop in Lagos ecosystem.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Fintech innovation continues; mobile money systems refine; content creation techniques mature; vernacular language technology improves; adaptation to economic constraints drives innovation.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: EdTech adoption persists post-COVID; skills gap widens between connected and disconnected populations; emigration depletes knowledge stock; bootcamps and online learning expand access for urban youth.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Rapid technology change outpaces training; institutional knowledge lost to emigration; conflict zones experience generational education gaps; traditional knowledge transmission continues in parallel.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Anti-colonial narratives power coup legitimation; Nigerian national identity contested amid reform hardship; diaspora success stories shape aspirational identities; trauma of violence accumulates in collective memory.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Sovereignty and anti-imperialism justify junta rule; reform as national necessity frames Nigerian policy; platform companies invoke innovation and inclusion; competing narratives fragment coherent regional identity.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Afrobeats global dominance continues; Nigerian artists achieve mainstream Western success; TikTok dances spread regional influence; fashion and aesthetics circulate through platforms; conflict zones see cultural production contract.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Revolutionary symbolism risks routinization; national symbols contested; platform culture accelerates trend cycles; meaning-making increasingly individualized and fragmented; shared cultural referents decline.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Religious frameworks provide meaning amid political chaos; prosperity gospel adapts to economic hardship; Islamic revivalism shapes Sahel discourse; fatalism and hope coexist in popular consciousness.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Digital worship normalized post-COVID; religious content dominates social media feeds; pilgrimage resumes; community religious practice persists; private devotion enabled by mobile access.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Mega-churches expand digital presence; traditional religious authorities compete with media-savvy preachers; jihadist recruitment continues through encrypted channels; religious institutions provide social services where state fails.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Pentecostal Christianity continues expansion; Salafi influence persists in Sahel; syncretic practices endure; diaspora religious connections maintained digitally; religious identity increasingly political marker.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Institutional religion maintains grip despite political turmoil; secularization limited to educated urban segments; spiritual meaning-making remains robust; the sacred provides resilience where political structures fail; existential questions answered through faith rather than ideology.
