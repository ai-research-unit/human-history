    2017 CE – 2022 CE — Information Warfare and Platform Battles

    Summary: The Russian state escalates its contest for control over digital information space through the failed Telegram ban, the Sovereign Internet Law, and intensified pressure on Western platforms, while simultaneously deploying information warfare techniques domestically and abroad, culminating in the pre-invasion information environment that would shape the war's opening phase—a period defined by the state's growing technical capacity for control confronting platforms and populations that develop countermeasures faster than the regime can close gaps.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Moscow consolidates as the control center for Russian digital infrastructure; international connectivity routes through limited exchange points that become targets for the Sovereign Internet Law's isolation requirements, while Ukraine's decentralized ISP market creates the distributed architecture that will prove resilient under attack.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The physical climate imposes no constraint; data center operations proceed regardless of season, and the infrastructure buildout for sovereign internet capabilities proceeds on political rather than environmental timelines.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: No natural hazards materially affect digital infrastructure; the hazards are entirely political and technical—cyberattacks, sanctions, and the self-imposed risks of attempted platform blocking.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Continued hydrocarbon revenues fund surveillance infrastructure expansion despite sanctions pressure; the SORM-3 data retention mandate requires substantial storage capacity that state resources can provision, and energy abundance enables the deep packet inspection deployment the Sovereign Internet Law requires.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: The data exhaust of mass surveillance accumulates in state databases; server farms multiply without environmental review, and the digital footprint expands while its material residue—electronic waste, energy consumption—remains unaddressed.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: COVID-19 arrives in early 2020, disrupting physical gathering while accelerating digital adoption; the pandemic provides justification for enhanced digital surveillance through contact tracing, and mortality spikes briefly before vaccination campaigns stabilize the situation.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Sanctions-induced food import substitution alters but does not degrade nutrition; the pandemic disrupts some supply chains, but basic provisioning remains adequate, and the biological substrate of the internet-using population stays stable.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: COVID-19 strains healthcare capacity; digital health passes and QR code systems deploy rapidly, demonstrating state capacity for mass digital identification that has surveillance applications beyond public health.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Dating apps mature as mainstream platforms; digital mediation of intimacy normalizes, and pandemic lockdowns intensify reliance on digital communication for social and romantic connection.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population stagnates around 144 million; emigration of technical talent accelerates post-2014 and again after 2021 Navalny protests, with the brain drain concentrated among those most capable of building digital alternatives.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: COVID-19 mortality concentrates among elderly populations, marginally affecting dependency ratios; the pandemic's demographic effect remains modest compared to Russia's structural mortality challenges.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Sanctions pressure and ruble volatility compress purchasing power; digital commerce expands through Yandex and emerging platforms, partially compensating for physical retail constraints during pandemic lockdowns.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Oil price volatility challenges but does not break the petro-state funding model; revenues remain adequate for surveillance infrastructure investment, and the energy-to-control conversion continues.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Tech sector labor markets tighten as emigration drains talent; state-aligned platforms (VK Company) face recruitment challenges, while remaining workers operate under increasing legal constraints on public expression.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Yandex diversifies into services (taxi, food delivery, fintech); domestic platforms generate surplus adequate for operations, but the tech sector remains small relative to extractive industries.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: State reserves rebuilt post-2014 provide sanctions resilience; the National Welfare Fund buffers economic shocks, creating capacity for sustained confrontation with Western platforms and their home governments.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Digital platforms intermediate expanding shares of commerce; the gig economy (Yandex.Taxi, delivery services) grows, creating new dependencies on platform infrastructure while sanctions disrupt hardware supply chains.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: The Central Bank develops digital ruble concepts; cryptocurrency usage grows among those seeking sanctions circumvention or capital flight, and the state's ambivalence toward crypto reflects competing priorities of control and evasion.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Fines on Western platforms (Google, Meta) provide modest revenue while primarily serving coercive purposes; the economic extraction is less significant than the demonstration of state power over foreign corporations.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Oligarchic control of domestic platforms consolidates under state supervision; Yandex restructures under sanctions pressure, and the defensibility of digital assets against both foreign sanctions and domestic pressure proves limited.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Surveillance data accumulates faster than analytical capacity; the SORM archive grows into a resource whose value depends on machine learning capabilities the state is still developing, creating a residue of unprocessed control potential.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Digital communication becomes essential for family coordination; pandemic isolation intensifies platform dependence, and generational platform preferences persist—older users on Odnoklassniki, younger on VKontakte and Telegram.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Civil society operates under intensifying "foreign agent" restrictions; opposition organizations migrate to Telegram, and Navalny's Anti-Corruption Foundation demonstrates the persistent capacity for digital organizing despite legal pressure.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Social media dominates leisure time; pandemic lockdowns eliminate alternatives, and platform engagement metrics surge while the state attempts to shape the content environment through pressure rather than direct control.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Digital literacy stratification deepens; VPN usage marks a boundary between those who access blocked content and those confined to the sanctioned information environment, creating parallel publics with divergent worldviews.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Roskomnadzor's technical capacity improves but fails the Telegram test—the 2018–2020 blocking attempt creates massive collateral damage without achieving its objective, demonstrating the gap between legal authority and technical implementation.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: The Sovereign Internet Law (2019) mandates deep packet inspection equipment and internet isolation capability; SORM expands, and the legal framework for complete information control is established even as implementation lags.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: The Navalny protests (January 2021) demonstrate persistent opposition capacity despite years of repression; the state responds with mass arrests and escalated legal pressure, and the social fabric of organized opposition fractures decisively as leaders are imprisoned or exiled, leaving atomized discontent without organizational outlet.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Pandemic anxiety combines with political tension; trust in institutions erodes, and the information environment fragments into separate realities—state television viewers inhabiting a different world than Telegram channel subscribers.

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Putin's constitutional amendments (2020) extend potential rule to 2036; the leadership cult continues but faces challenges from Navalny's anti-corruption investigations that puncture the image of competent authority.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: COVID-19 mismanagement strains legitimacy; Navalny's poisoning (August 2020) and subsequent imprisonment (February 2021) crystallize opposition but also demonstrate the costs of dissent, creating a chilling effect on public expression.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: The state demonstrates sustained will through the Navalny prosecution and protest suppression; constitutional manipulation shows long-term commitment to power retention, and the regime's resolve exceeds the opposition's capacity for sustained mobilization.

    External Relations & Posture: Information warfare against Western democracies intensifies; election interference operations, disinformation campaigns, and cyberattacks demonstrate offensive capability while justifying defensive "information sovereignty" measures.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: The 2021 protest suppression completes the exhaustion of the 2011–2012 generation's oppositional energy; emigration accelerates among the politically engaged, and the residue of disaffection disperses into private cynicism or exile rather than collective action.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Internet penetration reaches 85% of the Russian population; smartphone ubiquity makes digital access nearly universal among working-age adults, and the shared communicative infrastructure becomes a contested terrain rather than neutral substrate.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Telegram becomes the dominant channel for news and opposition coordination; its hybrid architecture—broadcast channels plus private messaging—defeats simple blocking, and information velocity through these channels exceeds state response capacity.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: The Sovereign Internet Law mandates traffic routing through state-controlled exchange points; deep packet inspection enables real-time monitoring, and the surveillance archive grows toward the aspiration of total record—all communications captured and stored.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Website blocking extends to independent media designated "foreign agents"; VPN restrictions tighten legally while remaining technically permeable, and the discourse norms split between public self-censorship and private circumvention.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Algorithm-driven amplification on domestic platforms promotes state-aligned content; independent journalism migrates to Telegram where no algorithm mediates distribution, creating attention economies with different dynamics.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Coordinated disinformation campaigns degrade signal quality on contested topics; the Internet Research Agency's techniques mature, and the information environment becomes a deliberate fog rather than a clear channel, with verification capacity overwhelmed by manufactured content volume.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: State promotion of "traditional values" intensifies; LGBTQ+ "propaganda" restrictions expand, and the moral vocabulary serves political control through claims of civilization defense against Western degeneracy.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: The legal framework for information control reaches comprehensive scope—the Sovereign Internet Law, expanded foreign agent designations, "fake news" criminalization, and extremism statutes that criminalize ordinary political speech.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Courts process opposition figures through predetermined verdicts; Navalny's prosecution demonstrates the legal system's subordination to political instruction, and platform fines accumulate without meaningful judicial review.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Constitutional speech protections become formally meaningless; compliance costs for platforms become prohibitive for those unwilling to cooperate with state demands, and individual users face prosecution for social media posts under broadly interpreted laws.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: The Navalny poisoning exemplifies total impunity; no investigation, no accountability, and the message to potential opponents is unmistakable—the state can act without legal constraint, and the gap between rights formally held and actually protected becomes absolute.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Information security doctrine fully subordinates academic inquiry; research funding flows to surveillance and control technologies, and the theoretical frame of "information sovereignty" replaces earlier notions of internet as democratizing force.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Deep packet inspection, facial recognition, and network analysis tools deploy at scale; the technical capacity for surveillance approaches comprehensive coverage while circumvention techniques (VPNs, encrypted messaging) spread in parallel.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Technical education continues producing capable engineers who face choices between emigration, state-aligned employment, or domestic opposition work; the skill base for both control and resistance exists, with the contest determined by resource access rather than knowledge gaps.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: The technical failure of the Telegram ban demonstrates that legal authority cannot substitute for technical competence; the state learns from this failure, investing in capacity that previous initiatives lacked, and the knowledge required for effective control accumulates through iteration.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: World War II memory intensifies as identity anchor; Victory Day observances expand, and the narrative of Russia besieged by enemies provides cultural foundation for information warfare framing.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: "Information sovereignty" becomes the explicit justification for control measures; the narrative frames Western platforms as instruments of hostile interference, legitimating restrictions as defense rather than repression.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Digital culture bifurcates between state-promoted patriotic content and oppositional material circulating through circumvention channels; meme warfare emerges as a cultural form, with Navalny investigations achieving viral distribution despite blocking.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Opposition cultural forms exhaust themselves against sustained repression; protest aesthetics become repetitive, and the symbolic vocabulary of resistance fails to renew while state-promoted forms achieve hollow dominance without genuine cultural vitality.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: The Orthodox-state synthesis deepens; official discourse frames the contest as civilizational and spiritual, with Russia defending traditional values against Western nihilism in a cosmic frame.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Digital Orthodoxy expands—church social media, online services—while the sacred and national merge more completely; religious observance becomes difficult to distinguish from political loyalty.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: The Moscow Patriarchate's alignment with state policy intensifies; the church supports information control measures as protecting spiritual health, and institutional religion functions as regime auxiliary.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Religious pluralism contracts under legal pressure; minority religions face restrictions, and the Orthodox-nationalist synthesis expands to fill the spiritual field while secular alternatives retreat.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: The pre-war period accumulates spiritual exhaustion without resolution; the opposition's hopes repeatedly crushed, its leadership imprisoned or exiled, the possibility of change within the system foreclosed—the residue settles as a void awaiting either catastrophe or transcendence, the sacred dimension of political possibility emptied, to be filled by the war that follows.
