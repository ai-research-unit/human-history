    5000 BCE – 2000 BCE — Pastoral Neolithic and Early Saharan Desiccation

    Summary: Progressive Saharan desiccation forces a fundamental economic transition from lakeside fishing to mobile cattle pastoralism combined with emergent cultivation; populations shift southward into the Sahel as northern lakes shrink and disappear; the binding constraint is diminishing rainfall and the carrying capacity of shrinking pasture; adaptive flexibility provides margin against shock but system operates closer to environmental limits than the preceding wet phase; trajectory is one of compression, adaptation, and intensification.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Saharan lakes contract and fragment; Niger and Senegal drainages remain viable but reduced; Mega-Chad shrinks dramatically; drying forces populations into narrowing corridors along permanent water; travel across former lake beds and emerging desert becomes more difficult; southward migration routes channeled by terrain.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Monsoon belt retreats southward; rainfall declines by 100–300 mm across the window; interannual volatility increases as climate destabilizes; dry spells lengthen; secular trend unmistakably toward aridity; Green Sahara conditions end by mid-window.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Drought becomes primary hazard — multi-year dry spells threaten herds and crops; flood risk declines as rivers shrink; desertification advances from the north; exposure to environmental failure increases sharply relative to the preceding period.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Aquatic resources collapse as lakes dry; wild game declines on shrinking grasslands; domesticated cattle become primary protein reserve; grazing land contracts southward; lithic and wood resources still available but patchier; endowment declining relative to stable pastoral demand.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Low population density and dispersed pastoral settlement produce minimal waste load; sink capacity ample relative to human output; no saturation.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Cattle introduce new disease ecology (bovine pathogens, associated parasites); waterhole concentration increases transmission risk; overall mortality baseline stable but disease portfolio shifts; endemic conditions persist.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Pastoral diet centers on milk, blood, and occasional meat supplemented by gathered plants and emerging cultivated grains; nutritional status adequate though more precarious than lake-margin abundance; vitality maintained but margin thins.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Pastoral societies develop marriage systems linked to cattle exchange (bride-wealth patterns may originate here); pair-bonding stable; reproductive biology unremarkable.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population contracts in drying north, concentrates in remaining viable zones; density increases locally in Sahel refugia; net replacement positive but growth slows; outmigration to the south continues through the window.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Pastoral mobility challenges elderly and infirm; dependency absorbed within extended kin networks; cattle wealth provides buffer; load manageable but tighter than in sedentary fishing communities.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Cattle pastoralism provides core subsistence — milk the daily staple, meat for special occasions; wild foods supplement; pearl millet cultivation emerges by ~3000 BCE adding storable grain; provisioning floor adequate but closer to edge than preceding aquatic phase.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle dominant; cattle provide traction, transport, and stored energy (meat, milk); fire for cooking and metalworking (late window); per-capita energy capture modestly higher than pure foraging due to livestock.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Household and lineage-based labor; herding tasks divided by age and gender; no coerced labor; cooperative kin labor for large tasks (corralling, milking, planting).

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Cattle herds constitute walking surplus — accumulating animals beyond immediate need; grain cultivation adds storable surplus; productive capacity expanding through diversification; surplus modest but growing.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Cattle themselves serve as reserve — slaughtered or exchanged in crisis; grain stored in pots and bins (Dhar Tichitt shows stone granaries); milk preserved as curds; buffering capacity improved by diversification but drought can wipe reserves rapidly.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Regional exchange of cattle, grain, salt, and lithics between pastoral groups and early cultivators; movement patterns facilitate exchange along seasonal routes; no long-distance trade infrastructure yet; logistics pastoral and foot-based.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Cattle serve as wealth store and medium of exchange; bride-wealth and compensation payments create credit-like obligations; reciprocal sharing persists; deferred exchange now materialized in livestock.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Senior kin and herd-owners accumulate and redistribute cattle; no formalized taxation; redistribution through kinship obligations, feasting, and bride-wealth; extraction remains embedded in social relations.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Cattle ownership introduces portable, accumulable wealth; stratification increases — some lineages amass larger herds; raiding emerges as threat requiring defense; inequality modest but widening relative to egalitarian forager baseline.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Cattle dung used as fuel and building material (recycled); slaughter waste dispersed; agricultural waste minimal; byproduct load low and largely recycled.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Extended kin networks essential for herd management and drought response; trust radius expands to include pastoral allies and exchange partners; mutual aid intensifies as environment becomes harsher.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Status increasingly linked to cattle wealth and herd size; senior herd-owners gain prestige; lineage identity sharpens as competition for shrinking resources increases; ethnic or clan boundaries may harden.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilineal descent likely strengthens with cattle inheritance through male lines; extended patrilocal households manage herds; marriage alliance cemented by cattle exchange; kinship structures elaborate.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Clan and lineage councils coordinate movement, grazing rights, and dispute resolution; age-grade systems possible (attested in later Sahelian pastoralists); self-organization above band level emerging.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Seasonal gatherings at water sources and pastures allow sociability, ceremony, and mate exchange; cattle serve social as well as economic function (prestige display, ritual); leisure integrated with herding rhythm.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Elder councils mediate disputes over cattle, water, and grazing; compensation in cattle institutionalizes dispute resolution; mobility still allows fission; grievance largely absorbed but stakes higher than forager period.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Emerging big-man or chief patterns — senior herd-owners exercise influence but lack institutionalized power; authority personal and contingent; no state formation yet; power diffuse but concentrating.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Influence passes within lineages but not through fixed rules; sons of wealthy herd-owners advantaged; transfer contested and negotiated within kin groups.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Authority legitimated by generosity (redistribution), wisdom, and ritual efficacy; ancestral narratives support lineage prestige; no formal political ideology.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No formal administration; lineage elders coordinate through oral consensus; memory and oral communication sufficient for pastoral-scale organization; capacity adequate.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Adult male heads of household participate in lineage and clan councils; women's influence exercised through domestic sphere; mobilization for defense or migration collective; participation broad within patriarchal limits.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Relations with neighboring pastoral and cultivating groups governed by exchange, intermarriage, and occasional conflict; territorial claims to grazing and water emerge; external relations tense where resources scarce; no state-level threats.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No standing military; adult males armed with spears, bows, clubs; raiding parties form ad hoc for livestock theft and defense; force capacity distributed but mobilizable.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Cattle raiding emerges as endemic threat; internal order maintained by lineage authority and compensation systems; violence episodic but real; threat environment elevated relative to forager baseline.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Face-to-face speech remains sole channel; pastoral mobility accelerates information flow across landscape; seasonal gatherings concentrate transmission; velocity still limited to travel pace.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Living memory alone — oral tradition carries genealogies, territorial knowledge, herding lore, and historical memory; no writing or external storage; persistence depends on unbroken human transmission; knowledge vulnerable to demographic loss but lineage depth adds redundancy.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Lineage elders control authoritative speech on genealogy and tradition; no formal censorship; discourse norms govern appropriate speech by age and status.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Audiences remain small-scale (camp, lineage gathering); important information (grazing, water, threats) amplified through pastoral networks; attention focused on survival-critical and social knowledge.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Oral transmission accurate for practical knowledge but genealogies and histories drift over generations; verification through elder consensus; noise load manageable in small-scale society.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Reciprocity, kinship obligation, and respect for elders underpin moral order; cattle theft a serious offense requiring compensation; hospitality obligations strong; justice rooted in balance and restitution.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Customary law transmitted orally; rules governing marriage, inheritance, grazing, and compensation for injury or theft; codes stable but adapted to pastoral conditions; evolution gradual.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Lineage and clan elders adjudicate disputes; compensation in cattle standard remedy; liability collective within lineage; enforcement through social pressure and threat of feud; formal courts absent.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual rights embedded in lineage membership and status; autonomy constrained by kinship obligation; compliance burden moderate and internalized through socialization.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Wrongs across lineage or clan boundaries harder to redress; raiding creates uncompensated losses; impunity gap widens relative to tight-knit forager communities but remains limited.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: No formal inquiry; practical knowledge of cattle husbandry, pasture cycles, water sources, and weather signs extensive and refined; explanatory paradigms remain embedded in oral tradition and animist cosmology.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Cattle breeding and management techniques develop; milking, bleeding, and meat preservation methods refined; pearl millet cultivation introduced (~3000 BCE) requiring planting and harvest knowledge; pottery and leather-working techniques continue; all transmitted through apprenticeship.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Youth learn through participation in herding and household tasks; specialized knowledge (healing, ritual, smithing where present) restricted to practitioners; knowledge stock adequate for pastoral-agricultural economy; skill distribution tracks lineage and gender.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Aquatic fishing knowledge fades as lakes disappear — obsolescence driven by environmental change; new pastoral and agricultural knowledge accumulates; decay and accumulation in rough balance.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Pastoral identity crystallizes — cattle become central symbol; ancestral narratives link lineages to founding herds and homeland; origin myths adapt to new landscape; deep memory of the Green Sahara may persist in oral tradition.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Rock art transitions from aquatic to pastoral themes — cattle herds, herders, pastoral scenes dominate later panels at Tassili and Ennedi; pottery continues with regional styles; material culture reflects pastoral priorities (leather, bone, horn).

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Adaptive and resilient but sobering — awareness of environmental decline shapes outlook; mobility and flexibility valued; risk-consciousness heightened; mood pragmatic rather than confident.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Aquatic-era symbols lose relevance; pastoral symbols gain power; transition involves symbolic renewal rather than exhaustion; meaning actively reconstructed around cattle and lineage.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Animist worldview continues — spirits inhabit landscape, cattle, ancestors; cattle may acquire sacred significance (prefiguring later Sahelian cattle cults); human fate bound to herd and kin; outlook balanced between fatalism and active propitiation.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Cattle feature in sacrifice and ritual; life-stage ceremonies (birth, initiation, marriage, death) marked by cattle exchange; rain-calling and fertility rituals probable as drought anxiety rises; sacred practice intensifies.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: No institutional religion; ritual specialists (diviners, rainmakers, healers) hold informal authority tied to efficacy; doctrine oral and flexible; authority personal rather than institutional.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Pastoral spiritual traditions spread with southward migration; local hunter-gatherer traditions absorbed or displaced; gradual diffusion rather than sharp replacement; syncretism probable at contact zones.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No disenchantment; sacred meaning intensifies around cattle and crisis response; environmental stress increases rather than diminishes spiritual engagement; sacred need fully addressed within adapted cosmology.
