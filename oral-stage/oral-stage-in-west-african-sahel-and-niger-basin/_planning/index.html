    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The West African Sahel and Niger Basin

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 130000 BCE – 1955 CE

    Region: The West African Sahel and Niger Basin — the semi-arid transition zone between the Sahara and the tropical forests, stretching from the Atlantic coast of Senegal through Mali, Niger, and Chad to Lake Chad; including the Niger River bend, the inland delta, and the southern Saharan margins.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Middle Stone Age sites, Holocene lakeside settlements, pastoral rock art, early iron-working centers (Nok, Dhar Tichitt), and urban centers (Jenne-jeno, Timbuktu). Arabic sources document Sahelian empires from ~800 CE. Griot oral traditions preserve deep historical memory. French colonial documentation expands from ~1890. Window ends when colonial administrative records become the dominant institutional medium (~1955 CE independence era).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Middle Stone Age Occupation (130000–40000 BCE)

    Early Modern Humans: North and West Africa key regions for Homo sapiens evolution; MSA industries indicate behavioral complexity.

    Aterian Industry: ~145,000–20,000 BP; tanged points; extends into Sahara during wet phases; connection to Sahel debated.

    Climate Fluctuations: Sahara alternates between wet and dry phases; human occupation tracks favorable periods.

    Sparse Evidence: Few well-dated Sahelian MSA sites; erosion and sedimentation limit preservation.

    Lithic Technology: Prepared-core techniques; Levallois and related methods; regional variation.

    Population Dynamics: Low density; highly mobile; occupation concentrated around water sources.

    2. Late Stone Age Adaptation (40000–12000 BCE)

    MSA/LSA Transition: Gradual shift to microlithic technologies; timing varies by region.

    Climate Context: Last Glacial Maximum ~26,500–19,000 BP; Sahara hyperarid; Sahel contracted southward.

    Refugia: Populations likely concentrated in well-watered areas; Niger bend, Lake Chad basin.

    Microlithic Technologies: Small backed pieces; composite tools; bow and arrow likely adopted.

    Regional Variation: Different adaptations in different ecological zones.

    Sparse Record: Few sites; climate-driven abandonment of northern Sahel.

    3. Green Sahara/African Humid Period (12000–5000 BCE)

    African Humid Period: ~11,700–5,000 BP; dramatically increased rainfall; "Green Sahara" supports lakes, rivers, wildlife.

    Aquatic Adaptation: Lakeside settlements; abundant fish, hippo, crocodile; harpoons; pottery early (some of Africa's earliest).

    Gobero (Niger): ~10,000–4,500 BP; cemetery site; two populations; rich burial goods.

    Rock Art: Saharan pastoral and aquatic scenes; depicts cattle, wildlife, humans; at Tassili, Ennedi, Tibesti (Sahel margins).

    Early Fishing Economy: Bone harpoons; fish remains; seasonal lakeside camps.

    Population Growth: Abundant resources support larger populations; semi-sedentary settlements.

    4. Pastoral Neolithic (5000–2000 BCE)

    Cattle Pastoralism: Domesticated cattle present by ~7000 BCE in Sahara; herding economy develops.

    Dhar Tichitt (Mauritania): ~2000–500 BCE; stone-built settlements; millet cultivation; cattle herding; proto-urban.

    Saharan Desiccation: Gradual aridification from ~5000 BCE; southward population movement.

    Millet Domestication: Pearl millet (Pennisetum glaucum) domesticated in Sahel ~3000–2500 BCE; sorghum follows.

    Herding-Farming Mix: Pastoral and agricultural strategies combine; mobility varies.

    Cultural Continuity: Pastoral traditions continue into historical period (Fulani, Tuareg ancestors).

    5. Iron Age Emergence (2000–500 BCE)

    Early Iron Smelting: Independent African iron smelting ~2500–1500 BCE; Termit (Niger), Taruga (Nigeria).

    Nok Culture: ~1500 BCE – 500 CE; Nigeria; terracotta figurines; iron working; agricultural villages.

    Agricultural Intensification: Millet, sorghum, fonio expand; village-based farming consolidates.

    Population Growth: Iron tools enable forest clearance; agricultural expansion southward.

    Craft Specialization: Iron smithing; pottery traditions; regional styles develop.

    Exchange Networks: Iron, copper, salt, gold traded regionally.

    6. Trans-Saharan Networks and Urban Emergence (500 BCE – 300 CE)

    Trans-Saharan Trade Beginnings: Camel introduced ~3rd century BCE; regular caravan routes develop.

    Gold and Salt: Sahel gold sources (Bambuk, Buré) traded for Saharan salt; foundational exchange.

    Jenne-jeno: ~250 BCE – 1400 CE; earliest urban center in sub-Saharan Africa; no defensive walls; dispersed urbanism.

    Complex Societies: Social stratification; craft specialization; long-distance trade; pre-state complexity.

    Pottery and Iron: Distinctive regional traditions; sophisticated metallurgy.

    Oral Tradition: Griot traditions may have deep roots; historical memory transmitted orally.

    7. Ghana Empire (300–1000 CE)

    Wagadu/Ghana: First major Sahelian empire; Soninke-speaking; capital at Kumbi Saleh (debated).

    Gold Trade Control: Ghana controls gold-salt exchange; taxes trade; wealth accumulation.

    Arab Sources: Al-Bakri (1068) describes Ghana; rich court, powerful king, dual-city capital.

    Islamization Begins: Muslim merchants and scholars in towns; rulers remain traditionally religious initially.

    State Structure: King with court; provincial governors; tribute from subject peoples.

    Oral Administration: No indigenous writing; state functions through oral communication and memory.

    8. Mali Empire (1000–1350 CE)

    Sundiata Keita: Traditional founder (~1235); epic of Sundiata preserved by griots; defeat of Sumanguru.

    Mansa Musa (r. 1312–1337): Famous pilgrimage to Mecca (1324); legendary wealth; Cairo gold market disruption.

    Timbuktu: Major commercial and scholarly center; Sankore mosque; Islamic learning.

    Trans-Saharan Florescence: Gold, salt, slaves, copper traded; Saharan routes busy.

    Islamic Learning: Arabic literacy among scholars and clerics; but state and majority remain oral.

    Griot Tradition: Professional remembrancers; royal genealogies; Sundiata epic; oral history institution.

    9. Songhai Ascendancy (1350–1600 CE)

    Songhai Empire: Succeeds Mali; Gao as capital; Sonni Ali (r. 1464–1492) expands empire.

    Askia Muhammad (r. 1493–1528): Islamic reform; pilgrimage; scholarly patronage; administrative reorganization.

    Timbuktu Peak: Scholars, libraries, madrasas; Ahmed Baba and others; Arabic manuscripts.

    Moroccan Invasion (1591): Battle of Tondibi; firearms defeat Songhai cavalry; empire collapses.

    Fragmentation: Post-1591; no single successor state; regional powers compete.

    Written vs. Oral: Islamic scholarship produces manuscripts; but governance, law, history remain primarily oral for most people.

    10. Post-Imperial States and Jihads (1600–1850 CE)

    Successor States: Bambara kingdoms (Segu, Kaarta); Mossi states; Hausa city-states.

    Fulani Jihads: Islamic reform movements; Sokoto Caliphate (1804); Toucouleur (1850s); new states.

    Sokoto Caliphate: Usman dan Fodio; largest 19th-century African state; Islamic administration.

    Slave Trade: Trans-Saharan and Atlantic trades extract population; regional instability.

    Oral Tradition Continues: Griots, marabouts, elders transmit knowledge; Arabic learning restricted to scholars.

    Pre-Colonial States: Complex polities with oral administration; written Arabic for religious/scholarly purposes.

    11. Colonial Conquest (1850–1920 CE)

    French Expansion: Military campaigns from Senegal eastward; Samori Ture resistance; Tukolor defeat.

    Conquest Completed: By ~1900, French control entire Sahel; German (Togo), British (Nigeria) also present.

    Administrative Imposition: French colonial administration; written records, censuses, taxation.

    Traditional Authority Transformed: Chiefs become colonial agents; oral authority subordinated.

    Arabic Scholarship Continues: Marabouts, Quranic schools; but colonial French documentation dominates.

    Oral Traditions Recorded: Colonial ethnographers document griot traditions; oral knowledge enters written archive.

    12. Colonial Consolidation and Oral Twilight (1920–1955 CE)

    French West Africa: Federated administration; standardized governance; extensive documentation.

    Administrative Records: Birth registers, tax rolls, land surveys, court records; written record becomes institutional basis.

    Education: Limited French schooling; literacy spreads slowly; oral majority persists.

    Griot Traditions Persist: Oral history, genealogy, music continue; but subordinated to documentary state.

    Independence Era: 1955–1960; transition to post-colonial states; oral traditions valued as national heritage.

    Oral Threshold: By ~1955, colonial and post-colonial administrative documentation is the medium institutions run on; griot traditions persist as cultural heritage rather than institutional memory.
