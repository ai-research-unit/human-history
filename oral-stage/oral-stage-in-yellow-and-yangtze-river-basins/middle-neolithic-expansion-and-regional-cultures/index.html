    6000 BCE – 4500 BCE — Middle Neolithic Expansion and Regional Cultures

    Summary: Agricultural populations radiating outward from core domestication zones across the Yellow and Yangtze basins, with regionally distinct ceramic and subsistence traditions crystallizing: painted-pottery Yangshao villages in the loess uplands, Hemudu pile-dwellings and wet-rice paddies in the lower Yangtze, Daxi rice farmers in the middle Yangtze. The binding constraint is arable land suitable for each regime, and the system holds wide margin against shock given low population density relative to carrying capacity. Direction of change is expansive: populations growing, territories filling, village clusters multiplying.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Two great drainage systems dominate: the Yellow River cutting through loess plateau and North China Plain, the Yangtze threading lowlands, middle basin, and Sichuan; intervening highlands segment the basins into distinct ecological corridors, with transit friction highest at mountain crossings and lowest along river valleys and coastal plains.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Mid-Holocene climatic optimum; warmer and wetter than present across both basins; strengthened East Asian monsoon delivers reliable summer rains; interannual variability exists but secular trend is stable warmth, widening the band suitable for cultivation.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Yellow River prone to channel shifts and flooding across the alluvial plain; Yangtze lowlands subject to seasonal inundation; seismic activity along fault zones; settlement patterns adapted to flood risk, and overall exposure moderate given dispersed village placement with margin to relocate.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Loess soils of exceptional fertility in the north; alluvial silts and wetland margins in the Yangtze lowlands; abundant timber in uplands; clay for pottery widespread; lithic raw materials (chert, obsidian) regionally available; drawdown negligible against vast endowment.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Human and agricultural waste absorbed by open landscape with effectively unlimited capacity; ash, organic refuse, and fecal matter dispersed or returned to fields; no sink saturation at prevailing population densities.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic parasitic and gastrointestinal infections associated with sedentism and pig husbandry; no evidence of epidemic disease; mortality elevated in infancy but baseline death rate within renewal capacity; trend stable.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Skeletal evidence suggests adequate nutrition with moderate stature; millet-based diet in north, rice in south, supplemented by pig, dog, fish, and gathered plants; seasonal stress possible but not chronic; nutritional status stable or improving as agriculture intensifies.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Physiological fertility high; pair-bonding within village or lineage networks; no artificial fertility controls attested; reproductive pressures favor successful cultivators and cooperative kin groups.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Birth-to-death ratio positive; population expanding steadily; density rising but still low relative to carrying capacity; village clusters grow and bud daughter settlements into adjacent territories; age structure weighted toward youth.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Orphaned, widowed, and aged dependents absorbed within extended kin and village networks; dependency load manageable given productive surplus and cooperative labor pooling; no evidence of structural strain.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Cultivated cereals (millet in north, rice in south) provide reliable caloric base; supplemented by pigs, dogs, fish, deer, gathered nuts and tubers; household pooling buffers individual shortfall; population sits comfortably above subsistence edge.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle remains sole power source; fire for cooking, pottery firing, and forest clearance; no draft animals yet harnessed; per-capita work ceiling modest but sufficient for village-scale agriculture and construction.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Labor organized at household and lineage level; tasks divided by age and sex; cooperative labor for communal projects (house-building, land clearance); no coerced labor attested; compensation through reciprocal obligation.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Agricultural productivity generates surplus above subsistence; storage pits attest surplus grain; productive capacity expanding as new land brought under cultivation; surplus modest but growing.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Subterranean storage pits for grain; ceramic vessels for seeds and prepared food; reserves sufficient to buffer a poor harvest; village-level pooling provides secondary cushion; margin adequate.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Inter-village exchange of pottery, obsidian, ornaments; regional distribution networks emerging; logistics limited to human portage and small watercraft; exchange velocity low but increasing as regional interaction intensifies.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No monetary instruments; value stored in durable goods (pottery, ornaments, stored grain); deferred reciprocity within kin and village networks carries obligations across time; credit informal and personal.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No centralized extraction; surplus remains at household or lineage level; communal allocation for feasts and cooperative labor; redistribution through reciprocal obligation rather than tribute.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Modest differentiation in burial goods suggests emerging but limited inequality; land and stored grain primary assets; defense through kin solidarity and village cohesion rather than fortification.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Organic waste returned to fields or middens; pottery sherds and lithic debitage accumulate at village margins; waste volume trivial relative to absorptive capacity; no routing pressure.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust radius extends through kin network and village community; reciprocal aid for labor, food sharing, and crisis support; inter-village relations cooperative but bounded; trust fabric thickening as settlement density increases.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Status differentiation modest; elder males and successful households hold prestige; lineage identity primary; regional ceramic styles mark cultural boundaries; stratification incipient, not yet rigid.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilineal or ambilineal descent probable; extended family households; marriage alliances between lineages; inheritance of land use rights, stored goods, and household position within kin group.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Village as primary unit of collective action; lineage elders coordinate labor, resolve disputes, organize rituals; no formal institutions above village level; associational fabric strong at local scale.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Seasonal festivals, communal feasting, and ritual gatherings provide shared leisure; music, dance, and storytelling probable; sociability embedded in agricultural calendar and life-cycle ceremonies.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Disputes mediated by kin elders; mobility to new land remains possible safety valve; ritual and feasting dissipate tension; unresolved grievance rare given kin-based resolution and exit option.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Authority diffuse and localized; village headmen or lineage elders coordinate collective action; no supra-village polity; power distributed across kin-based communities; trend stable.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Leadership passes within lineage by seniority or demonstrated competence; no formal succession rules attested; transfer informal and accepted; not a source of instability.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Authority legitimated through kinship, age, and demonstrated ability; ancestral precedent and oral tradition reinforce elder status; no specialized political ideology; legitimacy high and uncontested.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No formal administration; coordination through kin networks and oral consensus; record-keeping absent; decision-making face-to-face and collective; capacity limited to village scale.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Adult males participate in village councils; collective decision by consensus; no organized factions or parties; dissent expressed through discussion or departure; channels open.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: No inter-polity diplomacy; relations between villages mediated by kinship, exchange, and marriage alliance; occasional conflict over resources possible but no sustained warfare attested; external pressure minimal.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No specialized military; adult males bear hunting weapons (bows, spears, axes); violence capacity distributed; no fortifications or standing forces; coercive apparatus nil.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Low internal disorder; kin-based norms enforce behavior; banditry and predation absent at attested population densities; enforcement capacity exceeds threat load with wide margin.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Oral transmission through face-to-face speech; reach limited to immediate community and visiting kin; velocity slow, bounded by walking pace and seasonal gathering; no amplification beyond human voice.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Living memory sole medium of storage; no writing, no durable notation; information persists through repetition, song, and oral formula; knowledge vulnerable to forgetting and death of carriers; persistence dependent on continuous oral rehearsal.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Elders control authoritative narrative; taboos on sacred knowledge and origin stories; no formal censorship but normative pressure shapes what is repeated; permitted band stable.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Audience reception immediate and communal; amplification through repetition in ritual and teaching; attention sustained by social context; no viral spread beyond community.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Oral transmission accurate within short chains; drift accumulates across generations; no verification mechanism beyond elder consensus; signal-to-noise adequate for village-scale coordination.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Justice conceived as reciprocity and kin obligation; fairness means balance within the group; breach of trust is primary wrong; moral sense stable and universally shared within community.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No written law; binding custom and taboo govern conduct; rules transmitted orally and enforced by kin pressure; evolution slow, by accretion of precedent.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Disputes resolved by kin elders through mediation and consensus; enforcement through social sanction (shame, exclusion); liability assigned to kin group; throughput adequate for low caseload.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual autonomy within kin framework; protection through kin solidarity; compliance burden low, embedded in daily life; rules predictable within community.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Impunity rare within kin-governed community; wrongs addressed through restitution or feud; unredressed grievance possible only if offender flees beyond kin reach; gap minimal.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Inquiry practical and observational; knowledge of seasons, soils, plant behavior, and animal habits; no formal theory; explanatory paradigm animist and ancestral; discovery frontier at agricultural technique and craft.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Agricultural methods (tillage, planting, harvest timing) transmitted orally; ceramic production techniques refined regionally; woodworking (Hemudu) sophisticated; reproducible methods encoded in practice and apprenticeship.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Knowledge transmitted within family and lineage; no formal schooling; skill distribution broad for basic tasks, narrow for specialized crafts; stock growing as techniques accumulate.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Earlier foraging techniques fading as agriculture dominates; knowledge lost when carriers die without transmission; no archival mechanism; decay rate moderate, offset by active oral teaching.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Origin narratives tied to ancestors and founding settlements; regional identity expressed through ceramic style (Yangshao painted ware, Hemudu cord-marked ware); collective memory oral and place-bound.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Painted pottery with geometric and fish motifs (Yangshao); sophisticated carpentry and lacquerwork (Hemudu); jade ornaments beginning; aesthetic norms regionally distinct and elaborating.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Mood confident given expanding resources and successful cultivation; risk perception moderate; behavioral temper cooperative and outward-looking; trend optimistic.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Cultural forms in active elaboration, not exhaustion; symbols and styles vital and meaningful; no anomie or meaning decay attested; forms renewing through regional variation.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Animist cosmology; spirits inhabit landscape, ancestors influence living; human place embedded in continuous spiritual ecology; balance between piety and pragmatic engagement with spirits; no fatalism attested.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Ancestor veneration at household and village level; seasonal agricultural rituals; offerings of food and goods; shamanic intermediaries probable; ritual practice embedded in daily and calendrical cycle.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: No formal priesthood or religious institution; ritual authority held by elders and specialists (shamans); doctrine oral and flexible; authority localized and contested only informally.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Regional spiritual traditions coexist; no missionary expansion or displacement; syncretism through intermarriage and exchange; dominant cosmology stable within each culture area.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Sacred order intact and unchallenged; no drift toward secularism or existential void; spiritual need fully anchored in animist-ancestral framework; disenchantment absent.
