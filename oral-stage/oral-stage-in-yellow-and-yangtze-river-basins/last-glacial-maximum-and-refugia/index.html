    25,000 BCE – 15,000 BCE — Last Glacial Maximum and Refugia

    Summary: At the height of the last Ice Age, cold and aridity compress human populations into scattered refugia—the middle Yangtze valleys, southwestern karst basins, and sheltered coastal pockets—where small bands persist through broad-spectrum foraging, their survival knowledge and social bonds transmitted entirely through speech and memory against environmental conditions that leave little margin for error.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Yellow River basin is largely depopulated or very thinly occupied; loess deposition accelerates, burying older surfaces; the North China Plain is cold, windswept steppe; the Yangtze basin remains more habitable, with the middle reaches (modern Hubei, Hunan) and southwestern karst country (Guizhou, Yunnan) offering sheltered valleys and limestone caves; coastal areas are exposed by lower sea levels, but cold and dry; spatial friction is extreme—mountain barriers, seasonal flooding, and resource scarcity confine populations to isolated pockets.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Global temperatures are 4–7 degrees Celsius below Holocene averages; the East Asian monsoon weakens drastically, reducing summer rainfall; winters are severe in the north, with permafrost extending into northern China; even the south is cooler and drier than today; interannual volatility remains high, with cold snaps and multi-year droughts capable of collapsing local resources; the climate regime is at its harshest sustained level of the Pleistocene.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Dust storms intensify as loess accumulates; flood hazard is reduced by lower precipitation but flash floods remain possible in mountain valleys; cold exposure is the primary hazard—hypothermia, frostbite, and resource failure during cold spells; fire in dry vegetation is possible; populations in refugia face less hazard than those on exposed plains, but margin is thin everywhere.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Large fauna (mammoth, woolly rhinoceros, horses, deer) persist in the north but at lower densities; in southern refugia, subtropical fauna (deer, wild pigs, monkeys, small mammals) and diverse plant foods (tubers, nuts, wild rice in favorable microhabitats) sustain foraging; stone for toolmaking is available in most regions; firewood is critical and may be scarce in degraded or cold environments; the energy endowment is contracted relative to earlier and later periods.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Population density is so low that human waste output is negligible; abandoned sites are rapidly buried by loess in the north or overgrown in the south; no sink saturation; environmental feedback is from climate, not human impact.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Infectious disease pressure remains low in small, isolated populations; zoonotic exposure from game processing poses some risk; cold exposure, injury, and resource failure are the leading causes of death; infant and childhood mortality is high; life expectancy at birth is likely 25–30 years; mortality spikes during resource crashes.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Diet is constrained by reduced plant diversity and game availability; broad-spectrum foraging (small game, fish, shellfish, roots, nuts) buffers against single-resource failure but overall nutritional margins are thin; skeletal evidence suggests periodic nutritional stress; physiological robustness is maintained in survivors, but population health is precarious.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Healing knowledge is oral and limited: wound tending, fracture setting, herbal remedies known within the band; no specialist healers; care for the sick and injured is constrained by band resources and mobility needs; in extremis, the vulnerable may be abandoned.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Fertility is constrained by nutritional stress and prolonged lactation; pair-bonding persists, likely with kin-regulated mating rules; exogamous mate exchange between refugial bands maintains genetic diversity but is limited by isolation and low encounter rates.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population density is at its Pleistocene nadir in this region; total population may number in the hundreds to low thousands across both basins combined; birth rates are low, death rates high; net growth is near zero or negative; refugial populations are fragmented and vulnerable to local extinction.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: The dependent load (young, old, infirm) is proportionally significant in small bands; attrition is high during resource stress; the capacity to carry dependents is limited; this is a survival regime, not a growth regime.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: The provisioning floor is immediate-return foraging with no storage capacity beyond a few days; hunting, fishing, and plant gathering must succeed continuously; the floor is close to carrying capacity limits, with little margin; household pooling distributes risk within the band.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle and fire are the only energy sources; fire is critical for warmth, cooking, and possibly game drive; no animal traction; per-capita energy throughput is low; the energy ceiling constrains all activity to what hands and fire can achieve.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Labor is organized by age and sex within the band; hunting, gathering, and processing tasks are coordinated orally; no coercion beyond social pressure; task allocation is flexible and consensus-based.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Production is for immediate consumption; surplus is rare and fleeting—a successful hunt is shared and consumed quickly; no durable surplus accumulates; productive capacity is limited to band labor.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Reserves are minimal: dried meat, cached plant foods may last days or weeks; no granaries, no formal pooling institutions; buffering depends on mobility and fallback resources; reserves are inadequate against prolonged resource failure.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Exchange occurs when bands encounter each other—probably seasonally and infrequently in this period; raw materials (stone, shell, ochre) and ornaments circulate; logistics are limited to personal carrying; no trade routes; isolation reduces exchange frequency.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No currency; reciprocal obligation is the mechanism of deferred exchange, enforced by memory and reputation; prestige goods exist but are not systematically convertible; credit is personal and oral.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No extraction apparatus; redistribution is egalitarian sharing within the band; no chiefs, tribute, or rent; surplus, when it occurs, is consumed collectively.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Wealth differences are minimal; portable tools, weapons, and ornaments are personal property; land is not owned; accumulation is impossible; asset defense is unnecessary.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Waste (butchering debris, ash, knapping refuse) is left at campsites; no routing decisions; no accumulation of refuse or mispricing.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Kinship organizes all social relations: descent, marriage, residence, and cooperation; inheritance is of knowledge and portable goods, transmitted orally; no property in land.

    Association & Mutual Aid: The band (perhaps 20–40 individuals in stressed conditions) is the unit of association; mutual aid is obligatory among kin; no institutions beyond kinship; reciprocity and reputation enforce cooperation.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Leisure is constrained by survival demands but not absent: fireside gathering, storytelling, song, and play persist as the medium of sociability and cultural transmission.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Stratification is minimal; status accrues to skilled hunters and knowledgeable elders but confers no coercive power; group structure is fluid; bands may shrink, merge, or fragment in response to conditions.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No administration; decision-making is collective and oral; leadership is situational; no offices, bureaucracy, or formalized external relations.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No standing force; all able-bodied adults can fight; weapons are hunting tools; violence is interpersonal, not organized; no fortifications; interband conflict is probably rare given low population density and isolation.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Friction is discharged through mobility, mediation, and band fission; unresolved disputes lead to separation; no accumulated disorder or decaying apparatus; social structure is simple and resilient.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Leadership is personal: the competent hunter, the wise elder, the persuasive speaker; authority cannot be inherited or formalized; influence depends on demonstrated skill and generosity.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Legitimacy is embedded in kinship and reciprocity; no separate political order exists; allegiance is to kin and band; dissent is expressed by departure.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Collective action requires consensus; will is mobilized for hunts, moves, and survival tasks; resolve is tested by repeated hardship; sustained collective effort depends on shared necessity.

    External Relations & Posture: Relations with other bands are rare due to isolation; posture is wary; no expansion or conquest; occasional encounters may be for mate exchange or resource information sharing.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Sentiment is shaped by survival anxiety; trust is high within the band, guarded toward outsiders; mood tracks resource availability; behavioral temper is cautious, risk-averse.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Prolonged hardship may produce despair, but band structure keeps apathy contained; disaffection leads to departure; spent will after resource crashes may contribute to group dissolution, particularly if leadership fails.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Fully grammatical spoken language is the medium; mutual intelligibility extends to neighboring bands but contact is rare; no writing, no notation; exclusion is by isolation or deafness.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Channels are face-to-face; transmission velocity is limited to human travel; news of distant conditions arrives slowly if at all; no amplification.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Memory is the only record; knowledge persists through oral repetition—songs, stories, instructional routines—but is vulnerable to transmission failure; loss of elders means loss of knowledge; isolation threatens cultural persistence.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Discourse is governed by kinship respect, age deference, and taboo; no formal censorship; framing is embedded in myth and convention.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Reception is limited to those present; amplification is through retelling; isolation limits the spread of information between bands; each refugial population may diverge culturally.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Signal integrity depends on individual memory; noise accumulates through forgetting and embellishment; no external verification; within-band consensus is the only check.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Justice is embedded in reciprocity and kinship obligation; sharing is mandatory; hoarding and deception are wrong; revenge for wrongs is legitimate; the moral sense is enforced by gossip and exclusion.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No written law; norms are customary and oral; taboos govern sacred matters, mating, and food distribution; regulation is informal.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Disputes are adjudicated by elders or consensus; liability is personal and kin-based; sanction ranges from rebuke to expulsion; no formal courts.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Rights are implicit in kinship status; protections depend on kin advocacy; compliance burden is low—norms are internalized.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Impunity is limited by small group size; unredressed wrongs may fuel feuds; the gap between justice owed and delivered is small but present when enforcement fails.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Inquiry is empirical but embedded in myth; observation of environment, weather, and animal behavior guides action; theory is narrative, not abstract; survival knowledge is the frontier.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Technical knowledge is critical: stone toolmaking, fire-making, tracking, plant identification, shelter construction, cold-weather survival techniques; methods are transmitted orally and by demonstration.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Education is lifelong and informal; children learn by observation and participation; the knowledge stock is held in elder memories; skill distribution is broad; specialization is limited to individual aptitude.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Obsolescence is slow; knowledge decay is a serious risk in isolated populations—loss of skilled elders can mean loss of techniques; error correction depends on practical testing; isolation threatens intellectual persistence.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Identity is rooted in kinship, landscape, and origin myth; foundational narratives explain the world and the band's place in it; memory is oral and vulnerable to loss.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: The legitimating narrative is mythological: ancestors established the way; spirits sanction the norms; no separate ideology; tradition is authority.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Material culture is portable and functional: stone tools, bone implements, shell and tooth ornaments, ochre; artistic expression is in personal adornment and perishable media; no monumental art; cave sites may preserve some traces, but evidence is sparse in this region compared to Europe.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Forms are stable; no exhaustion in the modern sense; meaning is renewed through ritual and storytelling; anomie is rare in small, integrated groups; the threat is cultural extinction, not exhaustion.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: The cosmos is animate: spirits inhabit animals, plants, landforms, and the dead; the world is layered and interconnected; human existence is embedded in this larger order; outlook is shaped by survival anxiety but also by trust in spiritual guidance.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Ritual marks life transitions, seasonal events, and critical moments (successful hunts, deaths, crises); practices include offerings, taboo observance, and probably trance or shamanic performance; devotion is collective.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: No institutional religion; shamans or ritual specialists hold knowledge but not coercive authority; doctrine is oral and locally variable; no orthodoxy.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Isolation limits diffusion; each refugial population may develop distinctive spiritual traditions; syncretism occurs when bands merge; no displacement by conquest; spiritual evolution is local and continuous.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Disenchantment is absent; the world remains enchanted; the sacred suffuses daily life; no secular sphere exists; spiritual need is continuously met; the threat is extinction of the tradition-bearing group, not erosion of belief.
