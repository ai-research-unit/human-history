    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 50000 BCE – 1200 BCE

    Region: The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins — the two great river systems of eastern China, from the loess plateau and North China Plain drained by the Yellow River (Huang He) to the Yangtze (Chang Jiang) lowlands, middle basin, and Sichuan; bounded by the Pacific coast to the east and the highlands of the interior to the west.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Upper Paleolithic cave sites, Neolithic village sites with exceptional preservation (Hemudu, Banpo), Longshan walled settlements, and early Bronze Age palatial complexes (Erlitou, Erligang). Ceramic sequences well established. Oracle bone inscriptions appear ~1200 BCE, marking the threshold to written record. Window ends as Shang dynasty administration adopts writing.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Upper Paleolithic Settlement (50000–25000 BCE)

    Modern Human Arrival: Anatomically modern humans present in East Asia by ~50,000–40,000 BP; replacement of archaic populations (Denisovan admixture in some lineages).

    Cave Sites: Zhoukoudian Upper Cave (~30,000 BP); Tianyuan Cave (~40,000 BP); skeletal remains and artifacts.

    Blade Industries: Regional Upper Paleolithic industries; blade and bladelet technologies; some prepared-core techniques.

    Environment: Fluctuating between warm and cold phases; deciduous and coniferous forests; large game (deer, wild cattle, elephants in south).

    Population: Low density; mobile bands; extensive territories.

    Material Culture: Bone and antler tools; personal ornaments (beads, pendants); ochre use.

    2. Last Glacial Maximum Adaptation (25000–12000 BCE)

    LGM Climate: ~26,500–19,000 BP; cooler and drier; expanded grasslands; reduced forest cover.

    Population Distribution: Concentration in favorable areas; Yellow River valley, Yangtze lowlands as refugia.

    Microblade Technology: Distinctive microblade industries develop in northern China; composite tools.

    Subsistence: Large and small game; plant gathering intensifies; broad-spectrum foraging.

    Late Glacial Warming: After ~15,000 BP, climate ameliorates; forests expand; resources increase.

    Regional Differentiation: Northern microblade traditions distinct from southern pebble-tool and blade industries.

    3. Terminal Pleistocene and Incipient Cultivation (12000–8000 BCE)

    Post-Glacial Warming: Rapid climate amelioration; forest expansion; monsoon strengthening.

    Early Pottery: Among world's earliest pottery; Xianrendong, Yuchanyan caves (~18,000–16,000 BP); predates agriculture.

    Wild Rice Management: Middle-lower Yangtze; wild rice exploitation; incipient cultivation by ~10,000 BCE.

    Millet Beginnings: Northern China; wild millet (foxtail, broomcorn) gathered; manipulation begins.

    Sedentism Increases: Longer site occupation; storage features; investment in place.

    Technology: Ground stone tools; pottery for cooking and storage; fishing intensifies.

    4. Early Neolithic and Agricultural Origins (8000–6000 BCE)

    Rice Domestication: Lower Yangtze; Kuahuqiao, Hemudu cultures; fully domesticated rice by ~6000 BCE.

    Millet Domestication: Yellow River valley; Cishan, Peiligang cultures; foxtail and broomcorn millet.

    Pig Domestication: Pigs domesticated independently in both regions; central to economy.

    Village Formation: Permanent villages; semi-subterranean houses; storage pits; cemeteries.

    Pottery Traditions: Cord-marked, painted, and plain wares; regional styles emerge.

    Population Growth: Agricultural productivity supports larger populations; village clusters form.

    5. Middle Neolithic Expansion (6000–4500 BCE)

    Yangshao Culture: Yellow River valley ~5000–3000 BCE; painted pottery; large villages (Banpo, Jiangzhai).

    Hemudu Culture: Lower Yangtze ~5000–4500 BCE; pile dwellings; rice paddies; sophisticated woodworking.

    Daxi Culture: Middle Yangtze ~5000–3300 BCE; rice cultivation; distinctive pottery.

    Regional Ceramic Traditions: Painted (Yangshao), cord-marked (north), and other regional styles.

    Population Expansion: Agricultural populations spread; forest clearance; new territories occupied.

    Social Organization: Village-based; lineage structures; modest differentiation in burials.

    6. Late Neolithic Intensification (4500–3500 BCE)

    Yangshao Florescence: Largest villages; elaborate painted pottery; ceremonial structures.

    Jade Working Begins: Early jade artifacts; precursor to later elaboration.

    Hongshan Culture: Northeast China ~4700–2900 BCE; jade working; ceremonial architecture; goddess figurines.

    Inter-Regional Exchange: Increased movement of prestige goods; jade, pottery, obsidian.

    Craft Specialization: Pottery production intensifies; some attached specialists.

    Mortuary Differentiation: Some burials with more goods; emerging inequality.

    7. Longshan Horizon (3500–2500 BCE)

    Longshan Culture: Yellow River ~3000–2000 BCE; black burnished pottery; walled settlements; wells.

    Liangzhu Culture: Lower Yangtze ~3300–2300 BCE; elaborate jade work (cong, bi, axes); elite burials; proto-urban.

    Walled Settlements: Defensive walls of rammed earth; inter-polity conflict; fortified centers.

    Social Stratification: Sharp inequalities in burial goods; elite-commoner distinction crystallizes.

    Craft Specialization: Jade workshops; pottery kilns; specialized production zones.

    Warfare: Weapons in burials; violent deaths; competitive polities.

    8. Late Longshan and Proto-State (2500–2000 BCE)

    Large Centers: Taosi, Shimao; monumental construction; urban-scale populations.

    Shimao: Northern Shaanxi ~2300–1800 BCE; massive stone walls; jade in walls; elite precinct.

    Elite Burials: Elaborate tomb construction; jade, pottery, sacrificial victims.

    Bronze Experimentation: Earliest bronze objects appear; metallurgy adopted from northwest.

    Political Competition: Multiple competing polities; no single dominant center.

    Collapse/Transformation: Liangzhu collapses ~2300 BCE (flooding?); power shifts to Yellow River valley.

    9. Erlitou and First State (2000–1600 BCE)

    Erlitou: Henan ~1900–1500 BCE; largest site; palatial compounds; specialized workshops.

    Palatial Complex: Monumental buildings; enclosed courtyards; elite residence and ritual.

    Bronze Casting: First large-scale bronze production; ritual vessels (jue, jia); weapons.

    Territorial Expansion: Erlitou influence extends across Central Plains; network of secondary sites.

    Xia Tradition: Later texts describe Xia dynasty; possible historical kernel; debate continues.

    State Formation: Administrative complexity; tribute extraction; elite ideology.

    10. Erligang Expansion (1600–1400 BCE)

    Erligang Phase: Zhengzhou capital ~1600–1400 BCE; massive rammed-earth walls; palatial zone.

    Bronze Industry: Large-scale production; ritual vessel sets; weapons standardized.

    Network Expansion: Erligang-style bronzes at distant sites (Panlongcheng, Sanxingdui); exchange or control.

    State Consolidation: Administrative infrastructure; writing likely exists but not preserved (bamboo, wood).

    Transition to Late Shang: Shift from Zhengzhou to Anyang region.

    Religion and Ritual: Ancestor veneration; sacrificial practices; shamanic elements.

    11. Shang Consolidation at Anyang (1400–1250 BCE)

    Yinxu (Anyang): Royal capital ~1300–1046 BCE; palace-temple complex; royal cemetery.

    Bronze Ritual: Elaborate vessel sets; piece-mold casting technique; distinctive styles.

    Elite Mortuary Complex: Royal tombs with sacrificial victims (hundreds); chariots; jade; bronze.

    Craft Workshops: Bronze, bone, jade, pottery production zones; attached specialists.

    Social Hierarchy: King, aristocracy, commoners, slaves; sharp stratification.

    Pre-Writing State: Administration functions orally; records likely on perishable materials.

    12. Oracle Bone Divination and Writing Threshold (1250–1200 BCE)

    Pyromantic Divination: Heating turtle plastrons and cattle scapulae; cracks interpreted.

    Oracle Bone Inscriptions: Questions to ancestors inscribed on bones; dates, topics, outcomes.

    Writing System: Logographic script; ~4,000+ characters; grammar matches later Chinese.

    Royal Archive: Inscriptions preserve royal divinations; historical events recorded.

    Institutional Writing: Writing serves state administration; calendar, military, agriculture, tribute.

    Oral Threshold: Writing becomes the medium Shang institutions run on; marks end of oral stage.

    13. Jade as Sacred Medium (50000–1200 BCE)

    Earliest Jade: Nephrite and jadeite artifacts from Neolithic onward; inherent value.

    Hongshan Jade: Northeast China ~4700–2900 BCE; elaborate forms (pig-dragons, cloud-shaped plaques).

    Liangzhu Jade: Lower Yangtze ~3300–2300 BCE; cong (tubes), bi (discs), axes; massive quantities in elite burials.

    Symbolic Continuity: Jade forms and meanings persist into Bronze Age; Shang jade continues traditions.

    Sacred Authority: Jade connects human and spirit worlds; marks legitimate power; ancestral sanction.

    Oral Context: Jade objects meaningful within oral narratives of origin, authority, and cosmos.
