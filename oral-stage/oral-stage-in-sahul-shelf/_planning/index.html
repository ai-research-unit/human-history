    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Sahul Shelf

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 65000 BCE – 1930 CE

    Region: The Sahul Shelf — the greater landmass comprising Australia, New Guinea, and Tasmania that formed a single continent during Pleistocene low sea stands; including the tropical north, desert interior, temperate southeast, and the highlands of New Guinea.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Pleistocene occupation sites (Madjedbebe, Lake Mungo), rock art sequences spanning tens of thousands of years, shell middens, stone tool assemblages, and megafaunal extinction layers. Indigenous Australian and New Guinean oral traditions preserve deep-time knowledge. No indigenous writing system existed before European contact. Window ends when colonial administrative records become the dominant medium (~1930 CE in remote areas).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Initial Colonization (65000–50000 BCE)

    Maritime Crossing: First humans reached Sahul via deliberate water crossings from Sunda (Southeast Asia); multiple crossings likely; distance of 60–90 km between islands required watercraft.

    Madjedbebe (Northern Territory): Earliest reliably dated site ~65,000 BP; ground-edge axes; ochre processing; edge-ground technology earlier than anywhere else.

    Genetic Evidence: Mitochondrial and Y-chromosome lineages indicate founding population; deep genetic continuity with modern Aboriginal Australians.

    Coastal Occupation: Sea level ~60–70 m lower than present; much early coastal evidence now submerged.

    Lake Mungo (NSW): ~40,000–42,000 BP; cremation (world's oldest known); ritual behavior; ochre burials.

    Rapid Spread: Archaeological evidence suggests occupation across Australia within 5,000–10,000 years of initial arrival.

    2. Pleistocene Expansion (50000–35000 BCE)

    Continental Spread: Sites across all major environmental zones by ~35,000 BP; desert, tropics, temperate zones, New Guinea highlands.

    Tasmanian Occupation: Tasmania reached by ~40,000 BP; land bridge connection allowed pedestrian access.

    Technology: Core and flake industries; edge-ground axes (earliest globally); bone tools; ochre use widespread.

    Rock Art Beginnings: Some cupules and engravings may date to this period; difficult to date directly.

    Population: Low density; highly mobile bands; extensive territorial ranges.

    Environmental Knowledge: Adaptation to vastly different environments indicates sophisticated ecological knowledge transmitted orally.

    3. Megafaunal Interaction (35000–20000 BCE)

    Megafauna Present: Giant wombats (Diprotodon), giant kangaroos (Procoptodon), marsupial lion (Thylacoleo), giant birds (Genyornis), giant reptiles.

    Extinction Timing: Most megafauna extinct by ~46,000–40,000 BP in mainland; some persist longer in Tasmania; overlap with humans debated.

    Causation Debate: Climate change, human hunting, fire-stick farming, or combination; evidence contested.

    Genyornis Eggshell: Burn patterns suggest human consumption; supports hunting hypothesis.

    Fire Regimes: Increased charcoal in sediments correlates with human arrival; landscape burning alters vegetation.

    Oral Memory: Some suggest oral traditions preserve megafaunal memory (controversial; difficult to verify).

    4. Last Glacial Maximum (20000–12000 BCE)

    Peak Aridity: LGM ~26,500–19,000 BP; expanded deserts; contracted forests; water scarcity.

    Population Contraction: Many inland areas abandoned or sparsely occupied; refugia in coastal and well-watered areas.

    Tasmanian Isolation: Sea level drop creates land bridge initially; rising waters isolate Tasmania ~14,000 BP.

    Adapted Technologies: Portable grinding stones; seed processing; drought-adapted subsistence.

    Social Networks: Long-distance exchange maintained despite low populations; ceremonial gathering sites.

    New Guinea Highlands: Occupied but at lower elevations; upward expansion post-LGM.

    5. Post-Glacial Transformation (12000–8000 BCE)

    Sea Level Rise: ~120 m rise drowns vast coastal plains; Australia's coastline approaches modern configuration.

    Island Formation: Tasmania separates ~14,000 BP; New Guinea separates ~8,000 BP; many smaller islands created.

    Coastal Adaptation: Shell middens increase; maritime economies intensify along new coastlines.

    Forest Expansion: Woodlands and rainforests expand; different resource bases available.

    Population Redistribution: Interior reoccupied as conditions ameliorate; new territories along coasts.

    Tasmanian Divergence: Isolated population develops distinct material culture; bone point loss; simpler tool kit.

    6. Early Holocene Stabilization (8000–4000 BCE)

    Modern Climate: Holocene climate optimum; relatively stable conditions; modern vegetation patterns establish.

    Regional Adaptations: Distinct regional cultures crystallize; coastal, riverine, desert, highland specializations.

    Shell Middens: Extensive midden accumulation indicates intensive coastal resource use.

    Technology: Ground-edge axes remain; bone tools; plant-fiber technologies (inferred from later ethnography).

    New Guinea Highlands: Population growth in highlands; early evidence of plant management.

    Social Organization: Territorial boundaries likely more defined; seasonal aggregation patterns.

    7. Mid-Holocene Intensification (4000–1500 BCE)

    Kuk Swamp Agriculture: New Guinea highlands show wetland cultivation by ~7000 BP; taro, yam, banana; among world's earliest independent agricultural origins.

    Australian Intensification: Population growth; increased site density; resource intensification without agriculture.

    Dingo Arrival: Dingo enters Australia ~3,500–4,000 BP; introduced from Southeast Asia; thylacine extinction on mainland.

    Backed Artefacts: Small backed blades appear widely; composite tool technologies.

    Exchange Networks: Shell ornaments, ochre, stone traded over hundreds of kilometers.

    Ceremony and Gathering: Large ceremonial gatherings documented ethnographically may have deep roots.

    8. Late Holocene Regional Diversification (1500 BCE – 500 CE)

    Population Growth: Archaeological site density increases; population estimates rise.

    Small Tool Tradition: Backed blades, microliths, tula adzes regionally distributed.

    Rock Art Elaboration: Regional styles flourish; X-ray art in Arnhem Land; Wandjina figures emerge.

    Aquatic Resources: Fish traps, weirs, eel traps constructed; Lake Condah eel aquaculture.

    Ceremonial Complexity: Evidence for increased ceremonial activity; stone arrangements.

    New Guinea: Highland societies intensify; pig husbandry; ceremonial exchange systems.

    9. Complex Economies and Social Elaboration (500–1200 CE)

    Aquaculture Systems: Lake Condah (Victoria) eel traps and channels (~6,600 BP origin, elaborated later); Brewarrina fish traps (NSW); systematic management.

    Inter-Group Ceremony: Large ceremonial gatherings (corroborees) for exchange, marriage, dispute resolution.

    Kinship Networks: Complex moiety and section systems regulate marriage and obligation; oral transmission essential.

    Regional Specialization: Some groups specialize in producing trade goods (ochre, stone axes, fiber).

    New Guinea Big-Man Systems: Competitive exchange; pig feasts; oratory and display.

    Oral Tradition: Songlines, Dreaming narratives structure landscape knowledge; initiation transmits specialized knowledge.

    10. Expanding Exchange Networks (1200–1600 CE)

    Long-Distance Trade: Stone axes traded 500+ km; pearl shell, ochre, pituri (native tobacco) circulate widely.

    Torres Strait Exchange: Torres Strait Islanders mediate between New Guinea and Australia; maritime specialists.

    Seasonal Gatherings: Bunya festivals (Queensland); bogong moth feasts (Alps); large-scale aggregations.

    Pre-Contact Complexity: Population estimates 500,000–1,000,000+ for Australia; sophisticated land management.

    Macassan Contact Beginnings: Indonesian (Macassan) trepangers may begin visiting northern Australia by late period.

    Oral Archives: Dreaming narratives encode ecological knowledge, territorial boundaries, law, and history.

    11. Macassan Contact (1600–1788 CE)

    Trepang Industry: Macassans from Sulawesi harvest sea cucumber (trepang) along northern Australian coast annually.

    Material Exchange: Aboriginal people receive iron, cloth, tobacco, rice; provide labor and local knowledge.

    Cultural Influence: Macassan words enter local languages; didgeridoo possibly influenced by Macassan pipes; rock art depicts Macassan boats.

    Population Dynamics: Introduced disease possible but limited evidence; trade relationships formalized.

    Tamarind Trees: Planted by Macassans; archaeological signature of camps.

    Limited Agricultural Adoption: Despite contact, Aboriginal economies remain non-agricultural; complex reasons.

    12. British Colonization and Initial Disruption (1788–1850 CE)

    First Fleet (1788): British colonization begins at Sydney Cove; Indigenous sovereignty not recognized.

    Epidemic Disease: Smallpox (1789) devastates Sydney region populations; later waves of measles, influenza, tuberculosis.

    Frontier Violence: Warfare, massacres, and guerrilla resistance; Pemulwuy, Windradyne, and others resist.

    Dispossession: Land seized; traditional economies disrupted; population collapse begins.

    Mission and Fringe-Dwelling: Some Aboriginal people move to missions or town fringes; cultural disruption.

    Oral Tradition Persists: Despite disruption, oral transmission continues in many areas.

    13. Colonial Expansion and Indigenous Dispossession (1850–1900 CE)

    Pastoral Frontier: Sheep and cattle stations expand across continent; Aboriginal labor often exploited.

    Gold Rush Impact: Population influx; further dispossession; violent conflicts.

    Reserve System: Aboriginal people concentrated on reserves; movement restricted; cultural practices suppressed.

    Population Nadir: Aboriginal population reaches lowest point (~93,000 by 1901); some areas near extinction.

    Oral Traditions Stressed: Elders die; ceremonies disrupted; but knowledge transmission continues in remote areas.

    European Documentation: Anthropologists begin recording languages and traditions; written record supplements oral.

    14. Administration Consolidation and Oral Twilight (1900–1930 CE)

    Protection Policies: Aboriginal Protection Boards control lives; "protection" often means institutionalization.

    Mission Era: Missions consolidate; children separated from families; language suppression.

    Remote Persistence: Central and Western Desert, Arnhem Land largely uncontrolled; oral cultures continue.

    Anthropological Documentation: Spencer, Gillen, Radcliffe-Brown document traditions; oral knowledge enters written archive.

    Oral Threshold: By ~1930, government administrative records become the medium institutions run on for most Aboriginal people; oral culture persists but within documented colonial framework.

    New Guinea: Australian administration from 1906/1920; similar transition occurs later (1960s–1970s).

    15. Rock Art as Continuous Archive (65000 BCE – 1930 CE)

    Earliest Art: Cupules, engravings at Madjedbebe; ochre use from first occupation.

    Gwion Gwion (Bradshaw) Figures: Kimberley; ~17,000–12,000 BP; elegant human figures; cultural discontinuity debated.

    X-Ray Art: Arnhem Land; depicts internal anatomy of animals; ~4,000 BP onward.

    Wandjina: Kimberley rain spirits; repainted through time; oral narratives explain meaning.

    Stencil Art: Hand stencils widespread; personal marks across millennia.

    Contact Art: Ships, horses, Europeans depicted; continuous tradition into colonial era.

    Oral Context: Rock art meaningless without oral narrative; images are mnemonic prompts for stories held in memory.
