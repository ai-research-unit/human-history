    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Southern African Plateau

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 150000 BCE – 1945 CE

    Region: The Southern African Plateau — the elevated interior of southern Africa including the highveld, Kalahari, and Drakensberg escarpment; bounded by the Limpopo River to the north, Atlantic coast to the west, Indian Ocean coast to the east, and Cape fold belt to the south; encompassing modern South Africa, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Namibia, Lesotho, and Eswatini.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Middle Stone Age sites with early symbolic behavior (Blombos Cave, Pinnacle Point), Later Stone Age rock art, Iron Age settlements and stone ruins (Great Zimbabwe, Mapungubwe), and historical-period sites. San rock art and oral traditions document deep-time knowledge. Portuguese, Dutch, and British sources provide external documentation from ~1500 CE. Indigenous societies remained primarily oral until colonial education expanded (~1945 CE for remote areas).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Middle Stone Age Origins (150000–100000 BCE)

    Anatomically Modern Humans: Southern Africa likely a key region for Homo sapiens evolution; Florisbad, Klasies River Mouth provide early skeletal evidence.

    Early MSA Technology: Prepared-core techniques (Levallois); points, blades, scrapers; more sophisticated than Acheulean.

    Coastal Adaptation: Pinnacle Point (South Africa) shows shellfish exploitation by ~160,000 BP; marine resource use early and significant.

    Ochre Use: Red ochre processing at early sites suggests symbolic or functional applications.

    Population Dynamics: Glacial-period population bottlenecks possible; southern Africa may have been refugium.

    Climate Context: Marine isotope stages fluctuate; interglacial and glacial phases affect resources.

    2. Middle Stone Age Florescence (100000–40000 BCE)

    Blombos Cave: ~100,000–70,000 BP; engraved ochre blocks; shell beads; bone tools; sophisticated symbolic behavior.

    Still Bay Industry: ~75,000–71,000 BP; bifacially worked points; distinctive technology.

    Howiesons Poort: ~65,000–59,000 BP; backed geometric pieces; compound tools; innovation pulse.

    Symbolic Revolution: Shell beads, ochre crayons, engraved objects indicate language, planning, social identity marking.

    Toba Eruption (~74,000 BP): Global climate impact; debate over southern African effects; possible population contraction.

    Technological Variability: Multiple industries suggest dynamic adaptation; not linear progression.

    3. Late MSA and LSA Transition (40000–20000 BCE)

    Transitional Technologies: Post-Howiesons Poort assemblages; gradual shift toward microlithic tools.

    Population Continuity: Genetic evidence suggests continuous population; no major replacement.

    Climate Fluctuations: Last Glacial Maximum approaching; cooler and drier conditions.

    Rock Art Beginnings: Some painted stones and engravings may date to this period; art tradition emerging.

    Subsistence: Continued broad-spectrum foraging; large game, small game, plant foods.

    Regional Variation: Different technological traditions in different ecological zones.

    4. Later Stone Age Hunter-Gatherers (20000–2000 BCE)

    San Ancestors: Genetic and cultural continuity with later Khoisan peoples; click languages; foraging lifeway.

    Rock Art Florescence: Southern African rock art tradition; Drakensberg paintings; ritual, trance, shamanic imagery.

    Wilton Tradition: ~8000–2000 BCE; small backed tools (segments, crescents); bow and arrow hunting.

    Stable Adaptation: Relatively stable foraging lifestyle; seasonal mobility; band organization.

    LGM Refugia: During Last Glacial Maximum, populations contract; coastal and well-watered areas occupied.

    Holocene Expansion: Post-glacial population growth; reoccupation of interior.

    5. Pastoral Arrival and Early Bantu (2000 BCE – 500 CE)

    Khoekhoe Herders: Sheep and cattle pastoralists arrive ~2000 BCE; spread through western southern Africa; distinct from San foragers.

    Khoisan Interaction: Complex relationships between herders and foragers; intermarriage, conflict, exchange.

    Early Bantu Farmers: Bantu-speaking farmers with iron, cattle, crops reach eastern southern Africa ~300 CE; Kwale, Nkope traditions.

    Iron Technology: Smelting and forging transform economy; axes, hoes, weapons.

    Cattle Economy: Cattle as wealth, bridewealth, political capital; central to emerging societies.

    Pottery Traditions: Distinctive ceramic styles mark cultural groups; stylistic boundaries.

    6. Early Iron Age Expansion (500–1000 CE)

    Bantu Settlement Consolidates: Agricultural villages spread across eastern plateau; mixed farming economy.

    Toutswe Tradition: ~700–1300 CE; eastern Botswana; cattle posts; social hierarchy based on cattle.

    Cattle Accumulation: Elite control of cattle herds; loans, clientship, political power.

    Settlement Patterns: Central Cattle Pattern (Huffman); spatial organization reflects social structure.

    Regional Exchange: Iron, copper, pottery, salt traded between regions.

    San Marginalization: Hunter-gatherers pushed to marginal areas (Kalahari, mountains); some absorption.

    7. Zimbabwe Tradition and State Formation (1000–1450 CE)

    Mapungubwe: ~1075–1290 CE; first state in southern Africa; hilltop elite residence; gold burials; Indian Ocean trade connections.

    Great Zimbabwe: ~1270–1450 CE; capital of Zimbabwe state; massive stone walls; 18,000+ population; gold and ivory exports.

    Indian Ocean Trade: Connection to Swahili coast (Kilwa); gold, ivory exchanged for glass beads, cloth, ceramics.

    Social Hierarchy: Sacred kingship; elite-commoner distinction; cattle and gold as wealth.

    Stone Architecture: Drystone walling technique; enclosures, towers; political and religious symbolism.

    Collapse/Transformation: Great Zimbabwe abandoned ~1450; ecological, political, or economic causes debated.

    8. Mutapa, Rozvi, and Late Iron Age (1450–1700 CE)

    Mutapa State: ~1450–1760; northern Zimbabwe; successor to Great Zimbabwe tradition; Portuguese contact from 1505.

    Portuguese Presence: Trading posts on Zambezi; prazo system; limited territorial control; firearms introduced.

    Rozvi State: ~1680–1830s; emerges under Changamire Dombo; defeats Portuguese; dominates Zimbabwe plateau.

    Khami Tradition: Stone-walled sites of Rozvi period; continuity with Zimbabwe tradition.

    Regional Interaction: Competition between states; trade routes; cattle raiding.

    Oral Traditions: Royal genealogies, spirit mediums (mhondoro) preserve history; transmitted orally.

    9. Mfecane Disruption (1700–1840 CE)

    Zulu State Formation: Shaka kaMenzangakhona (r. ~1816–1828) creates centralized military state; amabutho regiments; conquest and incorporation.

    Mfecane/Difaqane: Cascade of displacement, conflict, and state formation across southern Africa; Ndebele, Sotho, Swazi, Gaza emerge.

    Boer Trek (1835–1840): Dutch-speaking settlers move inland; clash with African states; establish Boer republics.

    Cape Colony: British control from 1806; frontier conflicts with Xhosa; missionary activity.

    Violence and Displacement: Massive population disruption; refugees, consolidation, new polities.

    Oral Tradition Under Stress: Disruption affects transmission; but also generates new historical narratives (izibongo praise poetry).

    10. Colonial Conquest and Mineral Revolution (1840–1910 CE)

    Diamond Discovery (1867): Kimberley; transforms regional economy; migrant labor system begins.

    Gold Discovery (1886): Witwatersrand; Johannesburg; massive capital inflow; British-Boer tensions.

    Zulu Defeat: Anglo-Zulu War (1879); Isandlwana, Ulundi; Zulu independence ends.

    Rhodesia: British South Africa Company; occupation of Zimbabwe (1890); land seizure; Ndebele and Shona defeated.

    South African War (1899–1902): British vs. Boers; concentration camps; British victory.

    Reserve System: African populations confined to reserves; labor extraction for mines and farms.

    11. Segregation and Oral Twilight (1910–1945 CE)

    Union of South Africa (1910): White minority rule; Natives Land Act (1913) restricts African land ownership.

    Pass Laws: Movement control; labor regulation; bureaucratic documentation intensifies.

    Mission Education: Limited literacy spreads; oral tradition persists but literate record increasingly dominant.

    Migrant Labor System: Oscillating migration between reserves and mines; social disruption.

    Southern Rhodesia: Settler rule; Land Apportionment Act (1930); similar segregation patterns.

    Administrative Records Dominate: By ~1945, government documentation is the medium institutions run on; oral tradition persists within communities but subordinate to written administrative system.
