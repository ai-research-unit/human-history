    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Andean Cordillera and Pacific Coast

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 14000 BCE – 600 CE

    Region: The Andean Cordillera and Pacific Coast — from the tropical coasts of Ecuador through the desert littoral of Peru and northern Chile, the highland valleys and altiplano from Colombia to Bolivia, and the western slopes that connect coast to sierra.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Initial Settlement (14000–11000 BCE)

    Monte Verde, Chile: The defining site for early South American occupation. Radiocarbon dates from the MV-II layer cluster around 14,500–14,200 cal BP (Dillehay 1989, 1997). Artifacts include wooden tools, plant remains (including wild potato), mastodon bone, and residential structures. MV-I may extend occupation to ~18,500 BP but remains contested.

    Coastal Migration Hypothesis: The Pacific coastal route is now the predominant model for initial peopling (Erlandson et al. 2007; Braje et al. 2017). Marine isotope evidence and site distributions support a kelp-highway model with rapid southward movement along productive coastlines.

    Fishtail Points: The diagnostic projectile-point tradition of earliest South America, distributed from Ecuador to Patagonia by ~13,000–11,000 BP. Associated with megafauna hunting (mastodon, ground sloth, horse) at sites like Tagua Tagua (Chile) and Tibitó (Colombia).

    Highland Entry: Evidence from Guitarrero Cave (Peru, ~12,500 BP) and Pachamachay (Peru, ~11,800 BP) shows early use of high-altitude environments for hunting and plant gathering.

    2. Terminal Pleistocene Transition (11000–8000 BCE)

    Megafaunal Extinction: The South American megafauna (mastodon, ground sloths, native horses, glyptodonts) disappeared by ~10,000 BP. Causes debated; most models invoke human hunting pressure combined with rapid climate change during the Younger Dryas and early Holocene (Barnosky & Lindsey 2010).

    Younger Dryas (12,900–11,700 BP): Brief cold reversal affected Andean glaciers and vegetation zones. Impact on human populations less clear than in North America, but possible contributor to economic restructuring.

    Paiján Complex (Peru): North coast lithic tradition (11,500–8000 BP) characterized by elongated stemmed points. Associated with diverse subsistence: marine resources, land mammals, plant foods. Sites include Pampa de los Fósiles, Quirihuac.

    Early Archaic Diversification: Post-megafaunal economies diversified into regional niches: coastal shellfish collecting, highland hunting of camelids and deer, seasonal transhumance patterns (Rick 1980).

    3. Maritime Intensification and Chinchorro (8000–5000 BCE)

    Chinchorro Culture (northern Chile/southern Peru): Defined by artificial mummification beginning ~7000 BP — the oldest known deliberate mummification tradition globally. Black mummies (~7000–4800 BP), red mummies (~4800–3700 BP), and mud-coated variants. Sites: Arica, Camarones (Arriaza 1995; Marquet et al. 2012).

    Marine Resource Dependence: Stable isotope analysis shows Chinchorro populations derived 80–90% of protein from marine sources (fish, shellfish, marine mammals). Bone pathologies (auditory exostoses) confirm diving for shellfish.

    Ring Site Complexes: Shell middens arranged in rings or arcs along the Peruvian and Chilean coasts (8000–4000 BP). Represent repeated seasonal aggregations. Examples: La Paloma, Chilca.

    Las Vegas Culture (Ecuador, 10,000–6000 BP): Early Holocene coastal adaptation with evidence of plant management (bottle gourd, squash) before full domestication. Site 80 burials show sedentism and territorial marking.

    Technological Markers: Fishhooks (bone, shell), harpoons, net weights, reed watercraft (caballitos de totora precursors). Cotton not yet available; nets made from wild plant fibers.

    4. Highland Domestication (6000–3000 BCE)

    Potato Domestication: Solanum tuberosum domesticated in the Lake Titicaca basin, with genetic and archaeological evidence placing the process between 8000 and 5000 BP (Spooner et al. 2005). Wild progenitors (S. brevicaule complex) still grow in the region.

    Quinoa and Chenopods: Chenopodium quinoa domesticated in the altiplano, with clear morphological changes by ~5000 BP. Cañihua (C. pallidicaule) also domesticated. Sites: Chiripa, Qaluyu.

    Other Highland Crops: Common bean (Phaseolus vulgaris) domesticated in both Mesoamerica and Andes independently. Oca, ulluco, mashua (tubers) added to the highland complex. Chili peppers (Capsicum spp.) domesticated early, possibly by 8000 BP.

    Camelid Domestication: Llama (Lama glama) and alpaca (Vicugna pacos) domesticated from wild guanaco and vicuña between 6000 and 4000 BP. Evidence from Telarmachay (Junín puna) shows gradual shift from hunting to herding by ~4000 BP (Wheeler 1984; Mengoni Goñalons 2008). Llamas for transport/meat; alpacas for fiber.

    Guitarrero Cave: High-altitude site (2580 m) with evidence of plant cultivation (beans, chili, lucuma) by 8000 BP. Demonstrates early highland horticulture alongside hunting-gathering.

    Seasonal Transhumance: Herder-cultivator economies emerged with vertical movement between puna pastures and valley fields. Pattern persists into ethnographic present.

    5. Preceramic Monumentality (3500–1800 BCE)

    Norte Chico/Caral-Supe Civilization: Cluster of ~30 major sites in the Supe, Pativilca, Fortaleza, and Huaura valleys. Flourished 3000–1800 BCE. Distinguished by monumental architecture (platform mounds, sunken circular courts) in the complete absence of ceramics (Shady Solís 2006).

    Caral: The largest Norte Chico site; 66-hectare urban core with six major platform mounds (largest: Pirámide Mayor, 18 m high, 150 × 160 m base). Population estimates: 3,000–20,000 in the valley system. Radiocarbon dates: 2627–2020 BCE for monumental construction.

    Maritime-Agricultural Foundation: Economy combined cotton and gourd cultivation (for fishing technology) with intensive marine harvesting. Maize present but minor. The "maritime foundations of Andean civilization" model (Moseley 1975) holds that marine protein sustained early complexity.

    Kotosh Religious Tradition: Highland architectural tradition (Huánuco region) with small chambers featuring crossed-hands reliefs and ritual hearths. Kotosh temple: ~2000 BCE. Related to later Initial Period traditions.

    Social Organization: Evidence of labor mobilization, surplus accumulation, and ideological coordination without state apparatus or writing. Quipu precursors possible but unconfirmed.

    Absence of Pottery and Weaving: Neither ceramics nor loom-woven textiles present; cotton processed by twining/netting. Pottery appears only after 1800 BCE.

    6. Initial Period (1800–900 BCE)

    Pottery Introduction: Ceramics appear on the coast ~1800 BCE, likely diffusing from Ecuador/Colombia where earlier traditions exist (Valdivia, 3500 BCE). Adoption rapid along north and central coasts.

    Cupisnique Culture: North coast tradition (1500–500 BCE) with stirrup-spout vessels, feline iconography, goldwork. Precursor and partial contemporary of Chavín. Sites: Caballo Muerto, Huaca de los Reyes.

    U-Shaped Ceremonial Centers: Coastal architectural template with central mound, flanking wings, and sunken court. Examples: Garagay, Cardal, La Florida. Built 1800–800 BCE; many burned and abandoned.

    Chavín Precursors: Highland temple traditions with Kotosh-style features and increasing iconographic complexity. Emergence of jaguar/serpent/raptor imagery.

    Maize Intensification: Maize (introduced earlier from Mesoamerica) becomes agronomically significant during Initial Period. Irrigation systems expand.

    Metallurgy: Gold and copper working begins; hammering and annealing techniques. Ornaments appear in elite contexts.

    7. Chavín Phenomenon (900–200 BCE)

    Chavín de Huántar: Major ceremonial center at 3180 m in the Mosna Valley (Ancash). Old Temple (~900–500 BCE) and New Temple (~500–200 BCE) phases. Lanzón monolith (4.5 m granite stela with Staff God image) in central gallery.

    Staff God/Fanged Deity: Central iconographic complex featuring a frontal supernatural with staff/s, feline attributes, serpent hair, and elaborate regalia. Diffused across coastal and highland sites.

    Spheres of Influence: Chavín-style iconography, ceramics, textiles, and metallurgy found from Ecuador to the south coast of Peru. Not a political empire but a religious/ideological network, possibly with oracle and pilgrimage functions (Burger 1992).

    Strombus and Spondylus: Ritual importance of marine shells — Strombus (Pacific coast of Ecuador) for trumpets, Spondylus for offerings. Long-distance exchange networks documented.

    Technological Developments: Improved metallurgy (gold-silver alloys, repoussé, soldering). Textile innovations. Expanded irrigation.

    Collapse: Chavín de Huántar shows evidence of destruction and abandonment ~200 BCE. Regional traditions diverge (Early Intermediate Period begins).

    8. Paracas Culture (700 BCE – 1 CE)

    Paracas Peninsula (Ica coast): Two main phases. Paracas Cavernas (~700–300 BCE): shaft tombs, incised polychrome pottery, early textiles. Paracas Necropolis (~300 BCE–1 CE): bundle burials with spectacular embroidered mantles.

    Textile Mastery: Paracas Necropolis textiles among the finest prehistoric fabrics known. Embroidered figures (supernatural beings, animals, shamanic transformation scenes) in up to 190 colors. Burial bundles contain 50–100 textile offerings.

    Cranial Modification: Deliberate cranial shaping widespread. Multiple styles (elongation, broadening) potentially marking ethnic or status distinctions.

    Iconography: Oculate Being (frontal figure with large eyes, streaming appendages) dominant motif. Continuity into Nazca tradition.

    Mortuary Focus: Extreme investment in burial goods indicates ancestor veneration and afterlife beliefs. Little evidence of monumental architecture.

    Trophy Heads: Decapitation and head-taking documented; heads modified as ritual objects.

    9. Moche Culture (100 BCE – 600 CE)

    Geographic Extent: North coast of Peru, from Piura to Huarmey valleys. Not a unified state; probably multiple polities sharing culture (Quilter & Castillo 2010).

    Huacas del Sol y de la Luna (Moche Valley): Dual pyramidal complex. Huaca del Sol: largest adobe structure in the Americas (340 × 160 m base, originally 40+ m high, ~143 million adobes). Huaca de la Luna: elaborate polychrome murals of Ai Apaec (Decapitator God).

    Ceramic Portraiture: Stirrup-spout portrait vessels with individualized human faces — unique in pre-Columbian art. Also elaborate narrative scenes (combat, sacrifice, supernatural beings).

    Metallurgy: Advanced gilding, silver-copper alloys, lost-wax casting. Elite ornaments (nose rings, ear spools, headdresses) in gold and silver.

    Sipán Burials (Lambayeque, ~300 CE): Royal tombs with warrior-priests in full regalia, sacrificial retainers, and massive metal offerings. Iconographic scenes from ceramics matched to burial contents.

    Irrigation: Large-scale canal systems (Chicama-Moche intervalley canal) supported intensive maize agriculture. Vulnerability to El Niño flooding.

    Sacrifice and Warfare: Iconographic and osteological evidence of ritual combat, capture, and blood sacrifice. Huaca de la Luna plaza yielded remains of sacrificial victims.

    Collapse (~600–700 CE): Severe El Niño events (possibly 536 CE volcanic event and subsequent climatic disruption) implicated in Moche decline. Political fragmentation and Wari influence follow.

    10. Nazca Culture (1–600 CE)

    Nazca Lines (Pampa de Nazca/Palpa): Geoglyphs created by removing oxidized surface stones to reveal lighter ground. Figures (hummingbird, monkey, spider, etc.) and geometric lines/trapezoids. Dated 200 BCE–600 CE. Function debated: ritual pathways, astronomical alignments, water/fertility ceremonies (Aveni 1990; Silverman & Proulx 2002).

    Cahuachi: Major ceremonial center (24 km², 40 mounds) with pilgrimage function rather than urban habitation. Episodic construction and ritual burial. Abandoned ~450 CE.

    Polychrome Ceramics: Distinctive slip-painted pottery with up to 15 colors. Iconography: supernatural beings (Anthropomorphic Mythical Being), trophy heads, agricultural motifs.

    Puquios (Subterranean Aqueducts): Filtration galleries tapping groundwater aquifers. Engineering response to hyper-arid environment. Some still functional.

    Trophy Heads: Perforated skulls cached at Cahuachi and in burials. Isotopic analysis shows some are local, others foreign.

    Textiles and Featherwork: Continuation of Paracas textile tradition with Nazca iconographic vocabulary. Feather tunics and fans.

    Desert Adaptation: Intensive exploitation of narrow river valleys (Nazca, Ingenio, Palpa) in extremely arid coastal desert. Vulnerable to hydrological variability.

    11. Early Tiwanaku (100–600 CE)

    Lake Titicaca Basin: Site of Tiwanaku located at 3850 m on southern shore (Bolivia). Occupied from ~400 BCE; monumental construction begins ~100 CE.

    Monumental Core: Akapana (cross-shaped stepped platform, 17 m high), Kalasasaya (semi-subterranean court with stone stelae), Puma Punku (precision-cut andesite blocks). Gateway of the Sun with Staff God figure.

    Raised-Field Agriculture (Suka Kollus): Elevated planting platforms with canals between, creating microclimates for frost protection. Supported dense populations at high altitude. Large-scale field systems around Lake Titicaca.

    Population and Urbanism: Urban core estimated at 10,000–40,000 inhabitants. Craft specialization (ceramics, textiles, metallurgy). State-level organization debated.

    Iconography: Staff God/Weeping God complex with rayed solar imagery. Stylistic links to earlier Pucara tradition and later expansion.

    Exchange Networks: Tiwanaku materials (pottery, textiles) found in coastal valleys, indicating long-distance exchange. Llama caravans for transport.

    Expansion Phase (post-600 CE): Major expansion into Moquegua (Peru) and northern Chile begins ~600 CE, beyond this article set's window.
