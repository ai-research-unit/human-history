    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The North European Plain

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 40000 BCE – 1000 CE

    Region: The North European Plain — the lowland corridor stretching from the Atlantic coast of France through the Low Countries, the North German Plain, Poland, and into the East European Plain of western Russia; bounded by the Baltic and North Seas to the north and the central European uplands to the south.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Upper Paleolithic open-air and cave sites, Mesolithic settlements, Neolithic longhouses and megaliths, Bronze Age burials and hoards, Iron Age oppida and bog deposits, Roman frontier forts, and Migration Period cemeteries. Classical authors (Caesar, Tacitus) describe Germanic societies. Runic inscriptions appear late but remain limited. Full literacy arrives with Christianization (~800–1000 CE in most areas).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Upper Paleolithic Colonization (40000–25000 BCE)

    Modern Human Arrival: Anatomically modern humans replace Neanderthals ~45,000–40,000 BP; Aurignacian industry marks initial colonization.

    Aurignacian: ~43,000–33,000 BP; blade technology; bone and antler tools; earliest art (Hohle Fels figurines, Geißenklösterle flutes in Swabian Jura on plain's margin).

    Gravettian: ~33,000–25,000 BP; mammoth hunting; Venus figurines; Dolní Věstonice (Moravia); Pavlovian variant.

    Mammoth Steppe Ecology: Periglacial steppe-tundra; mammoth, woolly rhino, horse, reindeer; seasonal aggregation sites.

    Population Dynamics: Low density; highly mobile bands; long-distance raw material transport indicates extensive territories.

    Cave Art: Concentrated in Franco-Cantabrian region (western margin); portable art more widespread.

    2. Last Glacial Maximum and Recolonization (25000–12000 BCE)

    Ice Sheet Expansion: Scandinavian and British ice sheets reach maximum ~26,500–19,000 BP; North European Plain largely uninhabitable.

    Refugia: Populations contract to southern refugia (Franco-Cantabrian, Italian, Balkan); some persistence in periglacial zones.

    Magdalenian Recolonization: ~17,000–12,000 BP; rapid northward expansion as ice retreats; sophisticated bone/antler technology; cave art peak.

    Hamburg and Ahrensburg Cultures: Late Glacial reindeer hunters on North German Plain ~15,000–10,000 BP.

    Megafaunal Extinction: Mammoth and woolly rhino extinct by ~12,000 BP; horse and reindeer persist regionally.

    Younger Dryas: Cold snap ~12,900–11,700 BP; temporary steppe expansion; final Late Glacial challenge.

    3. Mesolithic Forest Adaptation (12000–6000 BCE)

    Holocene Warming: Rapid forest expansion; deciduous woodland replaces tundra; sea level rise floods Doggerland.

    Maglemosian Culture: ~9000–6000 BCE; North German Plain and southern Scandinavia; microlithic composite tools; dugout canoes; fishing emphasis.

    Ertebølle Culture: ~5300–3950 BCE; coastal adaptation; shell middens; pottery adoption before agriculture; complex hunter-gatherers.

    Subsistence: Red deer, roe deer, wild boar, aurochs; fishing (pike, eel); hazelnut gathering; broad-spectrum economy.

    Settlement: Seasonal camps; some persistent base camps near water; population growth.

    Technology: Microliths for arrows; bone fishhooks; dugout canoes; bark containers.

    4. Linear Pottery Culture (6000–4000 BCE)

    LBK (Linearbandkeramik): ~5500–4500 BCE; first farming culture on North European Plain; spreads from Danube to Paris Basin and Poland.

    Longhouse Villages: Timber longhouses 20–40 m long; clustered settlements; loess soil preference for farming.

    Agriculture: Emmer wheat, einkorn, barley, peas, lentils; cattle, pig, sheep, goat; garden horticulture.

    Expansion Dynamics: Demic diffusion with colonist farmers; some indigenous forager absorption; genetic admixture.

    Violence: Evidence of conflict (Talheim, Asparn-Schletz massacres); defended settlements.

    Indigenous Interaction: Mesolithic populations persist in marginal zones; gradual assimilation or displacement.

    5. Middle Neolithic Diversification (4000–2500 BCE)

    Funnel Beaker (TRB): ~4300–2800 BCE; northern Europe from Netherlands to Poland; first farmers in previously Mesolithic zones; megalithic tombs.

    Megalithic Tradition: Passage graves, dolmens, long barrows; ancestral cult; communal burial; labor mobilization.

    Globular Amphora: ~3400–2800 BCE; eastern variant; mobile pastoralism emphasis; corded decoration.

    Secondary Products Revolution: Milk, wool, traction power from livestock; plow agriculture; wheeled vehicles.

    Social Differentiation: Causewayed enclosures; ritual centers; emerging inequality in burial.

    Flint Mining: Large-scale extraction (Spiennes, Krzemionki); specialized production; long-distance trade.

    6. Corded Ware and Bell Beaker (2500–1500 BCE)

    Corded Ware: ~2900–2350 BCE; single grave burials with cord-decorated pottery; battle axes; expansion from Pontic-Caspian steppe.

    Steppe Ancestry: Genetic evidence shows massive influx (~75% ancestry replacement in some areas) from Yamnaya-related populations; Indo-European language spread.

    Bell Beaker: ~2800–1800 BCE; distinctive pottery and archery kit; metallurgical connections; complex interaction with Corded Ware.

    Early Bronze Age Transition: Copper and tin bronze adoption; Únětice culture emerges ~2300 BCE.

    Social Organization: Individual burials replacing collective; warrior ideology; male prestige goods.

    Settlement: Mix of longhouse villages and smaller farmsteads; some hillforts.

    7. Bronze Age Consolidation (1500–800 BCE)

    Nordic Bronze Age: ~1700–500 BCE; Scandinavia and northern Germany; rich bronze deposits; solar symbolism; rock art.

    Lusatian Culture: ~1300–500 BCE; Poland and eastern Germany; fortified settlements; cremation cemeteries.

    Urnfield Expansion: ~1300–750 BCE; cremation burial rite spreads; large cemeteries; population growth.

    Long-Distance Trade: Amber from Baltic; tin from Cornwall; bronze artifacts; prestige goods exchange.

    Settlement Hierarchy: Some large fortified centers; agricultural intensification; field systems.

    Bronze Technology: Local casting; distinctive regional styles; weapon deposits in bogs and rivers.

    8. Iron Age and Pre-Roman Germanic (800 BCE – 50 CE)

    Hallstatt Contacts: Central European Iron Age influence reaches plain; iron technology adoption ~800–600 BCE.

    La Tène Expansion: Celtic cultural influence ~500–100 BCE; some La Tène artifacts in northern regions.

    Germanic Ethnogenesis: Jastorf culture (~600–300 BCE) in northern Germany; proto-Germanic language differentiation.

    Bog Deposits: Weapon offerings, human sacrifices (Tollund Man, Grauballe Man); ritual landscape.

    Tribal Organization: Caesar and Tacitus describe tribes (Suebi, Chatti, Cimbri, Teutones); kings, warriors, assemblies.

    Settlement: Dispersed farmsteads; some nucleated villages; hill forts (Ringwälle).

    9. Roman Frontier (50 CE – 400 CE)

    Limes Germanicus: Roman frontier established ~50 CE; Rhine and Danube boundaries; forts and watchtowers.

    Free Germania: Beyond frontier; Roman trade goods (wine, bronze vessels, glass) flow in; Germanic goods (amber, furs, slaves) flow out.

    Marcomannic Wars: ~166–180 CE; major conflicts with Germanic confederations; Roman power strained.

    Social Stratification: Elite graves with Roman imports; warrior aristocracy develops; larger political units.

    Tacitus' Germania: ~98 CE; ethnographic description of Germanic society; tribes, religion, customs (filtered through Roman lens).

    Late Roman Crisis: ~3rd–4th centuries; increased Germanic pressure; frontier breaches; foederati settlements.

    10. Migration Period (400–700 CE)

    Völkerwanderung: Mass movements of Germanic peoples; Goths, Vandals, Burgundians, Franks, Angles, Saxons, Lombards.

    Western Roman Collapse: ~476 CE; Germanic kingdoms replace Roman administration in west.

    Frankish Expansion: Merovingian dynasty; Clovis's conversion (~496); northern Gaul becomes Frankish heartland.

    Anglo-Saxon Migration: ~5th–6th centuries; settlement of Britain from North German Plain and Jutland.

    Settlement Shift: Some Roman towns decline; new elite centers; village reorganization.

    Material Culture: Polychrome jewelry; weapon burials; furnished graves indicating pagan belief.

    11. Viking Age and Christianization (700–1000 CE)

    Scandinavian Expansion: ~793 (Lindisfarne) onwards; raiding, trading, settlement from Ireland to Russia.

    Carolingian Contact: Charlemagne's Saxon Wars (~772–804); forced Christianization; Frankish administration extends.

    Runestones: Runic inscriptions increase; commemorative monuments; limited literacy but writing present.

    Conversion Process: Denmark officially converts ~965 (Harald Bluetooth); Norway ~1000; gradual process.

    Trade Networks: Hedeby, Birka, Wolin; long-distance trade; silver economy; Varangian routes to Byzantium.

    Oral Threshold: By ~1000 CE, ecclesiastical documentation becomes the medium institutions run on; vernacular literacy emerges later.
