    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Congo Basin

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 130000 BCE – 1945 CE

    Region: The Congo Basin — the vast equatorial rainforest drained by the Congo River and its tributaries, from the Atlantic coast to the Albertine Rift, bounded by the Cameroon highlands to the northwest, the East African plateau to the east, and the savannas of Angola and Zambia to the south.

    Note on Evidence: The Congo Basin presents extreme archaeological challenges. Acidic forest soils rapidly destroy organic materials; pottery and stone tools survive but are poorly dated at most sites. Much of what is known derives from linguistic reconstruction, ethnographic analogy, and limited stratified excavations (Matupi Cave, Shum Laka, Ituri sites). Colonial-era ethnographies and oral traditions collected in the 19th-20th centuries provide data for later periods but must be read critically.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Early MSA Occupation (130000–70000 BCE)

    Matupi Cave (Ituri, DRC): The most important stratified site in the Congo Basin. Occupation spans from >40,000 BP to recent periods. Lower levels contain MSA artifacts consistent with dates possibly extending beyond 70,000 BP. Microlithic industries and evidence of forest exploitation (Van Noten 1977; Mercader 2002).

    Lupemban Industry: Distinctive MSA tradition characterized by lanceolate bifaces, found across central Africa from the Atlantic to the Rift. Dated ~300,000–30,000 BP in various contexts. Indicates occupation of forest and forest-margin environments during Middle Pleistocene.

    Environmental Context: During MIS 5 (130,000–70,000 BP), the Congo Basin alternated between wetter and drier phases. Rainforest extent fluctuated; human populations likely tracked forest-savanna mosaics. No direct fossil evidence of hominins from the basin core due to poor preservation.

    Sparse Record: Archaeological coverage remains extremely thin. Most early sites cluster at forest margins or in caves with better preservation. Interior rainforest occupation is inferred from tool distributions but lacks dated contexts.

    2. Late Pleistocene Persistence (70000–12000 BCE)

    Last Glacial Maximum (26,500–19,000 BP): Forest contracted to refugia in the basin center and along the eastern highlands. Savannas expanded across much of the region. Human populations persisted in forest refugia; genetic evidence suggests continuous occupation.

    Pygmy Genetic Antiquity: Population genetics indicates divergence of forest-forager populations (ancestors of modern Aka, Mbuti, Twa) from other African lineages by ~60,000–70,000 BP. This suggests ancient, continuous forest adaptation (Patin et al. 2009; Verdu et al. 2009).

    Matupi Cave Upper Levels: Dated sequences show continuous occupation through the Late Pleistocene with microlithic industries suited to forest hunting. Bone tools, some with barbs, indicate specialized hunting technology.

    Tool Transitions: Lupemban gives way to varied regional industries (Tshitolian in lower Congo, other microlithic traditions elsewhere). Limited dating precision due to preservation issues.

    Forest Refugia Model: The basin's role as a forest refugium during glacial periods meant human populations here were insulated from the savanna expansions affecting surrounding regions.

    3. Early Holocene Forager Adaptation (12000–4000 BCE)

    Forest Re-expansion: Beginning ~12,000 BP, the African Humid Period brought increased rainfall. Rainforest expanded to near-modern extent by ~8000 BP. Forest-adapted forager populations expanded with it.

    Specialized Forest Hunting-Gathering: Archaeological and ethnographic evidence indicates highly specialized adaptations: net-hunting, honey-collecting, processing of forest tubers and nuts, exploitation of forest elephants and duikers.

    Pygmy-Farmer Relationship Precursors: Modern Pygmy populations (Aka, Mbuti, Baka, Twa) are genetically and linguistically distinct from neighboring Bantu farmers but have adopted Bantu languages. This symbiotic relationship developed later but forager populations existed independently before Bantu arrival.

    Early Ceramics at Margins: Pottery appears at forest-margin sites (Shum Laka in Cameroon highlands, ~7000 BP) before agriculture. Indicates increasing sedentism and possibly contact networks.

    Shum Laka (Cameroon Highlands): Important stratified site at forest-savanna boundary. Occupation spans ~30,000 BP to recent. Burials indicate complex mortuary practices by ~8000 BP. Ancestral population to later Bantu speakers.

    4. Bantu Expansion into Forest Margins (4000–1000 BCE)

    Origin Zone: Linguistic and archaeological evidence places the Bantu homeland in the Nigeria-Cameroon borderlands (Grassfields region). Proto-Bantu spoken ~4000–3000 BCE.

    Agricultural Package: Early Bantu cultivated yams (Dioscorea spp.), oil palm (Elaeis guineensis), and possibly plantains (introduced from Asia, debated timing). Cattle and small livestock later additions outside tsetse belt.

    Western Bantu Stream: One major expansion route moved south through the Congo Basin forest. Linguistic subgrouping shows Western Bantu languages spreading along river systems into the forest.

    Early Iron-Working: Iron smelting appears in central Africa by ~2500 BP (Obobogo, Cameroon; Tchissanga, Congo). Earlier dates proposed but contested. Iron tools enabled more efficient forest clearance.

    Settlement Pattern: Small villages at forest edges, with slash-and-burn clearings. Interior forest remained domain of forager populations.

    Population Interactions: Genetic evidence shows admixture between incoming Bantu farmers and indigenous forager populations. Foragers adopted Bantu languages while maintaining distinct subsistence practices.

    5. Iron Age Consolidation (1000 BCE – 500 CE)

    Iron Technology Spread: By 500 BCE–0 CE, iron-working spread throughout the basin margins. Local ore sources exploited. Iron hoes, axes, and weapons transformed forest clearance and warfare.

    Agricultural Expansion: Slash-and-burn cultivation (bananas/plantains, yams, oil palm, later maize and cassava from Americas) supported larger populations. Forest clearance intensified.

    Village Society: Settlements of 50–500 people became standard. Leadership based on kinship seniority, ritual authority, and wealth in people (wives, dependents, slaves). No state formation in core forest.

    Pygmy-Farmer Symbiosis: Formalized exchange relationships developed: forest products (meat, honey, medicinal plants) traded for agricultural goods, iron tools, pottery. Forager groups became attached to farmer villages as clients/dependents.

    Linguistic Consolidation: By 500 CE, Bantu languages dominated the basin except for isolated forager vocabularies. Linguistic diversity within Bantu indicates local differentiation.

    Trade Networks: Long-distance exchange of copper (from Katanga), salt, dried fish, and prestige goods. River systems served as transport arteries.

    6. Mature Village Society (500–1500 CE)

    Political Organization: Acephalous village-based systems predominated in the core forest. Leadership typically gerontocratic and ritual-based. Big-man systems in some areas. No states or kingdoms in interior forest; state formation only at margins (Kongo, Luba, Lunda developed at forest edges or beyond).

    Subsistence Base: Mixed farming: bananas/plantains (dominant calorie source), yams, cassava (post-1500), oil palm, plus hunting and fishing. Palm wine production important.

    Material Culture: Iron tools (hoes, knives, spears), pottery, woven raffia cloth, carved wooden objects. Little survives archaeologically from forest environments.

    Trade and Currency: Raffia cloth squares and copper crosses served as currency in different regions. Salt from coastal evaporation and forest sources highly valued.

    Social Organization: Matrilineal descent common in western regions; patrilineal in east. Bridewealth in iron goods, cloth, or currency. Slavery present but domestic in character.

    Oral Tradition: All record-keeping oral. Genealogies, migration histories, ritual knowledge maintained by specialists. No writing system indigenous to the region.

    7. Atlantic Trade Era (1500–1880 CE)

    Portuguese Contact (1480s): First European contact with Kingdom of Kongo at coast. Gradual penetration of trade networks into interior.

    Slave Trade: Atlantic slave trade drew millions from Congo Basin over four centuries. Major export routes via Kongo, Loango, and later Stanley Pool. Interior populations raided by coastal intermediaries. Demographic devastation estimated at 4–8 million exported, with multiples killed in capture.

    Trade Goods: Imported cloth, firearms, liquor, metal goods exchanged for slaves, ivory, later rubber and palm oil. Trade items transformed local economies and power structures.

    Kingdom of Kongo: Western margin state (capital at Mbanza Kongo). Christianized by Portuguese; literate administration attempted but remained marginal. Collapsed under slave-trade pressures by 1700s.

    Interior Adaptations: Trade items (guns, cloth, beads) penetrated deep into forest via exchange networks. Some populations withdrew deeper into forest to avoid raiding.

    Arab-Swahili Trade (East): From ~1800, Zanzibari traders reached eastern Congo seeking ivory and slaves. Tippu Tip's trading empire (1860s–1890s) destabilized eastern regions.

    Persistent Orality: Despite coastal literacy, interior populations remained entirely oral. European travelers relied on interpreters; indigenous voices recorded only in colonial-era transcriptions.

    8. Congo Free State (1880–1908)

    Berlin Conference (1884–85): European powers partition Africa. Leopold II of Belgium secures personal control of Congo Basin as "Congo Free State" — not a Belgian colony but his private property.

    Rubber Terror: Wild rubber extraction using forced labor. Villages required to meet quotas on threat of mutilation (hand-severing) and massacre. Death toll estimates: 1–10 million from violence, starvation, and disease (Hochschild 1998).

    Force Publique: Colonial militia recruited from various African populations, officered by Europeans. Instrument of quota enforcement and terror.

    Resistance: Local armed resistance occurred but was suppressed. Flight into deep forest served as refuge for some populations.

    Documentation: Atrocities documented by missionaries (E.D. Morel) and Roger Casement (1903 report). Indigenous testimony collected but filtered through European recorders. Oral tradition preserved memories passed to later generations.

    International Outcry: Congo Reform Association campaign (1904–1913) led to transfer to Belgian state.

    9. Belgian Colonial Administration (1908–1945)

    Transfer to Belgium: 1908 — Belgian government assumes control; formal colony. Exploitation continues but with reduced brutality.

    Extraction Economy: Mining (copper, diamonds, gold in Katanga and Kasai) and plantation agriculture. Forced labor (porterage, infrastructure) remained central.

    Limited Education: Missionary schools provided basic literacy to a small fraction; education in French/Flemish, not local languages. Purpose: produce clerks and catechists, not scholars.

    Persistent Orality: For the vast majority of the population, oral tradition remained the sole medium of record, history, and knowledge transmission through 1945. Written records produced by and for Europeans.

    Demographic Recovery: Population began recovering after rubber terror. Still heavily rural and village-based.

    World Wars Impact: Congo provided strategic materials (uranium for atomic bomb, rubber, copper) during both world wars. Congolese troops served in East African campaigns.
