    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Eurasian Steppe

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 40000 BCE – 1850 CE

    Region: The Eurasian Steppe — the continuous grassland corridor stretching from the Pannonian Basin and Pontic-Caspian steppes through Central Asia to Mongolia and Manchuria; bounded by the taiga to the north and the mountain chains (Caucasus, Hindu Kush, Tian Shan, Altai) and deserts to the south.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological evidence includes kurgans (burial mounds), settlement sites, and artifact assemblages. Historical periods documented by literate sedentary neighbors (Greek, Persian, Chinese, Byzantine, Arab, Russian sources). Oral traditions preserved in later written compilations (Secret History of the Mongols). The steppe populations themselves remained predominantly oral until Russian colonial education in the 19th-20th centuries.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Upper Paleolithic Mammoth Steppe (40000–15000 BCE)

    Mammoth Steppe Biome: During the Late Pleistocene, the steppe zone extended far north as "mammoth steppe" — cold, dry grassland supporting megafauna (mammoth, woolly rhinoceros, bison, horse, reindeer).

    Gravettian and Related Industries: Eastern Gravettian (Pavlovian, Kostenkian) industries spread across the western steppe ~30,000–20,000 BP. Characterized by mammoth-bone architecture, Venus figurines, and sophisticated bone/ivory tools.

    Key Sites: Kostenki-Borshchevo (Don Valley), Dolní Věstonice (Moravia), Sungir (Russia) — show elaborate burials with grave goods indicating social differentiation.

    Mal'ta-Buret' Culture: Siberian variant (~24,000–15,000 BP) with distinctive figurines and genetic connection to later populations (Ancient North Eurasian component).

    Subsistence: Mammoth hunting central; communal drives and processing. Bone used for fuel, construction, tools. Plant foods limited by cold.

    2. Terminal Pleistocene Transition (15000–8000 BCE)

    Deglaciation: Warming from ~15,000 BP transformed the steppe; forests expanded from the south; mammoth steppe contracted northward.

    Megafaunal Extinction: Mammoth, woolly rhino, and giant deer disappeared by ~10,000 BP. Horse and bison persisted in reduced steppe zones.

    Epipaleolithic Adaptations: Diversified hunting (horse, bison, deer, small game); fishing in river valleys; gathering intensifies. Microlithic technologies.

    Population Continuity and Change: Some genetic continuity from Upper Paleolithic; new populations entering from multiple directions.

    3. Mesolithic and Neolithic Foundations (8000–4500 BCE)

    Holocene Climate Optimum: Warmer, wetter conditions; forest-steppe mosaic.

    Pottery Adoption: Ceramics appear early in the steppe region (Elshanka, ~7000 BCE), among the earliest in Europe.

    Bug-Dniester Culture: Western Ukraine, ~6500–5500 BCE; mixed foraging-farming; contact with Balkan Neolithic.

    Kelteminar Culture: Central Asian variant (~6000–3000 BCE); fishing-hunting economy around Aral Sea and Amu Darya; decorated pottery.

    Early Domesticates: Cattle, sheep, and goats present by 5000 BCE, spreading from Near East via Caucasus and/or Balkans.

    Horse Use: Wild horse hunting continues; domestication process begins in this period (debated timing).

    4. Eneolithic Transformation (4500–3000 BCE)

    Sredny Stog Culture: Dnieper-Don region, ~4500–3500 BCE; cattle herding; some horse remains with bit wear suggesting riding/control.

    Khvalynsk Culture: Middle Volga, ~4700–3800 BCE; copper artifacts; kurgan burials with social differentiation; cattle sacrifice.

    Horse Domestication: Botai culture (Kazakhstan, ~3700–3100 BCE) shows intensive horse exploitation; debate over riding vs. meat focus. Genetic evidence indicates modern domestic horses derive primarily from Pontic-Caspian region.

    Wheeled Vehicles: Earliest wagon remains and depictions ~3500–3000 BCE in Pontic-Caspian region. Enables pastoral mobility revolution.

    Kurgan Burial Tradition: Mound burial emerges as characteristic steppe practice; elite individuals with weapons, animals, vehicles.

    Early Yamnaya: Proto-Yamnaya horizon develops from Khvalynsk and related groups.

    5. Yamnaya Horizon (3000–1800 BCE)

    Yamnaya Culture: ~3000–2300 BCE; Pontic-Caspian steppe. Mobile pastoralism with cattle, sheep, horse; wagon-based economy; kurgan burials in supine position with ochre.

    Yamnaya Migrations: Genetic evidence shows massive population movement west into Europe (Corded Ware) and east (Afanasievo in Altai/Mongolia), ~3000–2500 BCE.

    Afanasievo Culture: Earliest Bronze Age culture in southern Siberia/western Mongolia; Yamnaya-derived; earliest metallurgy and herding in eastern steppe.

    Catacomb Culture: Succeeds Yamnaya in Pontic region ~2800–2200 BCE; catacomb graves; continued pastoral economy.

    Metallurgy: Copper and bronze working expands; Caucasian and Ural ore sources exploited.

    Indo-European Dispersal: Linguistic evidence associates Yamnaya with Proto-Indo-European language spread.

    6. Andronovo Complex (1800–800 BCE)

    Sintashta Culture: Southern Urals, ~2100–1800 BCE; fortified settlements; earliest chariots (spoked-wheel, horse-drawn); elaborate warrior burials; bronze metallurgy.

    Chariot Warfare: Two-horse chariot spreads from Sintashta across Eurasia; revolutionizes warfare and elite display.

    Andronovo Horizon: ~1800–1200 BCE; vast expansion across Kazakhstan and southern Siberia; tin-bronze metallurgy; semi-sedentary settlements; cattle/sheep/horse economy.

    Proto-Indo-Iranian: Linguistic and material evidence links Sintashta/Andronovo to Indo-Iranian language branch; Vedic/Avestan parallels in ritual (horse sacrifice, fire cult).

    Karasuk Culture: Late Bronze Age (~1500–800 BCE) in southern Siberia; new metallurgical styles; possible influx from China.

    Climate Stress: Aridification ~1200–800 BCE pushes populations toward greater mobility; transition to mounted pastoralism.

    7. Scythian and Sarmatian Period (800 BCE – 200 CE)

    Mounted Nomadism Crystallizes: True horse-mounted pastoralism becomes dominant lifestyle; composite bow, cavalry warfare.

    Royal Scythians: Dominate Pontic steppe ~700–300 BCE; Greek colony contacts; Herodotus descriptions; royal kurgans (Solokha, Chertomlyk) with gold artifacts.

    Scythian Animal Style: Distinctive artistic tradition featuring predators, prey animals, combat scenes; gold and bronze objects.

    Pazyryk Culture: Altai mountain variant; frozen tombs preserve textiles, tattoos, horses, carpets (~500–300 BCE).

    Saka and Eastern Scythians: Central Asian variants; contacts with Achaemenid Persia; Issyk Golden Man.

    Sarmatian Expansion: ~300 BCE – 200 CE; Sarmatians displace Scythians on Pontic steppe; heavy cavalry (cataphracts); female warrior burials.

    Xiongnu Confederacy: Eastern steppe (~200 BCE – 100 CE); major power challenging Han China; possible ancestor of Huns.

    8. Hunnic Period (200–600 CE)

    Xiongnu Collapse: Xiongnu confederacy fragments ~100 CE; populations move westward.

    Hunnic Migration: Groups identified as Huns appear in Pontic steppe ~370 CE; crush Goths; precipitate Migration Period.

    Attila's Empire: ~434–453 CE; Huns dominate from Pannonia; tributary system over Germanic peoples; raids on Roman Empire.

    Hun Collapse: Attila's death (453 CE) followed by rapid disintegration; subject peoples revolt.

    Rouran Khaganate: Dominates eastern steppe ~330–555 CE; relation to later Avars debated.

    Avars: Appear in Pannonia ~560s; Avar Khaganate lasts to 796 CE; possible Rouran connection.

    Power Vacuum: Frequent shifting of steppe hegemony; Bulgars, Khazars emerge.

    9. Turkic Khaganates (600–1000 CE)

    First Turkic Khaganate: ~552–603 CE; Göktürks overthrow Rouran; control vast steppe from Manchuria to Black Sea; splits into Eastern and Western.

    Orkhon Inscriptions: ~730s CE; earliest Turkic writing; monuments to Bilge Khan and Kul Tigin; heroic ideology.

    Khazar Khaganate: ~650–969 CE; Pontic-Caspian steppe; unique Jewish conversion of elite; trade hub between Byzantium, Caliphate, and steppe.

    Uighur Khaganate: ~744–840 CE; Mongolia; alliance with Tang China; Manichaean conversion; urban center at Ordu-Baliq.

    Islamization: Arab conquest of Central Asia (8th century); gradual conversion of Turkic groups; Volga Bulgars convert ~922.

    Kyrgyz Destruction of Uighurs: 840 CE; disperses Uighur populations; Kyrgyz briefly dominate Mongolia.

    Khitan Liao: ~907–1125 CE; Mongolic-speaking; dominate eastern steppe and northern China.

    10. Mongol Unification and Empire (1000–1300 CE)

    Pre-Mongol Fragmentation: ~1000–1200 CE; multiple competing tribes (Kereit, Naiman, Tatar, Merkit) on Mongolian plateau.

    Chinggis Khan: Temüjin unifies Mongol tribes by 1206; elected Great Khan; military innovations (decimal organization, merit-based promotion, terror tactics).

    Mongol Conquests: 1207–1260 CE; largest contiguous land empire: Jin China (1234), Khwarezm (1221), Russia (1240), Baghdad (1258), Song China (1279).

    Pax Mongolica: Trade and communication across Eurasia; Silk Road security; exchange of technologies, diseases, ideas.

    Khanate Division: Empire fragments after Möngke Khan (d. 1259): Yuan (China), Ilkhanate (Persia), Chagatai (Central Asia), Golden Horde (Russia/Western steppe).

    Secret History of the Mongols: ~1240; oral tradition committed to writing; key source for Mongol culture and Chinggis biography.

    11. Post-Mongol Fragmentation (1300–1600 CE)

    Golden Horde Decline: ~1300–1480; internal conflicts; Black Death devastation; Muscovite Russia asserts independence (1480).

    Timur (Tamerlane): ~1370–1405; last great steppe conqueror; Chagatai origins; devastates rivals but empire fragments at death.

    Uzbek Khanate: ~1500–1600; Shaybanids; displace Timurids in Transoxiana; Sunni Islam.

    Kazakh Khanate: ~1465–1847; forms from Uzbek breakaway; maintains pastoral nomadism; three jüz (hordes).

    Crimean Khanate: ~1441–1783; Genghisid; Ottoman vassal; slave raiding into Russia/Poland.

    Dzungar Khanate: ~1634–1758; last nomadic empire; Oirat Mongols; Buddhist.

    12. Qing and Russian Encroachment (1600–1850 CE)

    Qing Conquest of Mongolia: ~1636–1691; Inner Mongols submit; Khalkha Mongols incorporated after Dzungar pressure.

    Dzungar-Qing Wars: ~1687–1758; culminates in Dzungar genocide (~1755–1758); 80% population killed; Qing controls Xinjiang.

    Russian Expansion: ~1550–1850; Cossack advance across Siberia; forts control river routes; Kazakh hordes submit gradually (1731–1847).

    Kazakh Subordination: Junior jüz submits 1731; Middle jüz 1740s; Senior jüz 1820s–1840s; khanate abolished 1847.

    Nomadic Autonomy Ends: By 1850, entire steppe under Qing or Russian control; pastoral economy continues but political independence lost.

    Persistent Orality: Despite Mongol script, Tibetan Buddhist literacy, and Islamic education in some areas, the majority of steppe population remained primarily oral through 1850.
