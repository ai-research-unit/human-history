    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The North American Eastern Woodlands and Mississippi Basin

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 16000 BCE – 1750 CE

    Region: The North American Eastern Woodlands and Mississippi Basin — from the Atlantic seaboard through the Appalachian Mountains to the Great Lakes and Mississippi River system; including the Ohio Valley, Southeast coastal plain, Gulf Coast, and the prairies bordering the Great Plains.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Paleoindian kill sites, Archaic shell middens, mound complexes (Poverty Point, Adena, Hopewell, Mississippian), and contact-period sites. No indigenous writing system; oral traditions partially recorded by European observers and later ethnographers. European documentary sources begin with Spanish entrada (1539) and increase through colonial period. Window ends when European administrative records become the medium institutions run on (~1750 CE).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Paleoindian Settlement (16000–10000 BCE)

    Pre-Clovis Evidence: Meadowcroft Rockshelter (Pennsylvania) shows occupation possibly as early as ~16,000 BP; debated but increasingly accepted. Page-Ladson (Florida) has ~14,500 BP deposits with artifacts.

    Clovis Horizon: ~13,200–12,800 BP; fluted points widespread across Eastern Woodlands; megafauna association (mastodon, mammoth).

    Glacial Context: Laurentide Ice Sheet retreating; proglacial lakes; rapidly changing environments; tundra and boreal forest ecotones.

    Megafauna: Mastodon, mammoth, giant beaver, ground sloth present; hunting evidence at sites like Kimmswick (Missouri).

    Population: Small, mobile bands; rapid colonization of deglaciated territory; carrying capacity limited.

    Lithic Technology: High-quality flint and chert sources exploited; long-distance raw material transport indicates mobility.

    2. Early Archaic Adaptation (10000–6000 BCE)

    Holocene Transition: Climate warming; deciduous forest expansion; modern biomes establish; megafauna extinction complete by ~10,000 BCE.

    Regional Point Traditions: Dalton, Kirk, Bifurcate traditions indicate regional differentiation; stylistic provinces emerge.

    Subsistence Diversification: White-tailed deer becomes primary game; nut masting (oak, hickory, chestnut); small mammals; riverine resources.

    Seasonal Mobility: Macro-band and micro-band seasonal patterns; fall nut harvests; winter dispersal; spring fish runs.

    Technology: Ground stone tools appear (axes, atlatl weights); woodworking capacity increases.

    Population Growth: Site density increases; territorial ranges likely contract; regional populations establish.

    3. Middle and Late Archaic (6000–3000 BCE)

    Climate Optimum: Hypsithermal warm period ~6000–3000 BCE; prairie expansion in Midwest; coastal transgression.

    Shell Mound Cultures: Green River (Kentucky), Tennessee River shell middens; substantial midden accumulation indicates repeated, intensive occupation.

    Poverty Point: Louisiana site complex (~1700–1100 BCE); massive earthwork construction; long-distance exchange (copper, galena, soapstone); non-agricultural complexity.

    Long-Distance Exchange: Copper from Lake Superior; marine shells; Great Lakes and Gulf connections.

    Incipient Horticulture: Eastern Agricultural Complex emerges; sunflower, sumpweed, chenopod, squash cultivated by ~3000 BCE; not yet staple dependence.

    Mortuary Elaboration: Differential burial treatment; grave goods increase; social differentiation evident.

    4. Early Woodland and Adena (3000–500 BCE)

    Pottery Introduction: Fiber-tempered pottery (Stallings Island) ~3000 BCE; sand/grit tempered wares spread; enables food storage and processing.

    Adena Culture: Ohio Valley ~800–100 BCE; conical burial mounds; log tombs; grave goods include gorgets, tubes, copper ornaments.

    Eastern Agricultural Complex Intensification: Cultivated plants more prominent; storage pits; semi-sedentary villages.

    Burial Mound Tradition: Earthen mound construction for mortuary purposes; ancestor veneration; lineage markers.

    Ceremonial Objects: Adena tablets, tubular pipes, copper artifacts; shared symbolic vocabulary.

    Settlement Patterns: Small hamlets; seasonal camps; mound centers as gathering places.

    5. Middle Woodland and Hopewell (500 BCE – 400 CE)

    Hopewell Interaction Sphere: ~200 BCE – 400 CE; exchange network spanning Eastern Woodlands; shared mortuary ceremonialism rather than political unity.

    Core Areas: Ohio Valley (Hopewell site, Mound City, Newark); Illinois Valley (Havana); other regional variants.

    Exchange Items: Obsidian from Yellowstone; mica from Appalachians; copper from Lake Superior; marine shells from Gulf; silver from Ontario; grizzly bear teeth from Rockies.

    Earthwork Complexes: Geometric enclosures; Newark earthworks cover 4 square miles; astronomical alignments debated.

    Mortuary Ritual: Charnel houses; cremation; bundled burials; elaborate grave goods for select individuals.

    Subsistence: Eastern Agricultural Complex crops; hunting and gathering remain important; maize appears but minor.

    6. Late Woodland (400–1000 CE)

    Hopewell Decline: Long-distance exchange networks contract ~400 CE; causes debated (climate, social, political).

    Bow and Arrow Adoption: Replaces atlatl ~600–800 CE; smaller projectile points; hunting efficiency increases.

    Population Growth: Site density increases; competition; territorial boundaries; fortified sites appear.

    Maize Intensification: Maize becomes significant ~800 CE; Northern Flint varieties adapted to short growing seasons.

    Late Woodland Cultures: Effigy Mound (Wisconsin); Weeden Island (Florida); various regional traditions.

    Village Nucleation: Larger, more permanent villages; defensive considerations; social complexity increases.

    7. Mississippian Florescence (1000–1350 CE)

    Mississippian Emergence: ~1000 CE in American Bottom (Cahokia region); platform mound construction; maize-based agriculture.

    Cahokia: Peak ~1050–1200 CE; population estimates 10,000–20,000; Monks Mound largest earthwork north of Mexico; urban planning; Woodhenge astronomical marker.

    Chiefdom Organization: Ranked societies; paramount chiefs; tribute systems; hereditary leadership.

    Platform Mound Centers: Etowah (Georgia), Moundville (Alabama), Spiro (Oklahoma), Angel (Indiana); regional capitals.

    Southeastern Ceremonial Complex: Shared iconography (falcon warriors, bi-lobed arrows, chunkey players); elite ideology; warfare motifs.

    Subsistence: Intensive maize agriculture; floodplain farming; three sisters (maize, beans, squash) established.

    8. Late Mississippian (1350–1540 CE)

    Cahokia Decline: Depopulation by ~1300–1350 CE; American Bottom largely abandoned; causes debated (deforestation, flooding, political instability, climate).

    Regional Persistence: Moundville, Etowah continue; some centers abandoned before European contact.

    Fort Ancient: Ohio Valley ~1000–1650 CE; village-based societies; possible ancestral Shawnee.

    Oneota: Upper Midwest ~1000–1650 CE; agricultural villages; shell-tempered pottery; possible ancestral Siouan, Ho-Chunk.

    Political Fragmentation: Dispersed population centers; smaller polities; some defensive nucleation.

    Protohistoric Period: Some indirect European effects (disease?, trade goods) may precede direct contact.

    9. European Contact (1540–1650 CE)

    De Soto Entrada (1539–1543): Spanish expedition through Southeast; documents paramount chiefdoms (Cofitachequi, Coosa); introduces epidemic disease.

    Disease Epidemics: Smallpox, measles, influenza devastate indigenous populations; 50–90% mortality estimated in affected areas; social collapse.

    Early Trade: European goods (metal, beads, cloth) enter exchange networks; firearms gradually appear.

    Political Collapse and Reformation: Chiefdoms fragment; new coalescent societies form; refugee communities.

    Colonial Footholds: Spanish Florida missions (1565+); Jamestown (1607); Plymouth (1620); early interactions.

    Documentary Sources: European accounts describe indigenous societies; bias but primary evidence for contact period.

    10. Colonial Entanglement (1650–1750 CE)

    Beaver Wars (1640s–1680s): Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) expansion for fur trade access; displaces Great Lakes populations; widespread conflict.

    Covenant Chain: English-Haudenosaunee alliance system; diplomatic protocols; wampum records.

    Indian Slave Trade: Southeast raiding for captives sold to Charleston; Yamasee War (1715–1717); devastating to indigenous populations.

    Mission Systems: Spanish Florida; French Illinois Country; Jesuit relations; acculturation pressures.

    Population Nadir: Indigenous populations reach lowest points; survivors consolidate into new tribal configurations.

    Oral Tradition Persists: Despite European documentation, indigenous societies remain primarily oral; wampum, winter counts as memory aids but not alphabetic writing.

    Threshold Marker: By ~1750 CE, colonial administrative records become the medium institutions run on; indigenous oral cultures persist but within documented colonial framework.
