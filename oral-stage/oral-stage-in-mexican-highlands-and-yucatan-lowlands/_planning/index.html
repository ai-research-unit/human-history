    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Mexican Highlands and Yucatan Lowlands

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 16000 BCE – 400 BCE

    Region: The Mexican Highlands and Yucatan Lowlands — from the northern deserts through the Basin of Mexico, Oaxacan highlands, and Gulf Coast lowlands to the Yucatan Peninsula and southern Maya highlands; including the Valley of Mexico, Tehuacan, the Isthmus of Tehuantepec, and Peten.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Paleoindian kill sites, Archaic cave deposits (Guilá Naquitz, Tehuacan caves), early village sites, and Formative period centers (San Lorenzo, La Venta, Monte Albán). Olmec and Zapotec iconography and early glyphic experiments appear toward window's end. Full Mesoamerican writing systems develop after 400 BCE.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Initial Peopling (16000–12000 BCE)

    Pre-Clovis Evidence: Human footprints at Valsequillo (~40,000 BP claimed, contested); more secure evidence at sites like Tlapacoya (~22,000 BP) and early levels at various caves.

    Clovis Presence: Clovis points (~13,000–12,700 BP) found in northern Mexico; represents widespread Paleoindian horizon.

    Coastal and Interior Routes: Debate continues over Pacific coastal vs. interior migration routes; both may have contributed.

    Megafauna Association: Mammoth, horse, camel, ground sloth present; hunting likely but kill-site evidence limited in Mesoamerica proper.

    Climate Context: Late Pleistocene; cooler and wetter than present; pine-oak forests at lower elevations.

    2. Terminal Pleistocene Adaptation (12000–9000 BCE)

    Megafaunal Extinction: By ~10,000 BP, megafauna extinct in Mesoamerica; causes debated (climate, human hunting, combination).

    Younger Dryas: Cold snap ~12,900–11,700 BP affects vegetation zones; human adaptation required.

    Diversified Foraging: Shift to smaller game, plants, aquatic resources; generalist subsistence strategies.

    Lithic Traditions: Post-Clovis point types (Folsom-like, other stemmed points); regional differentiation begins.

    Tehuacan Caves: Early levels at Coxcatlan and other Tehuacan caves document Late Paleoindian/Early Archaic transition.

    3. Early Archaic (9000–6000 BCE)

    Guilá Naquitz (Oaxaca): Key cave site with exceptional preservation; wild plant remains, early squash (possibly cultivated by ~8000 BCE).

    Tehuacan Valley Sequence: Ajuereado and El Riego phases; seasonal mobility; diverse plant and animal exploitation.

    Incipient Cultivation: Early manipulation of squash, bottle gourd, possibly chili peppers; not yet dependent agriculture.

    Seasonal Round: Wet-season dispersal for hunting/gathering; dry-season aggregation in favorable locations.

    Regional Variation: Highland vs. lowland adaptations; coastal shell middens in some areas.

    4. Teosinte-Maize Transition (6000–4000 BCE)

    Maize Domestication: Genetic and archaeological evidence places maize domestication in Balsas River region ~9000–7000 BP; spread and intensification ~6000–4000 BCE.

    Teosinte to Maize: Multi-generational selection from wild teosinte (Zea mays parviglumis); key genetic changes in plant architecture.

    Tehuacan Evidence: Coxcatlan phase (~5000–3400 BCE) shows increasing maize remains; still supplementary to wild foods.

    Other Domesticates: Squash varieties, beans, chili peppers, avocado, bottle gourd domesticated in this broad period.

    Persistent Mobility: Populations still semi-mobile; agriculture not yet primary subsistence.

    5. Late Archaic Intensification (4000–2500 BCE)

    Increasing Sedentism: Longer site occupation; more substantial structures; storage features.

    Pottery Emergence: Earliest ceramics ~2500–2300 BCE in various regions (Purrón phase in Tehuacan, early Barra on Pacific coast).

    First Villages: Small hamlet-scale settlements with pit houses; agricultural dependency increasing.

    Maize Intensification: Larger cob sizes; more productive varieties; maize becomes dietary staple.

    Regional Exchange: Obsidian from highland sources; marine shells; long-distance networks forming.

    6. Early Formative Villages (2500–1800 BCE)

    Village Spread: Permanent agricultural villages across highlands and lowlands; population growth.

    Social Differentiation: Burial evidence suggests emerging inequality; differential grave goods.

    Barra and Locona Phases (Soconusco): Pacific coast early ceramics; sophisticated pottery traditions.

    Mokaya Culture: Soconusco region; early complexity; precursor to Olmec interaction.

    Public Architecture: Small platform mounds appear; community ritual spaces.

    Ceramic Traditions: Regional pottery styles develop; markers of cultural identity.

    7. Olmec Emergence at San Lorenzo (1800–1200 BCE)

    San Lorenzo Tenochtitlan: Gulf Coast site (Veracruz); major center by ~1400 BCE; plateau modified for monumental display.

    Colossal Heads: First carved ~1400–1200 BCE; basalt boulders transported from Tuxtla Mountains (~80 km); ruler portraits.

    Long-Distance Trade: Obsidian from Guatemala and Pachuca; jade from Motagua Valley; serpentine, magnetite.

    Elite Ideology: Distinctive iconography (were-jaguar, maize deity); shamanic transformation themes; rulership symbols.

    Site Layout: Platform groups; drainage systems; elite residential areas; craft workshops.

    Olmec "Mother Culture" Debate: Extent of Olmec influence on Mesoamerican development debated; certainly influential but not sole source.

    8. San Lorenzo Apogee (1200–900 BCE)

    Peak Power: San Lorenzo at maximum extent and influence ~1200–1000 BCE; largest Gulf Coast center.

    Monument Production: Ten colossal heads; numerous thrones (altars); stelae and smaller sculptures.

    Widespread Influence: Olmec-style objects and iconography appear across Mesoamerica; trade, emulation, or direct contact.

    Craft Specialization: Jade working, basalt carving, pottery production; attached specialists.

    San Lorenzo Destruction: ~900 BCE monuments mutilated and buried; cause unclear (internal conflict, conquest, ritual).

    Subsistence: Maize agriculture, riverine resources, hunting; floodplain farming.

    9. La Venta Succession (900–400 BCE)

    La Venta: Succeeds San Lorenzo as dominant Gulf Coast center; ~900–400 BCE; island in coastal swamps.

    Pyramid Construction: Great Pyramid (~30 m high, clay construction); Complex A with buried offerings.

    Massive Offerings: Buried serpentine blocks, jade mosaics, figurines; ritual caching.

    Olmec Iconography Continues: Modified repertoire; maize symbolism elaborated; "Olmec dragon" complex.

    Tres Zapotes: Third major Olmec center; continues traditions after La Venta decline.

    Long Count Precursors: Stela C at Tres Zapotes has early Long Count date (debated); calendrical notation developing.

    10. Oaxacan Complexity (900–400 BCE)

    San José Mogote: Largest Valley of Oaxaca center ~1150–500 BCE; public buildings, elite residences.

    Chiefdom Organization: Three-tier settlement hierarchy; chiefly competition; iconographic display.

    Danzante Figures: Early carved captive/sacrifice images (~600–500 BCE); glyphic elements associated.

    Monte Albán Foundation: ~500 BCE; hilltop center founded by confederation or conquest; defensive position.

    Early Writing Experiments: Zapotec glyphic notation among earliest in Mesoamerica; still primarily oral society.

    Long-Distance Exchange: Obsidian, marine shell, jade; Gulf Coast contacts; peer polity interaction.

    11. Highland Mexico Development (800–400 BCE)

    Basin of Mexico Centers: Tlatilco (cemetery site with elaborate burials), Cuicuilco (circular pyramid).

    Cuicuilco: Major center ~800–200 BCE; circular stepped pyramid; population ~20,000 before volcanic destruction.

    Chinampas Precursors: Early raised-field agriculture in lake-bed areas; intensification.

    Regional Interaction: Olmec-style objects at Tlatilco; local traditions with external influences.

    West Mexico Shaft Tombs: Distinctive burial tradition in Jalisco/Colima/Nayarit region; separate development.

    12. Mesoamerican Interaction Sphere (600–400 BCE)

    Pan-Regional Exchange: Shared iconographic elements (feathered serpent, rain deity, maize symbolism) across regions.

    Epi-Olmec Transition: Olmec influence transforms into regional variants; no single dominant center.

    Glyphic Experiments: Zapotec, Epi-Olmec, early Maya glyphic notations develop; not yet full writing systems.

    Calendrical Notation: 260-day cycle and Long Count elements appearing; astronomical observation.

    Oral Threshold: Complex societies with monumental architecture, long-distance trade, emerging hierarchy, but writing not yet the medium institutions run on; oral tradition still primary.
