    6,000 BCE – 4,000 BCE — Middle Archaic and the Teosinte-Maize Transition

    Summary: The binding constraint is spoken language as the sole medium through which cultivation knowledge passes — seed selection, planting timing, and processing technique exist only in oral instruction and are lost if not transmitted. Within this two-millennium window, forager communities in highland Mexico complete the domestication of maize from its wild teosinte ancestor, a discrete macro-event that will transform Mesoamerican subsistence; the transition is accomplished through incremental selection guided by accumulated oral knowledge, with Guilá Naquitz and the Tehuacán Valley caves documenting the shift from wild teosinte to recognizably domestic maize cobs.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The domestication event concentrates in highland valleys: the Valley of Oaxaca (Guilá Naquitz at 1,500 m), the Tehuacán Valley (~1,500 m), and the Balsas River drainage where wild teosinte grows natively. These intermontane basins offer temperate climate, seasonal rainfall, and alluvial soils suited to early cultivation. Movement between highland valleys and lowland zones continues but the highlands become the center of agricultural innovation, their isolation encouraging local experimentation.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The Mid-Holocene (6,000–4,000 BCE) brings a drier interval in some regions, pushing foragers toward more reliable water sources and encouraging intensified plant manipulation. Seasonal monsoon rains define the agricultural calendar: plant in May-June, harvest in October-November. This rainfall regime makes maize cultivation viable but imposes a narrow planting window that must be tracked orally and transmitted across generations.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Volcanic activity continues in the Trans-Mexican Volcanic Belt; ash deposits periodically fertilize highland soils. Drought stress is more pronounced than in the Early Archaic, creating selection pressure for drought-tolerant maize varieties. Flash floods in highland arroyos threaten camps and early plantings. Bands buffer hazard through mobility and site selection transmitted as oral knowledge of safe locations.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Wild teosinte (Zea mays ssp. parviglumis) grows in the Balsas drainage and adjacent highlands — the genetic ancestor of domestic maize. Squash, beans, chili, and avocado continue under manipulation. Obsidian remains the premier toolstone; sources at Otumba and Pachuca are increasingly exploited. Animal resources (deer, rabbit, turkey) supplement the growing plant component of diet. The endowment is transitional: wild resources still dominant but cultivated plants rising in importance.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Population remains sparse enough that waste load is negligible. Midden deposits at rockshelters contain ash, bone, maize cob fragments, and lithic debitage but do not accumulate to problematic levels. Sink capacity remains unlimited for practical purposes.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Disease burden remains low. Sedentism is increasing but population density is still insufficient for epidemic transmission. Parasitic infections from water and stored food may increase slightly as people spend longer periods at cultivation sites. Mortality baseline is unchanged from the Early Archaic: injury, childbirth, and occasional food stress dominate.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: The dietary transition introduces new nutritional patterns. Maize provides carbohydrate calories but is deficient in niacin and certain amino acids unless processed with alkali (nixtamalization, which may develop later). Skeletal evidence is sparse but does not indicate nutritional decline; the mixed diet of cultivated plants, wild foods, and game maintains adequate nutrition.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Healing knowledge continues to pass orally. Medicinal plant use expands as foragers become more sedentary and familiar with local flora. No specialized healer class exists, but certain individuals accumulate expertise transmitted through apprenticeship. Recovery capacity remains limited by lack of surgical knowledge.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Pairing remains exogamous across bands. Increasing sedentism may begin to alter pairing patterns as bands spend more time at fixed locations. Physiological fertility is unimpaired. Child-carrying costs decrease slightly as mobility decreases, potentially allowing shorter birth intervals.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population density increases modestly, perhaps doubling over the window to 0.1–0.2 persons per square kilometer in favorable highland valleys. Maize cultivation supports more people per unit area than foraging alone. Growth remains slow; the agricultural transition is gradual, not a sudden transformation.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Dependency patterns shift slightly as sedentism increases. Elders can remain at base camps while productive adults forage or tend plots. The elderly become more valuable as repositories of accumulated cultivation knowledge — their extended survival serves the group's interest. Overall dependency load remains light.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Subsistence transitions from foraging-dominant to mixed foraging-cultivation. Maize, squash, and beans provide an increasing share of calories; hunting and gathering continue to supply protein and dietary variety. The provisioning floor rises as cultivation reduces subsistence risk, but dependence on rainfall timing introduces new vulnerabilities. Oral knowledge of planting calendars becomes critical.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle remains the sole power source. Fire is used for cooking, land clearing (possibly slash-and-burn), and processing. No animal traction exists. Energy conversion improves as cultivated plants yield more calories per unit labor than wild gathering — the fundamental logic of the agricultural transition.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Labor organization remains kin-based but becomes more differentiated. Cultivation introduces new tasks: plot clearing, planting, weeding, harvesting. Gender and age divisions may sharpen: women's gathering expertise transfers to plant cultivation; men continue hunting. Coercion remains absent; cooperation is enforced by kinship and reciprocity.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Surplus capacity begins to increase. A successful maize harvest can be stored longer than wild seeds, extending the food supply into the dry season. However, surplus is still modest — enough to buffer seasonal variation, not to support non-producing specialists. The transition enables surplus but does not yet generate stratification.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Storage technology improves as cultivation incentivizes preservation. Pit caches, ceramic containers (appearing late in the window), and woven granaries hold maize, beans, and squash seeds. Reserves extend to several months' consumption, reducing reliance on mobility as a buffer. The shift from movement to storage marks a fundamental economic transition.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Exchange patterns continue down-the-line trade in obsidian, shell, and exotic goods. New trade in seeds, cuttings, and agricultural knowledge may occur between bands, though evidence is indirect. Logistics remain foot-based. Trade networks expand slightly as population density increases and more groups participate in regional exchange.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No formal value medium exists. Stored grain functions as a form of wealth — surplus maize can be traded or gifted for social advantage. Deferred reciprocity continues: obligations are remembered and enforced through oral agreement and reputation. Credit remains purely social.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No extraction institutions exist. Redistribution operates through sharing norms applied to harvests as to hunts: successful producers share with kin and band members. The transition introduces a new basis for potential inequality — differential access to stored surplus — but this potential is not yet realized.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Wealth inequality remains minimal. Stored grain and productive plots represent new forms of wealth, but sharing norms constrain accumulation. Defense of assets is social — reputation and kinship deter theft — rather than physical. The concept of land ownership does not yet crystallize.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Organic waste (cob fragments, chaff, husks) accumulates at processing sites. Maize waste may be fed to dogs or burned. No persistent pollution develops. Financial waste concepts remain inapplicable.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Kinship remains the organizing principle. As cultivation increases, land-use rights may become more formalized — a family's plot passes to descendants, creating incipient lineage property. Inheritance of material goods remains minimal, but inheritance of cultivation knowledge (which plots, which varieties, which timing) becomes crucial and passes orally through family lines.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Mutual aid extends to agricultural labor — planting and harvesting may require more hands than a nuclear family provides. Work parties draw on kin and neighbors, reinforcing social bonds. Seasonal aggregations continue for ritual and exchange. The agricultural transition strengthens mutual-aid networks by creating new opportunities for cooperation.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Leisure patterns shift with the agricultural cycle. Intensive work during planting and harvest is balanced by slack periods. Evening sociability around fires continues; storytelling and song transmit cultivation knowledge alongside myth. Sociability reinforces the oral tradition on which agricultural success depends.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Stratification remains minimal but new bases for status emerge. A family with abundant stored surplus gains prestige through generosity. Cultivation knowledge becomes a form of social capital — those who know when to plant, which seeds to select, how to process and store gain authority. Status is still achieved, not ascribed, but the seeds of differentiation are planted.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No formal administration exists. Decision-making remains consensual and oral. The agricultural transition does not yet require coordination beyond the band level. Scheduling is embedded in oral tradition, not administrative apparatus.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No specialized coercive apparatus exists. Conflict over resources may increase as cultivation makes locations more valuable, but violence remains interpersonal and managed through kinship. Weapons are still subsistence tools (atlatl, spear) that double for conflict.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Social friction remains low. Increased sedentism may create new tensions over plot access, but kinship mediation and fission remain available. No excluded class or standing grievance accumulates. Apparatus decay is inapplicable.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Sentiment remains intimate and kin-based. Confidence may increase as stored surplus reduces subsistence anxiety. The behavioral temper is increasingly oriented toward the agricultural cycle — attention to rainfall, planting timing, and harvest success. Trust networks expand slightly as sedentism increases face-to-face interaction with neighbors.

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Leadership remains informal. Agricultural expertise becomes a new source of authority: the elder who remembers drought-resistant varieties or optimal planting times commands attention. Oratorical skill matters — leaders must persuade through speech, explaining and justifying decisions to skeptical band members.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Legitimacy remains personal. Successful cultivation enhances a leader's standing; crop failure undermines it. Allegiance attaches to those who provide security and knowledge. Exit remains the primary check on authority — dissatisfied individuals or families can leave and join other groups.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Collective action expands to include communal agricultural tasks. Will is mobilized through discussion at band gatherings. Coordination across bands remains limited to seasonal aggregations for ritual, exchange, and intermarriage. No supra-band political units exist.

    External Relations & Posture: Relations with other groups remain governed by kinship and trade. The agricultural transition may reduce territorial fluidity — groups become more attached to productive land. Posture remains cautious and reciprocal, but defense of cultivation sites may become more salient than in the purely foraging past.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Disaffection is managed through exit and fission. The agricultural transition creates new stakes — leaving means abandoning cultivated plots — but mobility remains viable. Apathy is rare; the demands of cultivation require active engagement. Exhaustion of will does not accumulate at this scale.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Speech remains universal. The agricultural transition adds new vocabulary for plants, cultivation techniques, and seasonal timing. Regional dialects continue to differentiate. No script exists; all agricultural knowledge is encoded in oral form. Gestural and pictorial communication continues to supplement speech.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Channels remain face-to-face and oral. Information about cultivation techniques spreads through kinship networks and inter-band contact at seasonal gatherings. Velocity is slow but adequate — agricultural knowledge accumulates over generations, not seasons. A useful innovation may take decades to spread across the region.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Memory remains the sole storage medium. Cultivation knowledge — which seeds to save, when to plant, how to process — is encoded in oral instruction, song, and myth. Persistence depends on transmission; the loss of knowledgeable elders can set a group back generations. The stakes of transmission failure rise as cultivation becomes more important.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Discourse norms continue to privilege elder authority. Agricultural knowledge may be treated as family patrimony, shared within lineages but guarded from outsiders. Certain cultivation secrets (the best seed varieties, optimal processing techniques) may be restricted, creating incipient knowledge asymmetries.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Attention focuses on agricultural matters during critical seasons. Stories encode planting calendars and processing techniques; songs mark seasonal transitions. Amplification occurs through retelling; successful techniques spread as groups observe their neighbors' success and ask how it was achieved.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Signal integrity depends on repeated practice. Agricultural knowledge is tested against outcome: techniques that produce good harvests are reinforced; those that fail are discarded. Verification is empirical, built into the cultivation cycle. Noise is low; information volume remains manageable for oral tradition.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Justice continues to center on reciprocity. New questions arise: who has right to use a cleared plot? What claims does shared labor create? The moral foundation expands to encompass agricultural property relations, though these remain embedded in kinship rather than formalized in law. Generosity with surplus remains a core virtue.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No formal law exists. Customary norms expand to cover agricultural situations: rights to cleared land, obligations of labor sharing, claims on stored surplus. These norms evolve through precedent held in collective memory. Taboos continue to carry supernatural sanction.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Disputes over land use and surplus are adjudicated through kinship mediation. Liability may extend across generations — a family's claim to land derives from ancestors who cleared it. Sanction remains informal: shaming, compensation, ostracism. Violence over resources may increase but is constrained by kinship networks.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Rights crystallize around land use: the family that clears and cultivates a plot has claims on it. These rights are not formalized but socially recognized. Protection derives from kinship and reputation. Compliance burden remains light; rules are familiar and internalized.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Impunity remains limited by social proximity. As sedentism increases, disputes are harder to escape — parties must continue to interact. This creates pressure for resolution but also risk of chronic grievance. Redress through compensation or mediation remains the norm; unredressed wrong accumulates slowly if at all.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: The domestication of maize represents a major intellectual achievement: sustained, multi-generational observation and selection that transforms a wild grass into a productive crop. Inquiry is practical — which plants to favor, which conditions produce better yields — but its accumulation constitutes applied botany. Paradigm remains animist; spirits are propitiated for good harvests.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Technique expands dramatically. Seed selection (choosing the largest, most productive cobs for replanting) is the core method, transmitted orally from parent to child. Plot preparation, planting depth, weeding, and harvest timing all constitute technical knowledge. Processing techniques (grinding, possibly early nixtamalization) are developed and refined. All are transmitted through oral instruction and demonstration.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Education remains embedded in practice. Children learn cultivation by working alongside adults. The knowledge stock expands significantly — each generation adds to the accumulated understanding of maize and associated crops. Skill distribution broadens as more families practice cultivation, but specialized expertise (the best seed-savers, the most knowledgeable processors) confers status.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Some foraging knowledge may begin to decay as cultivation absorbs more attention. However, mixed subsistence maintains most foraging skills. Knowledge decay remains the primary risk — an epidemic that kills elders, a family line that dies out, could erase generations of accumulated cultivation expertise. Transmission is the fragile link.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Maize begins to enter mythos. The plant that feeds becomes sacred; origin stories may start to incorporate maize as a gift from spirits or ancestors. Place-names and memory attach to productive sites. Identity begins to include "those who grow maize" alongside kinship and locality markers. The transformation is gradual but foundational.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: The prevailing order remains simple and needs little justification. However, the agricultural transition introduces new claims: families that clear and plant have rights to harvest. Legitimation of these claims derives from labor invested, transmitted as oral precedent. No elaborate ideology is required at this scale.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Expression continues in rock art, personal ornament, and perishable forms. Maize imagery may begin to appear, though evidence is sparse for this early period. Material culture expands with cultivation tools: planting sticks, storage baskets, grinding implements. Architecture remains minimal — brush shelters and rockshelters — but more permanent camps emerge at cultivation sites.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Form exhaustion does not occur. Expressive tradition adapts to incorporate agricultural themes. Symbols remain potent and actively used. Anomie does not develop; shared agricultural practice reinforces community meaning-making.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: The cosmos remains animate. Plants, including cultivated ones, possess spirit and require propitiation. The agricultural cycle reinforces cyclical cosmology: planting, growth, harvest, death, renewal. Existential outlook expands to include gratitude and obligation to the plants that sustain life — reciprocity extends from human relations to the plant world.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Ritual expands to accompany cultivation: planting ceremonies, harvest thanks, first-fruits offerings. Practice remains collective and oral — prayers and songs transmitted across generations. Sacred sites may include especially productive plots or locations where maize was first successfully grown. Devotion is embedded in agricultural labor itself.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: No specialized religious institutions exist. Ritual specialists may hold knowledge of agricultural rites, but they are not a separate class. Authority remains distributed among elders. Orthodoxy is tradition, enforced by consensus and the empirical test of harvest success.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Cultivation spreads with its spiritual accompaniments. Groups that adopt maize also adopt associated rites and beliefs. Syncretism is natural — new plant spirits join existing pantheons. No displacement or conversion occurs; spiritual change is gradual and additive.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Disenchantment does not occur. The agricultural transition reinforces enchantment: the mysterious productivity of plants, the dependence on unseen forces (rain, fertility), all demand spiritual interpretation. The sacred remains fully engaged in daily life. No residue of unfulfilled transcendence accumulates.
