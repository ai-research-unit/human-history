    1200 BCE – 900 BCE — San Lorenzo Apogee and Olmec Expansion

    Summary: A three-century trend covering the peak of San Lorenzo as the dominant Gulf Coast center and the spread of Olmec influence across Mesoamerica, ending with the deliberate destruction and abandonment of San Lorenzo circa 900 BCE. The binding constraint is the elite's capacity to sustain long-distance exchange networks and monument production while dependent on a limited agrarian surplus and lacking coercive institutions; the central tension is the widening gap between elite claims to cosmic authority and the fragile material base supporting them. Margin against shock is thin, and the period closes with systemic rupture — monument mutilation suggesting violent internal conflict or ritual termination. Direction of change is toward maximum elaboration followed by collapse.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: San Lorenzo plateau dominates the Coatzacoalcos River floodplain, providing defensive elevation and display visibility; river corridors remain primary transit routes for basalt transport and trade goods; dense tropical forest continues to impose high overland friction; exchange networks now extend to Guatemala, Oaxaca, and the Basin of Mexico.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Tropical wet-dry regime persists with heavy monsoonal rains and pronounced dry season; interannual variability in monsoon timing creates periodic stress on planting schedules; no documented secular shift within this window.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Floodplain dependence maintains exposure to seasonal flooding; hurricane risk present; volcanic activity from Tuxtla Mountains quiescent but potential; margin against major flood remains narrow; no catastrophic event documented until final destruction.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Alluvial fertility maintained by seasonal flooding; basalt quarries in Tuxtla uplands heavily exploited for monument production; obsidian, jade, serpentine, and magnetite continue to arrive through exchange; local energy stock adequate but monument-scale projects strain surplus capacity.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Increased population and craft production raise waste output but remain well within absorptive capacity of tropical soils and waterways; no evidence of degradation or sink stress.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Tropical disease burden continues — intestinal parasites, fevers, insect vectors; mortality baseline stable; no epidemic documented; population growth suggests pathogen load manageable.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Diet remains maize-based with significant riverine protein; floodplain agriculture productive; nutritional status adequate; skeletal evidence limited but suggests maintenance of physiological vitality established earlier.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Pairing patterns stable within kin-regulated frameworks; elite polygyny probable given wealth concentration; reproductive capacity sufficient to support continued demographic growth.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: San Lorenzo and satellite communities reach peak population, perhaps five thousand to ten thousand in the immediate region; density still below carrying capacity but rising; renewal positive; growth rate probably slowing as local ceiling approaches.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Dependency absorbed within household and lineage structures; no systemic overhang; productive cohort not strained by dependent load.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Maize agriculture on floodplain levees supplemented by fishing, hunting, and collecting; subsistence floor adequate but margin thinning as population grows and elite extraction increases; household pooling remains primary buffer.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle remains sole energy source; fire for craft production; no mechanical or animal power; per-capita energy ceiling unchanged; monumental projects require maximum seasonal labor mobilization, approaching limits of corvee capacity.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Labor mobilized through feasting obligations, kinship duty, and ideological commitment; corvee intensifying for monument production; coercion remains indirect but compulsion increasing as elite demands grow; specialized craft labor attached to elite households.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Agricultural surplus near maximum sustainable level; craft workshops produce jade ornaments, fine ceramics, and stone sculpture at peak output; surplus capacity fully committed to elite projects; no slack remaining.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Household granaries continue; some evidence of communal storage at elite compounds; buffer depth limited, perhaps one poor harvest; risk-pooling strained as elite extraction intensifies.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Long-distance networks at maximum extent — Guatemalan obsidian, Motagua jade, Oaxacan greenstone, Pacific shell, Basin of Mexico goods; river and porterage logistics sophisticated; exchange remains embedded in elite gift-giving and alliance; no market infrastructure.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Prestige goods (jade, greenstone celts, fine ceramics) serve as value stores; deferred reciprocity obligations structure elite partnerships; no monetary medium; credit embedded in kinship and alliance debts that may strain under competitive pressure.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Elite extraction intensifying through expanded feasting obligations and monument sponsorship; redistribution flows back as ceremonial participation and prestige-good gifts to subordinate elites; extraction load approaching limits the productive base can bear.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Wealth concentration at maximum — ten colossal heads, numerous thrones, elite compound elaboration; commoner-elite gap at widest point; elite defend assets through ritual authority, alliance networks, and kinship; no fortification; instability potential rising.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Production waste increases with craft intensification but remains within sink capacity; basalt chips, jade debitage, ceramic sherds dispersed in middens; no toxic accumulation.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust within kin networks intact; inter-elite trust strained by competitive display and exchange rivalry; reciprocal aid obligations stretched as extraction demands increase; trust fabric under pressure but not yet torn.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Two-tier stratification sharpened — elite lineages marked by exclusive access to jade, monumental portraiture, and ritual prerogatives; commoner majority increasingly differentiated from elite; boundaries hardening; factional competition among elite lineages intensifying.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Kinship structures stable; elite inheritance contested as stakes rise; subordinate lineage claims may challenge dominant lines; household formation continues under kin regulation; inheritance disputes probable though not documented.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Village councils and communal work groups continue; labor demands strain associational capacity; self-organization below elite level may be weakening under extraction pressure; fabric thinning.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Communal feasting at peak scale provides sociability and elite legitimation venue; ball game possibly present though court evidence limited; leisure concentrated in ceremonial contexts increasingly controlled by elite.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Ritual participation and feasting continue to provide discharge; elite arbitration available; but growing extraction and inequality may outpace discharge capacity; friction accumulating.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Chiefdom authority at maximum; rulers command labor for colossal head production and control long-distance exchange; power personalized in individual rulers depicted in portraits; centralizing trend reaches limit of non-bureaucratic organization.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Succession remains within elite lineages but intensifying competition probable; ten colossal heads suggest multiple rulers or contested claims over the period; transfer a flashpoint as factionalism rises; final destruction may reflect succession crisis.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Rulers claim cosmic mediation through were-jaguar transformation and maize deity association; legitimacy propagated through monumental display at peak intensity; narrative credibility may be straining as demands exceed delivery; erosion not directly attested but instability at window's end suggests limits reached.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Administration remains personal and kin-based; no bureaucracy, writing, or formal records; capacity sufficient for peak mobilization but no institutional buffer against ruler failure; integrity dependent on personal networks vulnerable to disruption.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Commoner participation limited to labor and ritual attendance; no voice in decisions; elite factionalism is the mobilization dynamic; dissent expressed through withdrawal or factional alignment; channels not opening.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: San Lorenzo dominant regionally; Olmec-style objects and iconography spread to Oaxaca, Basin of Mexico, Pacific coast, and Maya lowlands; influence through emulation and exchange rather than conquest; no external military threat documented; expansion of prestige sphere at maximum.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No standing army; elite retainers serve as warriors when needed; weaponry includes obsidian-edged clubs, spears, and knives; force sufficient for internal control and limited raiding; monument destruction at 900 BCE suggests internal violence the apparatus could not prevent or that it perpetrated.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Internal order strained by elite competition and commoner grievance; monument mutilation and burial circa 900 BCE indicates violent rupture — whether internal faction war, popular revolt, or ritual termination unclear; threat exceeded enforcement capacity.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Oral transmission via messenger along trade routes; velocity unchanged; reach at maximum extent through exchange networks spanning Mesoamerica; elite control information flow through monopoly on long-distance contacts.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: No writing system; information persists in oral memory, ritual performance, and monumental iconography; colossal heads and thrones store political claims in durable basalt; but information remains vulnerable to specialist death, monument destruction, and population dispersal — all realized at window's end.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Elite framing monopoly at peak; monumental iconography propagates ruler authority; oral tradition controlled by ritual specialists; no formal censorship; discourse norms enforce deference; band not documented as changing.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Monumental display dominates attention at San Lorenzo center; feasting performances amplify elite messaging; audience reach at maximum for face-to-face polity; reception patterns stable.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Face-to-face communication maintains signal integrity; oral transmission drift a long-term risk; no evidence of noise crisis; verification through communal repetition adequate.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Justice grounded in kinship reciprocity and cosmic balance; elite claims to supernatural sanction underwrite moral order; foundations stable but elite impunity may strain popular acceptance.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No formal law; oral custom and taboo enforced through kinship and ritual sanction; no evolution attested; regulatory apparatus non-existent in formal sense.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Chiefs and kin elders adjudicate disputes; enforcement through social pressure and supernatural threat; liability assigned within kinship; no courts; throughput adequate for scale.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: No formal rights; protection through kin membership; compliance burden light for custom-embedded behavior; extraction demands may feel burdensome but frame as obligation rather than compliance with external rule.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Elite impunity widening with stratification; commoner grievance against elite extraction limited in redress options; accumulated wrong may fuel instability realized at window's end; gap between justice owed and delivered widening.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Practical investigation of materials, hydrology, and astronomy continues; cosmological paradigm integrating natural and supernatural causation unchanged; no paradigm shift; discovery frontier at stone-working and iconographic elaboration.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Basalt carving reaches highest sophistication — ten colossal heads weighing up to fifty tons each transported and carved; jade-working technique refined; ceramic production diversified; agricultural water management maintained; technical repertoire at peak.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Apprenticeship transmission within craft lineages; ritual specialists hold cosmological knowledge; literacy absent; skill concentration among specialists at maximum; general population retains subsistence and household knowledge; stock stable.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Risk of knowledge loss rising as specialist concentration increases; destruction and abandonment at 900 BCE will trigger significant knowledge decay when specialists disperse or die; decay latent, realized at window's end.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Olmec identity crystallized around San Lorenzo as sacred center, were-jaguar transformation complex, and maize deity cult; origin narratives not directly attested but collective memory centers on colossal head rulers as founding figures; identity at maximum coherence.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Monument production at peak — colossal heads, thrones depicting emergence from earth-mouth, stelae, small jade and stone figurines; style defines Mesoamerican influence; aesthetic conventions canonical; material culture at apogee.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Elite mood reflects confidence in cosmic authority; commoner sentiment not directly attested; growing extraction may produce resentment beneath surface compliance; disposition shifting toward tension by window's end; monument destruction suggests mood rupture.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Iconic forms at maximum potency; no exhaustion yet; but rapid termination of the tradition at 900 BCE suggests either sudden rupture or elite overreach that hollowed meaning faster than new forms could sustain it; decay incipient at close.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Layered cosmos with supernatural forces animating nature; rulers as transformer-shamans bridging realms; existential orientation toward cosmic balance maintenance; piety and fatalism intertwined; outlook stable but failure of elite mediation may trigger doubt.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Ritual elaboration at peak — monument placement, bloodletting, buried offerings, communal ceremony at platforms; devotional infrastructure concentrated at San Lorenzo; ritual calendar structured around agricultural and ceremonial cycles; practice intensive.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Elite lineages monopolize ritual authority; no separate priesthood; doctrine embedded in iconography and oral tradition; orthodoxy enforced through elite control; authority at maximum but vulnerable to delegitimation if rulers fail.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Olmec spiritual complex spreading rapidly across Mesoamerica through elite emulation and exchange; earlier local traditions incorporated or subordinated; diffusion at maximum; influence defines pan-regional style; no resistance documented.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No disenchantment during apogee; sacred meaning at maximum production; but monument destruction at 900 BCE suggests spiritual crisis or deliberate desacralization — either rulers' cosmic claims failed catastrophically or successor faction ritually terminated the order; discharge concentrated at termination event rather than accumulating gradually.
