    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Eastern Mediterranean Coast and Jordan Rift Valley

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 50000 BCE – 2000 BCE

    Region: The Eastern Mediterranean Coast and Jordan Rift Valley — the Levantine corridor from the Taurus foothills to the Sinai, including the coastal plain, the Galilee highlands, the Jordan Valley, and the Transjordanian plateau; bounded by the Mediterranean to the west and the Syrian-Arabian steppe to the east.

    Note on Evidence: The Levant possesses one of the densest archaeological records globally. Stratified cave sequences (Kebara, Hayonim, el-Wad), open-air sites (Ohalo II, Abu Hureyra, Jericho, 'Ain Ghazal), and abundant radiocarbon dating provide exceptionally detailed chronology. This is the primary region for documenting the origins of agriculture.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Mousterian (50000–45000 BCE)

    Neanderthal Presence: Neanderthals occupied the Levant from ~120,000 BP. Late Mousterian assemblages (Tabun C, D-type) are associated with Neanderthal remains at Kebara, Amud, and Tabun caves.

    Early Modern Humans: Modern humans present at Skhul and Qafzeh by ~100,000 BP, but appear to withdraw or hybridize; reappearance in the transition period.

    Tabun Cave Sequence: Key stratified site (Mount Carmel) spanning Middle to Upper Paleolithic; provides reference chronology.

    Mousterian Technology: Levallois point production, side-scrapers, bifacial foliates. Varied reduction strategies suggest adaptive flexibility.

    Climate Context: MIS 3 (~60,000–30,000 BP) with oscillating conditions; Mediterranean woodland and steppe mosaics.

    2. Initial Upper Paleolithic (45000–30000 BCE)

    Emiran Industry: Transitional industry featuring Emireh points (retouched Levallois points); found at Boker Tachtit, Ksar Akil. Marks Middle-to-Upper Paleolithic transition ~47,000–43,000 BP.

    Ahmarian Tradition: Earliest blade-based Upper Paleolithic; characterized by el-Wad points and bladelet production. Sites: Kebara, Üçağızlı.

    Modern Human Expansion: Genetic and archaeological evidence indicates modern human populations expand into the Levant, possibly replacing or absorbing residual Neanderthal groups.

    Symbolic Behavior: Shell beads (Nassarius at Üçağızlı, Ksar Akil), ochre use intensify; evidence of personal ornamentation.

    Mobility Patterns: Mobile hunting bands; seasonal movement between coastal and interior zones.

    3. Upper Paleolithic (30000–20000 BCE)

    Levantine Aurignacian: Related to but distinct from European Aurignacian; bone tools, carinated scrapers. Less elaborate than European counterpart.

    Artistic Expression: Limited compared to Europe; some incised stones and bones; continued use of beads and ochre.

    LGM Onset: Last Glacial Maximum (~26,500–19,000 BP) brings cooler, drier conditions; vegetation shifts; population density may decline.

    Site Use: Cave occupation continues (Hayonim, Kebara); open-air sites less visible archaeologically.

    Hunting Emphasis: Gazelle, fallow deer, wild cattle, wild boar primary prey.

    4. Kebaran Complex (20000–15000 BCE)

    Microlithic Technology: Geometric microliths (lunates, trapezes) dominate; hafted in composite tools.

    Mobility: Small, highly mobile bands; evidence of frequent site abandonment and reoccupation.

    LGM Adaptation: Reduced site sizes; exploitation of broader resource range; increased emphasis on small game.

    Key Sites: Kebara Cave (type site), Ein Gev I, Neve David.

    Social Organization: Band-level society; no evidence of sedentism or storage; immediate-return economy.

    5. Geometric Kebaran (15000–12500 BCE)

    Climate Amelioration: Late glacial warming (Bølling-Allerød); vegetation expansion; wetter conditions.

    Plant Processing Intensification: Ground stone tools (pestles, mortars) increase; wild cereal exploitation intensifies.

    Site Aggregation: Larger sites; evidence of longer occupation periods; possible early sedentism.

    Mortuary Elaboration: Burials at sites like Neve David; early cemetery development.

    Regional Variants: Mushabian in the Negev and Sinai; Ramonian; Geometric Kebaran proper in Mediterranean zone.

    6. Early Natufian (12500–11000 BCE)

    First Sedentary Villages: Year-round occupation at sites like Eynan (Ain Mallaha), Hayonim Cave and Terrace, Wadi Hammeh 27.

    Architectural Features: Stone-founded circular structures (diameter 2–9 m); storage pits; organized village layouts.

    Cereal Harvesting: Sickle blades with characteristic gloss; grinding slabs; intensive wild wheat and barley collection.

    Cemeteries: Formalized burial grounds; secondary burial; grave goods including shell ornaments, stone objects.

    Social Complexity: Evidence of increased social differentiation; decorated objects; possible ritual specialists.

    Fauna: Gazelle remains dominant; early possible dog domestication.

    Eynan (Ain Mallaha): Major Early Natufian village in the Huleh Basin; stone structures, cemetery with ~100 burials, elaborate burials with animal associations.

    7. Late Natufian (11000–9700 BCE)

    Younger Dryas Crisis (~12,900–11,700 BP): Cold, dry period; vegetation stress; reduced cereal availability.

    Settlement Dispersal: Some sites abandoned; smaller, more mobile occupations; increased reliance on meat.

    Regional Variability: Core Natufian zone contracts; peripheral populations may intensify movement.

    Continuity of Technology: Lunates and other Natufian tools persist; some reduction in elaborate material culture.

    Subsistence Stress: Isotopic evidence suggests dietary stress in some populations.

    Prelude to Agriculture: Selective pressure may have favored proto-cultivation as wild resources declined.

    8. Pre-Pottery Neolithic A (9700–8500 BCE)

    Agricultural Transition: First evidence of cultivated emmer wheat, einkorn, barley; morphological domestication underway.

    Jericho (Tell es-Sultan): Walled settlement (1.5 ha); tower (8.5 m high); thousands of inhabitants; earliest large-scale communal construction.

    Settlement Pattern: Villages in optimal agricultural zones (Jordan Valley, Damascus Basin); permanent architecture.

    Ritual Architecture: Communal buildings with benches, plastered floors; skull removal and caching practices begin.

    Technology: Continuation of microlithic tradition; groundstone elaboration; no pottery.

    Key Sites: Jericho, Netiv Hagdud, Gilgal, Tell Aswad, Mureybet (phase III).

    Social Organization: Village-scale communities; emerging communal ritual; possible kin-based landholding.

    9. Early PPNB (8500–7500 BCE)

    Domestic Animals: Goats domesticated by ~8000 BCE; evidence from 'Ain Ghazal, Abu Hureyra, Çayönü.

    Architectural Change: Rectangular, multi-room houses replace earlier circular forms; lime plaster floors.

    Expanded Geographic Range: PPNB settlements spread across the Levant, into Anatolia and Cyprus.

    Technology: Naviform blade production; projectile points (Jericho, Byblos types).

    Key Sites: Beidha, early 'Ain Ghazal, Yiftahel.

    Ritual Elaboration: Skull plastering begins; lime-plaster statues; communal buildings.

    10. Late PPNB (7500–6500 BCE)

    Mega-Sites: 'Ain Ghazal (10–14 ha), Basta, Abu Hureyra reach populations estimated at 2,000–3,000.

    Full Domestic Package: Wheat, barley, lentils, peas; goats, sheep, pigs, cattle all present.

    Plastered Skulls: Elaborate treatment of ancestral skulls with lime plaster and shell eyes; found at Jericho, 'Ain Ghazal, Tell Ramad.

    Lime-Plaster Statues: 'Ain Ghazal cache of large (~1 m) human figures; ritual significance.

    Long-Distance Exchange: Obsidian from Anatolia; seashells from Mediterranean and Red Sea; greenstone from Cyprus.

    Pressure on Resources: Faunal evidence at 'Ain Ghazal shows shift from wild game to domestic animals; possible deforestation and overgrazing.

    11. PPNC / Final PPNB (6500–5800 BCE)

    Settlement Contraction: Many large sites abandoned or reduced; 'Ain Ghazal population decline.

    Environmental Stress Hypothesis: Deforestation, soil degradation, and climate fluctuation (8.2 ka event approaching) implicated in collapse.

    Pastoral Specialization: Some populations shift to increased reliance on herding; mobile pastoralism emerges.

    Continuity in Some Areas: Smaller villages persist; not total abandonment but restructuring.

    Proto-Halaf and Northern Contacts: Connections to emerging northern Mesopotamian traditions.

    12. Pottery Neolithic (5800–5200 BCE)

    Pottery Adoption: First ceramics appear; coarse wares initially, then decorated.

    Yarmukian Culture: Defined by incised pottery with herringbone patterns; clay figurines; continued village farming.

    Sha'ar Hagolan: Large Yarmukian village (20+ ha) in Jordan Valley; street layout; figurines; groundstone art.

    Settlement Pattern: Return to nucleated villages; population recovery in some areas.

    Lodian Culture: Southern variant; coarser pottery; different stylistic affiliation.

    Technology: Pottery allows new cooking and storage techniques; dietary expansion.

    13. Late Pottery Neolithic (5200–4500 BCE)

    Wadi Rabah Culture: Central/northern Levantine pottery tradition; bow-rim jars; connections to northern Syria.

    Regional Diversity: Multiple local traditions; less cultural unity than PPNB.

    Exchange Networks: Long-distance exchange continues but on different networks.

    Pastoral Component: Growing importance of pastoral groups; movement between settled and mobile lifeways.

    Technology: Continued ceramic development; possible introduction of plow agriculture.

    14. Chalcolithic (4500–3600 BCE)

    Ghassulian Culture: Type site Teleilat el-Ghassul (Jordan Valley); distinctive painted pottery, ivory carvings, basalt vessels.

    Metallurgy: First copper smelting and casting in the region; Nahal Mishmar "Cave of the Treasure" hoard (400+ copper objects, sophisticated lost-wax casting).

    Beer Sheba Culture: Subterranean settlement in Negev; copper workshop sites.

    Social Differentiation: Elite goods (ivory, copper, maces) indicate increasing inequality; possible chiefdoms.

    Ritual Sites: Gilat site (cult center); En-Gedi "temple"; elaborate burial customs.

    Subsistence: Mixed farming; olive/grape cultivation may begin; pastoral transhumance.

    Settlement Hierarchy: Small sites to larger centers; regional organization emerging.

    15. Early Bronze Age I (3600–3100 BCE)

    Proto-Urban Settlements: First walled towns appear; Megiddo, Beth Shan, Tel Erani, Arad.

    Egyptian Contact: Late Predynastic/Early Dynastic Egyptian presence in southern Levant; Narmer serekh at sites; Egyptian settlement at Tel Erani.

    Regional Polities: Local rulers; evidence of wealth differentiation; large public buildings.

    Technology: Potter's wheel introduced; metallurgy advances.

    Continuity and Change: Development from Chalcolithic base; new settlement pattern emphasizing defensible tells.

    Key Sites: Early Megiddo, Arad I-II, Tel Erani.

    16. EB II–III (3100–2500 BCE)

    Urban Florescence: Walled cities with populations of several thousand; Megiddo, Hazor, Beth Yerah, Khirbet Kerak.

    Palace Economies: Centralized storage; administrative buildings; standardized production.

    Fortifications: Massive city walls; towers; evidence of organized warfare.

    Egyptian Relations: Sustained contact; trade in timber, wine, oil; possible military campaigns (Narmer, Den, Khasekhemwy).

    Khirbet Kerak Ware: Intrusive pottery tradition (~2700–2400 BCE) from Transcaucasia; evidence of population movement.

    Exchange: Timber to Egypt; copper; wine and oil production intensifies.

    17. EB IV / Intermediate Bronze Age (2500–2000 BCE)

    Urban Collapse: Cities abandoned; dispersed village and pastoral settlement.

    Climate Factor: 4.2 ka event (~2200 BCE) implicated; aridity spike.

    Egyptian Withdrawal: Old Kingdom collapse; Egyptian presence in Levant ends.

    Pastoral Expansion: Increased evidence of mobile pastoral groups; shaft-tomb cemeteries.

    Regional Diversity: Multiple local traditions emerge; less cultural uniformity.

    Continuity Elements: Farming continues in favored areas; not total depopulation.

    Transition to Middle Bronze: By ~2000 BCE, recovery and reorganization leading to MB I.
