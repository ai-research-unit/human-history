    8500 BCE – 7500 BCE — Early Pre-Pottery Neolithic B and Goat Domestication

    Summary: The thousand-year window following the PPNA witnesses a second structural revolution in human subsistence: the addition of domestic animals — first goats, then sheep, pigs, and cattle — to an already established cereal-pulse complex. Rectangular, lime-plastered multi-room houses replace the earlier circular forms, signaling a shift toward modular household units and longer residential investment. Settlement expands beyond the Jordan Valley heartland into the Mediterranean hills and the northern Levant, linking the region to emerging PPNB networks in Anatolia and Cyprus. The binding constraint is the labor and land needed to sustain mixed agropastoral production without depleting soil or browse; the system holds moderate margin because wild resources remain available as fallback. The trajectory is one of geographic expansion, architectural elaboration, and deepening reliance on managed species.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Levantine corridor retains its north-south axis of low-friction transit along the Jordan Rift and coastal plain; the Galilee highlands and Transjordanian plateau present moderate gradients favoring dispersed settlement; passes through the Taurus foothills link the region to Anatolian obsidian sources.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Early Holocene optimum conditions deliver warmer, wetter winters than today; Mediterranean oak-pistachio woodland advances into formerly steppe zones; seasonal rainfall reliably supports rain-fed cereals in the 300–600 mm isohyet band; interannual variability is moderate and within the margin of village-scale storage.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: The Dead Sea Transform remains seismically active; localized flooding along Jordan tributaries recurs episodically; drought cycles of one to two years can stress stored reserves but do not yet trigger abandonment — the system holds adequate slack.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Flint nodules remain abundant in limestone exposures; bitumen seeps at the Dead Sea provide hafting material; obsidian — absent locally — is imported from Anatolian sources (Cappadocia, Lake Van); fuel wood and browse are plentiful as woodland cover is near its Holocene maximum.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Human and animal waste disperses into open landscape beyond village margins; middens accumulate but settlement densities remain low enough that absorptive capacity is nowhere near saturation; deforestation pressure is incipient but not yet binding.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Proximity to domestic goats introduces zoonotic exposure absent in foraging populations, but herd sizes remain small and contact episodic; endemic gastro-intestinal parasites persist; mortality remains dominated by trauma and infection rather than epidemic disease.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: The mixed diet of cereals, pulses, wild game, and early domestic meat maintains skeletal robustness comparable to PPNA populations; dental pathology increases with carbohydrate intake but overall stature is stable; nutritional margin is adequate.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Sedentary village life shortens birth spacing relative to mobile foragers; lactation intervals may decline; pair-bonding occurs within village contexts; reproductive rates rise modestly, supporting population growth.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Village populations range from a few dozen to several hundred; regional density increases as new sites are founded across the PPNB expansion zone; birth rates exceed death rates, driving steady demographic growth.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Infant mortality remains high; adults rarely survive past fifty; the dependency ratio is manageable because productive adults predominate and communal structures absorb orphans and the elderly without approaching capacity limits.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Mixed agropastoral production supplies grain, pulses, milk, and occasional meat; wild gazelle and other game remain significant; the floor is secure but still tied to local rainfall and herd health; household pooling buffers shortfalls.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle remains the primary mover; domestic goats provide some traction-equivalent labor (transport, browse clearing) and convert inedible scrub into meat and milk; wood-fired hearths supply heat and lime-burning capacity; per-capita energy throughput rises modestly.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Household labor predominates; task specialization by age and sex is evident in craft debris distributions; communal labor for lime-plastering and construction is episodic and reciprocal; coercion is minimal, organized through kin obligation rather than authority.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Cereal yields support year-round consumption with modest surplus; surplus flows into seed stock, animal fodder, and exchange goods; production capacity grows as cultivated area expands but remains constrained by manual tillage.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Rectangular architecture includes dedicated storage bins; plastered floors protect grain from pests and moisture; reserves typically cover one to two lean seasons; the buffer is adequate for single-year drought but would strain under consecutive failures.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Long-distance exchange networks import Anatolian obsidian, marine shells from the Mediterranean and Red Sea, and greenstone from Cyprus; exchange is embedded in social reciprocity rather than market transactions; logistics depend on human porterage along established routes.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No currency or standard unit of account exists; value is stored in livestock, stored grain, and prestige objects; deferred reciprocity — obligations to share future surplus — structures inter-household and inter-village relations.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Communal buildings suggest collective feasting and ritual redistribution; no centralized extraction authority is attested; surplus flows through kinship and ritual channels rather than taxation.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Differentiation in house size and burial goods indicates emerging inequality, but disparities remain modest; land and herds are defended through kin solidarity; no fortifications exist — defense is social rather than architectural.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Animal dung accumulates near pens and is likely returned to fields as fertilizer or burned as fuel; knapping debris, ash, and food waste concentrate in middens outside living quarters; waste volume is low relative to absorptive capacity.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Village-scale face-to-face interaction supports high interpersonal trust; mutual aid networks extend across households and, through exchange, to neighboring villages; the trust radius is expanding as the PPNB network grows.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Status differentials appear in burial treatment and house elaboration but remain permeable; lineage and residence define primary identity; ethnic or regional distinctions are weakly marked; boundaries are fluid.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Multi-room rectangular houses imply nuclear or small extended family units; inheritance likely follows patrilineal lines given the emphasis on skull curation of male ancestors; bride exchange may link villages.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Communal buildings — non-domestic structures with benches and plastered floors — serve as foci for supra-household coordination; guilds or formal associations are not attested; coordination remains kin- and ritual-based.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Communal feasting at ritual structures provides periodic release from labor; games and play are not directly attested but assumed from cross-cultural parallels; sociability centers on shared meals and ritual gatherings.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Disputes likely resolve through kin mediation and elder arbitration; emigration to new settlements offers an exit valve; ritual may discharge accumulated tension; standing unmetabolized grievance is low.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Authority is diffuse and consensual; village elders and ritual specialists hold influence but not coercive power; no inter-village polity or paramount leader is attested; decision-making is face-to-face and collective.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Leadership roles, where they exist, likely pass through seniority or demonstrated competence; skull curation suggests ancestor authority rather than living dynastic succession; transfer is informal and unremarkable.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Ancestral authority — materialized in plastered skulls and lime-plaster statues — legitimates kin-group claims to land and ritual precedence; no dynastic or heroic narratives are recoverable.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Administration is absent; coordination occurs through kinship and ritual; no records, seals, or tokens are attested; capacity is limited to village-scale collective action.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: All adult villagers likely participate in communal decisions; mobilization for labor or defense is episodic and voluntary; dissent finds outlet in emigration; the system is inclusive within its small scale.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Villages are embedded in a shared PPNB cultural horizon stretching from the southern Levant to Anatolia and Cyprus; relations are cooperative, mediated by exchange and intermarriage; no evidence of systematic warfare or territorial conflict.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Hunting weapons — bows, spears, projectile points — double as defensive arms; no standing military or fortifications exist; force is individual or kin-group mobilized, not institutionalized.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Intra-village violence is not prominent in skeletal evidence; raiding or banditry may occur but does not leave clear archaeological traces; disorder is within the system's absorptive capacity.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Information travels at the speed of walking messengers and visiting kin; inter-village communication follows exchange routes; news reaches across the Levant over weeks to months; transmission is slow but reliable within the network.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: No writing or proto-writing exists; all information persists in living memory, oral recitation, and material markers (plastered skulls, statues); knowledge survives only as long as it is actively transmitted; loss at generational turnover is significant.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Ritual specialists and elders likely control sacred narratives; everyday discourse is unregulated; taboos may govern speech about the dead or ritual practices; the permitted band is wide but ritually bounded.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Communal gatherings concentrate attention; repetition in ritual contexts amplifies core narratives; attention is face-to-face and episodic; no mechanism for broadcast or mass amplification exists.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Oral transmission degrades over generations; ritual formalization slows drift; verification relies on consensus among elders; noise load is moderate given small population and slow information flow.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Reciprocity, kinship obligation, and respect for ancestors form the moral bedrock; theft, violence, and breach of sharing norms are wrongs; justice is restorative and embedded in social relations.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No formal law code exists; binding norms are customary and orally transmitted; taboos around sacred spaces and objects carry quasi-legal force; evolution is slow and conservative.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Disputes are mediated by kin elders; restitution and compensation replace punishment; liability is collective (kin-group responsibility); formal tribunals are absent.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual rights are embedded in kin status; protections extend through kinship networks; compliance burden is low — norms are internalized and violations rare; outsiders lack protected status.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Kin-based enforcement may leave wrongs between unrelated parties unresolved; emigration offers escape from accountability; standing impunity is low given village-scale transparency but not zero.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Systematic inquiry is not differentiated from practical knowledge; the reigning paradigm is animist-ancestral, attributing causation to spirits and ancestors; observation of plant and animal behavior drives domestication but is not formalized as theory.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Lime-plaster production requires controlled pyrotechnology; naviform blade knapping demands high skill; goat husbandry involves selective breeding; techniques are reproducible and transmitted through apprenticeship.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Skills pass orally and by demonstration within households; specialized knowledge (flint-knapping, lime-burning, ritual) may concentrate in particular lineages; the stock is distributed unevenly but not monopolized.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Circular house construction is abandoned in favor of rectangular forms; some PPNA techniques fade; loss is gradual and replaced by new methods; decay is balanced by innovation.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Ancestral origins, founding narratives of the village, and the taming of wild species likely anchor collective identity; plastered skulls and statues materialize these narratives; identity is kin- and place-based.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Lime-plaster statues and skull portraits represent the earliest large-scale figurative sculpture; geometric painted plaster appears; material culture is austere but symbolically charged; aesthetic conventions are shared across the PPNB network.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: The expanding settlement frontier and reliable subsistence support a confident, expansive mood; risk-taking in colonization and long-distance exchange reflects optimism; the behavioral temper is active and exploratory.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: The cultural forms are young and vital; no evidence of exhaustion or hollowing; symbols retain their potency; anomie is not detectable in the archaeological record.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: An animist-ancestral cosmology prevails; the dead remain active agents requiring propitiation; the boundary between living and dead is permeable; fatalism coexists with ritual agency — humans can influence outcomes through correct practice.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Skull removal, plastering, and curation anchor ancestor veneration; lime-plaster statues may represent ancestors, deities, or mythic figures; communal buildings host collective rites; daily devotion is embedded in household routines.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Institutionalized clergy are not attested; ritual specialists likely exist within kin groups; doctrine is oral and flexible; authority is local and lineage-based, not centralized.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: The PPNB cultural horizon spreads a broadly shared cosmology across the Levant and beyond; local variants exist but no displacement or major fracturing is evident; diffusion is expansive.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: The sacred remains potent and unquestioned; no secularizing drift or existential void is detectable; spiritual need is fully anchored in ancestor veneration and animist practice; disenchantment is absent.
