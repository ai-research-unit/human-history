    8,500 BCE – 7,500 BCE — Early Pre-Pottery Neolithic B and Goat Domestication

    Summary: A thousand-year trend in which Levantine villages add managed herds of goats and later sheep to the cereal-legume complex already in place, completing the founder package of Near Eastern agriculture; spoken instruction coordinates the new skills of penning, culling, breeding selection, and seasonal movement, handing down herding knowledge that no silent gesture could transmit, while the binding constraint of oral memory still caps how far technical repertoire can expand beyond face-to-face teaching.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Jordan Rift, its flanking highlands from Galilee to Transjordan, and the inner Syrian steppe define the arena; villages cluster where perennial springs meet arable terraces, while semi-arid margins now enter use as seasonal grazing ground for newly managed flocks.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Early Holocene warmth and enhanced winter rains persist, supporting cereal yields and browse for goats; interannual variability remains moderate, and no severe aridification pulse interrupts the millennium.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Flash floods in wadi systems and occasional seismic tremors along the Rift pose localized risk, but village placement on terraces and tells buffers most settlements; drought risk is cushioned by spring-fed sites.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Fertile soils on alluvial fans and colluvial slopes sustain grain crops; wild progenitors of goat and sheep occupy adjacent uplands, providing the stock from which managed herds are drawn; flint nodules and basalt outcrops supply lithic raw material; obsidian continues to arrive from Anatolia.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Localized dung accumulation around pens enriches soils rather than saturating them; ash middens grow but do not yet degrade village environs; deforestation for fuel and building timber begins to thin woodland fringes, though regeneration still keeps pace.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Close contact with penned goats introduces zoonotic exposure not present among pure foragers, yet endemic disease load remains low; infant and maternal mortality persist at high background rates typical of early agrarian populations.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Grain and legume diets supply adequate calories; the addition of milk, blood, and occasional meat from managed herds improves protein intake; skeletal evidence shows moderate stature with some dental caries from starchy food.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Herbal remedies, bone-setting, and midwifery operate within kin groups; no specialized healer stratum is visible, though communal ritual may incorporate healing invocations; recovery capacity matches the modest injury load of village life.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Sedentary life and improved nutrition raise completed fertility above forager norms; monogamous or mildly polygynous households form the reproductive unit; birth intervals shorten as weaning foods become available.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population grows steadily; villages expand from dozens to low hundreds of inhabitants; the region's total population rises across the millennium, filling arable niches in the Rift and adjacent uplands.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: High child mortality leaves orphans absorbed by kin networks; the aged remain few; the productive cohort easily supports the dependent load, and no demographic drag accumulates.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Cultivated emmer, einkorn, barley, lentils, and peas form the staple base; managed goat herds add a mobile protein reserve; wild plant gathering and gazelle hunting continue as supplements; the provisioning floor rises above forager baselines.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle powers tillage and harvest; goats provide no traction but convert marginal browse into storable calories; firewood fuels cooking and lime-plaster production; per-capita energy capture increases modestly with herd additions.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Household units organize planting, harvest, and herd-tending; labor remains kin-based and cooperative, with no evidence of coerced work; seasonal tasks—lambing, shearing, culling—demand coordination spoken through and remembered.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Grain yields exceed immediate consumption, permitting storage in plastered bins; goat herds accumulate as live surplus; the combination creates a buffer against single-crop failure, though overall surplus remains modest by later standards.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Plastered pits and mud-brick bins hold grain; livestock on the hoof serve as walking reserves convertible in lean seasons; no formal treasury exists, but household and communal stores provide several months' cushion.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Obsidian from Cappadocia, marine shells from the Mediterranean, and greenstone beads circulate through down-the-line exchange; no market centers exist, but prestige goods move hundreds of kilometers, indicating sustained inter-village contact.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No monetary medium exists; value inheres in stored grain, livestock, and prestige objects; reciprocal gift obligations bind households and villages, with spoken promises tracking deferred exchanges across seasons.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No state apparatus extracts tribute; communal labor for ritual structures represents the only visible pooling beyond the household; redistribution flows through kinship and feast hospitality.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Differential house sizes and burial goods suggest emerging wealth gradients, though extremes remain modest; livestock raiding is possible but unattested; walls or towers serve communal defense rather than elite protection.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Dung is recycled as fuel and fertilizer; bone, horn, and hide enter craft production; middens accumulate but are periodically leveled or relocated; no toxic byproduct saturates the local sink.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilocal residence and patrilineal descent likely organize households; property—land, herds, stored grain—passes within kin lines, though rules remain customary and orally transmitted.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Village-level cooperation pools labor for construction, herding, and defense; age-graded or lineage-based work groups coordinate seasonal tasks; reciprocal aid buffers household shortfalls.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Communal feasting accompanies ritual events; bone gaming pieces and decorated objects suggest leisure activities; storytelling and song transmit lore and entertain.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Status gradients appear in burial goods and house elaboration but remain shallow; no hereditary elite stratum is visible; ritual specialists or lineage heads may hold prestige without coercive power.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No formal bureaucracy exists; village affairs are managed through kin councils and oral consensus; memory of agreements and precedents resides in elder recall, capping administrative reach at the community scale.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No standing warrior class; adult males bear hunting weapons usable for defense; communal walls at some sites suggest collective security concerns, but organized warfare remains episodic and small-scale.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Disputes settle through kin mediation and compensation; violence levels are low; the social fabric absorbs friction without recorded breakdown, though inter-village raiding cannot be ruled out.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Village solidarity and kinship loyalty anchor trust; the shared project of herding and farming sustains cooperative temper; anxiety centers on harvest failure and herd loss rather than external threat.

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Lineage elders and ritual specialists hold persuasive authority; leadership is situational—coordinating hunts, herding decisions, ritual—rather than institutionalized; charisma matters but confers no coercive power.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Allegiance flows to kin group and village community; legitimacy rests on reciprocity, ritual efficacy, and elder wisdom; no ruler demands obedience, and consent is embedded in daily cooperation.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Collective will mobilizes for construction projects, communal hunts, and defense; the capacity for unified action is real but limited to village scale; no supra-village polity directs broader efforts.

    External Relations & Posture: Inter-village relations mix exchange and competition; marriage alliances and trade partnerships link communities; hostility is possible but not dominant; no expansionist drive is visible.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Affective exhaustion is not recorded; the period is one of expansion and optimism; the residue of disaffection does not accumulate visibly.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Spoken language with full grammatical complexity enables instruction in herding, planting, and ritual; all community members share the code; no literacy exists; knowledge travels only as far as voice and memory carry.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Face-to-face speech is the sole channel; messengers on foot link villages within days; information spreads slowly across the region, gated by travel time and the need for personal contact.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: No durable record exists; all tradition, genealogy, and technical knowledge resides in living memory, refreshed through repetition, song, and ritual recitation; loss of key individuals means loss of knowledge.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Elders and ritual specialists shape what is said in public; taboos govern sacred speech; everyday discourse is open within the community but constrained by etiquette and hierarchy.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Communal gatherings amplify messages; repetition and mnemonic devices aid retention; attention spans are shaped by oral performance traditions rather than competing media.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Oral transmission is vulnerable to drift and loss; the signal degrades across generations unless actively rehearsed; no external verification exists; noise accumulates as memory fades.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Reciprocity, kin loyalty, and respect for elders anchor the moral order; harm to kin or community members demands redress; theft and violence are wrong, though outsiders may fall outside the moral circle.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No written law exists; binding rules are customary, transmitted orally, and interpreted by elders; taboos govern sacred spaces, burial, and certain foods; evolution is slow and conservative.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Disputes are adjudicated by kin councils or village elders; liability falls on kin groups; sanctions include compensation, shaming, and in extreme cases expulsion; no formal court or code exists.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual autonomy is embedded in kin obligation; rights inhere in group membership; compliance burden is low, enforced by social pressure rather than formal apparatus.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Wrongs within the community are generally redressed through mediation; wrongs by outsiders may go unanswered; the gap between justice owed and delivered is small within the moral community, larger beyond it.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: No formal inquiry exists; understanding of animal behavior, plant cycles, and soils is empirical and practical; explanatory frameworks are mythological, attributing fertility and failure to spirits or ancestors.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Herding techniques—selective culling, penning, seasonal movement—are developed and transmitted orally; plaster production, lithic knapping, and architectural methods represent sophisticated craft knowledge; technique advances incrementally.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Children learn by observation and apprenticeship; knowledge stock resides in skilled practitioners; distribution is broad for basic tasks, narrow for specialized crafts like obsidian knapping or ritual performance.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Skills die with practitioners if not transmitted; the oral medium is vulnerable to knowledge loss; no archive preserves superseded techniques; decay is ongoing but masked by the small total stock.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Ancestor veneration and origin narratives root identity in place and lineage; plastered skulls and communal burials enact the link between living and dead; identity is local, kin-based, and tied to the village.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: The right order is the order of ancestors; ritual repetition legitimates continuity; no written ideology exists, but oral tradition justifies hierarchy, land tenure, and marriage rules by appeal to precedent.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Lime-plaster statues, decorated skulls, carved bone, and painted geometric motifs express symbolic concerns; rectangular multi-room houses with plastered floors mark architectural elaboration; material culture is functional and symbolic.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: No visible exhaustion; the period is one of cultural elaboration and expansion; forms are vital and evolving, not yet hollowed into convention.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: The cosmos is animated by spirits, ancestors, and forces immanent in animals and plants; human destiny is bound to the land and the lineage; death is a transition, not an ending, as skull caching attests.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Skull plastering, secondary burial, communal feasting, and seasonal rites mark the sacred calendar; ritual is public and participatory, binding the community to ancestors and to the forces that ensure fertility.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: No priesthood or temple institution exists; ritual specialists—possibly lineage heads—conduct ceremonies; doctrine is oral tradition, not codified dogma; authority is diffuse and situational.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: The PPNB symbolic complex—skull cult, plaster statues, communal ritual—spreads across the Levant and into Anatolia, indicating shared or diffusing cosmological ideas; no competing system displaces local belief.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No disenchantment is visible; the sacred saturates daily life; the cosmos remains enchanted, and spiritual need is fully met by existing practice.
