    7500 BCE – 6500 BCE — Late Pre-Pottery Neolithic B Florescence

    Summary: The millennium from 7500 to 6500 BCE marks the apex of Pre-Pottery Neolithic culture in the Levant before its contraction. Mega-sites such as 'Ain Ghazal and Basta reach ten to fourteen hectares and support populations estimated at two to three thousand — the largest agglomerations humanity has yet achieved. The full Neolithic package of domesticated cereals, pulses, goats, sheep, pigs, and cattle is now in place, and long-distance exchange networks move Anatolian obsidian, Mediterranean shells, and Cypriot greenstone across hundreds of kilometers. Plastered skulls and monumental lime-plaster statues materialize an ancestor cult of unprecedented elaboration. Yet the binding constraint is tightening: faunal sequences at 'Ain Ghazal show wild game declining and domestic species taking over, signaling that browse, fuel, and soil fertility are under pressure. The system holds diminishing margin as population approaches or exceeds local carrying capacity, and the trajectory bends from expansion toward incipient stress.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Jordan Rift and coastal lowlands remain the low-friction settlement axis; the Transjordanian plateau supports the largest mega-sites where springs and arable soils coincide; the Taurus passes continue to channel obsidian southward; terrain imposes no new barriers.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The Holocene Climatic Optimum persists; winter rainfall is adequate for rain-fed cereals; oak-pistachio woodland covers hillslopes; interannual variability remains moderate; no major climatic perturbation occurs within the window, though the 8.2 ka event looms just beyond its close.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seismic activity along the Dead Sea Transform continues at background rates; localized flooding and single-year droughts recur; the system absorbs these shocks without rupture, but larger or consecutive events would now stress reserves more than in the sparser PPNA.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Flint and bitumen remain abundant; Anatolian obsidian flows steadily through exchange; local fuel wood is increasingly drawn down around mega-sites; browse and wild game densities decline near large settlements; the resource base is shifting from abundance toward localized depletion.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Concentrated animal husbandry produces dung loads that accumulate in pens and middens; human waste and ash from lime-burning add to the load; absorptive capacity near mega-sites is approaching saturation, evidenced by soil nutrient depletion and the faunal shift from wild to domestic species.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Larger, denser settlements and sustained contact with domestic herds raise zoonotic exposure; gastro-intestinal parasites and respiratory infections likely increase; no epidemic die-off is documented, but the mortality baseline is creeping upward relative to smaller villages.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Cereal-dominated diets supply adequate calories but reduced protein diversity as wild game declines; dental pathology increases; skeletal stress markers appear more frequently; nutritional vitality is adequate but trending downward from earlier PPNB levels.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Sedentary village life continues to shorten birth spacing; population growth remains positive; pair-bonding operates within village and inter-village networks; no major shift in reproductive biology is attested.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Mega-site populations reach two to three thousand; regional density is at its Neolithic peak; birth rates still exceed death rates but the margin is narrowing as carrying capacity approaches; demographic momentum is high but slowing.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Infant mortality remains substantial; adult lifespan extends rarely past fifty; dependency ratios rise slightly as population grows; communal structures absorb dependents but the load is heavier than in smaller settlements.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: The full agropastoral package — wheat, barley, lentils, peas, goats, sheep, pigs, cattle — provides a secure but labor-intensive floor; wild game contributes a declining share; the floor is adequate but household pooling and storage are essential buffers.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human muscle remains primary; cattle and possibly donkeys provide traction and transport; wood-fired pyrotechnology supports lime-plaster production; per-capita energy capture is near the ceiling achievable without metal tools or plows; efficiency gains are incremental.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Household labor predominates; communal labor mobilizes for monumental construction (statues, communal buildings); craft specialization in flint-knapping, lime-burning, and herding is evident; coercion remains minimal, organized through kinship and ritual obligation.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Cereal and herd yields support surplus accumulation; surplus flows into storage, seed stock, animal increase, and prestige goods; production capacity is near local maximum given manual tillage and browse limits; expansion now requires colonization rather than intensification.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Large rectangular houses include substantial storage bins; communal storage in non-domestic buildings is possible; reserves cover one to two lean seasons; the buffer is adequate for normal variance but would fail under consecutive drought or herd disease.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Anatolian obsidian, Mediterranean and Red Sea shells, and Cypriot greenstone circulate through embedded exchange networks; logistics depend on human and possibly animal porterage; exchange intensifies as mega-sites become nodal points.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Value is stored in livestock, grain, and prestige objects (obsidian, shells, beads); no currency exists; deferred reciprocity binds households and villages across generations; credit is social, not monetized.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Communal feasting and ritual redistribution channel surplus through collective events; no centralized tax or tribute is attested; extraction is horizontal (kin and ritual) rather than vertical (political hierarchy).

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: House size and burial goods show widening differentiation; elite households accumulate more storage and prestige items; land and herds are defended through kin solidarity and ancestral claims; no fortifications exist.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Animal dung is recycled as fuel or fertilizer but accumulates faster than it disperses; knapping debris, ash, and food waste fill middens; deforestation around mega-sites indicates that waste and extraction are outrunning local regeneration.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Village-scale trust remains high; mega-site aggregation strains face-to-face networks, requiring new mechanisms (ritual, communal buildings) to maintain cohesion; mutual aid extends through kinship and ritual association; the trust radius is stretched but holding.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Status differentials visible in house size and burial treatment widen; lineage identity sharpens as ancestral claims to land and herds gain value; ethnic or regional identity is secondary to kin-group affiliation; boundaries harden slightly.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Multi-room rectangular houses shelter nuclear or small extended families; inheritance of land, herds, and ancestral skulls follows patrilineal lines; marriage exchange links lineages within and across villages.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Communal buildings and statue caches indicate supra-household organization; ritual sodalities or lineage councils may coordinate labor and ritual; formal associations beyond kinship are not clearly attested.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Communal feasting at ritual structures punctuates the agricultural cycle; sociability centers on shared meals, ritual gatherings, and lifecycle celebrations; leisure time is limited but significant.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Kin mediation and elder arbitration resolve disputes; emigration to smaller or newer settlements offers an exit valve as mega-site tensions rise; ritual discharge absorbs accumulated friction; unmetabolized grievance is present but not explosive.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Authority remains diffuse; ritual specialists and lineage elders hold influence; no paramount leader or coercive hierarchy is attested; decision-making is collective and face-to-face, though coordination challenges grow with settlement size.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Leadership roles, where they exist, pass through seniority, competence, or lineage precedence; skull curation anchors ancestral authority; succession is informal and non-conflictual in the attested record.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Ancestral authority — materialized in plastered skulls and monumental statues — legitimates kin-group claims and communal decisions; origin narratives and founding myths are not recoverable but presumably underpin legitimacy.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Administration remains absent; coordination relies on kinship, ritual, and face-to-face negotiation; no seals, tokens, or proto-bureaucratic devices are attested; capacity is limited to what communal assembly can achieve.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Adult villagers participate in communal decisions; labor mobilization for statue production and building construction is episodic and voluntary; dissent finds outlet in emigration; the system is participatory within kinship bounds.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Mega-sites are embedded in a pan-Levantine PPNB cultural horizon; relations with neighboring villages and distant exchange partners are cooperative; no evidence of organized warfare or territorial conquest; the external environment is benign.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Hunting and defensive weapons — bows, spears, projectile points — are household-held; no standing military or fortifications exist; coercive capacity is individual or kin-mobilized, not institutionalized.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Skeletal evidence does not indicate endemic violence; raiding or interpersonal conflict may occur at low levels; disorder is within the system's absorptive capacity; internal order is maintained through social mechanisms.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Information travels at walking speed via messengers, visiting kin, and exchange partners; inter-site communication follows trade routes; news propagates across the Levant over weeks to months; transmission is slow but reaches the entire network.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: No writing exists; all knowledge persists in living memory, oral recitation, ritual performance, and material symbols (skulls, statues, architecture); generational turnover risks significant loss; the record is fragile but actively maintained through ritual repetition.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Ritual specialists and elders control sacred narratives and ancestral knowledge; everyday discourse is unregulated; taboos govern speech about the dead, ritual secrets, and sacred objects; the permitted band is wide but ritually bounded.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Communal gatherings concentrate attention; monumental statues and ritual architecture focus collective perception; repetition in ritual contexts amplifies core narratives; no mechanism for broadcast beyond face-to-face assembly exists.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Oral transmission degrades over generations; ritual formalization and material anchors (skulls, statues) slow drift; verification relies on elder consensus and ritual correctness; noise load is moderate but growing with population and complexity.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Reciprocity, kinship obligation, ancestral respect, and fair sharing form the moral foundation; theft, violence within the community, and breach of ritual taboos are wrongs; justice is restorative and relational.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No formal law code exists; binding norms are customary and orally transmitted; ancestral precedent carries authority; evolution is slow, conservative, and resistant to innovation.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Kin elders and possibly ritual specialists mediate disputes; restitution and compensation are preferred to punishment; liability is collective (kin-group responsibility); formal tribunals are absent.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual rights are embedded in kin and village membership; protections extend through kinship networks; compliance burden is low — norms are internalized; outsiders and those without kin lack protected status.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Kin-based enforcement may fail where parties lack common kin or when powerful lineages shield members; emigration allows escape; standing impunity is present at low levels, manageable within village-scale transparency.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Systematic inquiry is not differentiated from practical knowledge; the reigning explanatory paradigm is animist-ancestral, attributing causation to spirits, ancestors, and ritual efficacy; observation drives husbandry refinement but is not formalized as theory.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Lime-plaster pyrotechnology reaches its peak with large statues; naviform blade production is highly skilled; animal husbandry includes selective breeding for docility and productivity; techniques are sophisticated and reproducible.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Skills pass orally and by demonstration; specialized knowledge (statue-making, ritual, flint-knapping) concentrates in particular lineages or sodalities; the knowledge stock is substantial but unevenly distributed.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Older PPNA techniques are fully superseded; some knowledge loss occurs at generational turnover; new techniques (larger-scale construction, statue-making) replace older forms; decay is balanced by accumulation.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Ancestral origins, village-founding narratives, and the domestication of animals and plants anchor collective identity; plastered skulls and monumental statues materialize lineage memory; identity is strongly kin- and place-based.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Lime-plaster statues from 'Ain Ghazal — some nearly a meter tall — represent the pinnacle of PPNB figurative art; plastered skulls with shell eyes are individually crafted portraits; material culture is symbolically dense and regionally shared.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: The mood is confident but increasingly strained; mega-site aggregation reflects ambition; resource pressure introduces anxiety beneath surface confidence; the behavioral temper is still active but less expansive than earlier PPNB.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Cultural forms remain vital; statues and skulls retain potency; no hollowing or cliche is evident; the cultural apparatus is at its peak elaboration, not yet exhausted.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: An animist-ancestral cosmology prevails; ancestors remain active agents requiring veneration; the boundary between living and dead is permeable and ritually managed; humans can influence outcomes through correct practice; fatalism coexists with ritual agency.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Skull removal, plastering, and curation anchor ancestor veneration; monumental lime-plaster statues — possibly representing ancestors, deities, or mythic beings — are cached in ritual contexts; communal buildings host collective rites; daily devotion is embedded in household routines.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Institutionalized clergy are not attested; ritual specialists likely exist within kin groups or sodalities; doctrine is oral and flexible; authority is local and lineage-based, not centralized.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: The PPNB cosmology is shared across the Levant and into Anatolia and Cyprus; local variants exist but no major schism or displacement occurs; diffusion remains expansive; the spiritual horizon is at its widest extent.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: The sacred remains potent and central to daily life; no secularizing drift or existential void is detectable; spiritual need is fully anchored; disenchantment is absent.
