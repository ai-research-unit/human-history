    7,500 BCE – 6,500 BCE — Late Pre-Pottery Neolithic B Florescence

    Summary: A millennium-long apogee in which Levantine mega-villages reach populations in the thousands, supported by the full founder-crop and livestock package, interconnected by long-distance obsidian and greenstone exchange, and unified by a shared symbolic complex of plastered skulls and monumental statuary; spoken language coordinates labor, transmits ritual, and preserves genealogy, yet the binding constraint of oral memory still limits administrative reach to what elders can recall and enforce face-to-face, setting an upper ceiling on organizational scale that the period strains but does not break.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Jordan Rift, its flanking highlands, and the Transjordanian plateau host the densest settlement; Ain Ghazal, Jericho, and Beidha occupy spring-fed locations where arable land, pasture, and water converge; the Mediterranean coast and Syrian steppe participate in the exchange network but support smaller communities.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The early Holocene optimum continues, with warm temperatures and reliable winter rainfall; conditions favor grain agriculture and browse for livestock; no severe climatic shock disrupts the millennium, though the 8.2 ka aridification event looms at its close.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Seismic risk persists along the Rift, and flash floods threaten low-lying sites, but village placement on tells and terraces mitigates damage; drought remains the primary concern, buffered by stored grain and mobile herds.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Soils remain fertile under rotational cultivation; flint and basalt are locally abundant; obsidian from Cappadocia and greenstone from distant sources flow through exchange networks; timber for construction is available but increasingly drawn from more distant woodlands.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Dung accumulation enriches fields but concentrates around settlements; ash and refuse middens grow, and some sites show soil depletion and salination from over-irrigation; the sink absorbs waste but approaches saturation at the largest villages, foreshadowing later stress.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Dense settlement and close animal contact raise pathogen load above earlier levels; skeletal evidence shows tuberculosis and other infections; infant mortality remains high, though adult life expectancy is moderate for the period.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: The diversified diet—grain, legumes, milk, meat—supports adequate nutrition; stature is comparable to earlier phases; dental pathology from starchy foods is common; overall vitality sustains population growth.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Kin-based care, herbal remedies, and bone-setting address illness and injury; no professional healer class is attested, but communal ritual may incorporate healing; capacity matches the disease load without surplus.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Sedentary life and reliable food supply support high fertility; households organized around monogamous or mildly polygynous pairs form the reproductive unit; birth intervals are short by forager standards.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population peaks; Ain Ghazal may reach several thousand inhabitants; the Levant as a whole supports more people than at any previous time; density presses against local carrying capacity at the largest sites.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: High child mortality and modest adult longevity produce a young age structure; the productive cohort supports dependents, but labor demands of intensive agriculture and herding absorb available hands; no severe demographic drag accumulates.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: The full Neolithic founder package—wheat, barley, legumes, goats, sheep, cattle, pigs—is in place; agriculture is intensive, with evidence of irrigation at some sites; the provisioning floor is high enough to support dense, permanent settlement.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human labor dominates; cattle may begin to serve as traction in late phases, but evidence is thin; firewood fuels cooking and lime-plaster production, the latter energy-intensive; per-capita energy capture is modest but sufficient for village-scale construction.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Household labor pools kin for farming and herding; communal labor mobilizes for construction of large structures and defenses; no evidence of coerced labor, though coordinated efforts imply persuasive or ritual authority; tasks are gendered and age-graded.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Surplus grain and livestock accumulate; storage facilities are substantial; surplus supports craft specialization, exchange, and communal feasting; the system produces above subsistence, though margins are not vast.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Plastered bins and pits hold grain; livestock serve as mobile reserves; communal and household stores buffer one to two poor harvests; no state treasury exists, but collective reserves are real.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Long-distance exchange reaches new intensity; Cappadocian obsidian, Mediterranean shells, Red Sea cowries, and Anatolian greenstone move through down-the-line networks; no market centers, but regular exchange partnerships link communities across hundreds of kilometers.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: No monetary medium; value resides in grain, livestock, and prestige goods; reciprocal obligations, tracked orally, structure credit; deferred exchange operates through kinship and alliance, not impersonal contract.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: No state extracts tribute; surplus pools for communal construction and feasting; redistribution flows through kinship and ritual hospitality; no centralized fiscal apparatus exists.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Differential house sizes and burial goods indicate emerging inequality, though extremes are limited; walls at some sites suggest concern with defense of accumulated wealth; raiding is possible but not dominant.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Dung is recycled as fuel and fertilizer; bone, horn, and hide enter craft use; refuse accumulates in middens; no toxic waste, but organic load at large sites strains local absorption capacity.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Patrilocal residence and patrilineal descent likely prevail; rectangular multi-room houses accommodate extended families; property—land, herds, stored grain—passes within lineages under customary oral rules.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Village-level cooperation organizes labor for construction, herding, and defense; lineage or age-grade associations coordinate tasks; reciprocal aid buffers household shortfalls; mutual aid is the norm.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Communal feasting marks ritual occasions; gaming pieces, decorated objects, and musical instruments suggest leisure; storytelling and song transmit tradition and entertain.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Status gradients are visible in house size, burial goods, and access to prestige items; no hereditary elite with coercive power is attested, but ritual specialists and lineage heads hold influence; stratification is shallow but present.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: No bureaucracy or written record; village affairs are managed through kin councils and elder consensus; memory of agreements, boundaries, and precedents resides in oral tradition; administrative reach is limited to what can be recalled and enforced face-to-face, capping organizational scale.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: No standing army or police; adult males bear weapons usable for hunting and defense; walls and towers at some sites suggest collective security; organized violence is small-scale and episodic, not institutionalized.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Disputes settle through kin mediation and compensation; violence levels are low; the social fabric absorbs friction, though late-phase site abandonments hint at mounting stress not fully discharged.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Lineage elders and ritual specialists hold persuasive authority; leadership is situational and based on skill, age, and ritual efficacy; no chief or king commands obedience; charisma matters but does not institutionalize.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Allegiance flows to kin group and village; legitimacy rests on reciprocity, ancestral sanction, and ritual performance; consent is embedded in daily cooperation, not extracted by force.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Collective will mobilizes for construction, ritual, and defense; the capacity for unified action is real at village scale; no supra-village polity coordinates broader efforts; resolve is strong but localized.

    External Relations & Posture: Inter-village relations combine exchange, alliance, and competition; marriage networks and trade partnerships link communities; hostility exists but does not dominate; no expansionist empire emerges.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Village solidarity and lineage loyalty anchor trust; the shared project of intensive farming and ritual sustains cooperative temper; anxiety centers on harvest, herd, and the ancestors' favor.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Affective exhaustion is not prominent in the record; the period is one of florescence, though late-phase abandonments may signal emerging stress; disaffection does not visibly accumulate until the close.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Spoken language with full grammatical complexity enables coordination of labor, ritual, and exchange; all community members share the code; no literacy exists; knowledge travels only as far as voice and memory carry.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Face-to-face speech is the sole channel; messengers on foot link villages; information spreads slowly, gated by travel time and personal contact; the exchange network doubles as an information network.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: No durable record; genealogy, boundary markers, and technical knowledge reside in living memory; loss of key elders means loss of knowledge; oral tradition is the only archive.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Elders and ritual specialists shape public discourse; taboos govern sacred speech; everyday talk is open within the community but constrained by etiquette; the permitted band is stable.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Communal gatherings amplify messages; repetition and mnemonic devices aid retention; oral performance traditions shape attention; no competing medium exists.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Oral transmission is vulnerable to drift; the signal degrades across generations unless actively rehearsed; no external verification; noise accumulates as memory fades and informants die.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Reciprocity, kin loyalty, and ancestral sanction anchor the moral order; harm to kin or community demands redress; theft, violence, and ritual violation are wrong; outsiders may fall outside the moral circle.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: No written law; binding rules are customary and oral; elders interpret precedent; taboos govern burial, sacred space, and certain foods; evolution is slow, conservative, and tied to tradition.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Kin councils and elders adjudicate disputes; liability falls on kin groups; sanctions include compensation, shaming, and expulsion; no court or code; justice is personal and mediated.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Rights inhere in group membership; individual autonomy is embedded in obligation; compliance burden is low, enforced by social pressure and ritual sanction rather than formal apparatus.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Wrongs within the community are generally redressed; wrongs by or against outsiders may go unanswered; the gap between justice owed and delivered is small internally, larger beyond the moral community.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: No formal inquiry; understanding of soils, plants, animals, and seasons is empirical and practical; explanatory frameworks are mythological, attributing outcomes to spirits, ancestors, and cosmic forces.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Lime-plaster production, architectural engineering, lithic knapping, and husbandry techniques represent sophisticated craft knowledge transmitted orally; method advances incrementally through trial and imitation.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Children learn by observation and apprenticeship; knowledge stock resides in skilled practitioners; basic agricultural skills are widely distributed; specialized crafts—statue-making, obsidian knapping—are narrower.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Skills die with practitioners if not transmitted; the oral medium is vulnerable to loss; no archive preserves superseded techniques; decay is ongoing but masked by the small total stock and active transmission.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Ancestor veneration roots identity in lineage and place; plastered skulls, communal burials, and monumental statues enact the link between living and dead; identity is local and kin-based, but shared symbolic forms create regional coherence.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: The right order is the order of ancestors; ritual repetition legitimates continuity; oral tradition justifies hierarchy, land tenure, and marriage rules by appeal to precedent and mythic charter.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Large lime-plaster statues with inlaid eyes, decorated plastered skulls, geometric painted motifs, and elaborate architecture express symbolic and aesthetic concerns; material culture is both functional and deeply symbolic; the PPNB style is distinctive and widespread.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: No visible exhaustion; the period is the florescence, not the decline; forms are vital and elaborating; meaning is potent and actively renewed through ritual.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: The cosmos is animated by spirits and ancestors immanent in animals, plants, and the land; human destiny is bound to lineage and place; death is a transition managed through elaborate mortuary ritual, not an ending.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Skull plastering, secondary burial, communal feasting, statue installation, and seasonal rites mark the sacred calendar; ritual is public, participatory, and village-centered, binding the living to the dead and to cosmic forces.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: No priesthood or temple institution; ritual specialists—possibly lineage heads—conduct ceremonies; doctrine is oral tradition; authority is diffuse, situational, and tied to performance efficacy.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: The PPNB symbolic complex—skull cult, plaster statues, shared mortuary practice—spreads across the Levant and into Anatolia, creating a zone of shared or converging cosmology; no competing system displaces it during this phase.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: No disenchantment; the sacred saturates daily life; the cosmos remains fully enchanted; spiritual need is met by existing practice, and no void opens.
