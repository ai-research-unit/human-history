    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Sunda Shelf

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 60000 BCE – 500 CE

    Region: The Sunda Shelf — the greater landmass comprising the Malay Peninsula, Sumatra, Java, Borneo, and connecting lowlands that formed a single continent (Sundaland) during Pleistocene low sea stands; including the maritime passages to Wallacea and the Philippine archipelago.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Pleistocene cave sites (Niah Cave, Tam Pa Ling), Hoabinhian assemblages, Neolithic sites with pottery and polished stone, and early metal-age burials. Submerged Sundaland landscapes hold evidence now underwater. Austronesian linguistic reconstruction provides deep-time cultural data. Indian and Chinese textual references appear toward window's end. Window closes as Indic writing systems and state formation establish written record as the institutional medium (~500 CE).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Initial Modern Human Settlement (60000–45000 BCE)

    Out of Africa Coastal Route: Modern humans reached Southeast Asia via southern coastal route; genetic and archaeological evidence suggests arrival ~65,000–50,000 BP.

    Tam Pa Ling (Laos): ~63,000–46,000 BP skeletal remains; early modern human presence in mainland Southeast Asia.

    Sundaland Geography: During low sea stands, Malay Peninsula, Sumatra, Java, Borneo connected by exposed continental shelf; vast coastal plains.

    Migration Pathways: Coastal and riverine routes through Sundaland; gateway to Wallacea and Sahul.

    Population Genetics: Deep ancestry in modern Southeast Asian populations; Negrito groups may represent early lineages.

    Limited Archaeological Visibility: Much early coastal evidence now submerged; cave sites provide windows into occupation.

    2. Pleistocene Expansion (45000–30000 BCE)

    Niah Cave (Sarawak): ~50,000–40,000 BP occupation; "Deep Skull" burial; continuous sequence into Holocene.

    Wallacea Crossings: Water crossings to Sulawesi, Flores (where Homo floresiensis present until ~50,000 BP); stepping stones to Sahul.

    Regional Diversification: Different populations adapting to rainforest, coastal, riverine environments.

    Technology: Pebble tools; bone implements; limited blade industries; bamboo tools inferred but not preserved.

    Homo floresiensis: Small hominin on Flores survives until ~50,000 BP; overlap with modern humans.

    Population Movement: Foundation populations for later Philippine, Indonesian, Melanesian diversity.

    3. Late Pleistocene Adaptation (30000–18000 BCE)

    Cave Occupation Intensifies: Niah, Tabon (Philippines), Song Terus (Java); stratified deposits.

    Foraging Intensification: Diverse rainforest resources; fruits, tubers, small game, arboreal mammals.

    Tabon Cave (Palawan): ~30,000–16,000 BP; human remains; flake tools; early Philippine sequence.

    Burial Practices: Intentional burials with ochre and grave goods indicate symbolic complexity.

    Rock Art: Some painted caves may date to late Pleistocene (Sulawesi art ~45,000 BP on Wallacea side).

    Social Organization: Mobile foraging bands; kin-based groups; extensive territorial ranges.

    4. Last Glacial Maximum (18000–10000 BCE)

    Sundaland Maximum Extent: Sea level ~120 m lower; vast exposed plains connecting present-day islands.

    LGM Climate: Cooler and drier; expanded savannas in some areas; contracted rainforests.

    Megafauna: Stegodon, orangutan, tiger, rhinoceros present; some extinctions during this period.

    Population Distribution: Populations likely concentrated on coastal plains now submerged; interior caves provide record.

    Technology Continuity: Hoabinhian tradition emerging; pebble and flake tools; little change in lithic industry.

    Isolation and Connectivity: Sundaland populations connected; Wallacea requires water crossing.

    5. Post-Glacial Transformation (10000–6000 BCE)

    Sea Level Rise: ~120 m rise drowns Sundaland plains; creates modern island geography by ~6000 BCE.

    Island Formation: Java, Sumatra, Borneo, Malay Peninsula separate; Philippine islands take modern form.

    Hoabinhian Tradition: Unifacial pebble tools; mortars and pestles; plant processing; widespread from Vietnam to Sumatra.

    Coastal Adaptation: Shell middens increase; maritime resource exploitation intensifies.

    Population Redistribution: Interior highlands and coasts become refugia; lowland populations displaced.

    Linguistic Divergence: Ancestral language families beginning differentiation.

    6. Early Holocene Maritime Adaptation (6000–4000 BCE)

    Island Stabilization: Modern coastlines established; island biogeography stabilizes.

    Shell Midden Economies: Intensive shellfish collection; fishing; coastal settlement patterns.

    Hoabinhian Persistence: Continues in some areas; gradual adoption of new technologies.

    Regional Exchange: Obsidian, shells, stone traded between islands; maritime networks forming.

    Proto-Austronesian: Ancestral Austronesian language family developing in Taiwan/southern China region.

    Incipient Cultivation: Some evidence of early plant management; not yet full agriculture.

    7. Neolithic Transition (4000–2500 BCE)

    Taiwan as Austronesian Homeland: Linguistic and archaeological evidence points to Taiwan as dispersal center.

    Rice and Millet Agriculture: Cultivation of rice (from Yangtze region) and millet; crops spread with Austronesian expansion.

    Pottery: Red-slipped and cord-marked ceramics; diagnostic Neolithic marker.

    Polished Stone Tools: Adzes, chisels for woodworking; canoe building technology.

    Pig and Dog Domesticates: Transported with Austronesian populations.

    Early Expansion: Movement from Taiwan to Philippines (~4000–3500 BCE); beginning of Austronesian dispersal.

    8. Austronesian Spread (2500–1500 BCE)

    Rapid Maritime Expansion: Austronesian speakers spread through Philippines, Indonesia, to Near Oceania.

    Lapita Cultural Complex: Eastern branch reaches Melanesia and western Polynesia; distinctive dentate-stamped pottery.

    Western Austronesian: Populations settle Indonesia; interaction with indigenous populations (Negrito, Melanesian-related).

    Outrigger Canoe Technology: Sophisticated sailing vessels enable long-distance voyaging.

    Agricultural Package: Rice, millet, taro, yams, pigs, chickens, dogs spread through the region.

    Population Replacement and Admixture: Genetic evidence shows mixing with pre-existing populations.

    9. Bronze Age and Dong Son (1500–500 BCE)

    Dong Son Culture: Northern Vietnam bronze-working tradition; large ceremonial drums (moko).

    Bronze Technology Spread: Dong Son drums and artifacts found throughout Island Southeast Asia; prestige goods.

    Social Stratification: Elite burials with bronze goods indicate hierarchy development.

    Exchange Networks: Bronze, glass beads, jade circulating through maritime networks.

    Mainland-Island Connection: Vietnamese and southern Chinese influence through trade.

    Agricultural Intensification: Wet-rice cultivation expanding in suitable areas.

    10. Iron Age Consolidation (500 BCE – 1 CE)

    Iron Technology: Iron smelting adopted; agricultural tools improve productivity.

    Sa Huynh Culture: Central Vietnam; jar burials; distinctive ornaments; maritime traders.

    Tabon Jar Burials: Philippines; continuing mortuary tradition with Chinese trade goods.

    Maritime Exchange Intensification: Indian Ocean trade networks linking Southeast Asia to India and beyond.

    Glass and Carnelian Beads: Indian trade goods appearing in Southeast Asian sites.

    Pre-State Complexity: Ranked societies; chiefly organization; no states yet.

    11. Early Indianization (1–300 CE)

    Indian Ocean Trade Boom: Roman demand for spices, aromatics drives trade through Southeast Asia.

    Indian Merchants and Brahmins: Bring Hindu-Buddhist religious concepts, Sanskrit terminology.

    Proto-State Formation: Local elites adopt Indic symbols and rituals to legitimize power.

    Funan: Mekong Delta polity emerging; Chinese sources describe; Indian influence evident.

    Oc Eo: Port site of Funan; Roman coins, Indian beads, local manufacture.

    Writing Emergence: Early Sanskrit inscriptions appearing; Indic scripts adapted.

    12. Threshold of Indic States (300–500 CE)

    Funan Consolidation: Major power controlling maritime trade; tribute missions to China.

    Early Champa: Linyi state on central Vietnamese coast; Sanskrit inscriptions; Hindu temples.

    Sanskrit Inscriptions: Earliest Indic inscriptions in Borneo (late 4th century), Java, Malay Peninsula.

    State Formation: Territorial polities with written administration; kingship legitimized by Indic ideology.

    Buddhism and Hinduism: Both traditions spreading; monastic and priestly establishments.

    Oral Threshold: By ~500 CE, written Sanskrit (and soon vernacular Indic scripts) become the medium institutions run on; oral tradition persists but state functions require documentation.
