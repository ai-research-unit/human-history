    Planning: Oral in The Aegean Archipelago — 1200 BCE – 750 BCE

    Age Constraint (FIXED)

    Oral: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate — anything can be said, far more held and handed down than imitation allowed, but it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost.

    This constraint governs every article: Linear B script has been lost; knowledge transmission is now oral. The Informational domain must reflect this — no writing, no archives, all record persistence through memory and recitation. Poetry, genealogy, and technical knowledge survive only through memorization and oral performance.

    Shared Evidence Base

    Mycenaean Collapse (~1200–1150 BCE)

    Key Sites: Mycenae, Tiryns, Pylos, Thebes, Athens (partial continuity) Dating: ~1200–1150 BCE (destruction levels, pottery chronology) Evidence: Burnt destruction layers at major palatial sites; Linear B archive cessation; population decline in surveys; some sites abandoned, others reduced; Athens shows continuity but diminished Significance: End of palatial economy and centralized administration; loss of literacy Sources: Cline 2014; Dickinson 2006; Middleton 2017 Contested Points: Causes debated (invasion, systems collapse, climate, earthquake); regional variation in timing and severity

    Loss of Linear B

    Context: Writing system used for palatial administration; disappears with palaces Evidence: No Linear B texts post-1200 BCE; no other writing system in use until Greek alphabet (~800–750 BCE) Significance: Creates fully oral period; no contemporary written records for ~400 years Sources: Palaima 2010; Sherratt 2003 Contested Points: Whether some limited literacy persisted in isolated pockets (no evidence for this)

    Submycenaean Period (~1100–1050 BCE)

    Key Sites: Kerameikos cemetery (Athens), Lefkandi, Perati Dating: ~1100–1050 BCE (pottery typology) Evidence: Simplified pottery; reduced grave goods; smaller cemeteries; contracted settlement; some continuity at Athens and Lefkandi Significance: Demographic and cultural nadir; transition to Early Iron Age Sources: Lemos 2002; Desborough 1972 Contested Points: Whether "Dark Age" terminology appropriate; regional variation

    Protogeometric Period (~1050–900 BCE)

    Key Sites: Athens (Kerameikos), Lefkandi (heroon), Knossos, Argos Dating: ~1050–900 BCE (pottery chronology) Evidence: Characteristic compass-drawn pottery decoration; iron technology adopted; cremation becomes common; Lefkandi "heroon" shows elite burial with Near Eastern imports Significance: Beginning of recovery; cultural reformulation; Lefkandi indicates some continuing contacts Sources: Lemos 2002; Popham et al. 1993 Contested Points: Lefkandi's exceptionalism debated; extent of population recovery

    Geometric Period (~900–700 BCE)

    Key Sites: Athens, Argos, Corinth, Eretria, Delphi, Olympia Dating: ~900–700 BCE (Early, Middle, Late Geometric subdivisions) Evidence: Elaborate pottery styles; increasing grave wealth; monumental kraters and amphorae; expansion of settlement; sanctuary foundation/expansion at Delphi, Olympia; emergence of hero cults Significance: Population growth; social complexity; regional identities; proto-urban nucleation Sources: Coldstream 2003; Morgan 1990; de Polignac 1995 Contested Points: Pace of "recovery"; whether "polis" appropriate for 8th century

    Lefkandi Heroon (~1000–950 BCE)

    Location: Euboea Dating: ~1000–950 BCE (pottery, radiocarbon) Evidence: Monumental apsidal building (~50m); elite burial with warrior cremation and sacrificed horses; rich grave goods including Near Eastern imports Significance: Exceptional elite display; continued external contacts; possible heroization Sources: Popham et al. 1993; Lemos 2002 Contested Points: Interpretation (tomb? abandoned building?); typicality vs. exceptionalism

    Sanctuary Development

    Key Sites: Olympia (dedications from ~1050 BCE; Altis formalization 8th c.); Delphi (8th c. expansion); Delos; Isthmia Evidence: Votive deposits; tripod cauldrons; bronze figurines; increasingly monumental architecture late 8th c. Significance: Religious centralization; inter-community gatherings; Olympic Games traditional date 776 BCE Sources: Morgan 1990; Scott 2010 Contested Points: 776 BCE date traditional rather than archaeological; sanctuary chronologies debated

    Iron Technology

    Transition: Bronze to iron occurs early in this period Evidence: Iron weapons and tools increasingly common from Protogeometric; bronze retained for prestige items Significance: Technological shift; iron more available than bronze (no tin imports needed) Sources: Snodgrass 1971; Morris 2000 Contested Points: Whether iron adoption driven by necessity (trade disruption) or choice

    Oral Poetry Tradition

    Evidence: Homeric epics (Iliad, Odyssey) preserve formulaic oral compositional techniques; internal anachronisms mixing Bronze and Iron Age elements; later written form preserves oral features Transmission: Bards (aoidoi) transmitted heroic narrative across generations; formulaic composition allows improvisation within tradition Significance: Primary vehicle for cultural memory; shapes Greek identity; preserved Bronze Age memories in transformed form Sources: Parry 1971; Lord 1960; West 2011 Contested Points: Historicity of Trojan War; degree of Bronze Age "memory"; exact date of crystallization (8th c.?)

    Paleoenvironment

    Climate: Generally similar to present; some evidence for drier conditions in LBA collapse period Landscape: Deforestation in some areas; Mediterranean vegetation; limited agricultural intensification Sources: Drake 2012; Finné et al. 2011 Contested Points: Role of climate in collapse remains debated

    Population Estimates

    Evidence: Settlement surveys; cemetery size changes; burial counts Pattern: Sharp decline 1200–1050 BCE; slow recovery 1050–900 BCE; accelerating growth 900–750 BCE Sources: Murray 2017; Snodgrass 1980 Contested Points: Magnitude of decline; visibility of population in archaeological record
