    Planning Document — Oral Stage in The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain

    Age Definition

    Oral Stage: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate. With a grammar almost anything can be said — the absent, the past, the possible, the abstract — and far more can be held and handed down than mute imitation allowed; public life is coordinated, remembered, ruled, and storied through speech. But it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost. The long age of oral culture, dominant until external written record becomes the medium institutions run on.

    Window: 55000 BCE – 300 BCE

    Region: The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain — from the Himalayan foothills and Hindu Kush passes through the Indus and Ganges river systems to the Deccan Plateau and peninsular coasts; including the Thar Desert, Western and Eastern Ghats, and the tropical south.

    Note on Evidence: Archaeological record includes Paleolithic industries, Mesolithic microlithic assemblages, and the well-documented Indus Valley civilization. Vedic literature (Rigveda, later Vedas, Brahmanas) composed orally during this period, written down only later. The Indus script remains undeciphered; its function may not have been fully linguistic. The window ends as Brahmi script emerges and Mauryan administration begins (~300 BCE).

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Initial Modern Human Settlement (55000–35000 BCE)

    Out of Africa Coastal Route: Genetic and archaeological evidence supports modern human arrival in South Asia via a southern coastal route ~55,000–50,000 BP. Mitochondrial haplogroups M and N indicate early diversification in South Asia.

    Jwalapuram (Andhra Pradesh): Middle Paleolithic and early Upper Paleolithic assemblages; crucially, artifacts found above and below the Toba ash layer (~74,000 BP), suggesting human presence before the supervolcanic eruption.

    Toba Catastrophe Theory: The Toba eruption may have caused population bottleneck; debated. South Asian evidence suggests some continuity.

    Early Industries: Pre-Upper Paleolithic industries show prepared-core technologies; transition to blade-based industries variable across the subcontinent.

    Sparse Record: Few skeletal remains; inference mainly from lithic assemblages and genetics.

    2. Upper Paleolithic Diversification (35000–15000 BCE)

    Blade-Based Industries: Regional Upper Paleolithic traditions develop; blade and bladelet technologies in western and central India.

    Bhimbetka Rock Shelters: Long occupation sequence in Madhya Pradesh; earliest cultural deposits from Upper Paleolithic; famous for later rock art.

    Rock Art Beginnings: Some petroglyphs and cave paintings may date to late Upper Paleolithic, though most date to Mesolithic and later.

    Last Glacial Maximum: Cooler, drier conditions ~26,500–19,000 BP; effects less severe than in higher latitudes but altered vegetation and resource distribution.

    Regional Differentiation: Western India, Deccan, Sri Lanka show distinct lithic traditions; long-term regional continuity.

    3. Mesolithic Intensification (15000–7000 BCE)

    Microlithic Technology: Geometric microliths (lunates, triangles, trapezes) dominate; composite tool hafting.

    Bagor (Rajasthan): Key Mesolithic site with sequence extending to Chalcolithic; large microlithic assemblage; animal remains suggest hunting and early herding.

    Langhnaj (Gujarat): Mesolithic cemetery; burials with grave goods; evidence of settled occupation.

    Rock Art Florescence: Bhimbetka and related sites show extensive painted shelters; hunting scenes, animals, human figures, abstract patterns.

    Subsistence Diversity: Hunting (deer, wild cattle, pig), fishing, plant gathering; some sites show early management of animals.

    Holocene Warming: Post-glacial climate improvement; forest expansion; monsoon strengthening.

    4. Mehrgarh and Agricultural Origins (7000–4500 BCE)

    Mehrgarh (Baluchistan): Key site documenting indigenous agricultural development. Period I (~7000–5500 BCE) shows transition from hunting-gathering to farming.

    Domestic Plants: Wheat (emmer, einkorn), barley, cotton, jujube domesticated or cultivated. Independent developments alongside Near Eastern influence.

    Domestic Animals: Zebu cattle domestication indigenous to South Asia; sheep and goats possibly from west. Cattle become central.

    Mud-Brick Architecture: Rectangular houses with multiple rooms; storage facilities; communal structures.

    Craft Development: Bone tools, stone blades, basketry, early pottery (Mehrgarh II onward); bead-making.

    Mortuary Practices: Burials with grave goods including ornaments; social differentiation emerging.

    5. Chalcolithic and Early Harappan (4500–2600 BCE)

    Regional Chalcolithic Cultures: Amri-Nal, Kot Diji, Sothi-Siswal, Damb Sadaat — multiple regional traditions with increasing complexity.

    Copper Technology: Copper working from ~4500 BCE; still limited; stone tools continue.

    Proto-Urban Growth: Larger settlements with planned layouts; defensive walls (Kot Diji); public buildings.

    Trade Networks: Lapis lazuli from Afghanistan; marine shells; turquoise; integration across regions.

    Pottery Standardization: Distinctive ceramic traditions; Kot Diji pottery; potters' marks.

    Writing Precursors: Potters' marks and symbols; proto-Indus signs appear; function unclear.

    6. Mature Indus Valley Civilization (2600–2000 BCE)

    Urban Florescence: Cities of Mohenjo-daro (40,000+ population) and Harappa; planned grid layout; citadel and lower town.

    Standardization: Uniform weights (binary and decimal systems), brick sizes, script across vast area (~1 million km²).

    Great Bath and Public Architecture: Elaborate drainage; large granaries or warehouses; ritual structures.

    Indus Script: ~400+ signs; found on seals, tablets, pottery; undeciphered; may not encode full language; likely administrative/identificatory.

    Craft Specialization: Bead-making (Chanhu-daro), metallurgy, textile production, pottery; specialized workshops.

    Trade: Mesopotamian contacts (Meluhha); Gulf trade; internal exchange networks.

    Social Organization: Debate over centralization; no clear palaces or temples; possible corporate or heterarchical governance.

    No Monumental Warfare Evidence: Lack of weapons caches, defensive urgency, or conquest iconography.

    7. Late Harappan Transformation (2000–1700 BCE)

    Urban Abandonment: Mohenjo-daro and Harappa decline; smaller settlements persist; urban population disperses.

    Eastward Shift: Population movement toward Ganges-Yamuna doab and Gujarat.

    Localization: Regional cultures emerge (Cemetery H, Jhukar, Rangpur); Harappan elements persist but fragment.

    Climate Stress: Possible aridification; Ghaggar-Hakra (ancient Sarasvati) drying; debated timing and causation.

    Continuity Elements: Craft traditions, weights, some symbols persist; not total collapse but transformation.

    8. Post-Urban and Indo-Aryan Arrival (1700–1200 BCE)

    Cemetery H Culture: Post-Harappan in Punjab; distinctive pottery; cremation burials; transition phase.

    Painted Grey Ware (PGW): Appears ~1200–1000 BCE; associated with early Iron Age and Vedic material culture.

    Indo-Aryan Migration: Linguistic and genetic evidence supports gradual influx of Indo-European speakers from Central Asia/steppe via Bactria-Margiana (BMAC) contact; not single invasion but complex process.

    Early Rigvedic Period: Rigveda's oldest hymns (Books 2–7) composed; geography centered on Sapta Sindhu (Punjab rivers).

    Pastoral-Agricultural Mix: Cattle central; horse and chariot prestigious; barley cultivation; semi-sedentary lifestyle.

    Material Culture Gap: Limited archaeological visibility of Rigvedic peoples; identification with specific sites debated.

    9. Early Vedic Society (1200–900 BCE)

    Rigveda Composition: Core hymns completed; transmitted orally with extreme precision; fire ritual (agni) central.

    Tribal Polities (Jana): Bharatas, Purus, and other tribes; kings (rajan) as war leaders; cattle raids.

    Battle of the Ten Kings: Rigvedic event; Sudas of Bharata defeats confederation; historical kernel in hymns.

    Social Structure: Emerging distinctions (brahmin, kshatriya, vaishya, shudra) but not yet rigid caste.

    Cattle Economy: Cattle as wealth measure; go- (cow) root in economic terms; dairying.

    Fire Ritual: Soma sacrifice; elaborate priestly recitation; oral preservation of hymns.

    10. Later Vedic Period (900–600 BCE)

    Eastward Expansion: Settlement pushes into Gangetic Plain; Kuru-Panchala region becomes center.

    Rice Cultivation: Wet-rice agriculture enables dense Gangetic settlement; iron tools assist forest clearance.

    Iron Age: Iron (krishna-ayas, "black metal") increasingly common; plows, weapons, tools.

    Later Vedic Texts: Yajurveda, Samaveda, Atharvaveda; Brahmanas (ritual commentary); elaboration of ritual.

    Varna System Crystallizes: Four-fold hierarchy more rigid; ritual purity concerns; Purusha Sukta ideology.

    Janapadas: Territorial kingdoms replace tribal janas; Kuru, Panchala, Kosala, Kashi, Videha.

    Painted Grey Ware Culture: Archaeological correlate; iron, horses, rice, wheat; modest towns.

    11. Second Urbanization (600–400 BCE)

    Mahajanapadas: Sixteen major kingdoms/republics listed in Buddhist texts; Magadha, Kosala, Vatsa, Avanti prominent.

    Urban Revival: Cities like Rajagriha, Varanasi, Taxila, Ujjain; Northern Black Polished Ware (NBPW) pottery.

    Iron Plow Agriculture: Intensive Gangetic cultivation; surplus supports urbanism.

    Coinage: Punch-marked silver coins emerge ~500 BCE; monetized exchange.

    Trade and Guilds: Merchant associations (shreni); long-distance trade routes.

    Writing Emergence: Brahmi script appears ~400–300 BCE; Kharosthi in northwest; administrative needs.

    12. Shramanic Challenge (600–300 BCE)

    Heterodox Movements: Buddha (~563–483 BCE) and Mahavira (~540–468 BCE) challenge Vedic orthodoxy.

    Buddhism: Four Noble Truths; Eightfold Path; sangha (monastic community); royal patronage later.

    Jainism: Ahimsa (non-violence); ascetic practice; Tirthankara lineage; lay and monastic communities.

    Upanishadic Speculation: Brahman-Atman identity; karma-samsara-moksha framework; philosophical inquiry.

    Oral Debate Traditions: Public disputation; wandering teachers; forest academies; memory and argumentation.

    Social Critique: Heterodox movements attract merchants, warriors; challenge brahmin ritual monopoly.

    13. Nanda Empire (400–300 BCE)

    Magadhan Dominance: Nanda dynasty (c. 345–321 BCE) controls most of northern India; enormous army.

    Bureaucratic Experiments: Revenue collection; standing army; administrative infrastructure.

    Alexander's Invasion (327–325 BCE): Greek forces reach Punjab; Porus defeated; troops refuse Ganges advance; withdrawal.

    Greek Accounts: First external written descriptions of India (Megasthenes later); comparisons with Mauryan state.

    Pre-Mauryan Context: Chandragupta Maurya's rise from this period; Kautilya's Arthashastra reflects late oral/early literate transition.

    14. Twilight of the Oral Age (350–300 BCE)

    Final Pre-Literate Phase: Vedic oral canon essentially complete; Brahmi and Kharosthi scripts in emerging use.

    Brahmi Script: Appears ~4th–3rd century BCE; Ashokan inscriptions (~260s BCE) first extensive use; administrative function.

    Oral Continuity: Vedas continue oral transmission even after writing available; oral prestige persists.

    Mauryan Foundation: Chandragupta (r. 321–297 BCE) establishes Mauryan Empire; marks transition to manuscript stage for state administration.

    Institutional Threshold: With Ashokan edicts and administrative records, written record becomes the medium institutions run on.
