    Planning: Oral in The Aegean Archipelago — 45,000 BCE – 2,000 BCE

    Age Constraint (FIXED)

    Oral: Spoken language becomes the primary substrate — anything can be said, far more held and handed down than imitation allowed, but it still rides on breath and memory: spoken or it does not exist, remembered or it is lost.

    This constraint governs every article: knowledge transmission is oral. The Informational domain must reflect this — no writing (writing appears only at the end with Linear A/B emerging after 2000 BCE), all record persistence through memory, speech, and oral tradition.

    Shared Evidence Base

    Upper Paleolithic (~45,000–15,000 BCE)

    Key Sites: Franchthi Cave (Argolid), Klissoura Cave (Argolid), Theopetra Cave (Thessaly) Dating: Earliest occupation ~40,000–38,000 BCE (radiocarbon, OSL) Evidence: Aurignacian-type blade industries; bone tools; marine shell ornaments; obsidian from Melos indicating early seafaring Significance: Documents initial colonization of Aegean by modern humans; early maritime capability Sources: Perlès 1999; Runnels 2009; Kourtessi-Philippakis 2011 Contested Points: Exact timing of initial arrival; relationship to European Aurignacian

    Last Glacial Maximum (~25,000–15,000 BCE)

    Context: Sea levels ~120m lower; many current islands connected to mainland or each other Evidence: Reduced site numbers; continued occupation at Franchthi and Klissoura; Epigravettian industries Significance: Demonstrates resilience through climatic extreme; sets stage for post-glacial expansion Sources: van Andel & Shackleton 1982; Runnels 2009 Contested Points: Population estimates uncertain

    Mesolithic (~10,000–7,000 BCE)

    Key Sites: Franchthi Cave (rich Mesolithic sequence), Maroulas (Kythnos), Kerame (Ikaria) Dating: ~10,000–7,000 BCE (radiocarbon) Evidence: Microliths; intensive marine resource use (fish, shellfish); Melos obsidian transport (100+ km); burials at Franchthi; wild plant processing Significance: Complex forager adaptation; seafaring capability demonstrated; foundation for Neolithic transition Sources: Perlès 2001; Runnels & van Andel 2008; Sampson 2008 Contested Points: Degree of sedentism; continuity vs. replacement at Neolithic transition

    Neolithic Transition (~7,000–6,000 BCE)

    Key Sites: Knossos (earliest levels), Argissa, Sesklo, Nea Nikomedeia, Franchthi Dating: ~7,000 BCE at Knossos; ~6,500–6,000 BCE in Thessaly (radiocarbon) Evidence: Full Neolithic package (domesticated cereals, legumes, sheep, goat, cattle, pig); pottery; sedentary villages; tells forming Significance: Introduction of agriculture from Anatolia/Near East; rapid spread; demographic transition Sources: Perlès 2001; Broodbank 2013; Reingruber & Thissen 2009 Contested Points: Colonization vs. acculturation debate; multiple introduction routes possible

    Middle Neolithic (~6,000–5,300 BCE)

    Key Sites: Sesklo, Dimini, Tsangli, Elateia Dating: ~6,000–5,300 BCE (radiocarbon, pottery typology) Evidence: Tell formation; painted pottery (Sesklo culture); village architecture; agricultural surplus Significance: Consolidation of Neolithic way of life; regional cultures crystallizing Sources: Kotsakis 1999; Perlès 2001 Contested Points: Social organization; degree of hierarchical differentiation

    Late Neolithic (~5,300–4,500 BCE)

    Key Sites: Dimini, Sesklo (upper levels), Sitagroi, Dikili Tash Dating: ~5,300–4,500 BCE (radiocarbon, pottery phases) Evidence: Dimini-style pottery; megaroid buildings; possible fortifications; copper objects appearing Significance: Increased social complexity; inter-regional contacts; metallurgy beginnings Sources: Perlès 2001; Maniatis 2014 Contested Points: Whether fortifications indicate conflict or other functions

    Final Neolithic (~4,500–3,300 BCE)

    Key Sites: Rachmani, Pevkakia, Zas Cave (Naxos), Ftelia (Mykonos) Dating: ~4,500–3,300 BCE (radiocarbon, ceramic typology) Evidence: Island colonization intensifying; first Cycladic settlements; copper metallurgy established; long-distance obsidian exchange Significance: Bridge to Bronze Age; Aegean integration; metallurgical foundation Sources: Broodbank 2000; Sampson 2002 Contested Points: Social organization; dating precision

    Early Bronze Age I (~3,300–2,700 BCE)

    Key Sites: Poliochni (Lemnos), Thermi (Lesbos), Eutresis, Lerna Dating: ~3,300–2,700 BCE (radiocarbon, ceramic phases) Evidence: Fortified settlements; bronze metallurgy; corridor houses; ceramic standardization; maritime trade expansion Significance: Urban nucleation; Aegean-Anatolian connections intensifying Sources: Renfrew 1972; Broodbank 2000 Contested Points: Degree of centralization; external vs. internal development

    Early Cycladic II (~2,700–2,200 BCE)

    Key Sites: Chalandriani (Syros), Phylakopi I (Melos), Kastri (Syros), Dokathismata (Amorgos) Dating: ~2,700–2,200 BCE (ceramic typology, radiocarbon) Evidence: Marble figurines (folded-arm type); silver metallurgy; fortified promontory sites (Kastri); cemeteries with rich grave goods; maritime trade networks Significance: Cultural fluorescence; pan-Aegean exchange; craft specialization Sources: Broodbank 2000; Renfrew 1972; Berg 2007 Contested Points: Social organization (egalitarian vs. hierarchical); figurine function

    EBA III Transition (~2,200–2,000 BCE)

    Key Sites: Lerna IV (House of Tiles destruction), Tiryns, Phylakopi II Dating: ~2,200–2,000 BCE (radiocarbon, stratigraphic correlation) Evidence: Destruction levels at multiple sites; "Lefkandi I/Kastri" ceramic horizon; population movements; reorientation toward Crete Significance: Disruption marking end of EBA; prelude to Minoan-influenced MBA Sources: Wiener 2014; Rutter 1993 Contested Points: Causes (invasion, climate, internal); relationship to wider "4.2 ka event"

    Paleoenvironment

    Pleistocene: Glacial-interglacial oscillations; dramatically lower sea levels during LGM; many islands connected Holocene: Sea level rise stabilizes by ~6,000 BCE; modern island configuration established; Mediterranean climate Sources: van Andel & Shackleton 1982; Broodbank 2013
