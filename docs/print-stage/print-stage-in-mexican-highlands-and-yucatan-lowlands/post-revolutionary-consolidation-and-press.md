# __1920 CE – 1940 CE__ — Post-Revolutionary Consolidation and Press
__Summary:__ Two decades of state-building following revolutionary violence, as Sonoran generals and then Cardenas consolidate a single-party regime, institutionalize revolutionary gains, and establish state leverage over the press; binding constraint is the construction of durable political institutions capable of absorbing factional competition without renewed civil war; economic reconstruction proceeding unevenly; direction of change toward centralized state capacity, corporatist organization, and managed nationalism.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Central plateau and transport corridors rebuilt under state direction; Yucatan integrated through henequen economy and federal authority; geographic fragmentation that enabled revolutionary factionalism being overcome by road and rail expansion.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Semi-arid highland climate with seasonal monsoons; drought years stressing agriculture periodically; no secular climate shift; agrarian reform attempting to buffer against climate volatility through ejido system.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Earthquake risk unchanged; infrastructure reconstruction improving resilience marginally; natural hazards secondary to political and economic challenges in state-building priorities.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Petroleum reserves nationalized 1938; silver and copper mining recovering; ejido lands redistributed but often marginal quality; resource endowment substantial but development constrained by capital scarcity.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Industrial development limited; urban waste absorption capacity adequate in growing cities; environmental degradation localized around mining and oil extraction; sink capacity not yet stressed at national scale.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Post-revolutionary public health campaigns reducing mortality gradually; endemic diseases persisting in rural areas; infant mortality high but declining; death rate moving toward replacement level.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Nutritional status improving slowly as agriculture recovers; ejido production variable; urban food supply stabilizing; bodies recovering from wartime deprivation but chronic malnutrition persisting in poor regions.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Family formation normalizing after wartime disruption; sex ratios rebalancing through generation replacement; traditional pairing patterns reasserting; fertility high as demographic recovery begins.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population recovering and growing toward 20 million by 1940; age structure normalizing; rural-urban migration beginning; demographic renewal robust after revolutionary losses.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Revolutionary-era widows and orphans aging into dependency or adulthood; new dependency from Cristero War casualties; productive cohort expanding; dependency ratio improving but revolutionary cohort carrying war trauma.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Ejido system providing subsistence floor for campesino population; urban provisioning improving with transport recovery; subsistence more secure than revolutionary decade but still precarious for landless and urban poor.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Petroleum extraction expanding until nationalization; hydroelectric development beginning; rail network functioning; draft animals recovered; energy capture increasing but per-capita power still limited.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ CROM and later CTM organizing urban labor under state corporatism; ejidatarios bound to state-controlled agrarian sector; wage labor expanding; coercion through political incorporation rather than naked force.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Agricultural production recovering but not yet surpassing pre-revolutionary levels; light manufacturing expanding in cities; oil production substantial until foreign company conflict; surplus generation modest and channeled through state.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Banco de Mexico established 1925 building monetary reserves; ejido granaries providing local buffering; state capacity for reserve management developing; institutional buffering improving but still thin.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Single currency restored; internal trade recovering along rebuilt rail lines; export trade in oil, silver, and henequen; logistics infrastructure improving; US economic relationship dominant.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Peso stabilizing after revolutionary inflation; banking system rebuilt under state influence; agricultural credit through ejido banks; capital formation constrained by revolutionary expropriations and foreign investor caution.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Tax collection capacity rebuilt; oil revenues significant before nationalization; surplus redistributed through agrarian reform, public works, and patronage networks; state as central surplus allocator.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Revolutionary elite accumulating through political position; hacendado class diminished but not eliminated outside agrarian reform zones; wealth concentration moderate; asset defense through political connection rather than legal title alone.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Industrial waste minimal outside oil and mining sectors; agricultural waste absorbed locally; urban sanitation improving; waste streams manageable at current development level.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Village solidarity institutionalized in ejido system; urban mutual aid through labor unions and neighborhood associations; trust radius extending through corporatist organizations; interfactional violence subsiding.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Revolutionary rhetoric emphasizing mestizo national identity; indigenous identity subordinated to class categories; campesino and worker identities politicized through corporatist organization; stratification by political access replacing colonial racial hierarchy.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Traditional family structure reinforced by revolutionary regime despite anticlerical marriage laws; ejido land inheritance regulated by state; extended family networks functioning as social security.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Corporatist organizations — CTM, CNC, CNOP — channeling association into state-controlled sectors; independent civil society constrained; self-organization permitted within regime-sanctioned frameworks.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Revolutionary festivals and state holidays institutionalized; cinema and radio expanding leisure options; cantina culture and fiestas continuing; sociability increasingly mediated by mass culture.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Corporatist organizations serving as grievance channels; petitioning the president institutionalized; Cristero War as violent discharge of religious grievance; residual agrarian and labor grievances absorbed but not fully resolved.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Sonoran dynasty (Obregon, Calles) consolidating power 1920–1934; Calles as Jefe Maximo behind successive presidents; Cardenas asserting presidential authority 1934–1940; power centralizing in presidency and party.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Obregon assassination 1928 triggering party institutionalization; PNR (1929) then PRM (1938) routinizing succession through party mechanism; sexenio system establishing non-reelection with orderly transfer.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Revolutionary nationalism as legitimating ideology; Cardenas land reform and oil nationalization as populist fulfillment of revolutionary promises; anti-imperialism and indigenismo as mobilizing narratives.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Federal bureaucracy rebuilt and expanded; secretariats multiplying; administrative capacity growing but corruption endemic; records maintained; institutional reach extending into countryside through ejido and school.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Mass mobilization through corporatist sectors; elections controlled but participation channeled; opposition parties marginalized; participation real but within regime-defined boundaries.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ US relations tense over oil nationalization 1938; Good Neighbor Policy moderating intervention; League of Nations membership; diplomatic independence asserted within US sphere of influence.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Federal army subordinated to civilian authority; rural defense forces (rurales) and agrarista militias; police forces developing; coercive capacity adequate for internal order without military dominance.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Cristero War 1926–1929 as major internal conflict; scattered rebellions suppressed; order restored across territory; banditry eliminated; internal threat environment manageable by late 1930s.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Telegraph and postal networks restored; radio broadcasting beginning with XEW 1930; newspapers distributed by rail to provincial cities; information velocity increasing but print still dominant mass medium.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ National archives reconstituted; ejido and civil registries maintained; newspaper morgues preserving public record; institutional memory rebuilding; record persistence improving with state capacity.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ State influence over press through paper supply (PIPSA from 1935) and advertising; self-censorship prevailing; revolutionary nationalism as hegemonic frame; opposition voices constrained but not entirely suppressed.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Urban literate public consuming daily papers; radio reaching broader audience including illiterate; muralism as mass visual communication; revolutionary message amplified through multiple channels.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Official information channels more reliable than revolutionary chaos; propaganda distinguishable from news by sophisticated readers; noise load reduced with single-party consolidation; signal integrity improving.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Revolutionary justice — land, labor rights, national sovereignty — as moral foundation; anticlerical legislation contested by Catholic population; social justice ideology institutionalized in Constitution.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ 1917 Constitution implemented unevenly; agrarian code elaborated; labor law developed; regulatory capacity expanding with state; legal framework revolutionary in principle, selective in application.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Federal judiciary subordinate to executive; amparo system functioning for individual rights; agrarian tribunals processing land claims; enforcement selective and politically influenced.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Constitutional rights expanding in scope; labor protections real within CTM framework; religious rights curtailed during Cristero period; compliance burden moderate; rights dependent on political incorporation.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Revolutionary-era violence unadjudicated; Cristero atrocities on both sides unredressed; political killings (Obregon assassination) prosecuted selectively; substantial impunity residue from revolutionary period.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Revolutionary nationalism replacing positivism as dominant paradigm; Mexican School of anthropology (Gamio) developing indigenismo; Marxist influence in labor and agrarian movements; intellectual life recovering in universities.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Agricultural extension services developing; engineering education expanding; oil industry technical capacity demonstrated before nationalization; applied knowledge growing but dependent on foreign expertise.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ SEP (Ministry of Education) expanding rural schools; literacy campaigns under Vasconcelos and successors; knowledge stock growing; skill distribution improving but urban-rural gap persisting.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Porfirian technocratic knowledge partially recovered; revolutionary generation's practical skills transmitted; institutional knowledge accumulating in expanding bureaucracy; decay rate lower than accumulation.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Revolutionary mythos codified — Zapata, Villa, Madero as martyrs; mestizo national identity promoted; pre-Columbian heritage celebrated; revolutionary nationalism as foundational identity framework.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Muralism (Rivera, Orozco, Siqueiros) as state-sponsored public art; revolutionary architecture in public buildings; popular arts celebrated; material culture expressing revolutionary nationalism.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Mood stabilizing from revolutionary trauma toward reconstruction optimism; Cardenista period generating populist enthusiasm; behavioral disposition toward state engagement and national identification.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Revolutionary forms fresh and generative; muralism at creative peak; corrido tradition continuing; meaning system coherent and expanding; form exhaustion not yet evident in revolutionary culture.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Catholic cosmology persisting despite anticlerical policy; revolutionary secular nationalism competing for existential allegiance; fatalism tempered by developmental optimism among engaged sectors.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Cristero War defending Catholic practice; post-modus vivendi (1929) religious practice resuming; popular devotion unbroken; Virgin of Guadalupe integrating revolutionary and Catholic identity.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Church institutional power curtailed — no religious education, limited clerical presence; hierarchy accepting modus vivendi; doctrinal authority maintained informally; institutional church weakened but not destroyed.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Revolutionary nationalism as quasi-religious framework spreading through schools and media; Guadalupan Catholicism syncretizing with revolutionary patriotism; Protestant missions marginal; spiritual landscape syncretic rather than displaced.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Anticlerical policy generating resistance rather than secularization; sacred meaning contested but not evacuated; revolutionary sacrifice providing transcendent meaning for regime supporters; disenchantment limited to anticlerical elite.<br><br>
