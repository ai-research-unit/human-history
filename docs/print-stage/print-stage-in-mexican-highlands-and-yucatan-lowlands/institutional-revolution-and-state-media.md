# __1940 CE – 1950 CE__ — Institutional Revolution and State Media
__Summary:__ Post-revolutionary Mexico under Avila Camacho and Aleman consolidates single-party rule and industrializes; the decade is bounded by WWII alliance with the United States and emerging Cold War alignment; the binding constraint is the regime's need to maintain popular legitimacy while demobilizing revolutionary radicalism; considerable slack exists in foreign exchange and US credit; direction of change runs toward technocratic capitalism, urbanization, and broadcast-supplemented state media control.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Central plateau remains the demographic and political core; rail network now extensive; road construction accelerates under Aleman; Yucatan still peripheral, linked by coastal shipping and limited rail; mountainous terrain continues to fragment internal markets outside the capital-Veracruz-Guadalajara triangle.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Seasonal monsoon pattern persists; interannual rainfall variability affects rainfed agriculture in the highlands; no major drought or flood catastrophe dominates the decade; secular climate stable within the window.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Seismic risk latent along Pacific subduction zone; volcanic activity quiescent; hurricane exposure in Yucatan and Gulf coast remains annual; infrastructure margins adequate for typical events; no catastrophic shock during the window.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Oil reserves nationalized under Cardenas now producing under Pemex monopoly; silver and base-metal mining continues; hydroelectric development expanding; coal limited; the endowment is substantial and drawdown moderate relative to reserves.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Mexico City air quality beginning to degrade as automobile use rises; industrial effluent entering rivers near Monterrey and the capital; absorptive capacity still large but early saturation signals appearing in the Valley of Mexico airshed.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic malaria, typhoid, and gastrointestinal disease persist in rural areas; DDT campaigns beginning; infant mortality declining but still elevated compared to industrialized states; urban sanitation improving; overall mortality trending downward.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Maize-bean diet adequate in calories for most; protein deficiency common in southern indigenous zones; child stunting prevalent in Chiapas and Oaxaca; urban populations better nourished; physiological vitality improving slowly.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ High fertility sustained by Catholic norms and pronatalist policy; age at marriage low in rural areas; polygyny rare; biological fertility unconstrained; population growth accelerating.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population approximately 20 million at window start, approaching 26 million by end; annual growth above 2.5 percent; rapid urbanization concentrating population in the capital; replacement ratio strongly positive; age structure young.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Young dependency ratio high but economically absorbed by extended-family pooling; aged cohort small; widowhood from revolutionary violence now generationally distant; orphan load not exceptional; dependency burden manageable.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Ejido system provides land floor for rural majority; subsistence maize cultivation persists; urban poor provision through informal markets; no mass famine; floor stable but low.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Oil production and refining under Pemex expanding; electrification spreading to provincial cities; animal traction still dominant in agriculture; per-capita energy consumption rising but well below US levels; conversion efficiency improving.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ CTM labor confederation incorporated into party-state; strikes subject to government mediation; rural labor tied to ejido or hacienda remnants; bracero program begins exporting labor to US; coercion low but mobilization channeled through corporatist structures.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Import-substitution industrialization accelerating; textile, cement, steel, and consumer goods production expanding; agricultural surplus modest; overall output rising at 5–6 percent annually; surplus capacity growing.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Foreign exchange reserves healthy; US credit available; grain storage limited; CONASUPO emerging to manage food supply; buffering against external shocks adequate for the decade.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Peso stable; import licensing and tariffs protect domestic industry; exports primarily oil, silver, and agricultural goods; rail and nascent trucking networks distribute goods; Mexico City wholesale markets dominate national commerce; logistics improving but regional fragmentation persists.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Peso convertible and relatively stable; Banco de Mexico manages monetary policy; credit expanding through development banks like Nacional Financiera; private banking concentrated; inflation moderate; value storage secure for peso-holders.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ State extracts through oil revenues, tariffs, and limited income tax; reinvests in infrastructure and patronage; agricultural surplus flows to urban consumers via price controls; extraction moderate; redistribution favors urban-industrial coalition.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Revolutionary land reform created ejido sector; industrial wealth concentrating among favored entrepreneurs; northern border industrialists rising; old hacienda class diminished; inequality moderate by regional standards; asset defense through political connection.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Industrial effluent discharged into rivers with minimal treatment; urban garbage accumulating; recycling informal; waste volume rising with industrialization but still modest relative to absorptive capacity; routing predominantly dump-and-dilute.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Village-level solidarity persists in rural areas; urban neighborhood networks forming in expanding colonias; compadrazgo ties remain strong; trust radius extends beyond kin but remains localized; mutual aid adequate for ordinary shocks.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Mestizo national identity promoted; indigenous populations subordinated but not legally excluded; urban middle class expanding; revolutionary generals now wealthy bourgeoisie; class boundaries more fluid than Porfirian era; stratification moderate and slowly leveling.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Patrilineal inheritance; large extended families; male-headed household norm; compadrazgo reinforcing alliance networks; family remains primary welfare institution; structure stable.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Labor unions, peasant leagues, and professional associations channeled into party sectors; independent organization discouraged; Catholic lay organizations surviving Cristero defeat; associational fabric thick but corporatized.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Cinema expanding rapidly; radio becoming mass medium; futbol professionalizing; cantina culture persists; religious fiestas continue despite anticlericalism; sociability robust.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Petition and patronage through party structures; labor disputes channeled through CTM; rural grievance absorbed by ejido system; emigration to US as safety valve; discharge mechanisms adequate; residual grievance accumulating among indigenous and marginalized groups.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ PRM transformed into PRI (1946); president holds supreme executive power for single six-year term; no reelection enforced; party monopolizes formal politics; power highly centralized in presidency; stable and consolidating.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Dedazo system — outgoing president selects successor; party ratifies; elections ritualized; transfer orderly and non-violent; succession mechanism institutionalized and accepted.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Revolutionary nationalism invoked; land reform and oil nationalization as founding myths; anti-imperialism; social justice rhetoric; legitimacy high among peasants and workers; intellectuals increasingly critical but contained.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Bureaucracy expanding; corruption endemic but functional; statistics bureau operational; tax collection improving; reach extending to provinces; administrative capacity moderate and growing; integrity compromised by patronage but not dysfunctional.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Mass organizations mobilize electoral support; genuine opposition marginalized; PAN emerging as loyal Catholic opposition; left contained within party; participation channeled not suppressed; mobilization capacity high but directed.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ WWII alliance with US; bracero program; oil exports; Cold War alignment anti-communist but rhetorically independent; relations with US asymmetric but stable; regional position dominant in Central America; external pressure low.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Military professionalized and subordinate to civilian rule; federal police expanding; rural police (rurales) legacy; army small relative to population; command firmly presidential; coercive capacity adequate for internal order.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Banditry suppressed; no active insurgency; labor and peasant unrest channeled through corporatist structures; crime present but not overwhelming; order maintained; threat environment benign.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Newspaper distribution via rail and road to provincial cities; telegraph dense; telephone expanding in urban areas; radio (XEW network) reaching national audience; cinema newsreels; transmission velocity high for elites, penetrating mass audience via broadcast.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ National archives functioning; civil registry operational; printed newspapers preserved; government publications systematic; institutional memory robust; persistence high for official records.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Press nominally free but dependent on state through PIPSA paper monopoly and government advertising; self-censorship pervasive; criticism of president rare; revolutionary nationalism the permitted frame; discourse band narrow but stable.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Literacy rising (perhaps 50 percent by 1950); newspaper readership concentrated in urban educated class; radio reaching broader audience including illiterate; amplification through party networks; reception expanding.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Official narratives dominant; rumor networks active in villages; fact-checking minimal; propaganda effective; noise load moderate; signal integrity adequate for regime purposes but critical information suppressed.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Revolutionary justice emphasizing social rights; land reform perceived as righteous; anticlerical measures now moderated; popular sense of justice aligned with regime narrative among beneficiaries; indigenous customary justice persisting locally.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ 1917 Constitution remains framework; labor code protective; agrarian law governing ejidos; civil and commercial codes Napoleonic heritage; regulatory expansion with industrialization; codification stable.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Federal courts subordinate to executive; amparo (constitutional injunction) available but politically constrained; enforcement selective; liability assignment favoring regime allies; throughput slow but functional.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Constitutional rights extensive on paper; labor protections real for unionized workers; indigenous rights nominal; arbitrary detention possible; compliance burden moderate for connected, heavy for marginal; predictability variable.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Political crimes unpunished; corruption tolerated; rural violence against indigenous communities rarely prosecuted; urban crime clearance low; impunity substantial for powerful; deficit accumulating but not destabilizing.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ UNAM the dominant institution; social science adopting dependency and structuralist frameworks; history serving national narrative; archaeology (Caso at Monte Alban) prestigious; natural science developing; paradigms derivative of European and US models.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Engineering methods imported via foreign firms and training abroad; agricultural extension beginning; medical technique improving in urban hospitals; industrial methods adopted in protected sectors; technique advancing through transfer.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Primary schooling expanding under SEP; rural schools reaching villages; literacy rising but unevenly distributed; secondary and university education elite; technical schools emerging; knowledge stock growing; skill distribution widening slowly.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Colonial-era knowledge irrelevant; positivist scientism discredited; pre-revolutionary techniques in agriculture persisting; some technical skill attrition in neglected provinces; decay moderate; error accumulation low.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Revolutionary nationalism dominant; mestizo synthesis celebrated; pre-Columbian past appropriated as heritage; Spanish colonial legacy ambivalent; Zapata and Cardenas as heroes; memory actively constructed through education and muralism.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Muralism now institutionalized; architecture modernist (University City under construction); cinema golden age (Cantinflas, Maria Felix); popular music (ranchera, bolero) broadcast nationally; material culture mixing indigenous, colonial, and modern elements.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Optimism about national progress; confidence in industrialization; urban aspiration rising; revolutionary fervor institutionalized into civic ritual; mood generally confident and forward-looking.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Revolutionary rhetoric becoming formulaic; muralism losing edge as it becomes official; land reform promises exhausted in many regions; some hollowing of revolutionary symbols among intellectuals; meaning decay beginning but not yet critical.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Catholic cosmology dominant despite state anticlericalism; folk Catholicism incorporating indigenous elements; fatalism persisting in rural areas; urban middle class somewhat secularized; existential outlook stable.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mass attendance recovering after Cristero era; Day of the Dead, pilgrimage to Guadalupe, village fiestas continuing; devotional practice robust despite official secularism; sacred calendar observed.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Church-state modus vivendi established; church retains moral authority without political role; clerical hierarchy rebuilding; Protestant missionaries marginal; institutional position stable and recovering.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Catholic hegemony unchallenged; syncretic folk practice stable; indigenous cosmologies surviving in remote communities; secular nationalism a parallel civic religion; diffusion minimal; no displacement.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Revolutionary anticlericalism creating some secular stratum among urban intellectuals and bureaucrats; scientific worldview spreading through education; disenchantment modest; sacred needs largely met through Catholic practice; discharge low.<br><br>
