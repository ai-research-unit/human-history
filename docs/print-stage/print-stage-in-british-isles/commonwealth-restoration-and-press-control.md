# __1649 CE – 1688 CE__ — Commonwealth, Restoration, and Press Control
__Summary:__ The execution of Charles I inaugurates a republican experiment under the Commonwealth and Protectorate, followed by monarchical restoration in 1660 and concluded by the Glorious Revolution of 1688; throughout, successive regimes attempt to control print through licensing and censorship, while coffee houses, religious controversy, and commercial intelligence ensure continued circulation; the binding constraint is the unresolved tension between press control and the commercial and religious demand for printed matter; system margin is narrow, with each settlement vulnerable to succession crisis; direction of change moves from republican experiment through restored monarchy toward constitutional settlement and the eventual lapse of licensing in 1695.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The British Isles geography remains stable; London dominates as political, commercial, and print center; England's relative compactness permits news circulation within days; Scotland and Ireland are more distant and harder to govern from London; maritime position facilitates trade and insulates against continental invasion while connecting to Atlantic commerce.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Little Ice Age continues with variable harvests; the 1650s and 1660s see generally adequate harvests with some difficult years; the severe winter of 1683-84 freezes the Thames; climate stress is present but not dominant; no catastrophic climatic event shapes the period's politics.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ The Great Plague of 1665 kills perhaps one hundred thousand in London; the Great Fire of 1666 destroys much of the City; these twin disasters dominate hazard exposure for the period; both are absorbed and London rebuilds, demonstrating significant recovery capacity despite enormous immediate loss.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Coal production expands, particularly from Newcastle supplying London; timber scarcity continues driving the fuel transition; wool, tin, and lead remain significant; colonial trade brings sugar, tobacco, and other products; no critical resource constraint binds the economy; the endowment supports modest growth.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ London's rebuilding after the fire improves some sanitation; urban waste and coal smoke remain localized problems; the Great Fire clears accumulated waste and rats, inadvertently ending the plague cycle; sink capacity is adequate; the countryside absorbs agricultural and domestic discharge without notable degradation.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ The Great Plague of 1665 is the last major bubonic outbreak in England, killing roughly fifteen percent of London's population; after 1666 plague recedes, improving the mortality baseline; smallpox, typhus, and childhood diseases continue; overall mortality pressure declines modestly through the period.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Nutritional status remains stratified by class; no major famine affects England; Ireland experiences severe dislocation under Cromwellian conquest; average physiological condition is stable for those with regular food access; the laboring poor remain vulnerable to seasonal and cyclical hunger.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Marriage patterns remain stable with relatively late marriage age; nuclear households predominate; fertility within marriage is high; parish oversight maintains low illegitimacy rates; no significant shift in reproductive patterns occurs; demographic renewal proceeds at modest pace.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ English population grows slowly toward approximately five million by 1700; London recovers from plague and fire losses through in-migration; Ireland's population is significantly reduced by Cromwellian warfare and transplantation; overall demographic trajectory is positive but not rapid.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ The Poor Law system continues providing parish relief; plague and war create orphans and widows absorbed into existing structures; the dependency load is elevated in the 1650s and 1660s but manageable; no systemic overload occurs; the framework functions adequately if unevenly.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Mixed agriculture maintains the subsistence floor; enclosure continues gradually; grain supply is generally adequate; Ireland's subsistence base is disrupted by conquest and land redistribution; England's provisioning is secure for most of the population; no major subsistence crisis occurs.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Coal increasingly displaces wood for heating and some industrial processes; water and wind power serve mills and shipping; human and animal muscle remain primary for most work; per-capita energy is rising modestly as the coal transition advances; the energy ceiling loosens incrementally.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Wage labor expands; apprenticeship structures urban trades; agricultural labor combines tenancy and hiring; Irish land confiscation creates a displaced Catholic tenantry under Protestant landlords; indentured servitude continues to colonies; coercion levels are moderate by period standards.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Textile exports remain significant; London crafts and commerce generate substantial surplus; Navigation Acts concentrate colonial trade; sugar and tobacco processing expand; the Great Fire destroys workshops but rebuilding follows; production capacity grows over the period, generating commercial expansion.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Grain storage provides harvest-to-harvest buffering; merchant capital accumulates; goldsmith-bankers hold deposits; no centralized reserves exist; crown finances improve under restored customs revenue; overall buffering capacity is modest but adequate for normal fluctuations.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ The Navigation Acts structure colonial trade through English ports; Dutch competition continues until naval victories secure English position; domestic markets connect through coastal shipping and improving roads; the Royal Exchange and coffee houses facilitate commercial intelligence; trade networks strengthen.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Silver and gold coinage circulate; goldsmith-bankers provide deposit and lending services; bills of exchange facilitate trade; the Great Recoinage of 1696 addresses currency debasement; the Bank of England is founded in 1694, just after the window, transforming credit infrastructure; this period sees the foundations laid.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Customs and excise provide crown revenue; the hearth tax is introduced and later repealed; parliamentary control of supply is established; no extraordinary wartime taxation burdens the population; extraction is moderate and increasingly regularized through parliamentary consent.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Land remains concentrated among gentry and aristocracy; Irish land redistribution massively transfers Catholic to Protestant ownership; merchant wealth grows in London; Restoration returns some confiscated royalist estates; overall distribution remains highly unequal with modest shifts among elites.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Urban waste accumulates in cities; coal smoke increases in London; industrial waste remains localized and pre-factory in scale; the Great Fire clears accumulated refuse; waste routing is dispersed through local dumping and watercourses; no critical sink saturation occurs.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Parish and neighborhood networks provide mutual support; guild and company structures continue; religious division between conformists and dissenters creates parallel communities; trust networks are robust within groups but strained across religious lines; the coffee house creates new spaces for cross-status sociability.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Hierarchical social order persists from aristocracy through gentry, merchants, and yeomen to laborers; religious identity—conformist, Presbyterian, Independent, Quaker, Catholic—crosscuts class; the Restoration reasserts traditional status markers; social boundaries are permeable for successful merchants but rigid for the poor.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Patriarchal nuclear households remain the norm; primogeniture governs landed inheritance; marriage strategies consolidate property; kinship networks provide support and connection; no significant structural change occurs in family organization; the framework is stable across the period's political upheavals.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Guilds and livery companies govern urban trades; parish vestries manage local affairs; dissenting congregations provide intense associational life for nonconformists; coffee houses create new public spaces for discussion and commercial exchange; associational fabric is dense and diversifying.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Restoration reopens theaters; alehouses, fairs, and horse racing provide popular entertainment; Puritan restrictions relax; gambling and sexual license characterize court culture; sociability rebounds from Commonwealth austerity; public recreational spaces expand with the reopening of cultural institutions.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Petitioning remains available; legal channels through courts continue; nonconformist grievances are partially absorbed through toleration after 1689; riot and protest occur but are contained; significant dissent persists but finds sufficient outlet through religious community and petition; residual grievance is present but not explosive.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ The Commonwealth abolishes monarchy and House of Lords; Cromwell's Protectorate concentrates power personally until his death in 1658; Restoration returns Charles II with parliamentary constraints; James II's catholicizing policies provoke crisis; the Glorious Revolution establishes parliamentary supremacy and constitutional monarchy; authority structure transforms fundamentally across the window.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Cromwell's death reveals the republic's succession failure; the Convention Parliament restores Charles II; James II succeeds peacefully in 1685 but is expelled in 1688; William and Mary's accession establishes parliamentary role in succession; transfer mechanisms remain contested but achieve workable settlement by window's end.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Divine right, ancient constitution, parliamentary sovereignty, and Protestant succession compete as legitimating narratives; the Exclusion Crisis debates Catholic succession; the Revolution Settlement grounds legitimacy in parliamentary consent and Protestant religion; narrative evolution reflects fundamental constitutional reconstruction.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ The Protectorate develops efficient military administration; Restoration reduces standing forces; the Treasury and Admiralty professionalize; customs and excise collection improves; local government continues through unpaid justices of the peace; administrative capacity grows incrementally, especially in revenue collection.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ The Restoration restricts radical participation; the Exclusion Crisis sees petitioning campaigns and electoral mobilization; party identities of Whig and Tory emerge; coffee houses facilitate political discussion; participation is constrained compared to the 1640s but more institutionalized; the Revolution involves coordinated elite action with popular acquiescence.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ The Commonwealth's navy defeats the Dutch; the Restoration continues naval rivalry; Charles II receives secret French subsidies; James II's French alignment alarms Protestant opinion; William's invasion is enabled by Dutch support; England emerges as a major Protestant power opposing French hegemony; geopolitical position strengthens decisively.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ The New Model Army is disbanded at the Restoration; a small standing army is maintained controversially; the navy expands as the primary strategic arm; militia provides domestic security; James II's expansion of the army under Catholic officers provokes opposition; the Revolution resolves force control in favor of parliamentary supremacy.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Republican plots and nonconformist dissent are surveilled and suppressed; Monmouth's Rebellion (1685) is crushed brutally; crime and disorder remain at normal early modern levels; the Jacobite threat emerges after 1688; internal order is generally maintained though political tension remains high; the monopoly on force is contested but functional.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Print remains the primary medium for political and commercial information; official newsbooks control news during the Protectorate; the Restoration licenses the London Gazette as the official organ; coffee houses circulate news and pamphlets; manuscript newsletters serve elite networks; velocity remains limited by physical transport but print density is high.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ State papers, parliamentary records, and parish registers continue; printed matter accumulates in private and institutional collections; the Great Fire destroys some records but most survive; the documentary base is extensive; print ensures unprecedented persistence; Pepys's diary exemplifies private record-keeping.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ The Licensing Act of 1662 reimposed pre-publication censorship through the Stationers' Company; enforcement varies with political temperature; Exclusion Crisis sees pamphlet proliferation despite controls; the Act lapses in 1679, is renewed, and finally expires in 1695; discourse norms alternate between restriction and relative openness; the direction is toward eventual press freedom.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Literacy continues at roughly thirty percent for males, higher in London and among dissenters; coffee houses multiply as reading and discussion venues; sermons reach non-literate audiences; printed ballads and broadsides reach popular audiences; amplification networks extend print's reach through oral transmission and public reading.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Competing pamphlets and rumors circulate during political crises; the Popish Plot (1678) demonstrates how false information can drive events; official gazettes claim authority but lack credibility with opposition readers; verification relies on personal judgment and source reputation; noise is significant during crises but absorbed during calmer periods.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Protestant conscience, common law tradition, and natural rights provide moral foundations; the execution of the regicides after 1660 demonstrates contested notions of justice; habeas corpus is asserted against arbitrary detention; the sense of justice remains strong but divided on fundamental political questions.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Common law continues as the primary framework; the Habeas Corpus Act of 1679 strengthens protections against arbitrary detention; the Clarendon Code restricts nonconformists; the Bill of Rights (1689) establishes fundamental constitutional principles; the legal framework stabilizes toward constitutional settlement.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Assizes and quarter sessions administer justice; Star Chamber's abolition removes prerogative courts; the Bloody Assizes after Monmouth's Rebellion demonstrate judicial severity; judges are subject to political pressure; enforcement capacity is adequate for routine matters but politicized in sensitive cases.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Habeas corpus protections are codified; jury trial continues; nonconformists face legal disabilities under penal laws; Catholic exclusion tightens; the Bill of Rights establishes parliamentary and subject rights; compliance burden varies by religious status; Protestant conformists enjoy substantial protections while dissenters face restrictions.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Regicides are executed at the Restoration; Irish Catholics lose land without redress; political prisoners suffer under varying conditions; the Bloody Assizes execute hundreds after Monmouth's Rebellion; impunity for the powerful and unredressed wrong for the defeated characterize political justice; routine civil justice functions adequately.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ The Royal Society is founded in 1660, institutionalizing experimental natural philosophy; Newton's Principia (1687) establishes the mechanical worldview; Locke develops empiricist philosophy and political theory; Hobbes's materialism circulates controversially; the paradigm shifts decisively toward observation, experiment, and mathematical formulation.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Navigation and shipbuilding improve supporting maritime expansion; surveying and cartography serve estate management and colonial administration; fire engine technology develops after the Great Fire; printing technology is mature; agricultural improvement is discussed though slowly implemented; applied knowledge advances incrementally.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Oxford and Cambridge continue training clergy and gentry; dissenting academies emerge for nonconformists excluded from universities; grammar schools serve middling ranks; literacy remains roughly stable; the knowledge stock expands through print and the Royal Society's communications; distribution remains class-stratified.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Scholastic natural philosophy declines as mechanical philosophy advances; alchemy persists but loses intellectual prestige; older medical theories continue alongside new anatomical knowledge; no catastrophic knowledge loss occurs; the direction is toward accumulation and paradigm replacement rather than decay.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Protestant identity and anti-Catholic anxiety intensify during Popish Plot and Exclusion Crisis; the memory of civil war shapes political caution; the ancient constitution provides political mythology; Restoration celebrates escape from Puritan austerity; the Revolution adds Dutch William and Protestant deliverance to the national story.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Restoration theater flourishes with Wycherley, Congreve, and Dryden; architecture follows Wren's classicism in rebuilding London; portraiture continues for elites; music revives at court; material culture reflects restored aristocratic elegance; the Great Fire enables comprehensive urban rebuilding in brick and stone.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Relief at war's end yields to Restoration festivity; plague and fire provoke providentialist interpretation; Popish Plot hysteria demonstrates susceptibility to panic; the Revolution is managed with remarkable calm; the collective mood moves from Restoration exuberance through crisis anxiety toward post-Revolution settlement confidence.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Puritan forms of piety persist in dissenting communities; Restoration court culture creates new fashions; neither set of forms is exhausted; contest between them generates vitality rather than anomie; meaning is actively created in competing cultural projects; no significant meaning void characterizes the period.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Protestant Christianity remains the cosmological framework; mechanical philosophy raises questions about divine intervention but most practitioners reconcile science and faith; providence remains a living category for interpreting events; secularism is nascent at most; the existential outlook remains firmly religious.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Parish worship resumes Anglican forms at the Restoration; dissenters maintain separate devotional communities; Quaker meetings develop distinctive practices; Catholic worship continues privately; family devotions persist among the godly; sacred practice diversifies across denominations while remaining central to social life.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The Church of England is restored with bishops and cathedrals; the Clarendon Code attempts to enforce conformity; dissenting denominations organize outside the establishment; the Toleration Act of 1689 permits licensed nonconformist worship; doctrinal authority is divided between establishment and tolerated dissent.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Protestant denominational diversity stabilizes; Catholic presence persists despite exclusion; no significant syncretism occurs; the direction is toward regulated pluralism rather than uniformity or radical innovation; the Revolution Settlement establishes Protestant hegemony with limited toleration.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Witch trials decline significantly; mechanical philosophy begins separating natural and supernatural causation; however, providence and miracle remain credible to most; disenchantment is incipient rather than accomplished; the spiritual need is met through established and dissenting religious communities; no significant void of meaning opens.<br><br>
