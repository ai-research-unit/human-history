# __1914 CE – 1921 CE__ — War, Revolution, and Civil War
__Summary:__ The Russian Empire collapses under the strain of World War I, triggering revolution, regime change, and civil war across the East European Plain; the binding constraint is state disintegration — political authority fragments, coercive capacity scatters, and no successor regime can consolidate power until 1921; the system holds zero margin against further shock; the direction of change is violent rupture followed by Bolshevik consolidation.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The vast lowland plain from Poland to the Urals imposes immense logistical friction on armies; poor roads, few railroads, and vast distances favor defensive attrition; the western borderlands become contested zones of occupation and retreat.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Continental climate with severe winters disrupts military operations; the 1917–1921 period sees harsh winters compounding supply breakdowns; no secular drift within the window.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ No major natural disasters; the hazard is anthropogenic — war, revolution, and famine; infrastructure destruction removes all margin against environmental stress.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Grain-producing regions of Ukraine and south Russia become contested territories; Donbas coal and Baku oil change hands repeatedly; access to resources fluctuates with military control.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Battlefield destruction, abandoned equipment, and unburied dead litter the landscape; urban sanitation collapses; absorptive capacity overwhelmed in combat zones and cities.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Typhus, cholera, and influenza epidemics sweep through armies and civilian populations; the 1918–1919 influenza pandemic compounds war losses; mortality rate spikes catastrophically above peacetime baseline.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Famine conditions in cities and contested regions; grain requisitioning depletes rural stocks; widespread malnutrition and starvation; physical vitality collapses especially among urban and displaced populations.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Mass mobilization separates families; wartime and revolutionary chaos disrupts marriage and fertility; birth rates decline sharply.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Massive population losses — military deaths, civilian casualties, famine, epidemic, emigration — produce demographic collapse; age structure skews toward the very young and old; replacement ratios collapse.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ War widows, orphans, disabled veterans, and refugees constitute an enormous dependency burden; the productive cohort is depleted and cannot absorb the load.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Urban provisioning collapses; grain requisitioning under War Communism strips the countryside; barter replaces money exchange; the population sits at the edge of starvation.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Industrial production collapses to a fraction of prewar levels; coal mines and oil fields change hands or cease operation; the energy conversion regime effectively disintegrates.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Conscription drains labor from agriculture and industry; War Communism introduces labor armies and compulsory mobilization; the coercion level rises to near-total.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Industrial output falls to perhaps 15–20% of 1913 levels by 1920; agricultural output collapses; surplus production is effectively zero.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Reserves exhausted by 1917; grain requisitioning depletes rural stockpiles; no buffer remains against further shock; the 1921 famine demonstrates the total absence of reserves.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Market exchange suppressed under War Communism; rail network disrupted by combat and fuel shortages; black markets and barter replace formal trade; logistics network fragmented.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Hyperinflation destroys the ruble's purchasing power; credit system collapses; barter and in-kind requisitioning replace monetary exchange; value storage impossible.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ War Communism extracts grain by armed requisition squads; "surplus" is defined administratively and taken by force; redistribution serves the Red Army and urban workers; extraction load approaches confiscation.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Bolshevik nationalization eliminates private property in land, industry, and finance; former elites stripped of assets; wealth concentration collapses through confiscation rather than redistribution.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Industrial waste negligible given production collapse; agricultural and military waste dispersed across the landscape; routing decisions irrelevant when production barely exists.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Trust radius contracts to immediate family and locality; class war rhetoric and denunciations fracture social bonds; mutual aid persists within villages and worker collectives but not across factional lines.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Class becomes the dominant identity marker; former nobles, bourgeoisie, and clergy targeted as class enemies; workers and poor peasants elevated rhetorically; ethnic and national identities mobilized by competing factions.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Family structures disrupted by mobilization, flight, and death; inheritance abolished for significant property; household formation delayed by chaos.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Soviets emerge as organs of self-organization; workers' committees, soldiers' committees, and peasant communes briefly flourish; independent associations suppressed as Bolshevik control consolidates.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Revolutionary festivals and mass mobilization replace traditional leisure; theater and agitprop serve political purposes; genuine leisure disappears in the struggle for survival.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Revolution provides catastrophic discharge of accumulated grievances; land seizures, factory takeovers, and score-settling release decades of suppressed tension; new grievances accumulate immediately under War Communism.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Imperial autocracy collapses in February 1917; Provisional Government never consolidates; Bolsheviks seize power in October; civil war fragments authority until Red victory in 1921; power centralizes violently toward the Communist Party.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Tsar abdicates; Provisional Government lacks legitimacy; Bolshevik seizure is a revolutionary rupture; Lenin's authority rests on party control and revolutionary prestige; transfer mechanisms remain improvised.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Bolsheviks claim legitimacy through socialist revolution and worker-peasant alliance; opponents invoke democracy, national independence, or restoration; no narrative achieves broad acceptance until Red victory.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Imperial bureaucracy disintegrates; Provisional Government cannot build replacement; Bolsheviks construct new apparatus from party cadres, Red Guards, and co-opted specialists; capacity minimal but growing by 1921.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Mass mobilization unprecedented — soviets, parties, trade unions, Red Army, White armies; participation coerced and voluntary; opposition increasingly suppressed as one-party rule consolidates.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Russia exits World War I through Brest-Litovsk (March 1918) at enormous territorial cost; Allied intervention supports Whites; new state emerges diplomatically isolated but surviving.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Imperial army dissolves; Red Army built from 1918; Cheka established for internal security; White armies, national movements, and foreign interventions create multi-sided conflict; coercive capacity fragmentary until Red consolidation.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Civil war, banditry, peasant uprisings, urban crime, and political terror render internal order nonexistent; the Kronstadt rebellion (1921) marks the final major challenge before consolidation.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Telegraph and rail disrupted by combat; newspapers remain primary channel but distribution erratic; oral networks and rumor fill gaps; velocity slows outside party-controlled zones.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Imperial archives preserved but access restricted; new Soviet record-keeping begins; much documentation lost in chaos; persistence uncertain for non-party records.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Press freedom briefly expands after February; Bolsheviks close opposition newspapers by mid-1918; party press becomes monopoly channel; framing shifts to revolutionary Marxist vocabulary; permitted discourse narrows rapidly.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Literacy remains limited; agitprop trains and posters reach non-readers; amplification serves Bolshevik messaging; attention focused on survival.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Rumor, propaganda, and disinformation create high noise load; verification impossible for most; signal integrity collapses outside immediate experience.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Revolutionary justice overrides formal law; class origin determines guilt or innocence; popular sense of justice satisfied by land redistribution and attacks on elites; terror justified as class war.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Imperial legal system abolished; revolutionary decrees replace law; codification minimal; regulation by decree and party directive.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Revolutionary tribunals and Cheka replace courts; summary justice predominates; enforcement arbitrary and politicized; liability determined by class position.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Individual rights suspended under revolutionary emergency; protection depends on class standing and party connection; compliance burden extreme — survival requires navigating arbitrary power.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Red and White terror produce mass impunity; atrocities committed by all sides go unredressed; judicial deficit essentially infinite.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Marxism-Leninism becomes official theory; bourgeois science initially tolerated for practical utility; revolutionary ideology displaces alternative paradigms in social thought.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Technical expertise desperately needed; former specialists co-opted despite class suspicion; military and industrial methods improvised under crisis conditions.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Schools disrupted; literacy campaigns begin; university enrollment shifts toward workers; knowledge stock preserved unevenly; skill distribution chaotic.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Imperial administrative and legal knowledge deliberately discarded; practical skills preserved for necessity; ideological purification removes "bourgeois" content; decay and deliberate erasure proceed together.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Revolution becomes foundational myth; October 1917 rewritten as heroic origin; Imperial past recast as oppression; competing national myths emerge in borderlands.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Revolutionary avant-garde flourishes briefly — Constructivism, Futurism, agitprop art; material culture impoverished by production collapse; artistic energy directed toward revolutionary themes.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Mood oscillates between revolutionary enthusiasm and exhaustion; fear and violence normalize; survival orientation dominates by 1920–1921; mood lifting only with NEP announcement.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Imperial forms — religious, monarchical, aristocratic — deliberately destroyed; revolutionary forms not yet exhausted; meaning creation active through revolutionary culture.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Marxist materialism promoted as official worldview; religious cosmology attacked but persists among peasantry; fatalism and millenarian hope coexist; secularization accelerates in urban areas.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Church services continue but face hostility; revolutionary rituals — red funerals, revolutionary holidays — compete with Orthodox practice; devotional infrastructure under attack.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Orthodox Church stripped of property and legal status; clergy targeted as class enemies; Patriarch Tikhon resists then accommodates; institutional authority collapses; doctrinal unity fractures under pressure.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Revolutionary faith partially displaces religious belief among youth and urban workers; traditional Orthodoxy persists in villages; no syncretism but sharp competition.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Religious cosmology loses public sanction; revolutionary enthusiasm provides substitute meaning for some; sacred need incompletely absorbed by political faith; disenchantment and belief coexist uneasily.<br><br>
