# __1905 CE – 1919 CE__ — Swadeshi, Extremism, and Wartime Press
__Summary:__ The window spans the radicalization of Indian nationalism from the Bengal partition through the First World War to the Rowlatt Acts. Territorial bounds remain British India, though partition divides Bengal into two provinces (1905–1911). The binding constraint is the collision between rising nationalist demands and British refusal to share real power, producing cycles of protest and repression. Margin against shock narrows as wartime inflation and influenza pandemic stress the population. Principal direction of change is escalation — from moderate petition to mass boycott, revolutionary violence, sedition trials, and finally post-war expectation of constitutional advance.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Railway network now extensive; telegraphic communication routine; Bengal partition creates administrative boundary bisecting the Gangetic delta; spatial friction reduced but partition line imposes new bureaucratic friction.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Monsoon variability continues; drought conditions in 1907 and 1918 affect harvests; long-term climate regime unchanged; agricultural vulnerability to rainfall failure persists in unirrigated districts.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Floods in Bengal and Orissa recur; cyclones strike coasts; famine threat reduced by improved rail transport for relief grain; influenza pandemic 1918–1919 kills millions — disease hazard acute.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Coal and iron extraction expands; Tata Iron and Steel Company founded 1907; jute and cotton remain primary exports; forest resources further depleted; drawdown rate accelerating with industrialization.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Industrial effluent increases around Calcutta and Bombay mills; urban sanitation still inadequate; river pollution visible near factory zones; absorptive capacity beginning to strain locally.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Plague continues in western India; malaria endemic; influenza pandemic 1918–1919 kills an estimated 12–17 million in India; mortality baseline spiking catastrophically at window's close.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Wartime inflation raises food prices; nutritional stress on poor intensifies; grain exports diverted to war effort; average vitality declining among laboring classes by 1918.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ High fertility persists; infant mortality remains severe; age-of-consent debates continue; widow remarriage slowly spreading among reform-minded groups; reproductive regime unchanged.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population growing slowly; influenza pandemic produces massive mortality shock in 1918–1919, temporarily reversing gains; density pressure mounting in Bengal and UP plains.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ War widows, pandemic orphans, and displaced agriculturalists accumulate; charitable institutions strained; joint family stretched by multiple crises; dependency load increasing.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Wartime inflation erodes purchasing power; grain prices rise sharply; subsistence margin for poor households shrinks; provisioning precarious in 1918–1919 crisis years.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Steam power expanding in railways and factories; Tata Steel brings integrated steelmaking; hydroelectric projects planned; per-capita energy access rising in industrial centers, stagnant in countryside.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Factory labor in Bombay and Calcutta mills; labor unrest begins (Bombay strikes 1908); indentured labor to overseas colonies continues until 1917 abolition; coercion through economic necessity rather than law.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ War boosts demand for Indian manufactures; jute and steel production rises; agricultural output constrained by weather; surplus generated but extracted for war contribution.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Grain reserves inadequate for pandemic-year disruption; war demands on treasury reduce fiscal buffer; private hoarding increases; buffering capacity declining under multiple pressures.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Wartime shipping disruptions affect imports; internal rail network handles war logistics; commercial telegraph fully developed; trade reoriented toward war supplies.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Rupee on gold-exchange standard; wartime inflation erodes purchasing power; urban banking expands; rural credit still dominated by moneylenders; war loans extract savings.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ War contributions extracted through loans and taxes; surplus flows to imperial war effort; land revenue collection intensifies; redistribution toward military expenditure.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ War profiteers and industrialists accumulate wealth; peasant indebtedness deepens; urban middle class squeezed by inflation; asset concentration widening.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Industrial waste output rising; urban garbage disposal inadequate; pandemic corpse disposal strains cremation and burial capacity; waste routing systems overwhelmed in crisis months.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Swadeshi movement expands trust radius among nationalists; Hindu-Muslim cooperation in Lucknow Pact 1916; mutual aid networks activated during pandemic; trust widening within anti-colonial coalition.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Caste reform movements active; Muslim political identity sharpens with Muslim League founding 1906; educated youth radicalize; communal categories increasingly politicized.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Joint family persists but urban migration strains kinship bonds; reform of personal law debated; inheritance patterns unchanged; family solidarity tested by pandemic losses.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Swadeshi societies, national schools, volunteer corps proliferate; Home Rule Leagues 1916; associational density thickening; caste associations organize politically.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Public meetings, processions, and hartals become political rituals; boycott of British goods socializes resistance; theatre and music carry nationalist themes; leisure spaces politicized.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Swadeshi agitation channels middle-class grievance; labor strikes emerge as outlet; agrarian discontent lacks organization; communal violence in some localities; discharge capacity strained by repression.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Morley-Minto reforms 1909 expand councils but retain official majorities; executive power still concentrated in British hands; provincial governments gain limited autonomy; centralizing authority challenged but intact.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Viceroy and governors appointed from London; legislative councils partly elected on restricted franchise; no succession mechanism toward self-government; transfer delayed by war.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ British legitimacy eroding among educated Indians; partition of Bengal seen as divide-and-rule; Montagu Declaration 1917 promises "progressive realization of responsible government" — expectations raised.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ ICS overstretched by war demands; provincial administration functions but wartime pressures reduce efficiency; intelligence capacity expanded to monitor revolutionaries; integrity strained.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Congress splits 1907 between Moderates and Extremists; reunites 1916 with Lucknow Pact; Home Rule Leagues mobilize mass support; participation expanding beyond English-educated elite.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ India contributes 1.3 million troops to World War I; war with Ottoman Empire complicates Muslim opinion; Ghadar movement operates from North America; external relations dominated by imperial war strategy.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Army expanded for war service; police reinforced for internal security; Defence of India Act 1915 enables detention without trial; arms restrictions tightened; coercive capacity fully mobilized.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Revolutionary conspiracies in Bengal and Punjab; Ghadar uprising suppressed 1915; sedition trials (Tilak 1908, 1916); bombs and assassinations sporadic; disorder contained but elevated.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Telegraph and postal systems fully developed; newspapers circulate nationally within days; international cables connect India to world news; velocity near real-time for major events.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Colonial archives expand; sedition trial records preserved; nationalist publications confiscated but copies survive; press runs large enough for broad distribution; persistence improving.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Press Act 1910 strengthens censorship powers; sedition prosecutions target extremist editors; Tilak imprisoned for Kesari articles; wartime censorship under Defence of India Act; discourse band narrowing sharply.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Swadeshi movement generates mass attention; nationalist newspapers amplify Congress demands; boycott slogans spread through bazaars; readership expanding beyond literate elite through public readings.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Propaganda and counter-propaganda multiply; rumor accelerates during war and pandemic; official news competes with nationalist framing; noise load increasing as discourse polarizes.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Sedition trials offend nationalist sense of justice; Jallianwala Bagh (April 1919) at window's edge crystallizes moral outrage; racial discrimination perceived as unjust; legitimacy of colonial law contested.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Press Acts, Sedition law, Defence of India Act expand repressive framework; Rowlatt Acts 1919 propose indefinite detention; Morley-Minto reforms create separate electorates — legal evolution politicized.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Special tribunals for sedition cases; summary procedures under Defence of India Act; enforcement swift and severe for political offenses; liability assigned through executive as much as judicial process.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Habeas corpus suspended for political detainees; press freedom curtailed; ordinary property and contract rights intact; compliance burden heavy for nationalist activists.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Dyer's massacre at Jallianwala Bagh (April 1919) epitomizes impunity — perpetrator exonerated by British opinion; sedition convicts lack appeal remedy; structural wrongs accumulating.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Economic nationalism (drain theory, Swadeshi economics) theorized; scientific research expands at Indian universities; Bose and Raman active; Western paradigms dominant but Indian contributions emerging.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Tata Steel demonstrates Indian industrial capability; engineering education expands; medical training produces Indian doctors for war service; technical method advancing in industrial sector.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ National schools founded as alternative to government education; literacy slowly rising; university enrollment increasing; skill distribution still concentrated in urban professional class.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Traditional knowledge systems declining further; Orientalist scholarship continues but practical relevance fades; Swadeshi rhetoric valorizes indigenous knowledge without halting decay.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Bengal partition galvanizes regional and national identity; Vande Mataram becomes anthem; 1857 reframed as first war of independence; foundational narratives sharpening against colonial rule.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Swadeshi aesthetics valorize khadi and indigenous craft; Bengal School of Art develops national style; nationalist theatre and poetry flourish; material culture politicized through boycott.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Radicalization among youth; anger at partition and repression; wartime expectations raised; mood shifting from cautious reformism to impatient demand; pandemic despair at window's end.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Moderate petition exhausted as form; new forms — hartal, swadeshi pledge, revolutionary martyrdom — carry meaning; old symbols refreshed by nationalist reinterpretation; meaning-making intensified.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Nationalist spirituality blends religious revivalism with political action; Tilak reinterprets Gita as call to action; Aurobindo's spiritual nationalism; pan-Islamism stirs Muslim opinion; existential stakes heightened.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Ganesh Chaturthi and Shivaji festivals politicized by Tilak; Eid and Muharram retain communal significance; pilgrimage continues; sacred practice increasingly inflected by nationalist symbolism.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Hindu Mahasabha and Muslim League organize communal constituencies; Aligarh and Deoband represent divergent Muslim paths; doctrinal authority fragmented but institutions consolidating.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Hindu and Islamic revivalism both expanding; syncretic traditions under pressure from reformist puritanism; missionary Christianity stable; spiritual boundaries hardening.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Revolutionary youth embrace secular or spiritualized nationalism; traditional piety persists among masses; pandemic provokes fatalism and religious response; disenchantment limited to radical fringe.<br><br>
