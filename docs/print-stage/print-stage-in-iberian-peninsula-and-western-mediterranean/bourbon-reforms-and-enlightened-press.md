# __1700 CE – 1808 CE — Bourbon Reforms and Enlightened Press__

The Bourbon century transforms print from Counter-Reformation instrument into Enlightenment vehicle, yet the binding constraint remains the Crown's insistence on administrative centralization without political liberalization: periodicals, academies, and economic societies propagate useful knowledge while Inquisitorial censorship persists, so that the public sphere expands within channels the state controls rather than against it, and the reformist press cultivates a readership dependent on royal patronage whose autonomy collapses when invasion shatters the Bourbon order.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Madrid consolidates as administrative center; the central meseta remains poorly connected to peripheral coastal zones. Roads improve under Charles III but overland transit stays slow compared to maritime routes. Atlantic orientation intensifies as American trade expands, while Mediterranean commerce declines relative to northern Atlantic powers. The Pyrenean barrier channels French influence through limited passes.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Little Ice Age persists into the early eighteenth century, with harsh winters and variable harvests. Droughts in the meseta and Andalusia periodically stress grain supply. Warmer decades mid-century ease agrarian pressure, though interannual volatility remains significant.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ The 1755 Lisbon earthquake devastates Portuguese printing infrastructure and sends shockwaves through Seville and Cadiz. Periodic floods affect the Guadalquivir and Ebro valleys. Epidemic outbreaks remain localized but disruptive.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ American silver continues to flow, though declining from Habsburg-era peaks. The peninsula lacks coal deposits comparable to northern Europe; charcoal and wood remain primary fuels. Water power in Catalonia supports early textile mechanization.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Urban expansion in Madrid, Seville, and Barcelona strains rudimentary sanitation. Rivers carry industrial and domestic waste; sink capacity adequate for the scale of activity, though localized pollution increases near tanneries and dye works.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic typhus, malaria in lowland Andalusia, and periodic smallpox outbreaks maintain high infant mortality. Urban death rates exceed birth rates; cities depend on rural migration. The mortality baseline improves slightly through the century but remains elevated by northwestern European standards.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Grain dependency exposes the population to harvest failure. Maize and potatoes spread slowly. Average stature remains low; nutritional deficiency affects working capacity. Slight improvements late century as New World crops diffuse.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Hospital infrastructure expands under Bourbon reforms; royal hospitals in Madrid improve. Medical education advances through anatomical theaters and surgical colleges. Church-run charity hospitals serve the poor; capacity remains uneven between cities and countryside.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Marriage patterns follow western European norms: relatively late marriage age, nuclear household formation. Ecclesiastical control over marriage persists. Fertility rates remain moderately high within marriage.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population grows from approximately 7.5 million (1717) to 10.5 million (1797). Recovery from seventeenth-century decline proceeds unevenly; coastal regions and Catalonia grow faster than the meseta. Internal migration feeds urban growth.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ High infant mortality reduces the dependency ratio among youth; adult mortality leaves widows and orphans supported through church charity and kinship networks. The dependency load remains manageable as population growth accelerates.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Grain supply remains precarious; the meseta depends on wheat, the periphery on mixed cropping. The 1766 Esquilache riots reveal urban food insecurity. Liberalization of grain trade under Charles III improves supply flexibility but intensifies price volatility.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and animal muscle dominate; water mills power textile production in Catalonia. Charcoal serves metallurgy. Per-capita energy remains low; the agrarian base limits surplus for non-agricultural activity.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Guild structures persist in urban crafts; rural labor varies from smallholding peasants in the north to landless jornaleros in Andalusian latifundia. Wage labor expands in Catalan textiles. Serfdom is absent but labor coercion through debt and custom remains significant in the south.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Catalan textile production grows, supplying American markets. Castilian wool declines as the Mesta loses privileges. Agricultural output rises with population but productivity gains lag. The surplus flows to landlords, church, and Crown rather than reinvestment.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Municipal granaries (positos) buffer grain supply but prove inadequate in major crises. Royal treasuries depend on American remittances and loans; Bourbon fiscal reforms increase revenue but expenses consistently exceed income. Debt accumulates through each war.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Cadiz monopolizes American trade until 1778 liberalization opens additional ports. The Consulado system organizes merchant guilds. Internal customs barriers (eliminated 1714 in Crown of Aragon) facilitate Castilian integration. Road improvements under Charles III enhance internal circulation but the network remains thin.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ The Bank of San Carlos (1782) attempts to stabilize credit; royal debt instruments (vales reales) circulate but depreciate. Merchant credit networks operate through family and guild ties. Monetary instability and repeated devaluations erode savings.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Crown and Church extract through tithes, alcabalas, and ecclesiastical levies. Bourbon reforms attempt to increase royal revenue at Church expense but face resistance. American remittances remain crucial; their decline strains the treasury.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Landownership concentrates among nobility and Church; entailment (mayorazgo) protects aristocratic estates. Urban commercial wealth grows in Barcelona and Cadiz. Inequality intensifies; the propertied defend holdings through legal privilege and ecclesiastical sanctuary.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Urban waste management improves marginally; Madrid's street cleaning advances under Charles III. Industrial waste from Catalan textiles enters rivers with minimal treatment. Financial mispricing accumulates in state debt obligations.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Primogeniture and mayorazgo preserve noble estates; middling groups divide property among heirs. Regional variation persists: stem families in the Basque Country and Catalonia, nuclear households in Castile. Church control over marriage and inheritance law remains firm.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Guilds organize urban crafts; cofradias (religious brotherhoods) provide mutual aid and burial. Economic Societies of Friends of the Country (Sociedades Economicas de Amigos del Pais), founded from 1765, create new associational space for provincial elites to discuss agricultural improvement and useful knowledge.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ The tertulia (salon conversation) emerges as a sociable form among urban elites; cafes appear in Madrid and Barcelona. Bullfighting consolidates as spectacle. Carnival and religious festivals structure popular leisure.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ The traditional hierarchy persists: nobility (grandees, hidalgos), clergy, commoners. Limpieza de sangre (blood purity) requirements decline but do not disappear. A nascent bourgeoisie emerges in commercial and textile centers. The gap between propertied and landless widens.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ The Nueva Planta decrees (1707-1716) eliminate Aragonese, Catalan, Valencian, and Majorcan fueros, imposing Castilian administrative uniformity. Bourbon centralization creates intendancies, reorganizes colonial administration. Corruption remains endemic but bureaucratic capacity increases. The Council of Castile oversees censorship and licensing.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ The standing army expands; Bourbon military reforms modernize training and equipment. The navy rebuilds after Habsburg decline, supporting Atlantic trade and colonial defense. Internal order depends on urban police (created 1782 in Madrid) and rural constabulary. The Inquisition retains arrest powers but uses them less frequently.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ The 1766 Esquilache riots demonstrate urban volatility; rural banditry persists in Andalusia and Catalonia. The apparatus contains disorder without eliminating its sources. Regional grievances from abolished fueros simmer in Catalonia and Aragon.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Charles III (r. 1759-1788) embodies enlightened absolutism; his personal authority enables reform. Ministers Aranda, Floridablanca, and Campomanes drive policy. Under Charles IV (r. 1788-1808), Godoy's favoritism erodes royal prestige. Leadership becomes contested as France revolutionizes.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Bourbon legitimacy rests on dynastic right consolidated through the War of Spanish Succession (1700-1714). The Crown commands allegiance through patronage, reform success, and Catholic identity. Legitimacy weakens late century as Godoy's regime discredits the court.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ The reformist program demonstrates sustained political will under Charles III; colonial reorganization, economic liberalization, and educational expansion proceed. Under Charles IV, will fragments; court faction undermines coherent policy. The French alliance pulls Spain into ruinous wars.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Alliance with France (Family Compacts) structures foreign policy. Rivalry with Britain shapes colonial defense. Spain fights Britain repeatedly (1739-1748, 1762-1763, 1779-1783, 1796-1808), with mixed results. The French Revolution strains the alliance; war with France (1793-1795) precedes renewed French alignment.

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Enlightenment optimism permeates the ilustrado elite; belief in progress through reform lifts the educated minority. Popular sentiment remains localized, religious, suspicious of foreign influence. The 1766 riots reveal the fragility of reform legitimacy among urban commoners.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ Provincial resentment from abolished fueros persists. Ilustrado reforms alienate traditionalist clergy and commoners without creating a broad reform constituency. By 1808, court scandals, military defeats, and food scarcity exhaust popular patience with the regime.


## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Castilian serves as the administrative and literary language; Catalan, Basque, and Galician persist in domestic use but lack print presence. Latin declines; French becomes the prestige language among elites. Literacy expands slowly; perhaps 25-30% of males can read by century's end.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ The royal postal system improves; regular courier routes connect major cities. The Gaceta de Madrid (official gazette from 1697) and Mercurio Historico y Politico transmit news weekly. Periodicals reach provincial subscribers within days. Speed remains slow by later standards but adequate for the administrative state.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Royal archives centralize under Bourbon administration; the Archivo General de Simancas and Archivo de Indias systematize colonial records. University libraries and private collections grow. The Inquisitorial archives document censorship activity. Persistence improves through standardized record-keeping.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ The Inquisition retains censorship authority; the Index prohibits heterodox works. Royal licensing (licencia) adds a second filter. Enlightened periodicals like El Pensador (1762-1767) and El Censor (1781-1787) probe limits, discussing social criticism within acceptable bounds. Discourse norms permit economic and agrarian reform discussion but forbid challenges to Church doctrine or royal authority. The band widens under Charles III, contracts after 1789 as revolutionary fear spreads.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ The reading public expands through tertulias, cafes, and Economic Societies. Newspapers and journals circulate among subscribers and in reading rooms. Amplification operates through oral retransmission; the literate read to the illiterate. Attention concentrates among urban elites; rural penetration remains limited.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Official publications maintain high reliability; the Gaceta de Madrid reports court news accurately. Underground pamphlets and foreign gazettes introduce competing narratives. Noise increases as the French Revolution generates conflicting accounts. Verification depends on reputation and official sourcing.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Catholic teaching provides the moral foundation; charity, hierarchy, and obedience structure the ethical framework. Enlightened reformers introduce utilitarian language of public utility without displacing religious foundations. Popular justice remains oriented toward local custom and patron-client reciprocity.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ The Nueva Recopilacion (1567, updated 1775) codifies Castilian law. Bourbon reforms attempt legal rationalization; the Novisima Recopilacion (1805) consolidates statutes. Colonial law (Leyes de Indias) regulates American territories. Regulatory activity intensifies; decrees on grain trade, guild reform, and Church property multiply.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Royal courts (audiencias, chancillerias) adjudicate civil and criminal cases. The Inquisition handles heresy and morals offenses with declining frequency. Justice remains slow; case backlogs persist. Sanction ranges from fines and exile to imprisonment; executions decline. Ecclesiastical courts retain jurisdiction over clergy.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Subjects hold traditional rights (fueros) where these survive; Bourbon centralization erodes regional protections. Property rights strengthen for the propertied; the landless lack legal protection against eviction. Compliance burden falls unevenly; guild regulations and licensing requirements constrain urban crafts.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Noble privilege provides de facto immunity; elite offenders escape justice through connections and sanctuary. The Inquisition's decline reduces religious persecution but leaves institutional impunity unaddressed. Unredressed grievance accumulates among landless laborers and subordinate regions.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Spanish Enlightenment (Ilustracion) imports French and Italian ideas within limits. Feijoo's Teatro Critico Universal (1726-1740) popularizes empiricism and skepticism toward superstition. Natural history, political economy, and agricultural science constitute active research fronts. The paradigm emphasizes useful knowledge over metaphysical speculation.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Royal factories (tapestries, porcelain, armaments) introduce foreign techniques. The Royal Botanical Garden (1755) and Mining School (1777) train specialists. Catalan textile producers adopt spinning innovations. Applied method advances in surveying, cartography, and civil engineering for road construction.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ University reform attempts modernization; the expulsion of Jesuits (1767) disrupts secondary education but opens institutional space for reform. Primary schools expand under municipal authority. The Colegios Mayores lose their aristocratic monopoly. Literacy and numeracy spread among urban middling groups but the rural majority remains excluded.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Scholasticism declines in universities; the new science displaces Aristotelian natural philosophy. Inquisitorial censorship retards reception of some European advances but does not halt the transition. The knowledge stock grows; transmission improves through academies and periodicals.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Imperial memory persists; the Habsburg past figures as golden age now declined. Catholic identity remains central; Spain understands itself as defender of the faith. The Black Legend (foreign anti-Spanish propaganda) shapes defensive cultural posture. Regional identities (Catalan, Basque) survive but lack print expression.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ The Bourbon dynasty legitimates through administrative competence and reform: efficient government, economic improvement, enlightened progress. The language of public utility (utilidad publica) permeates official discourse. Catholic legitimation continues alongside rationalist modernization.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Neoclassicism displaces Baroque extravagance in official architecture; the Prado, Botanical Garden, and Puerta de Alcala exemplify Bourbon Madrid. Goya spans the period, from tapestry cartoons to royal portraits. Printed books adopt cleaner typography; the Royal Printing Press (Imprenta Real) sets standards. Material culture becomes plainer, more functional among elites.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Baroque religious intensity fades; Enlightenment taste finds excess distasteful. Religious ceremony continues but loses affective power among educated elites. The Counter-Reformation synthesis exhausts itself; no new cultural synthesis replaces it before the revolutionary crisis.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Catholic cosmology frames existence; Providence governs history. Among ilustrados, deism and rationalism erode providential certainty without open rejection. The educated hold faith alongside skepticism; the popular majority retains traditional piety. Fatalism coexists with reform optimism.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mass attendance, confession, and feast-day observance structure the calendar. Pilgrimages continue; popular devotions to saints and the Virgin persist. Elite participation becomes more formal, less fervent. The gap between official religion and popular piety widens.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The Church remains wealthy and institutionally powerful; bishops wield significant influence. Bourbon regalism asserts Crown authority over ecclesiastical appointments and revenues. The Jesuits' expulsion (1767) removes the most intellectually active order. Doctrinal orthodoxy persists but enforcement softens. The Inquisition declines; its last execution occurs in 1781.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Catholicism remains unchallenged domestically; Protestantism does not penetrate. Colonial missions continue in America. The cosmology's reach contracts among educated elites who absorb Enlightenment skepticism through foreign publications and travel.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Ilustrado critique of superstition, miracles, and clerical excess introduces disenchantment among the educated minority. The sacred does not disappear but loses explanatory power in elite worldviews. Popular religion remains vital but increasingly diverges from elite sensibility. The spiritual need persists without full re-anchoring; the gap between enlightened and popular spirituality widens as a standing tension unresolved by century's end.
