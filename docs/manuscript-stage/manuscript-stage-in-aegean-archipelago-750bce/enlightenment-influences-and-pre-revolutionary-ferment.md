# __1,700 CE – 1,821 CE__ — Enlightenment Influences and Pre-Revolutionary Ferment

__Summary:__ Greek society under Ottoman rule experiences a slow cultural awakening as Phanariot elites, diaspora merchants, and European-educated intellectuals channel Enlightenment ideas into a nascent national consciousness; manuscript culture persists in monasteries and schools while printing presses in Venice, Vienna, and Bucharest multiply Greek-language books, preparing the informational infrastructure for revolution. The single most causal factor is the diaspora commercial network that funds schools, printing, and the circulation of revolutionary texts beyond Ottoman censorship.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Aegean archipelago remains fragmented across islands, coastal plains, and mountain villages, with the Greek mainland divided between Ottoman administrative districts (eyalets and sanjaks); movement depends on coastal shipping and pack routes over difficult terrain, while Constantinople, Thessaloniki, and the Ionian islands under Venetian rule until 1797 serve as the primary nodes; the diaspora extends the Greek world to Vienna, Odessa, Trieste, and Venice, creating a spatial network that bypasses Ottoman restrictions.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Little Ice Age continues with cooler winters and occasional drought years affecting grain harvests; the Mediterranean climate remains stable enough for olive, vine, and grain cultivation, but subsistence margins are thin in mountainous areas; no catastrophic climate events mark the period, though local famines occur intermittently.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Seismic activity affects the region episodically; piracy declines as Ottoman and European navies assert control, though corsairs persist in the eastern Aegean; plague outbreaks continue to strike periodically, including severe visitations in the 1710s and 1780s; the Russo-Turkish wars (1768–1774, 1787–1792) bring military devastation to coastal regions and the Peloponnese during the Orlov Revolt (1770).

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ The region lacks significant mineral wealth; timber is depleted in accessible areas; agricultural land is limited by terrain; olive oil, currants, and wine constitute the primary exportable products; water power drives local mills, but the energy regime remains pre-industrial, dependent on human and animal muscle, wood fuel, and wind for shipping.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Pre-industrial waste volumes remain modest; urban areas like Constantinople, Thessaloniki, and Smyrna accumulate refuse and effluent in traditional patterns; no industrial pollution; the landscape shows centuries of deforestation and soil erosion in heavily cultivated areas, but no acute environmental crisis marks the period.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Plague remains endemic, with significant outbreaks in 1713, 1759, and 1781–1783 affecting Greek populations; smallpox, typhus, and dysentery contribute to high infant and child mortality; life expectancy remains low by modern standards, probably under forty years; urban areas suffer higher mortality than isolated villages.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ The Greek population subsists on a Mediterranean diet of bread, olives, legumes, vegetables, and occasional fish or meat; nutritional adequacy varies by region and class; merchant families and Phanariot elites enjoy better fare; peasants in mountainous areas face seasonal scarcity; endemic malnutrition affects growth and vitality among the poor.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Medical care remains traditional, combining Greek folk remedies, Ottoman practices, and monastic hospices; the Patriarchate and monasteries maintain infirmaries; wealthy families may access European-trained physicians in major cities; most Greeks rely on local healers, midwives, and religious healing; no systematic public health infrastructure exists.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Marriage remains early and nearly universal; the Orthodox Church regulates marriage and prohibits divorce; fertility is high but offset by high infant mortality; extended families and village kinship networks structure pairing; dowry practices remain central to marriage negotiation among propertied classes.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ The Greek population gradually recovers from seventeenth-century declines, estimated at roughly 2–3 million by 1800 within Ottoman territories; migration to diaspora centers (Vienna, Odessa, Venice) drains some population but creates wealth-generating networks; population density remains low in mountainous areas, higher in fertile plains and coastal towns.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ High mortality leaves many orphans and widows; extended families absorb dependents; the Church and monasteries provide some charitable support; the aged and infirm rely on family networks; no state welfare exists; the dependency load is carried within traditional structures without overwhelming them.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Most Greeks remain peasant cultivators producing grain, olives, and vines for subsistence and local exchange; landlords (both Ottoman and Greek) extract rents and tithes; fishing supplements diet in coastal areas; subsistence is precarious but not catastrophically threatened except during war or plague; household pooling and village reciprocity buffer shortfalls.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Energy derives from human and animal labor, wood fuel, water mills, and wind-powered shipping; no industrial machinery exists; the Greek merchant marine expands significantly, representing a major concentration of wind-powered transport capacity; per-capita energy availability remains typical of pre-industrial agrarian societies.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Peasant labor is organized by household and village; corvee obligations to Ottoman authorities are lighter than under earlier regimes; wage labor exists in ports and commercial centers; merchant enterprises employ clerks, sailors, and agents; slavery is rare among Greeks though it persists in Ottoman practice; the labor relation is predominantly familial and customary rather than coerced.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Agricultural surplus is modest, extracted by landlords and Ottoman tax collectors; handicraft production (textiles, leather, metalwork) serves local markets; the merchant marine generates significant commercial surplus by carrying trade across the Mediterranean and Black Sea; Greek merchants accumulate capital in diaspora centers, creating the financial base for schools and printing.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Grain storage in villages and monasteries provides some buffer against harvest failure; merchant families hold liquid assets in commercial networks; no banking system exists, though bills of exchange circulate among merchants; reserves are thin and vulnerability to famine, war, and taxation remains high.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Greek merchants dominate Aegean and Black Sea shipping by the late eighteenth century; trade flows through Smyrna, Constantinople, Thessaloniki, Odessa, Trieste, and Venice; goods include grain, wine, olive oil, currants, cotton, and manufactured imports from Europe; commercial networks create channels for book distribution and revolutionary correspondence alongside merchandise.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Commerce operates through bills of exchange, credit among merchant families, and metallic currency (Ottoman and European coins); no paper currency exists; merchant networks function as informal credit systems; capital accumulates in diaspora centers and flows back to fund schools, churches, and eventually revolutionary activities.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ The Ottoman state extracts taxes through tax-farming (iltizam), a system prone to abuse; local notables (kodjabashis) mediate between villages and Ottoman authorities; the Church collects fees for sacraments and tithes from peasants; merchant wealth escapes most Ottoman taxation by operating through diaspora networks; little surplus returns as public goods.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Wealth concentrates among Phanariot families in Constantinople, diaspora merchants, and local notables; most Greeks remain poor peasants; merchant wealth is held in ships, goods, and credit rather than land; wealth defense involves dispersing assets across diaspora centers and avoiding Ottoman confiscation through inconspicuous accumulation and community solidarity.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Pre-industrial waste remains organic and dispersed; no industrial pollution; commercial activities generate modest refuse in ports; financial waste (bad debt, failed ventures) is absorbed within merchant networks without systemic crisis.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ The extended family remains the primary social unit; inheritance follows Byzantine-influenced patterns with division among sons and dowries for daughters; patronymic lineages structure village society; the Orthodox Church regulates marriage, inheritance, and family morality; kinship networks extend into diaspora communities, creating transnational family enterprises.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Greek communities maintain collective institutions including village councils, guilds in urban areas, and religious brotherhoods; the Patriarchate organizes the Greek millet, providing a quasi-governmental structure for Orthodox Christians; diaspora communities establish schools, churches, and charitable foundations; the Filiki Eteria (founded 1814) emerges as a secret revolutionary society linking these networks.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Social life centers on religious festivals, weddings, funerals, and seasonal celebrations; coffeehouses in towns provide spaces for male sociability and discussion; folk music and dance persist in village settings; the Carnival tradition survives in Greek areas; diaspora communities maintain Greek social customs while adopting some European practices.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Greek society is stratified into Phanariot elites (serving Ottoman administration), merchant families, clergy, village notables (kodjabashis), artisans, and peasants; the millet system places Greeks under Patriarchal jurisdiction for personal status; social mobility exists through commerce and education; the emerging intelligentsia forms a new status group linking merchants, clergy, and Western-educated scholars.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ The Ecumenical Patriarchate in Constantinople administers Greek Orthodox Christians, running courts for personal status, schools, and charitable institutions; local notables mediate with Ottoman authorities; the system is corrupt and oligarchic but functional; diaspora communities govern themselves through merchant councils and elected officers; no Greek state apparatus exists, though the revolutionary period will create provisional governments.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Greeks lack military forces; the klephts (mountain bandits) represent an armed tradition outside Ottoman control, romanticized as national heroes; armatoles serve as Ottoman-authorized militia; the Maniots and Souliots maintain semi-autonomous warrior communities; diaspora Greeks serve in Russian and European armies; the balance of force overwhelmingly favors the Ottoman state until revolt fragments it.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Banditry, vendetta, and localized violence persist in mountainous areas; the Ottoman system manages Greek grievances through the millet apparatus and local cooptation; the Orlov Revolt (1770) is brutally suppressed, creating martyrs and refugees; revolutionary ferment builds through secret societies rather than open disorder; social friction is channeled into diasporic emigration and commercial competition rather than rebellion until 1821.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Greek sentiment combines religious identity, historical memory of Byzantium and classical glory, resentment of Ottoman rule, and hope kindled by the French Revolution and Napoleonic upheavals; the mood shifts from resigned coexistence toward active nationalism over the period; the diaspora and intelligentsia cultivate patriotic fervor; trust networks deepen through commercial, educational, and conspiratorial ties.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Patriarchs, Phanariot princes, wealthy merchants, and village notables provide traditional leadership; new figures emerge from the intelligentsia: Adamantios Korais, the philologist promoting language reform from Paris; Rigas Feraios, the revolutionary martyr executed in 1798; military leaders like the klephts and future revolutionaries await their moment; charismatic authority increasingly attaches to the national cause rather than traditional office.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Ottoman legitimacy rests on power and the millet system's functional autonomy; Greeks feel no allegiance to the sultan beyond prudential submission; legitimacy inheres in the Orthodox Church and increasingly in the imagined Greek nation; the idea of a restored Greek state gains adherents among the educated; peasant allegiance remains local, familial, and religious rather than abstractly national.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Revolutionary will coalesces slowly through the period; the failure of the Orlov Revolt (1770) teaches caution; the French Revolution inspires; the Filiki Eteria (1814) organizes conspiratorial networks across the diaspora; by 1821 sufficient will has accumulated among merchants, intellectuals, and military adventurers to launch rebellion; peasant participation will follow local leadership and immediate grievances.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Greeks look to Russia as Orthodox protector and potential liberator; the Russo-Turkish wars raise and dash hopes; Napoleonic France briefly inspires; Britain and other powers represent potential allies or obstacles; the diaspora maintains ties across European states; Greek strategy depends on great-power intervention, a calculation that will prove accurate by 1827.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ Ottoman repression after the Orlov Revolt creates refugees and embitters survivors; fatalism and resignation persist among those who see no prospect for change; the generation of 1770 dies disappointed; but the diaspora and the young inherit their grievances without their exhaustion; by 1820 will has regenerated sufficiently for action.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Greek remains the language of Greek communities, though regional dialects vary; literacy is limited to clergy, merchants, and the educated; the Church conducts services in liturgical Greek; Korais and other reformers debate language standardization (katharevousa vs. demotic); the shared code of Orthodoxy and Greek identity unites communities even where literacy is thin.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Information travels by ship, caravan, and personal correspondence; merchant networks transmit news alongside goods; the diaspora accelerates information flow through commercial correspondence and printed matter shipped from Venice, Vienna, and later Bucharest; revolutionary ideas spread through secret society networks, manuscript letters, and clandestine meetings; Ottoman censorship is evaded through diaspora channels.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Manuscripts persist in monasteries, patriarchal archives, and private collections; Mount Athos, Patmos, and other monastic centers preserve Byzantine-era codices and produce new copies; printed books from diaspora presses accumulate in schools and private libraries; the revolutionary period will generate proclamations, newspapers, and administrative records that transition the information regime toward print.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Ottoman authorities censor seditious material, but enforcement is uneven and diaspora presses operate beyond reach; the Church enforces religious orthodoxy; Enlightenment ideas circulate among the educated despite efforts at suppression; Rigas's revolutionary pamphlets and Korais's philological works represent the new discourse; oral tradition carries folk songs celebrating klephts and resistance.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ The reading public is small but influential: clergy, merchants, teachers, and their families; schools multiply under diaspora patronage, creating new readers; revolutionary texts like Rigas's "Thourios" (war hymn) achieve wide oral circulation even among the illiterate; the amplification system combines print, manuscript copying, and oral transmission.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Information quality varies; rumors and false hopes circulate (expectations of Russian intervention repeatedly disappointed); commercial intelligence is relatively reliable; revolutionary communications use codes and trusted couriers; the overall information environment is noisy but the key message—national revival is possible—propagates effectively.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Greek moral sense combines Orthodox Christianity, Byzantine tradition, and folk custom; justice is conceived in religious terms (God's judgment, saintly intercession) and communal honor; Ottoman rule is felt as unjust subjugation; the Enlightenment introduces natural rights discourse among the educated; Rigas's revolutionary proclamations invoke both Christian liberation and Enlightenment principles.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Greeks live under multiple legal regimes: Ottoman law for criminal and property matters, Church canon law for personal status, and local custom for daily disputes; codification is minimal; the Patriarchate administers Orthodox personal law; village councils mediate local disputes; the revolutionary period will see attempts to create Greek national law.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Church courts handle marriage, inheritance, and religious disputes; Ottoman courts address cases involving Muslims or state interests; village elders arbitrate local conflicts; justice is personal, negotiated, and often corrupt; blood feud persists in some areas; sanctions range from fines and shaming to Ottoman imprisonment or execution for serious offenses.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Greeks possess limited rights as non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis); they may practice religion and govern personal status through the millet but face restrictions on dress, testimony, and public display; the compliance burden includes the cizye (head tax) and vulnerability to arbitrary exactions; Enlightenment discourse begins to articulate rights claims that will structure revolutionary demands.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Ottoman officials and powerful Muslims enjoy effective impunity against Greek complaints; Church and communal leaders may abuse their positions; klephts and bandits evade justice; the system generates accumulated grievances without adequate redress mechanisms; revolutionary violence will seek to settle accounts.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ The Greek intellectual world is transformed by contact with European Enlightenment; Korais in Paris applies philological method to Greek texts; others study medicine, science, and philosophy in European universities; classical Greek texts, long preserved in manuscript, are now printed and circulated; the paradigm shifts from Byzantine Orthodox learning toward European rationalism while retaining reverence for ancient Greece.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Practical knowledge includes traditional agriculture, seamanship, and handicrafts; merchant skills in accounting, correspondence, and navigation are transmitted through apprenticeship; European technical knowledge (printing, medicine, military science) enters through diaspora contacts; no systematic technical education exists, but practical competence suffices for commercial success.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Schools multiply under diaspora and Church patronage; the Patriarchal Academy in Constantinople, schools in Smyrna, Chios, Ioannina, and elsewhere teach Greek, classics, and some modern subjects; literacy spreads among merchant families and urban residents; the knowledge stock expands through imported books; skill distribution remains uneven, concentrated among elites and the Church.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Traditional Byzantine learning persists alongside new Enlightenment imports; no sharp rupture occurs; manuscript copying continues even as print expands; some traditional skills (icon painting, liturgical chant) are maintained; the intellectual challenge is synthesis rather than loss—integrating classical heritage, Orthodox tradition, and modern European thought into a coherent national culture.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Greek identity rests on Orthodox Christianity, Byzantine imperial memory, and increasingly on classical Hellenic heritage; the "Great Idea" of national restoration begins to take shape; folk memory preserves stories of Byzantine glory and Ottoman conquest; klephtic songs celebrate resistance; the intelligentsia constructs a narrative linking ancient, Byzantine, and modern Greeks into continuous national history.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ The emerging national ideology claims Greeks as heirs to Pericles and Plato, temporarily subjugated but destined for restoration; Enlightenment concepts of popular sovereignty and natural rights blend with Orthodox providentialist narratives; Rigas's vision extends to a multi-ethnic Balkan federation under Greek cultural leadership; the legitimating narrative is revolutionary, directed against Ottoman rule and implicitly against Phanariot collaboration.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Greek artistic expression includes Byzantine-style icons and church architecture, folk art, and handicrafts; diaspora wealth enables neoclassical influences in some church construction; folk music, poetry, and dance flourish; dress combines traditional elements with Ottoman and European influences; material culture reflects the layered identity—Orthodox, regional, and increasingly national.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Traditional forms retain vitality in village settings; Byzantine ecclesiastical art continues without marked exhaustion; the new national cultural synthesis is energizing rather than stale; folk traditions are revalued as expressions of authentic Greekness; the cultural challenge is to create new national forms, not to cope with exhausted ones.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Orthodox Christianity provides the dominant cosmological frame; God governs history; the Church mediates salvation; saints intercede; the fall of Constantinople was divine punishment, but restoration may be divine redemption; Enlightenment rationalism influences some intellectuals but does not displace popular piety; existential hope increasingly attaches to national liberation as sacred cause.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Orthodox liturgy, fasting cycles, pilgrimage, and icon veneration structure religious life; the Church calendar organizes time; sacraments mark life transitions; monks on Mount Athos and elsewhere maintain intense devotional practice; folk religion includes local saints, healing shrines, and protective rituals; religious practice is communal and identity-forming, binding Greeks across regional and class divisions.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The Ecumenical Patriarchate in Constantinople leads the Greek millet, though its authority is compromised by Ottoman patronage and internal corruption; monasteries (especially Mount Athos) preserve spiritual authority; the Church hierarchy is conservative, often opposing revolutionary agitation; clergy are divided between those loyal to the existing order and those sympathetic to national revival.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Orthodoxy remains unchallenged as the Greek religious frame; no significant conversion to Islam or Catholicism occurs; Protestant influences are negligible; Enlightenment deism attracts some intellectuals but does not spread widely; the spiritual map is stable, with Orthodoxy providing the religious ground for national identity.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Enlightenment rationalism introduces some secularizing pressure among the educated; the Church's collaboration with Ottoman authority generates cynicism; but widespread disenchantment does not occur; the national cause itself acquires sacred coloring, channeling spiritual energy into revolutionary commitment; martyrs like Rigas become quasi-religious figures; the residue is re-anchored in national-religious synthesis rather than left as void.
