# __1258 CE – 1534 CE — Ilkhanid, Jalairid, and Turkmen Successor States__

__Summary:__ The aftermath of the Mongol catastrophe sees partial recovery under Ilkhanid rule as the Mongol khans convert to Islam and attempt to restore irrigation and administration, followed by fragmentation under Jalairid, Turkmen (Qara Qoyunlu and Aq Qoyunlu), and Timurid competitors, while Baghdad devolves from world capital to provincial town contested among pastoral-military powers — a trajectory of incomplete recovery and renewed destruction that leaves the Tigris-Euphrates basin a weakened prize for the Ottoman-Safavid contest that will determine its next three centuries.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Baghdad retains its location at the rivers' conjunction but no longer commands the region — political centers shift to Tabriz, Sultaniyya, and later Samarkand and Herat under the Timurids; the twin rivers still provide irrigation potential and transport, but the network connecting them to productive hinterland has been devastated; the pilgrimage routes to Najaf and Karbala gain importance as Shi'i holy cities draw visitors.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The semi-arid climate persists unchanged; Little Ice Age cooling may affect some periods, though documentary evidence is thin; the irrigation system's sensitivity to climate variation is now catastrophic rather than manageable, since buffer capacity has been destroyed.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Flooding from uncontrolled river courses, plague epidemics recurring with devastating frequency (the Black Death arrives in the 1340s), and locust infestations compound existing degradation; Tamerlane's campaigns (1393, 1401) inflict a second catastrophe approaching the 1258 destruction in magnitude.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ The resource base remains unchanged — alluvial soil potentially productive, no local timber or minerals — but the capacity to exploit it has collapsed; abandoned irrigation districts revert to steppe; the date-palm economy of the south survives better than the grain agriculture of the Sawad.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Reduced population and production generate less waste; abandoned agricultural land ceases salination but also ceases production; the physical environment partially recovers in areas no longer intensively used, while urban areas lack the organization for systematic waste management.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ The Black Death (arriving 1347-1349 CE) devastates an already weakened population, killing perhaps one-third of survivors; subsequent plague recurrence becomes endemic, with major outbreaks documented every few decades; malaria persists in the marshes; smallpox and other diseases circulate.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ The collapse of irrigated agriculture reduces food security; date cultivation in the south provides a survival baseline; pastoral populations depending on animal products may fare better than settled cultivators; famine accompanies sieges and campaigns, as during Tamerlane's devastations.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Medical knowledge persists in transmitted texts but institutional capacity — bimaristans, court physicians — contracts severely; individual practitioners continue, but the systematic medical culture of the Abbasid period cannot be sustained.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Marriage and family patterns continue under Islamic law; population losses create conditions for polygyny among surviving males; slave imports from the Caucasus and Central Asia supply both labor and military recruitment; the demographic trough slows recovery for generations.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Baghdad's population may fall to 10,000-50,000 during the worst periods, a fraction of its Abbasid peak; the Sawad countryside thins to sparse pastoral occupation in many areas; recovery is slow and repeatedly interrupted by plague, warfare, and Tamerlane's campaigns; by 1534 CE, population remains far below ancient levels.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ The reduced population includes disproportionate elderly and young as working-age adults suffer highest mortality in warfare and plague; religious establishments and military garrisons require support from a contracted base; pastoral nomads represent a growing proportion of total population.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Agriculture retreats from irrigated grain cultivation toward rain-fed farming in the north and date cultivation in the south; pastoral nomadism expands into former agricultural zones; subsistence margins remain precarious throughout; urban populations depend on attenuated supply chains.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and animal labor continue as primary energy sources; water mills persist where canals function; the energy available for production declines proportionally with population and infrastructure loss; fuel scarcity intensifies in denuded landscapes.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Free peasant cultivation continues where irrigation permits; pastoral nomads organize around tribal structures; urban craft production persists at reduced scale; military slavery (mamluk systems) provides soldiers for successor states; corvee labor for canal repair cannot be mobilized at Abbasid scales.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Tax revenues collapse — one Ilkhanid survey reportedly shows revenues at one-tenth of late Abbasid levels; craft production continues for reduced markets; long-distance trade persists but shifts away from Baghdad; surplus available for investment, patronage, or military expansion remains minimal.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ State reserves are minimal; the successor states extract what they can but cannot accumulate buffers; waqf endowments preserve some assets but cannot substitute for productive investment; resilience against shock approaches zero during the worst periods.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ The Mongol Peace (Pax Mongolica) initially reconnects Iraq to overland routes across Asia, and Ilkhanid policies promote trade; after Ilkhanid collapse, the routes fragment and shift; Indian Ocean trade routes through Egypt and the Gulf marginalize Iraq; regional trade continues but at reduced volume.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Currency circulates in multiple standards as successor states issue their own coins; the Mongol paper money experiment fails catastrophically from inflation; precious metals remain the reliable store of value; credit networks thin with commercial contraction.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ The Ilkhanids attempt tax reform under Ghazan Khan (r. 1295-1304), converting arbitrary extraction to systematized assessment; successor states revert to predatory extraction; military elites take what remains; redistribution beyond military pay and religious endowments is minimal.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Movable wealth proves more defensible than land in an era of recurring invasion; merchant and religious families preserve assets through waqf conversion and geographic dispersion; pastoral wealth in herds recovers faster than agricultural wealth in damaged irrigation.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Reduced production generates less waste; systematic waste management is beyond administrative capacity; abandoned districts accumulate debris and then slowly revert to natural landscapes.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Islamic family law continues to structure kinship; tribal organization gains salience as state power weakens; pastoral nomad kinship patterns (patrilineal clans, tribal confederations) become more important to social organization; sayyid families claiming descent from the Prophet retain prestige.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Sufi orders provide the most robust associational structure, offering spiritual community, social support, and sometimes political organization; neighborhood and mosque-based communities persist in urban areas; tribal solidarity organizes pastoral populations; Christian and Jewish communities contract but survive.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Elite culture continues in attenuated form at successor-state courts; Tamerlane notably patronizes scholars and artists despite his military devastations; popular festivities and religious celebrations provide social cohesion; the contraction of urban life reduces venues for sophisticated sociability.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Military-pastoral elites — Mongol, Turkmen, and Timurid commanders — dominate the social apex; the ulama maintain their position through religious authority; merchants survive where trade persists; peasants and urban workers occupy the base; pastoral nomads occupy ambiguous positions between these strata.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ The Ilkhanids initially attempt systematic administration using Mongol and Persian practices; Ghazan Khan's reforms represent the high point; after Ilkhanid collapse (1335 CE), administrative capacity fragments among competing successor states; the Jalairids, Qara Qoyunlu, Aq Qoyunlu, and Timurids all lack stable administrative structures; by 1534 CE, Ottoman conquest will bring superior administrative capacity.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Mongol and then Turkmen cavalry dominate warfare; successor states maintain tribal military forces supplemented by mamluks; siege warfare and destruction of cities characterize the era; fortification of towns intensifies but cannot withstand determined assault; the region becomes a contested zone between larger powers.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Endemic warfare among successor states characterizes the entire period; Tamerlane's campaigns (1393, 1401) inflict catastrophic destruction on Baghdad; Bedouin raiding intensifies in areas of state weakness; banditry disrupts trade routes; the institutional decay begun in the late Abbasid period reaches its nadir.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Trauma from repeated catastrophes — the 1258 sack, plague, Tamerlane's devastations — pervades collective memory; fatalism and Sufi spirituality offer psychological resources; the population that survives has witnessed horrors that shape generational outlook; hope attaches to dreams of restoration rather than present flourishing.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Strong military leaders — Mongol khans, Turkmen tribal chiefs, Tamerlane — exercise personal authority based on martial success; Ghazan Khan represents an attempt at enlightened rule combining military power with administrative reform and Islamic piety; most successors lack either capability or longevity for constructive rule.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ The caliphate is gone (a shadow Abbasid caliphate continues in Mamluk Cairo but lacks connection to Iraq); Mongol legitimacy rests on conquest right and later on Islamic conversion; Turkmen chiefs claim tribal prerogative; Tamerlane claims descent from Chinggis Khan (through a fictive marriage) and championship of Islam; legitimacy remains contested and unstable.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Collective will fragments among competing tribal factions; no power achieves durable unification of the Tigris-Euphrates basin during this period; the population lacks capacity for coordinated resistance to invading forces; will exhausts itself in survival rather than construction.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Iraq becomes a contested zone rather than a power center; the Ilkhanids engage with Mamluk Egypt, Mongol khanates in Central Asia, and European powers seeking Mongol alliance against the Mamluks; successor states navigate among larger powers; by the fifteenth century, Ottoman and Safavid expansion will make Iraq a frontier zone between empires.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ The cumulative effect of catastrophe exhausts political imagination; population survives rather than thrives; resignation to foreign rule becomes the norm; only with Ottoman and then Safavid conquest will external powers impose stable (if despotic) order; spent will persists as the default condition.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Arabic remains the language of religious scholarship; Persian becomes the prestige literary and administrative language under Mongol and Turkmen rulers; Mongolian and Turkish function as military and court languages; literacy contracts with institutional decline but persists among ulama and administrators.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ The yam postal system of the Mongol empire provides rapid communication during Ilkhanid rule; after Ilkhanid collapse, formal communication systems atrophy; trade and pilgrimage routes carry information but without systematic organization; news travels by rumor and merchant report.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Manuscript production continues but at reduced scale; some Ilkhanid-period historical writing preserves crucial records (Rashid al-Din's Jami al-Tawarikh); religious texts continue to be copied; libraries survive where cities survive but suffer destruction in sieges; the manuscript heritage thins with each devastation.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ No state exercises systematic censorship; Sunni-Shi'i tensions shape what can be said in different settings; under Ilkhanids, religious diversity is initially tolerated, then Sunni Islam prevails after Ghazan's conversion; the range of discourse depends on local power configurations.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Madrasa education continues where institutions survive; Sufi networks transmit spiritual and practical knowledge; scholarly reputation travels through the diminished but still connected Islamic world; local audiences for manuscript production contract with population and prosperity.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Historical knowledge degrades as records are destroyed; surviving chronicles must be read critically for bias; the scholarly apparatus for verification thins; oral tradition fills gaps but introduces distortion; what is known about this period is fragmentary and contested.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Islamic moral principles persist as the framework for judgment; the contrast between Islamic justice ideals and the reality of conquest, massacre, and extraction generates moral dissonance; Sufi ethics emphasize acceptance of suffering and interior cultivation.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Sharia continues as the framework for personal and commercial law; the Mongol yasa (customary law) initially competes but fades after Islamization; the four madhabs persist, with different predominance in different areas; legal evolution slows as scholarly production declines.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Qadis continue to adjudicate where courts function; military rulers often bypass legal process; the weak have minimal recourse; massacre and collective punishment characterize conquest; regular legal process requires political stability that rarely obtains.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Dhimmi protections theoretically continue but enforcement depends on local rulers; Christians (especially Nestorians) suffer under some Mongol policies despite initial favor; Jews persist in reduced numbers; ordinary subjects' rights depend entirely on their rulers' disposition.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Conquerors operate with total impunity; Tamerlane's massacres go unpunished; military elites take what they wish; accumulated wrong reaches civilizational scale; the gap between justice owed and delivered becomes a chasm that only divine judgment could bridge.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Original intellectual production declines severely; the great synthesis of Islamic philosophy, theology, and science largely concludes before the period; Ibn Khaldun (writing in North Africa but reflecting on the Islamic world's trajectory) represents the period's most original thinker; astronomy and mathematics continue in Iran and Central Asia rather than Iraq.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Practical knowledge for irrigation, agriculture, and crafts survives where practitioners survive; military technology advances — siege warfare, gunpowder weapons gradually appear — but Iraq is a consumer rather than producer of innovation; applied knowledge persists through apprenticeship rather than institutional transmission.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Madrasas continue operating in surviving cities; mosque education provides elementary instruction; the knowledge stock contracts through manuscript destruction and death of scholars; skill distribution narrows as the economy contracts; literacy becomes rarer.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ The period is one of net knowledge loss; manuscripts destroyed in the 1258 sack, in plague, in Tamerlane's devastations are gone forever; some texts survive only in copies made before or after outside Iraq; technique decays as practitioners die without successors; the intellectual capital of the Abbasid golden age diminishes with each generation.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ The trauma of 1258 becomes foundational to Iraqi Islamic memory; nostalgia for the Abbasid golden age persists; Shi'i identity strengthens around the holy cities of Najaf and Karbala; Arab, Persian, and Turkic ethnic identities coexist within Islamic civilization; the memory of former greatness haunts the diminished present.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Mongol rulers after conversion claim to be protectors and revivers of Islam; Tamerlane presents himself as a renewer of religion while destroying Muslim cities; Turkmen chiefs claim tribal and Islamic warrants; legitimating narratives shift with each conqueror but lack stable foundation; the broken caliphate leaves no legitimate successor.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Persian miniature painting flourishes under Mongol and Timurid patronage, though centered in Tabriz, Samarkand, and Herat rather than Baghdad; calligraphy and manuscript illumination continue; architectural production shifts to the successor states' capitals; material culture in Iraq simplifies with economic contraction.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ The forms of Abbasid civilization — court ceremony, literary conventions, architectural styles — survive as fragments rather than living wholes; meaning attaches to religious practice and Sufi devotion rather than courtly or urban culture; the civilizational project has not ended but has been radically diminished.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Islamic cosmology persists; the problem of theodicy intensifies — why has God permitted such suffering to befall the faithful?; Sufi emphasis on interior experience and divine mystery provides answers unavailable in rationalist theology; fatalism deepens; hope locates in the afterlife rather than worldly restoration.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ The five pillars continue to structure religious life; Sufi devotional practices expand and intensify; pilgrimage to Najaf, Karbala, and the tombs of Sufi saints becomes central to popular piety; ta'ziya commemorations of Husayn's martyrdom at Karbala develop; ritual provides structure amid chaos.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The ulama persist as the bearers of religious authority; Sufi orders institutionalize further; madrasas continue where cities survive; the caliphate's absence leaves religious authority dispersed among scholars; sectarian boundaries between Sunni and Shi'i harden, anticipating the Ottoman-Safavid contest.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Islam is fully established; the question is which form — Sunni, Shi'i, Sufi synthesis — will predominate; Shi'i identity strengthens in southern Iraq around the holy cities; Turkmen rulers bring their own devotional traditions; the stage is set for the Safavid-Ottoman religious-political contest that will determine the region's sectarian map.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ The sacred does not lose its grip — if anything, suffering intensifies religious orientation; the question is not whether to believe but how to understand suffering within belief; Sufi spirituality provides the most satisfying answer, locating meaning in interior transformation rather than worldly outcome; the sacred persists through catastrophe, providing the only stable orientation in a world of recurring destruction.
