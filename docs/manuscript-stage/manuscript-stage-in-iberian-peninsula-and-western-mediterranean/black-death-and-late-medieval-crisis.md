# __1350 CE – 1400 CE__ — Black Death and Late Medieval Crisis

__Summary:__ The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean in crisis mode, as plague, pogroms, and political instability devastate population and disrupt the manuscript culture that had flourished under high medieval conditions; the binding constraint remains the Manuscript Stage, with written records essential for administering shrunken kingdoms, documenting legal succession, and preserving learning through catastrophic mortality, yet production contracts sharply as scriptoria lose personnel and patronage collapses.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The peninsula's geography remains fixed — the Meseta's interior plateau, the Ebro and Guadalquivir valleys, the Pyrenean barrier, and the Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts — but plague disrupts the spatial networks that connected them; trade routes contract as commerce falters and towns depopulate, increasing effective friction between regions that had been well-linked under the high medieval commercial expansion.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Little Ice Age onset brings cooler, wetter conditions across Iberia; the favorable climate of the Medieval Warm Period that had supported agricultural expansion gives way to greater interannual variability, contributing to harvest failures that compound plague mortality and economic disruption.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ The biological hazard of plague dwarfs all other exposures; the pandemic reaches Iberia via Mediterranean shipping routes in 1348, spreading rapidly through port cities and along trade corridors; urban concentrations suffer catastrophic mortality while isolated rural areas escape more lightly.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Agricultural land remains abundant but labor-scarce; the dramatic population collapse leaves fields uncultivated, vineyards untended, and pastoral systems disrupted; mineral resources in the peninsula continue extraction at reduced levels as mining communities suffer mortality.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ The mass mortality generates unprecedented volumes of human remains; urban burial capacity is overwhelmed, with plague pits and emergency interments becoming necessary; the sanitary crisis compounds the epidemiological disaster, though reduced population ultimately lessens long-term waste loads.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Yersinia pestis devastates the peninsula from 1348 onward; mortality estimates range from thirty to sixty percent in affected areas, with recurrent waves in 1362–1363 and 1374–1375 preventing demographic recovery; the disease baseline shifts catastrophically upward, and plague becomes endemic in Iberian cities for generations.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Surviving populations paradoxically experience improved nutrition as labor scarcity raises wages and reduces competition for food resources; those who survive multiple plague waves show acquired resilience, though the overall biological vitality of the population is shattered by mortality.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Medical knowledge proves impotent against plague; Galenic theory attributes the pestilence to miasma and corrupted air, leading to ineffective fumigation and aromatic treatments; quarantine measures develop empirically but cannot halt transmission; the healing professions suffer proportional mortality, reducing care capacity.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Mortality disrupts marriage patterns as widows and widowers remarry rapidly; fertility may temporarily rise among survivors as remarriage rates increase and opportunities open for younger cohorts, but population replacement remains insufficient against recurring plague waves.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population collapse is catastrophic and sustained; Iberia's population falls from perhaps six million to three or four million, with urban areas suffering disproportionately; Barcelona, Valencia, and other commercial centers lose half or more of their inhabitants; density falls well below pre-plague carrying capacity.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Plague mortality is somewhat indiscriminate but hits productive adult cohorts heavily; the dependency ratio shifts as orphans multiply and family structures fragment; monastic communities that had provided care services suffer mortality, reducing the institutional capacity to absorb dependents.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Labor scarcity raises the subsistence floor for survivors; wages rise, rents fall, and peasant conditions improve materially even as the overall economy contracts; abandoned villages dot the landscape, but remaining settlements consolidate around better land and resources.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ The labor force that powered agricultural and artisanal production collapses; watermills and other capital equipment stand idle without operators; energy conversion contracts proportionally to population, though per-capita work output may rise among survivors with access to better tools and land.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Labor relations shift toward the worker as scarcity empowers survivors to demand better terms; seigneurial attempts to reimpose corvee and freeze wages meet resistance; the balance between coercion and compensation tilts toward compensation, though lords attempt legal enforcement of labor obligations.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Total output collapses with population; surplus generation falls catastrophically as both producers and consumers die; luxury production, including manuscript illumination and fine book production, contracts as patronage disappears and skilled artisans perish.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Accumulated wealth provides some cushion, but granaries and treasuries draw down rapidly; the fiscal capacity of crowns contracts as tax bases shrink; monasteries and cathedrals lose income from depopulated estates, reducing their capacity to maintain scriptoria and libraries.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Mediterranean commerce contracts sharply as plague spreads along trade routes; the Catalan-Aragonese commercial network that had linked Barcelona to Italy, North Africa, and the Levant shrinks; Castilian wool exports to Flanders decline; internal trade networks fray as towns depopulate.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Credit networks seize as debtors die and creditors face collection failures; Jewish moneylenders, already targeted by popular resentment, face violence in the 1391 pogroms partly driven by debt obligations survivors wished to extinguish; price instability reflects collapsing demand and disrupted supply.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Royal revenues collapse as the tax base contracts; the Castilian and Aragonese crowns struggle to maintain administrative capacity and military forces; fiscal crisis compounds political instability, driving succession conflicts and civil wars.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Mortality creates opportunities for wealth consolidation among survivors who inherit from multiple deceased relatives; land values fall but labor values rise; noble families face extinction of lineages, enabling cadet branches and new men to acquire estates.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Material waste decreases with economic contraction, but the biological waste of mass death creates acute disposal crises; abandoned properties and unclaimed goods create legal complications that generate documentary production even as other manuscript activity declines.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Family structures shatter under mortality; complex inheritance disputes arise as entire lineages die out or collateral heirs emerge; orphans and widows multiply; remarriage patterns shift rapidly; the documentary apparatus of wills, testaments, and succession records works intensively to process the mortality.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Guilds and confraternities lose members to mortality; mutual aid networks strain under unprecedented demands; some institutions collapse entirely while others consolidate surviving populations; charitable foundations face overwhelming need with reduced resources.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Public gatherings become vectors of contagion; festivals and markets are suspended or restricted; the dance of death motif enters popular culture, and the carnivalesque takes darker tones; sociability contracts as fear of contagion discourages assembly.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Social mobility accelerates as mortality creates vacancies in all strata; servile tenures weaken as lords compete for scarce labor; Jewish communities, already marginal, face scapegoating culminating in the 1391 pogroms that destroy major aljamas in Seville, Cordoba, Valencia, and Barcelona.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Royal administration strains under mortality among clerks and officials; record-keeping continues but with reduced personnel and capacity; the Castilian civil war (1366–1369) between Pedro I and the Trastamara pretender Henry compounds administrative disruption, while the Aragonese crown faces its own succession crises.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Military capacity declines with population; the Castilian civil war demonstrates how contracted forces still enable decisive conflict; mercenary companies (routiers) displaced from France by the Peace of Bretigny enter Iberia, adding to violence; the 1391 pogroms reveal breakdown of public order as royal authority fails to protect Jewish communities.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Social disorder reaches crisis levels; the pogroms of 1391 represent catastrophic failure of the coercive apparatus to maintain order; forced conversions create the converso population whose ambiguous status will generate conflict for generations; banditry and local violence increase as central authority weakens.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Terror and despair dominate public mood; the inexplicable and seemingly divine punishment of plague generates apocalyptic anxiety; trust collapses as neighbors become potential plague vectors; scapegoating of Jews and other marginal groups channels fear into violence.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Royal authority weakens under mortality and political crisis; Pedro I of Castile's brutal rule and eventual assassination (1369) by his half-brother Henry of Trastamara reflects legitimacy collapse; the Aragonese dynasty faces extinction crises; personal authority of surviving leaders rests on force more than consent.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Dynastic legitimacy fractures in Castile's civil war; Henry of Trastamara's usurpation creates a Castilian monarchy dependent on noble support and compromised by illegitimate origins; the Aragonese succession dispute (resolved at Caspe in 1412) reflects similar legitimacy fragmentation.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Political will fragments among competing factions; the Castilian civil war demonstrates how contested succession paralyzes collective action; the Aragonese crown similarly experiences reduced capacity for unified action; the post-plague political order lacks the confident expansionism of the high medieval Reconquista.

__External Relations & Posture:__ External ambitions contract; the Castilian civil war draws in English and French intervention as part of the Hundred Years' War; Granada gains respite as Christian kingdoms turn inward; the Aragonese Mediterranean empire faces challenges from Genoa and others with reduced capacity to respond.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ Exhaustion pervades post-plague society; the trauma of mass death, civil war, and pogrom leaves populations depleted of energy and will; resignation and fatalism replace the confident expansion of the high medieval period; the collective will settles into survival mode.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Latin remains the language of church, law, and learning; Castilian, Catalan, Aragonese, Portuguese, and other vernaculars continue their expansion into documentary use; literacy concentrations suffer mortality proportionally, reducing the population capable of encoding and decoding written communication.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Information channels contract with commercial networks; news of plague spreads rapidly along the same trade routes that carry infection; royal communication networks strain under reduced personnel; the velocity of official correspondence slows as administrative capacity contracts.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Archives survive plague but face reduced maintenance; cathedral and monastic libraries continue but with fewer personnel to copy, preserve, and organize manuscripts; some scriptoria close entirely; the accumulated documentary heritage of the high medieval period persists but ceases to grow at former rates.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Plague generates new genres of documentation — plague treatises, testament records, death registers — while the crisis narrative frames public discourse; explanatory frameworks range from astrological to theological, with Jewish poisoning accusations representing the darkest edge of plague discourse.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Public attention focuses on survival and the meaning of catastrophe; the danse macabre and memento mori themes spread through visual and literary culture; attention to learning and intellectual production declines as immediate crisis absorbs capacity.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Documentary production becomes essential for processing the mortality crisis — wills, inheritances, property transfers — while the informational infrastructure that processes these records strains under volume and reduced capacity; some records are lost to disruption; noise increases as administrative continuity breaks.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Plague shakes confidence in cosmic justice; the apparently random mortality challenges theodicy; scapegoating of Jews represents a morally catastrophic attempt to locate human agency behind divine punishment; the forced conversions and pogroms of 1391 mark moral collapse of the convivencia ideal.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Legal systems strain to process unprecedented volumes of succession and property disputes; sumptuary legislation attempts to restrain social mobility enabled by mortality; labor regulations try unsuccessfully to fix wages and bind workers to lords; legal development continues but addresses crisis conditions.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Courts face backlogs as normal processes of justice are overwhelmed by mortality-related disputes; succession litigation multiplies; the 1391 pogroms proceed with minimal judicial consequences, demonstrating failure of the legal order to protect minority populations.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Jewish rights of residence and protection collapse in 1391 as royal authority fails to enforce them; converso status creates new legal categories of ambiguous protection; peasant rights strengthen de facto as labor scarcity enables negotiation, though formal servile statuses persist.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Impunity prevails in the pogroms; perpetrators of mass violence against Jewish communities face minimal consequences; the Trastamara usurpation demonstrates how successful violence can establish new legitimacy; unredressed wrong accumulates as the judicial system loses capacity to impose consequences.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Plague treatises represent active inquiry into causes and responses, though within Galenic and astrological frameworks that prove inadequate; the University of Salamanca continues teaching but with reduced enrollment and faculty; intellectual production contracts but does not cease entirely.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Empirical development of quarantine measures represents practical knowledge generation outside theoretical frameworks; maritime quarantine practices that will become standard in Mediterranean ports begin in this period; medical technique remains ineffective against plague but sanitary knowledge accumulates.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ University enrollment declines; cathedral schools lose faculty; the transmission of specialized skills is interrupted as masters die without training successors; literacy rates may fall as educational institutions contract; the accumulated knowledge stock persists in libraries but ceases to grow.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Some specialized knowledge is lost through mortality; scribal traditions suffer as trained copyists die and apprentices are not trained; Hebrew learning in Iberia faces catastrophic disruption as Jewish communities are destroyed or converted; some manuscripts are lost to violence and disruption.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ The plague becomes a foundational trauma reshaping collective memory; the danse macabre and ars moriendi traditions embed death-consciousness into cultural identity; the destruction of Jewish communities eliminates a constituent element of Iberian cultural synthesis; converso identity emerges as a new, ambiguous category.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ The Trastamara dynasty must construct legitimating narratives for usurpation; Henry of Trastamara's propaganda emphasizes Pedro I's tyranny and alliance with Jews and Muslims; religious orthodoxy becomes more central to political legitimation as crisis demands explanatory frameworks.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Artistic production contracts with patronage; monumental architecture slows; manuscript illumination becomes less elaborate; death imagery proliferates in what survives; the aesthetic register shifts toward the stark and morbid; material culture becomes less exuberant as resources contract.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ The high medieval cultural synthesis shows strain; convivencia as a lived reality collapses with the 1391 pogroms; the confident expansion of Romanesque and Gothic forms gives way to more austere expressions; cultural forms struggle to process unprecedented trauma.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Plague is interpreted through apocalyptic and providentialist frameworks; the mortality appears as divine punishment for sin; flagellant movements spread briefly; fatalism deepens as mortality seems inescapable; the existential outlook darkens toward acceptance of imminent death.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Death rituals intensify and transform under mass mortality; normal burial practices become impossible; masses for the dead multiply; votive devotion to plague saints (Sebastian, Roch) spreads; processions and collective devotional practices continue despite contagion risk, sometimes spreading infection.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Monasteries and cathedrals suffer proportional mortality; some houses collapse entirely; clerical ranks thin, reducing pastoral capacity; the institutional church persists but with diminished personnel and resources; religious orders face recruitment challenges.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ The forced conversions of 1391 create the converso phenomenon — a large population of nominal Christians of Jewish descent whose sincerity becomes suspect; this represents violent displacement of Judaism rather than organic religious change; Crypto-Judaism persists among some conversos.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Rather than disenchantment, the crisis produces intensified religious anxiety; the sacred remains potent but threatening; the failure of religious explanations to make plague meaningful generates not doubt but desperate searching for spiritual answers; the residue is not secularizing drift but traumatized piety.
