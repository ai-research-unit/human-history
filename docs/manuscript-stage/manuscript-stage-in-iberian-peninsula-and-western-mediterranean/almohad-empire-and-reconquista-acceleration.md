# __1147 CE – 1250 CE__ — Almohad Empire and Reconquista Acceleration
__Summary:__ North African Almohad dynasty replaces Almoravids and imposes stricter religious uniformity across al-Andalus while Christian kingdoms exploit Almohad succession crises to achieve decisive territorial gains; the binding constraint is the Christian coalition's military superiority crystallized at Las Navas de Tolosa (1212), margin for Muslim recovery collapses as Cordoba, Valencia, and Seville fall in rapid succession (1236–1248), direction of change is the transformation of al-Andalus from a major civilization to a single tributary remnant in Granada.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Sierra Morena passes become decisive military corridors; Guadalquivir valley's agricultural wealth makes it the strategic prize; Levantine coast's separate orientation allows Valencia's distinct trajectory; mountainous terrain of Granada region permits Nasrid survival.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Mediterranean climate continues; irrigation systems in conquered territories intact but transfer of management disrupts some maintenance; no major climatic anomalies documented; agricultural productivity sustained.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Drought years affect provisioning for military campaigns; no catastrophic natural disasters recorded; hazard exposure remains within normal Mediterranean parameters.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Agricultural land the primary conquest objective; Sierra Morena minerals accessible; timber in mountain zones; conquest transfers control of productive resources from Muslim to Christian hands progressively.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Urban populations continue in conquered cities; waste management systems inherited; siege warfare produces localized destruction; absorptive capacity adequate; no sink saturation.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic disease at typical levels; no major epidemic recorded; siege conditions increase localized mortality; battlefield deaths significant; overall mortality baseline elevated by warfare but not catastrophic.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Agricultural productivity maintained in unconquered zones; siege conditions create localized famine; population displacement disrupts provisioning temporarily; nutritional status variable by location and circumstance.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ War captives taken on both sides; sexual violence accompanies conquest; marriage patterns disrupted in conquered populations; reproductive pressures intensified by demographic upheaval.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Muslim population displaced from conquered territories through emigration, conversion, or enslavement; Christian settlers (repobladores) arrive; Mudejar (Muslim under Christian rule) populations remain in some areas; demographic transition underway.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ War casualties, widows, orphans, refugees increase dependency load; ransoming of captives a significant economic activity; attrition pressure high in frontier zones and conquered territories.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Irrigated agriculture continues in transferred territories; Christian lords inherit productive estates; Mudejar cultivators maintain agricultural techniques; subsistence floor preserved through conquest transition.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and animal labor; water mills; wind for maritime transport; Mudejar technical knowledge retained in conquered territories; energy conversion capacity maintained through transition.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Mudejar status creates new semi-coerced labor category; slavery continues; Christian seigneurial arrangements imposed; military orders (Calatrava, Santiago, Alcantara) organize frontier labor and defense; labor systems restructure under conquest.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Agricultural and artisanal production continues; conquest disruption temporary; Christian lords extract from productive base; surplus generation sustained though distribution changes hands.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Military campaigns require stored provisions; treasuries depleted by warfare; conquest transfers accumulated wealth; buffering capacity stressed by military mobilization on both sides.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Mediterranean trade continues; Italian merchants (Genoese, Pisans) profit from both sides; Castilian wool trade developing; conquest opens new trade routes northward; logistics of warfare test exchange networks.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Gold dinars and Christian coinage circulate; plunder and tribute redistribute wealth; credit networks span religious boundaries; Almohad monetary reform affects currency; value storage mechanisms adapt to conquest conditions.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Christian conquest redistributes land and revenue to crown, nobility, military orders, and church; Almohad taxation supports military resistance; extraction load shifts from Almohad to Christian recipients.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Conquest transfers assets massively; Muslim elites flee or lose property; Christian nobles, military orders, and church gain; Mudejar populations retain some property under negotiated surrenders; distribution revolutionized.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Siege destruction, battlefield debris, displaced populations; conquest creates exceptional waste loads; reconstruction in conquered cities addresses accumulated damage; routing disrupted but recovers.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Conquest shatters cross-religious cooperation patterns; Muslim communities under Christian rule maintain internal solidarity; Christian settler communities develop new networks; trust radius contracts to confessional and local boundaries.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Mudejar status creates new subordinate category; conquered Muslim populations stratified below Christians; Jewish communities continue in both zones; Old Christian-settler hierarchies form; stratification restructures around conquest.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Muslim inheritance patterns continue among Mudejars under negotiated terms; Christian fueros govern settler communities; family disruption through conquest, exile, and captivity; kinship structures stressed but adaptive.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Aljamas (Mudejar communities) negotiate collective status; Christian cofradias and guilds organize in conquered cities; military orders as distinctive associational form; self-organization reconfigures under new political reality.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Public festivals adapt to Christian calendar in conquered zones; some continuity in vernacular sociability; religious festivals structure leisure; sociability patterns shift with demographic change.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Emigration to Granada or North Africa available for displaced Muslims; negotiated surrender terms provide some protection; conversion as escape from subordinate status; grievance discharge inadequate for scale of dislocation; residual resentment accumulates.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Almohad caliphs rule from Marrakesh with governors in al-Andalus; Christian kingdoms (Castile, Leon, Aragon, Portugal, Navarre) consolidate under monarchs; power shifts decisively toward Christian crowns; fragmentation on Muslim side after 1212.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Almohad succession crises weaken resistance; Christian monarchs — Alfonso VIII, Fernando III, James I — provide stable leadership; Nasrid dynasty establishes in Granada (1238); transfer mechanisms favor Christian consolidation.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Almohads invoke religious reform and jihad; Christian Reconquista ideology frames conquest as sacred recovery; papal support (crusade status for Las Navas de Tolosa); legitimacy discourse overwhelmingly favors Christian expansion.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Christian kingdoms develop effective military and administrative organization; fueros govern conquered towns; royal chanceries produce records; Almohad administration fragments after 1212; capacity differential favors Christians.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Cortes (representative assemblies) develop in Christian kingdoms; nobility and church participate in governance; Muslim populations excluded from Christian political structures; mobilization capacity superior on Christian side.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Christian alliance at Las Navas de Tolosa (Castile, Aragon, Navarre, Portugal, crusaders) demonstrates coalition capacity; Almohad connections to North Africa provide diminishing support; papal backing for Reconquista; geopolitical balance shifts conclusively.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Heavy cavalry, siege technology, military orders give Christians military superiority; Almohad armies effective until Las Navas de Tolosa defeat; fortification of frontier; coercive capacity decisive for outcomes.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Frontier warfare endemic; conquered territories pacified through negotiated surrender and force; banditry and resistance sporadic; internal order reestablished under Christian rule with some turbulence.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Royal messengers, ecclesiastical networks, merchant correspondence; crusade preaching spreads news of campaigns; information flows support military coordination; velocity adequate for campaign logistics.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Christian chanceries produce charters documenting conquest and settlement; fueros recorded; Arabic manuscripts dispersed by conquest — some destroyed, some transferred, some preserved; record persistence disrupted in conquered zones, expanding in Christian administration.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Reconquista framing dominates Christian discourse; Almohad religious strictness constrains expression in Muslim zones; chronicles celebrate Christian victories; discourse norms shaped by war and religious mobilization.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Conquest news spreads through Christendom; crusade indulgences attract attention; epic poetry (Cantar de Mio Cid) amplifies frontier narratives; attention concentrated on military and religious themes.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Propaganda on both sides; atrocity claims and counterclaims; victory announcements require verification; noise elevated by warfare but information systems functional.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Christian just war doctrine legitimizes conquest; Islamic jurisprudence governs shrinking Muslim territories; negotiated surrenders create legal expectations; justice conceptions diverge by community and shift with conquest.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Fueros granted to conquered towns; Mudejar communities retain Islamic law for internal affairs; Christian royal law expands; Almohad Maliki enforcement continues in unconquered zones; legal plurality characteristic of transition.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Royal judges, ecclesiastical courts, communal tribunals in Christian territories; qadis continue among Mudejars for internal matters; enforcement follows political control; liability assignment restructures under new sovereignty.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Mudejar capitulation agreements specify rights and restrictions; religious minorities (Muslims, Jews) under Christian protection with limitations; compliance burden increases for subordinated populations.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Conquest violence often unpunished; property seizures normalized; some protections in negotiated surrenders; impunity gap significant during transition; backlog of unredressed wrong substantial.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Toledo translation school active — Arabic philosophy, medicine, astronomy transferred to Latin; Michael Scot, Herman of Carinthia among translators; Aristotelian philosophy reaching Latin Europe; intellectual frontier at translation centers.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Irrigation techniques transferred with Mudejar cultivators; medical, astronomical knowledge translated; practical methods persist through conquest; technical continuity in agriculture and craft.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Cathedral schools in Christian kingdoms; Islamic learning continues in Granada and North Africa; translation brings knowledge to Latin-reading scholars; skill distribution shifts with political control; literacy expanding in Christian administration.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Arabic libraries dispersed by conquest; some destruction, some preservation; translation salvages portion of knowledge stock; decay pressure from conquest significant; losses partially offset by translation transmission.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Reconquista narrative consolidates Christian Spanish identity; Santiago Matamoros (Saint James the Moor-slayer) symbolism; Visigothic heritage claimed; Muslim memory becomes diasporic; foundational narratives diverge decisively.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Christian architecture (cathedrals, churches) rises in conquered cities; Mudejar style blends techniques; mosque conversions to churches; material culture transforms under new patronage; artistic production continues across transition.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Triumphalism among Christians after Las Navas de Tolosa and major conquests; despair and resistance among Muslims; emigration reflects loss of hope; mood bifurcated by confessional position and conquest experience.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Christian forms ascendant — Gothic architecture arrives; vernacular epic develops; Andalusi forms persist in Granada; meaning systems reorganize around conquest outcome; no severe form exhaustion; renewal through transformation.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Christian providential interpretation dominates — conquest as divine will; Islamic questioning of Almohad failure; afterlife orientation persists across communities; existential outlook shaped by conquest experience on both sides.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mass celebrated in converted mosques; Christian pilgrimage to Santiago continues; Muslim practice maintained in Granada and Mudejar communities; devotional infrastructure transforms in conquered zones.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Bishoprics established in conquered cities; military orders combine religious and military functions; mendicant friars (Franciscans, Dominicans) arrive; Almohad doctrinal authority collapses; Christian institutional expansion accompanies conquest.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Islam displaced from most of peninsula; Christianity expands geographically; limited syncretism given communal boundaries; Granada becomes refuge; spiritual geography revolutionized.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Conquest interpreted in religious terms by both sides — divine favor or punishment; no disenchantment; religious meaning intensifies rather than erodes; spiritual frameworks remain compelling as explanatory systems for historical experience.<br><br>
