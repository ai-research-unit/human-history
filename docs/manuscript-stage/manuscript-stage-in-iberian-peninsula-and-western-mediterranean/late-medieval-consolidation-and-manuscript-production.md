# __1400 CE – 1479 CE__ — Late Medieval Consolidation and Manuscript Production

__Summary:__ The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean in recovery and consolidation, as the Trastamara dynasties stabilize Castile and Aragon, population slowly rebuilds, and manuscript production resumes in royal courts, monasteries, and emerging universities; the binding constraint remains the Manuscript Stage, with written records essential for the administrative consolidation, legal codification, and vernacular literary florescence that characterize the period, while humanist influences from Italy introduce new scholarly currents that manuscript circulation propagates.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The peninsula's geography remains unchanged, but spatial integration improves as recovery proceeds; the Meseta reconnects to peripheral regions through reviving trade networks; Castilian control extends more firmly over the Guadalquivir valley; Aragonese maritime connections across the Western Mediterranean rebuild; the effective friction between regions diminishes as depopulated corridors repopulate.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Little Ice Age conditions persist with cooler, more variable weather than the Medieval Warm Period; periodic droughts and harsh winters punctuate the recovery; agricultural systems adapt to the changed climate baseline, with some shift toward hardier crops and pastoral activities in marginal zones.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Plague remains endemic, with outbreaks in 1422, 1434, 1448, and 1465–1466, though mortality is lower than the initial pandemic; the biological hazard becomes a persistent background condition rather than an acute crisis; other environmental hazards remain at typical Mediterranean levels.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Abandoned agricultural land gradually returns to cultivation as population recovers; pastoral resources, particularly Castilian merino wool, become increasingly valuable for export; mining of iron, silver, and other metals continues at modest levels; the resource endowment remains abundant relative to recovering population.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Reduced population lessens environmental pressure; forests regenerate on abandoned marginal land; sanitary conditions in recovering towns improve gradually; the sink capacity remains well within the loads arriving from the contracted economic activity.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Plague recurs periodically but with declining mortality; endemic diseases remain present at typical pre-industrial levels; the mortality baseline, while elevated compared to the high medieval period, no longer represents a catastrophic crisis; population begins sustained recovery after mid-century.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Survivors and their descendants benefit from abundant land relative to population; per-capita food availability is higher than in the crowded pre-plague era; nutritional status and stature likely improve among the lower orders; the biological vitality of the population gradually rebuilds.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Medical knowledge remains within Galenic frameworks but incorporates empirical lessons from plague management; hospitals and charitable institutions rebuild; the Salamanca medical faculty continues training physicians; healing capacity gradually recovers toward pre-plague levels as institutional structures restabilize.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Marriage rates normalize as demographic crisis recedes; age at first marriage may decline, facilitating higher completed fertility; the reproductive effort of the population shifts toward recovery, though growth remains slow against recurrent plague outbreaks.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population growth resumes, though slowly; by 1479 Iberian population reaches perhaps five million, still below pre-plague levels; urban recovery is uneven, with some centers regaining vigor while others remain depressed; overall density increases but remains below the medieval maximum.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ The dependency burden normalizes as age structure rebalances over generations; the acute crisis of orphans and disrupted families from the plague years passes; monastic and charitable institutions that care for dependents recover capacity; the overhang clears gradually.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ The subsistence situation stabilizes well above crisis levels; peasants benefit from improved terms of tenure negotiated during the labor scarcity; the provisioning floor rises as land-labor ratios remain favorable; famines become less common than in the densely populated pre-plague era.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ The labor force rebuilds, enabling fuller utilization of water and wind power; agricultural energy capture increases as abandoned land returns to cultivation; the energy conversion system operates within capacity as population remains below pre-plague density.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Labor relations settle into new equilibria; attempts to reimpose strict serfdom largely fail; wage labor and sharecropping arrangements become more common; the balance between coercion and compensation remains tilted toward workers, though lords consolidate control where they can.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Production recovers gradually across sectors; wool production for export becomes increasingly important for Castile, feeding Flemish textile industries; Catalan and Valencian manufactures resume growth; surplus generation increases, though total output remains below pre-plague peaks; manuscript production resumes in scriptoria with new patronage from stabilized courts.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Royal and noble treasuries rebuild as tax revenues recover; commercial credit networks restabilize; the fiscal capacity of crowns improves, enabling more ambitious state-building; buffering capacity against shock increases though remains modest by later standards.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Mediterranean commerce revives, with Catalan-Aragonese merchants reconnecting Barcelona to Italian, North African, and Eastern Mediterranean markets; Castilian wool exports through Burgos to Flemish ports grow substantially; internal trade networks strengthen as towns recover; commercial infrastructure improves.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Credit systems restabilize; Genoese and Florentine banking houses operate in Iberian markets; the converso community, drawing on commercial skills, becomes important in finance and trade; monetary systems remain stable under Castilian and Aragonese crowns; capital formation resumes.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Royal revenues increase with economic recovery; the Castilian crown develops more sophisticated fiscal machinery; the Cortes retain power over extraordinary taxation while regular revenues grow; the Aragonese crown similarly consolidates fiscal capacity; taxation supports court culture and manuscript patronage.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Wealth concentration increases as noble families consolidate holdings acquired during the crisis; the converso community accumulates commercial wealth; peasant conditions remain better than pre-plague but begin to erode in some regions; land values recover, benefiting established holders.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Economic activity generates increasing waste as production grows, but the expanded sink capacity absorbs the load; urban waste management remains rudimentary but adequate for population levels; the economic residue clears without significant accumulation.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Family structures restabilize after plague disruption; inheritance patterns normalize; noble lineages that survived the crisis consolidate; the converso population establishes new family networks while maintaining some connections across the conversion divide; kinship remains central to social organization.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Guilds and confraternities rebuild membership; urban associations recover organizational capacity; mutual aid networks strengthen; the associational fabric thickens as crisis conditions recede; the mesta, the Castilian sheepherders' guild, becomes increasingly powerful as wool exports grow.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Festive life resumes; royal courts become centers of display and sociability; tournaments, hunting, and courtly entertainments flourish; urban festivals return; the carnivalesque and ludic dimensions of social life recover from plague-era contraction.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ The converso population constitutes a new and ambiguous social category, nominally Christian but suspect; limpieza de sangre (purity of blood) concerns emerge, distinguishing Old Christians from New; noble status remains prestigious while commercial wealth grows among conversos and others; social boundaries harden in some respects while remaining permeable in others.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Royal administration consolidates under the Trastamara dynasties; the Castilian councils (Consejo Real, later specialized councils) develop greater capacity; the Aragonese crown maintains its composite structure with distinct administrations for Aragon, Catalonia, and Valencia; universities at Salamanca, Valladolid, and elsewhere train administrators and jurists; documentary apparatus becomes more sophisticated.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Military capacity rebuilds; the Castilian crown develops more direct control over armed force, reducing dependence on noble levies; the Aragonese crown maintains Mediterranean naval capacity; fortification continues; the coercive apparatus strengthens relative to the chaos of the crisis years.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Social order improves as central authority consolidates; banditry declines; the violence of the crisis years recedes; however, tension around converso status creates new sources of friction; rural unrest occasionally flares as lords attempt to claw back concessions made during labor scarcity; the disorder residue diminishes but does not fully clear.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Public mood recovers from plague-era despair; confidence slowly returns as normal life resumes; however, suspicion of conversos creates an undercurrent of distrust; the collective temper moderates toward cautious optimism without fully recovering the confident expansionism of the high medieval period.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Strong monarchs emerge in both crowns: in Castile, John II (1406–1454) and the contested reign of Henry IV (1454–1474); in Aragon, the Compromise of Caspe (1412) installs the Trastamara Fernando I, followed by Alfonso V (1416–1458) and John II (1458–1479); personal authority of monarchs increases, though constrained by noble factions.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Trastamara legitimacy consolidates over generations; the usurpation origins fade from active memory; however, the Castilian succession crisis of 1474–1479 between Isabella and Juana la Beltraneja shows continuing vulnerability; legitimacy is contested but the principle of dynastic monarchy holds firm.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Political will coheres around dynastic consolidation; the Castilian and Aragonese crowns pursue state-building with increasing effectiveness; the wars against Granada resume intermittently, signaling reviving expansionist will; the marriage of Ferdinand and Isabella (1469) represents successful coordination of political will across the crowns.

__External Relations & Posture:__ External ambitions revive; Alfonso V of Aragon conquers Naples (1442), extending Aragonese power into Italy; Castilian rivalry with Portugal over Atlantic expansion intensifies; relations with Granada shift toward renewed pressure; the posture moves from defensive consolidation toward renewed expansion.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ The exhaustion of the crisis years gradually dissipates; however, noble factionalism creates ongoing political instability, particularly in Castile during Henry IV's reign; converso communities face mounting hostility; the disaffection residue partly clears but new sources of tension emerge around religious identity.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Latin remains central for learning, law, and church; vernacular languages continue expanding into documentary use; Castilian becomes increasingly standardized through royal and literary production; Catalan maintains its literary tradition; Hebrew use declines catastrophically with the destruction of Jewish communities; conversos largely adopt Christian communicative codes.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Communication networks strengthen with commercial recovery; royal courier systems become more regular; Mediterranean shipping carries correspondence as well as goods; the velocity and reliability of information transmission improve, though the fundamental technologies remain unchanged.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Archival practices develop greater sophistication; royal chancelleries produce growing volumes of documentation; cathedral and monastic archives are maintained and organized; the Crown of Aragon's archival system becomes particularly systematic; manuscript copying resumes in scriptoria with improved capacity; libraries grow through new production and acquisition.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Royal courts shape discourse through patronage of approved narratives; chronicles commissioned by monarchs frame political events; religious orthodoxy increasingly constrains public discourse about conversos and religious identity; the Inquisition, established in Castile in 1478, begins to systematize censorship.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Courtly audiences for literature and learning expand; university-trained readers form a growing public for scholarly and literary works; humanist texts from Italy circulate among educated elites; vernacular literature reaches broader audiences through public readings and performance.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Documentary systems achieve greater reliability as administrative capacity improves; record-keeping becomes more systematic; however, the ambiguity around converso identity creates informational noise about genealogy and religious sincerity; the verification of lineage becomes increasingly important and contested.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Religious orthodoxy becomes more central to moral foundations; the sin of heresy and its detection preoccupy moral discourse; limpieza de sangre begins to function as a moral category distinguishing pure from impure lineages; the convivencia ethos of the high medieval period yields to more exclusivist moral frameworks.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Legal codification advances; the Ordenamiento de Montalvo (1484) compiles Castilian law; royal legislation proliferates; university-trained jurists apply Roman and canon law principles; the legal framework for the emerging Inquisition develops; law becomes more systematized and written.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Royal justice extends its reach relative to noble and local jurisdictions; the Audiencias develop as appellate courts; the Inquisition, from 1478, creates a parallel jurisdiction for matters of faith; adjudicative capacity improves though access remains uneven; sanction becomes more regularized.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Converso rights remain formally equal to Old Christians, but informal discrimination increases; peasant rights achieved during labor scarcity face erosion in some regions; noble privileges consolidate; the compliance burden of proving religious orthodoxy and lineage begins to weigh on converso populations.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ The unpunished violence of 1391 continues to cast a shadow; converso communities seeking justice for past wrongs find little recourse; noble factionalism sometimes enables violence without consequence; however, ordinary crime faces more effective prosecution as royal justice expands; the impunity residue partly clears while new forms emerge.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Humanist learning from Italy reaches Iberian courts and universities; classical studies develop, with new attention to Latin style and Greek texts; scholasticism continues to dominate university curricula at Salamanca and elsewhere; the two currents coexist, with humanism gaining ground among courtly elites while scholasticism holds the institutions.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Practical techniques in navigation, cartography, and shipbuilding advance, laying groundwork for Atlantic exploration; agricultural and pastoral techniques adapt to changed demographic conditions; manuscript production techniques continue without major innovation, though paper becomes more widely used alongside parchment.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Universities recover and expand; Salamanca remains Castile's premier institution; new foundations emerge; literacy spreads slowly among urban populations; the knowledge stock grows through humanist acquisition of classical texts and continued scholastic production; skill distribution remains heavily concentrated among clergy and urban elites.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Hebrew learning in Iberia faces severe contraction as the Jewish scholarly community is destroyed or dispersed; some classical Arabic learning preserved in translation; Latin scholastic tradition shows continuity; humanist recovery of classical texts counters earlier losses; overall, the knowledge stock stabilizes and begins to grow again.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ The Reconquista narrative strengthens as legitimating mythology; the crusading identity of Castilian and Aragonese kingdoms consolidates; the memory of convivencia fades as exclusivist Christian identity becomes more central; converso identity remains ambiguous and contested, neither fully Christian nor permitted to remain Jewish.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Trastamara monarchs commission chronicles and literary works celebrating their rule; Alfonso V's Neapolitan court becomes a center of humanist culture; the ideology of religious unity and limpieza de sangre develops; royal patronage shapes cultural production toward legitimating narratives.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Late Gothic style flourishes in ecclesiastical and civil architecture; manuscript illumination reaches new heights of elaboration in royal and noble commissions; Hispano-Flemish painting emerges; material culture becomes more refined as prosperity returns; courtly display intensifies as competition for prestige through cultural patronage increases.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ The Gothic style shows elaboration rather than exhaustion, with Isabelline Gothic developing distinctive features; vernacular literary forms flourish, from the romancero to courtly poetry; however, the meaning systems of the pre-plague era, including the convivencia synthesis, have decayed and been replaced by more exclusivist frameworks.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Christian cosmology dominates more exclusively than in the high medieval period; the existential outlook remains shaped by plague memory but moderates toward conventional late medieval piety; apocalyptic anxiety recedes; the confidence of a unified Christian order gradually returns.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Devotional practices intensify around Marian and Christocentric piety; religious confraternities proliferate; pilgrimage to Santiago de Compostela revives; the liturgical calendar structures public life; conversos must perform Christian devotion under increasing scrutiny, creating anxiety around ritual practice.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The Church rebuilds institutional strength; mendicant orders remain influential; monasteries recover endowments and recruitment; the Castilian crown gains increasing control over Church appointments; the establishment of the Inquisition (1478) represents the crown's assertion of doctrinal authority in partnership with Rome.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Judaism is suppressed rather than displaced; Crypto-Judaism persists among some conversos; the surviving Muslim population (Mudejars) faces increasing pressure toward conversion; the spiritual landscape moves toward uniformity, with diversity driven underground rather than accommodated; the direction is toward consolidation of Catholic orthodoxy.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Rather than disenchantment, the period sees re-enchantment through intensified orthodoxy; the sacred remains potent and is defended through institutional means (the Inquisition); the spiritual residue is not secularizing drift but rather the anxiety and suspicion generated by the converso question and the apparatus of enforcement emerging to police belief.
