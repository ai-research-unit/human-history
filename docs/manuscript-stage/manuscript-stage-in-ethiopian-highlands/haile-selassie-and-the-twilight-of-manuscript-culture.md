# __1913 CE – 1945 CE__ — Haile Selassie and the Twilight of Manuscript Culture

__Summary:__ The Ethiopian Highlands under Haile Selassie's consolidation and reign, through Italian occupation and British-led liberation, with the binding constraint of the Manuscript Stage finally yielding to print as the dominant medium for government and public discourse, while manuscript culture persisted in ecclesiastical contexts — modernizing ambition and foreign invasion together hastening the transition from parchment to paper, from scriptorium to press.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The highland plateau retained its defensive advantage, with elevations of 1,500–4,500 meters impeding mechanized invasion yet also limiting internal road construction; Addis Ababa at 2,400 meters served as the fixed modern capital, centralizing administration as no itinerant court could.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Highland rains remained broadly reliable for teff and barley cultivation, though localized droughts stressed peripheral provinces; the Italian occupation's disruption of planting cycles compounded wartime food stress in occupied zones.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ The highlands faced no catastrophic natural disasters in this window, but the Italian invasion (1935–1936) constituted an anthropogenic shock of comparable magnitude, destroying infrastructure and displacing populations across the north and center.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Ethiopia's resource base remained largely agrarian, with modest gold and coffee exports; Italian occupation briefly exploited road-building potential but left no industrial infrastructure; the endowment supported pre-industrial production but not the mechanized warfare Italy deployed.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Population density remained low enough that agricultural waste posed no accumulated burden; Italian mustard gas attacks left localized chemical residue in battle zones, a novel pollutant Ethiopia had never before absorbed.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Malaria, typhus, and intestinal parasites maintained endemic pressure; the Italian campaign introduced aerial-delivered mustard gas, causing chemical burns and respiratory damage among combatants and civilians; occupation-era sanitary collapse elevated mortality in urban centers.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Peasant nutrition remained subsistence-adequate in peacetime, based on injera (teff bread), pulses, and occasional meat; Italian occupation disrupted harvests and markets, causing localized famine conditions, particularly in resistance-affected areas.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Traditional bone-setting, herbalism, and church-based healing persisted; Haile Selassie established a handful of modern hospitals and the Red Cross presence expanded, but coverage reached only Addis Ababa and a few provincial towns; wartime casualties overwhelmed available care.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Marriage patterns followed Orthodox Christian norms with arranged unions and church blessing; Italian occupation disrupted household formation in war zones but did not alter underlying reproductive biology.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population stood at roughly 10–12 million, growing slowly; Italian invasion and occupation killed an estimated 275,000–760,000 Ethiopians through combat, reprisals, and famine, a demographic shock that would take a generation to absorb.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ War widows, orphans, and disabled veterans accumulated during the Italian period; traditional kin networks and church charity absorbed the dependent load without state welfare apparatus.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Peasant households remained the productive base, growing teff, barley, wheat, and pulses; the provisioning floor held except where Italian scorched-earth tactics or partisan warfare disrupted planting and harvest.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and animal muscle remained the dominant energy sources; no electrification beyond Addis Ababa's limited grid; the Maria Theresa thaler and, increasingly, Ethiopian dollar facilitated exchange, but the economy ran on organic power.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Peasant labor remained semi-feudal under the gult and rist landholding systems, with tribute obligations to lords and church; Haile Selassie's centralizing reforms began to regularize taxation but did not abolish serfdom; Italian occupation imposed forced labor for road construction.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Coffee and hides generated export surplus; Haile Selassie's modernization drew on this revenue for arms purchases and infrastructure; Italian occupation extracted surplus through requisition, collapsing the export economy until liberation.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Grain stores remained household- and village-level; the imperial treasury accumulated modest reserves from customs and coffee taxes, sufficient to purchase arms and fund a small modern army but not to sustain prolonged industrial warfare; the treasury was evacuated during the Italian advance.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ The Addis Ababa–Djibouti railway (completed 1917) provided Ethiopia's sole modern export corridor; Italian blockade and invasion severed this lifeline; post-liberation, British lend-lease aid restored basic logistics.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ The Bank of Ethiopia (founded 1931) introduced modern currency and banking; Italian occupation imposed the lira; liberation restored the Ethiopian dollar; credit remained limited outside Addis Ababa's merchant community.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Haile Selassie centralized tax collection, reducing regional lords' autonomous extraction; Italian occupation extracted through requisition and forced labor; post-liberation, British military administration supervised fiscal reconstruction.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Land remained concentrated among nobility, church, and crown; Italian confiscation of aristocratic estates disrupted traditional holdings; liberation restored pre-war property arrangements without redistribution.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Agricultural waste recycled as fuel and fertilizer; industrial waste negligible; financial malinvestment minimal in the absence of developed capital markets.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Amhara and Tigrayan kinship followed bilateral descent with patrilineal emphasis; church-blessed marriage remained the norm; inheritance favored sons but allowed daughters shares; Italian occupation disrupted families but did not alter underlying structures.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Edir (burial societies) and mahber (religious associations) provided mutual aid below the state; these networks sustained communities through occupation and resistance.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Maskal and Timkat festivals continued as communal celebrations; tej houses and coffee gatherings sustained sociability; Italian occupation restricted public assembly, driving social life into private and church spaces.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Society stratified into royalty, nobility, clergy, peasantry, and marginalized artisan castes; Haile Selassie's centralization weakened regional nobility while elevating a new bureaucratic elite educated abroad; Italian occupation imposed racial hierarchy with Ethiopians subordinated to Italian settlers.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Haile Selassie established ministries, a written constitution (1931), and a bicameral parliament (advisory only); centralized administration replaced regional autonomy; Italian occupation dismantled Ethiopian institutions, ruling through military governors; liberation restored the pre-war apparatus under British supervision, with full sovereignty returning by 1944.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ The Imperial Guard and provincial levies formed the army; modern rifles purchased but no air force or armor; Italian invasion deployed tanks, aircraft, and chemical weapons, overwhelming Ethiopian forces at Maychew (1936); liberation came through British-led forces with Ethiopian irregular support (1941); post-war, British arms and training rebuilt the army.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Italian occupation generated massive disorder — executions, village burnings, and reprisals against civilians, particularly after the assassination attempt on Graziani (1937), which provoked massacres killing thousands; patriot resistance (arbegnoch) kept insurgency alive throughout occupation; liberation restored order but left grievances against collaborators unresolved.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Haile Selassie concentrated personal authority as no emperor since Menelik, projecting modernizing competence and divine-right legitimacy; his exile (1936–1941) paradoxically elevated his symbolic stature; the League of Nations speech (1936) made him an international symbol of resistance to fascism.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ The Solomonic claim, the church's blessing, and victory at Adwa underwrote Haile Selassie's legitimacy; defeat and exile tested but did not break allegiance; liberation renewed the covenant between emperor and people.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Haile Selassie's will to modernize drove reforms before the invasion; Italian conquest shattered state capacity, but patriot resistance demonstrated continued will to resist; liberation channeled that will into state reconstruction.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Ethiopia joined the League of Nations (1923), seeking collective security; the League's failure to halt Italian aggression discredited collective security and left Ethiopia diplomatically isolated; liberation came through British strategic interest in East Africa, not multilateral action; post-war Ethiopia aligned with the Allies.

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Pre-invasion confidence, grounded in the Adwa legacy, gave way to shock at defeat; occupation bred a mixture of sullen compliance and covert resistance; liberation restored national pride, amplified by Haile Selassie's exile and return as symbols of continuity.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ Defeat bred demoralization; occupation bred sullen withdrawal from public life; liberation re-anchored collective will, though the trauma of abandonment by the League left residual distrust of international institutions.


## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Amharic served as the spoken vernacular; Ge'ez remained the liturgical and literary language; literacy rates below 5%, confined to clergy and the small modern-educated elite; Italian occupation imposed Italian in administration without displacing vernacular speech.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Telegraph and postal service connected provincial capitals to Addis Ababa; radio arrived under Haile Selassie, broadcasting imperial pronouncements; Italian occupation controlled radio for propaganda; oral networks and church channels carried resistance communication.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ This dimension marks the hinge of the Manuscript Stage's twilight. Parchment manuscripts persisted in monasteries and churches for liturgy, hagiography, and canon law. But Haile Selassie's modernization established government printing presses, producing laws, regulations, and the Negarit Gazeta (official gazette) in print. Newspapers — Berhanena Selam, Addis Zemen — appeared in Amharic, reaching the small literate public. Italian occupation introduced Italian-language print propaganda while destroying or looting Ethiopian manuscripts and archives. Liberation restored Ethiopian print media; by 1945, print had become the dominant medium for government and public discourse, though manuscript production continued in ecclesiastical contexts — the binding constraint shifting from parchment to paper, from scriptorium to press.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Imperial censorship controlled print; criticism of the emperor remained taboo; Italian occupation imposed fascist censorship; post-liberation, imperial control resumed, tighter than before.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Print reached only the urban literate minority; radio expanded reach but remained state-controlled; the majority received information through oral channels — markets, churches, and travelers.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Italian propaganda and Ethiopian counter-narratives created competing information regimes; post-liberation, the state monopolized official truth; manuscript records in monasteries preserved an alternative ecclesiastical memory outside state control.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity provided the moral foundation, emphasizing piety, hierarchy, and divine order; the Italian invasion was experienced as a moral outrage — unprovoked aggression against a Christian nation by a nominally Christian power using banned weapons.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ The Fetha Nagast remained the theoretical legal code, supplemented by imperial edicts; the 1931 constitution codified (on paper) a limited monarchy, though practice remained autocratic; Italian occupation imposed Italian law; liberation restored Ethiopian codes.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Traditional courts (chilot) handled most disputes; the emperor served as final appellate authority; Italian military tribunals executed resistance fighters summarily; post-liberation, some collaborators faced trials, but most escaped sanction.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ The 1931 constitution nominally guaranteed rights but enforcement was absent; peasants remained bound by customary obligations to lords; Italian occupation stripped all rights from Ethiopians; liberation restored the pre-war regime without expanding rights.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Italian war crimes — chemical weapons, civilian massacres, the Graziani reprisals — went largely unpunished; post-war trials addressed only a few high-profile cases; the moral debt remained uncleared, feeding long-term grievance against Italy.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Traditional Ethiopian learning centered on Ge'ez church scholarship — exegesis, computation of the calendar, and theological debate; Haile Selassie sent students abroad for modern education in Europe and America, creating a small cadre familiar with Western science and administration; no indigenous scientific paradigm emerged.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ European technical methods arrived through advisors, arms purchases, and returned students; Italian occupation introduced road-building techniques but transferred no lasting technical capacity; post-liberation, modernization resumed with British and American technical assistance.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Traditional church schools (nebab bet, qene bet) trained clergy in Ge'ez literacy; Haile Selassie established modern schools on Western models, including Haile Selassie I University's precursors; enrollment remained tiny — a few thousand students; Italian occupation closed Ethiopian schools, opening Italian-language schools for settler children; liberation rebuilt the modern school system.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Traditional Ge'ez scholarship persisted but no longer served state administration; the transition to print-based bureaucracy rendered manuscript-based record-keeping obsolescent for civil purposes while preserving it for ecclesiastical ones; Italian looting destroyed or dispersed Ethiopian manuscripts, a knowledge loss only partially recovered.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ The Solomonic myth — descent from Solomon and Sheba, custodianship of the Ark — remained foundational; the Adwa victory (1896) had become central to national memory; Italian occupation challenged but ultimately reinforced Ethiopian identity through shared resistance.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Haile Selassie's legitimacy rested on Solomonic descent, church blessing, and modernizing competence; the League of Nations speech cast Ethiopia as a victim of aggression and a symbol of collective security's failure, a narrative that resonated internationally and domestically.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Church painting continued in traditional style; Addis Ababa's architecture mixed Ethiopian, European, and Indian influences; court dress combined traditional shamma with European formal wear; Italian occupation introduced fascist architecture (still visible in Asmara) and European goods; post-liberation, Ethiopian cultural forms reasserted themselves.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Traditional forms retained vitality through church practice; the Solomonic narrative gained renewed potency through resistance to occupation; no exhaustion of meaning — rather, the crisis renewed and intensified traditional symbols.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Ethiopian Orthodox Christianity provided the cosmological frame, placing Ethiopia as a new Israel under divine protection; Italian invasion tested but did not break this outlook — the occupation was interpreted as divine testing, liberation as divine vindication.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Liturgy in Ge'ez continued in churches and monasteries; feast days — Maskal, Timkat, Fasika — structured the year; Italian occupation restricted but could not suppress Orthodox practice; churches served as centers of covert resistance.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The Ethiopian Orthodox Church, headed by the Abuna (appointed from Alexandria until 1959), remained the dominant institution; the church blessed Haile Selassie's coronation and supported resistance; Italian occupation sought to co-opt the church, with limited success; post-liberation, church and state alliance resumed.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Islam persisted in lowland peripheries and some highland enclaves; Italian occupation briefly favored Muslims as a counterweight to Orthodox resistance; post-liberation, Orthodox dominance resumed; no significant Christian decline — rather, the crisis reinforced Orthodox identity.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ The small modern-educated elite showed some secular drift, but the mass remained devoutly Orthodox; the spiritual crisis of occupation and liberation renewed rather than eroded faith; no standing disenchantment — the sacred remained alive, and liberation was read as providential.
