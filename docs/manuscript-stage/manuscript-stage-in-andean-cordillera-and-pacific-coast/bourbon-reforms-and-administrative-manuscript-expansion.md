# __1700 CE – 1780 CE__ — Bourbon Reforms and Administrative Manuscript Expansion

__Summary:__ The Viceroyalty of Peru under Bourbon rule, where dynastic reformism drives an eightfold expansion of administrative manuscript production as the new intendancy system, scientific expeditions, and Enlightenment-influenced regulation generate unprecedented documentary flows, binding constraint remaining the Manuscript Stage infrastructure that enables systematic state legibility but still excludes the illiterate majority from direct participation in public records.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The viceregal territory spans coastal desert from Guayaquil to Atacama, the central sierra from Quito to Potosi, and the altiplano basin, with Lima the administrative node linked by mule tracks requiring weeks to reach highland centers; the 1717 and 1739 partitions create the Viceroyalty of New Granada (northern Andes) and anticipate the 1776 creation of the Rio de la Plata viceroyalty, shrinking Peru's jurisdiction but concentrating administrative attention on the remaining core.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Little Ice Age persists with moderate cooling; ENSO cycles produce periodic coastal flooding and highland drought, but irrigation infrastructure and vertical archipelago exploitation buffer shocks; no catastrophic climate event disrupts the period's administrative expansion.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ The 1746 Lima-Callao earthquake and tsunami kill approximately 5,000 and destroy much of the capital, prompting extensive reconstruction and generating massive documentary records of damage assessment, rebuilding contracts, and insurance claims; seismic risk remains constant along the Pacific margin.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Potosi silver production declines from its 17th-century peak but remains substantial; mercury from Huancavelica supplies amalgamation; guano deposits along the coast are not yet commercially exploited; timber for paper pulp remains imported from Europe via Spain, constraining local manuscript substrate production.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Mining effluent continues to contaminate highland watersheds around Potosi and Huancavelica; mercury pollution accumulates in soils and workers; urban waste in Lima and highland cities remains locally absorbed without reaching critical thresholds; the physical sink is adequate for period loads.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic typhus, dysentery, and respiratory illness persist; periodic smallpox outbreaks strike indigenous communities despite earlier demographic catastrophe having selected for survivors; Huancavelica mercury miners suffer chronic poisoning with shortened lifespans; baseline mortality remains elevated by European standards but stable.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Highland populations maintain adequate nutrition through potato, maize, and quinoa cultivation with llama and alpaca pastoralism; coastal populations access marine protein; mining communities suffer nutritional stress from labor demands and mercury exposure; creole urban populations enjoy adequate provisioning.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Colonial hospitals (Hospital de Santa Ana, Hospital de San Andres in Lima) serve urban populations; indigenous communities rely on traditional healers and herbal remedies; Jesuit pharmacies disperse before the 1767 expulsion; healing capacity is uneven, concentrated in urban centers, inadequate for mining camps.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Complex casta system regulates intermarriage among Spanish, indigenous, African, and mixed populations; ecclesiastical marriage records proliferate as documentation requirements intensify; fertility remains adequate for population maintenance across all groups.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Indigenous population stabilizes after two centuries of post-conquest decline, beginning slow recovery; creole and mestizo populations grow; African slave imports continue for coastal agriculture; total viceregal population approaches two million by 1780; density remains low outside urban centers and mining zones.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ The mit'a labor draft continues to extract working-age males from indigenous communities for Potosi service, leaving women, children, and elderly as dependents; mortality in mines and mercury works creates widows and orphans absorbed by extended kin networks; dependency load is chronic but contained within indigenous social structures.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Indigenous communities maintain subsistence through traditional agriculture augmented by European crops and livestock; tribute obligations and labor drafts extract surplus but rarely push below subsistence; periodic droughts and earthquakes disrupt provisioning locally but reserves and reciprocity networks buffer shortfalls.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and animal muscle remain the primary power sources; watermills serve grain processing; draft animals (mules, llamas) power transport and some mining operations; no significant technological advance in energy capture occurs during the period; per-capita energy ceiling remains constrained.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ The mit'a system continues to draft indigenous labor for Potosi mines under formal legal coercion; wage labor expands in urban centers and haciendas; African slavery persists on coastal plantations; Bourbon reforms attempt to regulate labor conditions without fundamentally altering the coercive structure.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Silver production at Potosi declines but remains the primary export; textile obrajes produce for regional markets; agricultural haciendas expand; Bourbon commercial reforms (comercio libre from 1778) stimulate trade; surplus flows to Spain as remittances and taxes, leaving limited local accumulation.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Royal treasury maintains reserves in Lima; merchants hold inventories; indigenous communities maintain traditional storage practices; credit networks centered on Lima merchant houses provide liquidity; reserves adequate for normal fluctuations, stressed by major disasters like the 1746 earthquake.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ The 1778 comercio libre decree opens additional ports to direct trade with Spain, bypassing the Cadiz monopoly; internal trade flows along established mule routes; Bourbon reforms improve customs documentation and reduce contraband; logistics remain slow but increasingly documented.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Silver peso remains the unit of account and medium of exchange; merchant credit networks extend throughout the viceroyalty; the Tribunal del Consulado regulates commercial disputes; bills of exchange circulate among merchants; indigenous communities maintain traditional reciprocity obligations alongside monetary transactions.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Bourbon fiscal reforms intensify tax collection, introducing new levies and improving enforcement; the alcabala (sales tax) expands; tribute collection from indigenous communities continues; customs duties increase under comercio libre; fiscal pressure rises but remains below levels that provoke open resistance until the late 1770s.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Wealth concentrates among peninsular merchants, creole hacendados, and the Church; indigenous communities hold communal lands under legal protection but face encroachment; the Jesuit expulsion (1767) transfers substantial ecclesiastical property to secular administration; inequality deepens as commercial reform favors established capital.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Mining waste continues to accumulate at Potosi and Huancavelica; urban waste managed through traditional channels; no significant change in waste routing practices; financial malinvestment limited by conservative credit practices and colonial oversight.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Spanish inheritance law governs creole families with primogeniture for entailed estates; indigenous communities maintain bilateral inheritance with communal land tenure; the casta system regulates intermarriage and determines legal status of offspring; ecclesiastical marriage remains the only legitimate union, extensively documented in parish registers.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Indigenous cofradias (confraternities) provide mutual aid and organize religious festivals; artisan guilds regulate urban crafts; merchant consulados coordinate trade; indigenous communities maintain traditional ayllu structures for labor coordination and resource sharing; the fabric of association remains dense and functional.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Religious festivals structure the calendar with processions, bullfights, and public celebrations; urban tertulias gather creole elites for conversation and literary discussion; indigenous festivals syncretize Catholic and traditional elements; sociability reinforces both colonial hierarchy and community solidarity.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ The casta system formally stratifies society by racial category with peninsular Spaniards at apex, creoles below, then mixed categories (mestizo, mulato, zambo), indigenous tribute-payers, and African slaves at bottom; boundaries remain legally rigid but social practice permits some mobility through wealth, education, or whitening (gracias al sacar); stratification is hardening as Bourbon reforms favor peninsulares over creoles for high office.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ The intendancy system (introduced 1784, anticipated by earlier reforms) replaces the corregimiento, centralizing provincial administration under royally appointed intendants; the Audiencia of Lima retains judicial functions; visitadores (inspectors) audit colonial administration; documentary production multiplies as each reform generates new record requirements, reports, and correspondence; administrative capacity increases but corruption persists.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ The colonial militia system provides local defense; regular army units garrison Lima and major cities; fortifications protect Callao harbor; indigenous communities lack formal military organization but retain capacity for coordinated resistance; the apparatus suffices for internal order but proves inadequate when confronted with mass uprising in the 1780s.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Low-level disorder persists in banditry, smuggling, and occasional local riots; judicial records document criminal proceedings; indigenous grievances accumulate as tribute and labor burdens intensify under Bourbon fiscal pressure; the apparatus contains disorder but does not resolve underlying tensions; friction accretes toward the crisis of the 1780s.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Viceroys appointed from Spain exercise formal authority; local caciques (indigenous chiefs) mediate between communities and colonial state; creole elites lack access to highest offices; no charismatic leader emerges to channel discontent until Tupac Amaru II in 1780; leadership is bureaucratic rather than personal.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ The Spanish Crown retains formal legitimacy through tradition, religion, and established authority; indigenous communities accept colonial rule as established fact while maintaining separate identity; creole allegiance is instrumental, eroding as discrimination intensifies; legitimacy weakens gradually but has not yet cracked.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Bourbon reformers in Madrid drive policy with centralizing will; local viceregal administration implements directives with variable enthusiasm; creole elites lack collective organization to resist; indigenous communities conserve energy, waiting; collective will is dispersed, reactive, not yet mobilized for resistance.

__External Relations & Posture:__ The viceroyalty functions as colony, its external posture determined by Madrid; British and Portuguese pressure on Spanish American periphery is felt as distant threat; no independent diplomatic capacity exists; the stance is defensive, inward-looking, focused on internal administration.

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Creole resentment grows as Bourbon reforms systematically favor peninsular Spaniards for high office; indigenous communities maintain outward compliance while inward grievance deepens over tribute increases and labor demands; public sentiment is cautious, watchful, increasingly strained; trust contracts along ethnic and status lines.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ Creole disaffection deepens as career paths narrow; indigenous communities harbor deep but unexpressed grievance; the combination creates potential energy for revolt; will is not spent but suppressed, accumulating pressure that will discharge in the Tupac Amaru rebellion.


## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Spanish remains the language of administration and commerce; Quechua and Aymara serve indigenous communities with some bilingual intermediaries; literacy remains confined to clerical, administrative, and commercial elites; the majority accesses public information through oral transmission and public proclamations read aloud; the shared code is stratified, not universal.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Official correspondence travels by mule courier along established routes, Lima to Cusco in two to three weeks, to Potosi in four to six weeks; the correo system improves under Bourbon administration; no newspaper exists until the Mercurio Peruano (1791, outside this window); transmission is slow, reliable for official channels, slower for private communication.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Administrative manuscript production expands dramatically as Bourbon reforms generate census records, tribute rolls, commercial registers, judicial proceedings, and inspection reports; notarial archives accumulate in Lima and provincial capitals; ecclesiastical records (baptism, marriage, death) continue systematic documentation; the Jesuit expulsion (1767) transfers vast documentary holdings to secular archives; persistence is systematic, survival rates high for official records.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ The Inquisition retains censorship authority over print and manuscript; Enlightenment texts circulate clandestinely among creole elites; scientific expeditions (Hipólito Ruiz botanical expedition 1777-1788) produce officially sanctioned secular knowledge; the permitted band remains narrow but widens slightly as Enlightenment influences penetrate; discourse norms remain formally Catholic, practically more diverse in elite circles.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Literate audiences remain small, perhaps 5-10% of the population; public proclamations reach broader audiences through oral transmission; rumors circulate rapidly through market networks and indigenous communities; amplification follows established social channels, privileging urban centers and commercial networks.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Official records maintain high integrity through notarial protocols and archival practices; oral transmission introduces distortion; the information load increases as reforms multiply documentation requirements; verification capacity keeps pace within official channels but cannot penetrate oral networks where rumor and misinformation circulate; noise load is moderate, concentrated in non-official channels.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Catholic moral teaching provides the official foundation; natural law arguments from scholastic tradition justify colonial hierarchy; indigenous communities maintain parallel moral frameworks emphasizing reciprocity and communal obligation; Enlightenment ideas of natural rights begin to circulate among creole elites; the sense of justice is plural, stratified by culture and education.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ The Laws of the Indies remain the foundational legal code; Bourbon reforms add layers of regulatory legislation, commercial ordinances, and administrative procedures; the Recopilación de las Leyes de Indias (1680) is supplemented by royal decrees (cédulas reales) that accumulate as manuscript copies in colonial archives; codification is active, expanding, increasingly complex.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ The Audiencia of Lima serves as supreme court; corregidores (later intendants) exercise local jurisdiction; ecclesiastical courts handle matrimonial and clerical matters; the Inquisition prosecutes heresy and some moral offenses; indigenous communities retain limited jurisdiction over internal disputes; adjudication is slow, expensive, favors those with resources to pursue litigation; sanction ranges from fines to imprisonment to corporal punishment to execution for serious crimes.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Indigenous communities retain legal protections under the Laws of the Indies, including communal land rights and prohibition of enslavement, but these are inconsistently enforced; compliance burden increases as Bourbon reforms multiply regulatory requirements; predictability of rules improves within official channels but local variation persists; rights are formal rather than effective for most of the population.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Corregidores notoriously abuse their positions through forced sales (repartimiento de mercancias) despite prohibition; tribute collectors extract unauthorized payments; mine operators evade labor regulations; impunity for powerful actors is endemic; indigenous petitions document grievances but rarely obtain effective redress; the justice gap is wide and widening as fiscal pressure intensifies.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Enlightenment natural philosophy reaches creole elites through clandestine circulation; Spanish scientific expeditions (Ruiz-Pavón botanical expedition, Alejandro Malaspina's later voyage) apply systematic observation to Andean flora, fauna, and geography; the University of San Marcos continues scholastic curriculum but some professors introduce modern ideas; the paradigm is transitional, scholasticism dominant but challenged.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Mining technology stagnates at established levels; agricultural practice continues traditional methods augmented by colonial introductions; textile production uses established techniques; no significant technical innovation originates locally; applied knowledge is conservative, adequate for existing production but not advancing.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ The University of San Marcos trains clergy, lawyers, and physicians; Jesuit colleges educate creole elites until the 1767 expulsion; literacy remains confined to perhaps 10% of the population, concentrated in urban centers and among clergy; the knowledge stock is substantial within elite circles, largely inaccessible to the majority; skill distribution is highly uneven.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Indigenous knowledge systems persist in oral tradition but transmission weakens as Spanish cultural hegemony deepens; quipu use has ceased except as antiquarian curiosity; some traditional agricultural and medical knowledge survives through practice; scholastic learning ossifies as Enlightenment alternatives emerge; obsolescence is gradual, loss continuing from earlier periods.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Creole identity crystallizes around American birth and resentment of peninsular privilege; indigenous communities maintain pre-Columbian memory through oral tradition, ritual, and encoded symbols; the Inca past becomes a resource for both creole patriotism and indigenous resistance; memory is plural, contested, politically charged.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ The colonial order is justified by Catholic mission, royal authority, and civilizing project; Bourbon reformers add Enlightenment rhetoric of rational administration and useful knowledge; indigenous subjects are framed as childlike wards requiring Spanish tutelage; the narrative remains officially unchallenged but creole intellectuals begin articulating alternative patriotic visions.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Baroque religious art continues in churches and convents with distinctively Andean inflections (Cuzco School painting); elite architecture follows Spanish models with local materials; indigenous textile traditions persist with syncretic imagery; the visual culture is richly layered, officially Catholic, privately preserving indigenous forms; color palette emphasizes gold, crimson, and deep blue in religious contexts, earth tones in domestic settings.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Baroque forms remain vital in religious contexts; colonial ceremonial retains meaning for participants; indigenous rituals maintain significance within communities; exhaustion is not yet evident; the symbolic vocabulary is stable, even expanding as creole patriotism develops new imagery.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Catholic cosmology provides the official frame, with heaven, hell, purgatory structuring afterlife expectations; indigenous communities maintain syncretic beliefs integrating Pachamama, mountain spirits (apus), and ancestor veneration with Catholic saints; fatalism and piety predominate; Enlightenment skepticism remains confined to tiny elite circles; the outlook is traditional, stable, providing meaning and orientation for most of the population.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Catholic sacraments (baptism, marriage, extreme unction) mark life transitions with extensive documentary record; daily devotion includes rosary, prayer, and saint veneration; indigenous festivals syncretize Catholic feast days with agricultural and ancestral rituals; pilgrimage to regional shrines continues; the sacred is enacted regularly, communally, binding society together across ethnic lines.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ The Archbishop of Lima heads the colonial church; religious orders (Franciscans, Dominicans, Augustinians, Mercedarians) maintain parishes, missions, and schools; the Jesuit expulsion (1767) removes the most intellectually active order and transfers substantial property; Inquisition authority persists but activity declines; doctrinal orthodoxy is enforced but accommodation of indigenous practice is pragmatic.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Catholicism is universal but syncretic, incorporating indigenous elements that the Church tolerates when not openly heterodox; no competing religious system is spreading; the dominant cosmology's reach is stable, perhaps deepening as indigenous populations internalize Catholic forms over generations.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Enlightenment ideas introduce incipient skepticism among tiny elite circles, but disenchantment is negligible for the vast majority; the sacred remains potent, meaningful, actively practiced; no significant drift toward secularism is yet evident; spiritual need is fully addressed within existing structures.
