# __1700 CE – 1780 CE__ — Bourbon Reforms and Administrative Rationalization
__Summary:__ The Andean viceroyalties under the new Bourbon dynasty experience intensified metropolitan control, fiscal extraction, and bureaucratic documentation across this eighty-year trend; the binding constraint is the crown's drive to maximize revenue from silver and tribute against entrenched colonial interests and indigenous resistance; the system retains margin through continued silver output and creole compliance but accumulates grievance as extraction pressure mounts; the trajectory is centralizing, rationalizing, and documentation-intensifying, setting conditions for crisis.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The same Andean cordillera, coastal desert, and altiplano impose transit friction; mule trains and tambo networks remain primary logistics; highland-to-port routes (Potosí-Arica, Cusco-Lima) carry silver and goods under administrative monitoring.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Generally stable climate with interannual ENSO-driven variability affecting coastal valleys and highland agriculture; no major secular shift documented; drought episodes stress provisioning periodically.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Seismic risk persists; the 1746 Lima-Callao earthquake and tsunami devastate the capital, killing thousands and requiring extensive reconstruction; volcanic and flood hazards episodic but absorbed.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Silver remains the dominant extractable resource; Potosí output declining from seventeenth-century peak but still significant; mercury supply from Huancavelica constrained; timber and fuel locally scarce in mining zones.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Mining tailings and mercury contamination accumulate around Potosí and Huancavelica; urban waste in Lima and provincial towns managed through colonial sanitation conventions; absorptive capacity adequate though degradation localized.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic disease (typhus, dysentery, respiratory illness) persists; epidemic outbreaks episodic; mortality elevated in mining zones and reducciones; general population recovering from earlier colonial demographic collapse.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Indigenous and mestizo populations subsist on Andean staples (potato, maize, quinoa); nutritional stress in mita labor drafts; elite diets adequate; stature and vitality reflect colonial stratification.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Colonial marriage patterns persist; indigenous endogamy encouraged by reducción structure; mestizo population growing; reproductive capacity stable.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population slowly recovering; indigenous numbers stabilizing after nadir; mestizo and creole sectors expanding; urban centers (Lima, Cusco, La Paz) growing modestly; density well below carrying ceiling.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Mita labor creates widowed households in source communities; dependency loads managed through ayllu structures; orphaned and aged absorbed within kinship networks; load significant but not overwhelming.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Indigenous communities maintain agricultural base; haciendas and obrajes supply regional markets; provisioning adequate in normal years; mita obligations and tribute extract surplus and stress household margins.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and llama muscle remain primary; water power for some milling; no technological breakthrough; per-capita work ceiling unchanged from earlier colonial period.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Mita labor drafts continue for Potosí and Huancavelica; wage labor expanding in some sectors; obraje textile workshops employ coerced and semi-free labor; coercion remains embedded but resistance mounting.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Silver production continues though below peak; textile obrajes, hacienda agriculture, artisan production generate surplus; productive capacity constrained by labor availability and mercury supply.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Colonial treasury accumulates for remittance to Spain; community granaries (in ayllu tradition) buffer subsistence; reserves adequate under normal conditions; shock absorption depends on harvest outcomes.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Commercial reform (culminating in 1778 free trade decree) opens more ports; internal trade networks through muleteering and fairs; Lima-Callao remains primary export node; logistics friction unchanged.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Silver coin (peso) as unit of account; merchant credit networks (consulado system) finance trade; church as major lender; credit access stratified; tribute and debt obligations structure indigenous exchange.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Bourbon reforms intensify extraction; tribute reassessments, alcabala (sales tax) increases, monopoly revenues (tobacco, aguardiente); intendancy system (1784) professionalizes collection; extraction load rising against productive base.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Wealth concentrated in peninsular merchants, creole hacendados, church; indigenous communities defend communal lands through legal petitions; concentration stable but creole resentment at peninsular privilege growing.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Mining and textile waste discharged locally; no major routing changes; accumulation continues but remains within regional absorptive capacity.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Indigenous ayllu reciprocity persists; cofradías (religious brotherhoods) provide mutual aid across strata; trust radius narrow beyond immediate community; colonial stratification limits cross-group solidarity.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Rigid caste system (peninsular, creole, mestizo, indigenous, African-descended) maintained; boundaries legally enforced though porous in practice; creole identity sharpening against peninsular privilege.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Spanish inheritance law (partible) applies; indigenous communities maintain parallel customs; household formation follows colonial patterns; kinship networks essential for economic and legal navigation.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Guilds regulate urban trades; cofradías organize religious and social life; indigenous cabildos persist in reducciones; associational fabric stable but subordinate to colonial structure.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Fiestas (religious and civic) structure communal life; bullfights, processions, and celebrations mark calendar; sociability channeled through church-sanctioned and colonial-permitted forms.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Legal petitions (indigenous communities to Audiencias), religious mediation, and fiesta serve as discharge channels; grievance accumulating as Bourbon extraction intensifies; discharge mechanisms strained by mid-century.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Bourbon centralization; viceroy in Lima exercises authority through corregidores (later intendants); Audiencias adjudicate; crown asserts direct control over colonial officials; centralizing trajectory.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Viceroys appointed from Madrid; corregidor/intendant appointments professional; no local succession crisis; metropolitan control routinized.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Crown legitimacy rested on divine right, patronage of church, and promise of order; Enlightenment reform rhetoric deployed to justify extraction; indigenous communities invoke royal justice against local abuses.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Intendancy reforms (1784) increase professionalization; visitas and censuses intensify documentation; corruption endemic but administrative reach expanding; manuscript bureaucracy thickening.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ No formal participation; petitions and legal cases provide narrow channels; creole elites excluded from highest offices; indigenous mobilization through community structures; discontent channeled but accumulating.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Spanish empire in relative decline; Bourbon alliances with France; colonial defense concerns (British threat); territorial reorganization (Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata, 1776) shifts administrative boundaries.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Colonial militias, small regular garrisons; no major military buildup until post-rebellion period; coercive capacity adequate for routine enforcement, untested against mass mobilization.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Banditry, local revolts episodic; major threat accumulating in indigenous grievance over mita, tribute, and corregidor abuses (repartimiento de mercancías); disorder rising toward rebellion threshold by 1780.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Manuscript correspondence via official couriers and commercial carriers; information travels Lima-Madrid in months; internal transmission along post roads; velocity unchanged from earlier colonial period.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Colonial archives (notarial, judicial, ecclesiastical, treasury) extensive; visitas, censuses, tax records accumulate; documentation intensifies under Bourbon reforms; retrieval capacity adequate for administrative purposes.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Inquisition censors printed and manuscript material; discourse constrained to Catholic orthodoxy and royal loyalty; Enlightenment texts circulate among elites despite prohibitions; censorship operational but porous.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Literate audience narrow (clergy, officials, elite); oral transmission amplifies news through markets and fiestas; rumor networks active; attention concentrated on local concerns.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Administrative records systematized; legal disputes generate contradictory documentation; noise manageable; verification through cross-referencing possible within bureaucratic system.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Catholic moral framework; indigenous communities maintain parallel justice conceptions rooted in reciprocity; tension between royal justice (theoretically protective) and local extraction abuses.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Laws of the Indies, royal cédulas, viceregal ordinances accumulate; Bourbon reforms add new regulations; legal corpus extensive and manuscript-based; codification efforts ongoing.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Audiencias (Lima, Charcas) hear appeals; corregidores/intendants administer local justice; ecclesiastical courts parallel; indigenous communities use legal system extensively; throughput strained but functional.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Indigenous communities theoretically protected as royal vassals; protective legislation unenforced locally; compliance burden (tribute, mita, repartimiento) heavy; gap between law and practice wide.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Corregidor abuses (forced sales, extraction) widespread; legal redress slow and uncertain; impunity for local officials endemic; grievance accumulates as redress mechanisms fail.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Enlightenment natural philosophy reaches colonial elites; scientific expeditions (Hipólito Ruiz, José Pavón botanical expedition 1777–1788) document natural history; scholastic frameworks persist in universities.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Mining techniques unchanged; mercury amalgamation continues; no major technical innovation; agricultural methods traditional; applied knowledge stable.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Universities (San Marcos) train clergy, lawyers, officials; literacy concentrated in elite and clerical strata; indigenous literacy rare; knowledge stock maintained through manuscript and oral transmission.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Indigenous knowledge (khipu reading, traditional medicine, agricultural techniques) eroding under colonial suppression; scholastic curriculum increasingly archaic; decay offset by manuscript preservation in archives and libraries.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Inca memory persists in indigenous communities; creole identity crystallizing around American birth and local saints; colonial baroque synthesis combines Iberian, indigenous, and African elements.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Cusco school painting continues; baroque church architecture and retablo production; textile and silver craft traditions; material culture reflects colonial hybrid forms.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Creole resentment at peninsular privilege simmering; indigenous grievance mounting under extraction pressure; surface compliance masking accumulating tension; mood darkening toward window's end.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Baroque forms ossifying into convention; religious art production routinized; colonial legitimacy narratives losing purchase as extraction intensifies; meaning decay evident in elite skepticism and popular disaffection.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Catholic cosmology dominant in public sphere; indigenous cosmological elements persist in syncretic forms; Enlightenment skepticism reaches educated elites; outlook mixed between piety and emerging secularism.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mass, confession, fiesta cycle structure religious life; indigenous communities integrate pre-Columbian elements into Catholic practice; devotional intensity high among popular strata.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Church wealthy and influential; Jesuit expulsion (1767) removes major institutional player, disrupts education and missions; secular clergy and other orders absorb functions; doctrinal authority stable but institutional landscape altered.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Syncretic Andean Catholicism stable; no major missionary expansion; indigenous practices persist under Catholic veneer; displacement completed in earlier period.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Elite skepticism growing under Enlightenment influence; popular piety robust; disenchantment concentrated in educated strata; sacred still potent for mobilization (as rebellion will demonstrate).<br><br>
