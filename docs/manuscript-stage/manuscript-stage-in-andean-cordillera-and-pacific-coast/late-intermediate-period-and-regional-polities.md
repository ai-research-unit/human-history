# __1000 CE – 1400 CE__ — Late Intermediate Period and Regional Polities
__Summary:__ Following the collapse of Wari and Tiwanaku, the Andean region fragments into competing regional polities — Chimú on the north coast, Chincha along the south coast, and highland kingdoms including the nascent Inca in Cusco — each maintaining administrative complexity and probable khipu record-keeping without imperial standardization; the binding constraint is military competition for productive valleys and trade routes, and the system holds variable margin against drought and warfare until Inca expansion absorbs most polities by 1400 CE; the trend is toward consolidation from fragmentation.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The same Andean cordillera and coastal desert strip as the prior window; fragmentation concentrates polities in defensible valleys and coastal oases; Chimú controls the Moche Valley irrigation complex, Chincha the Ica-Chincha littoral, Cusco Inca the intermontane basin; spatial friction favors regional autonomy over long-distance integration.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Climate stabilizes after the severe droughts that contributed to Middle Horizon collapse; ENSO cycles continue with moderate El Niño flooding and La Niña drought; the band widens compared to the preceding terminal phase but remains volatile.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Seismic and volcanic hazard unchanged; coastal polities exposed to El Niño flooding and sand encroachment; highland polities face frost and hail damage to crops; defensive margin rebuilt as populations recover and irrigation systems are restored.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Copper, silver, gold, Spondylus, and obsidian continue as key resources; camelid herds recover; coastal marine productivity high; endowment distribution favors regional specialization and exchange rather than self-sufficiency.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Population densities remain moderate; Chan Chan's urban concentration produces localized organic waste but within absorptive capacity; metallurgical activity leaves slag deposits without evidence of sink saturation.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic disease load comparable to prior period; no epidemic disruptions recorded; sanitary conditions adequate in dispersed settlements but Chan Chan's urban density may elevate localized pathogen exposure.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Nutritional status recovers after collapse-phase stress; coastal populations benefit from marine protein; highland populations maintain diverse agro-pastoral diets; skeletal evidence shows adequate stature and low anemia in stable phases.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Ayllu-based pairing continues; elite polygyny documented for Chimú and emerging Inca nobility; fertility adequate for demographic recovery and modest growth.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population recovers gradually from Middle Horizon collapse; Chan Chan reaches 30,000–60,000 at peak; highland populations dispersed but growing; density remains below Middle Horizon peaks until late in window.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Warfare increases male mortality among combatants; ayllu and state structures absorb widows and orphans; dependency load manageable but warfare-induced attrition concentrates in frontier zones.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Coastal irrigation agriculture (Chimú) and highland mixed farming maintain the provisioning floor above bare survival; fishing, camelid herding, and vertical-archipelago strategies buffer regional shortfalls; household pooling within ayllus continues.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and llama muscle remain the conversion regime; Chimú irrigation systems capture hydrological energy indirectly; per-capita work ceiling unchanged; no new prime movers introduced.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Regional states continue mit'a-style rotational labor; Chimú organizes craft specialists (metalworkers, weavers) in attached workshops; coercion levels moderate within redistributive frameworks; Inca mit'a emerges in Cusco valley.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Chimú metal and textile production reaches high sophistication; agricultural surplus supports urban population at Chan Chan; surplus capacity rebuilt but distributed across competing polities rather than unified.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Regional storehouses continue but smaller than Middle Horizon imperial systems; khipu-based inventory likely maintained; reserves adequate for normal variation but inter-polity warfare disrupts buffer accumulation.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Long-distance exchange persists; Chincha emerges as maritime trading power with balsa rafts reaching Ecuador; Spondylus, metals, coca, and textiles circulate; market exchange more prominent on coast than highlands.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Textiles and metals serve as value stores; Chincha maritime traders may use standardized exchange equivalents; deferred reciprocity continues within ayllus; no monetary innovation.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Regional lords extract tribute through labor and goods; Chimú noble compounds at Chan Chan concentrate redistributive flows; extraction sustainable where military competition does not drain reserves.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Elite wealth visible in Chan Chan compounds, Chimú metalwork, and mortuary goods; stratification sharpens compared to prior period; asset defense through fortification and military retinue.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Organic waste recycled; metallurgical byproducts localized; urban Chan Chan generates concentrated refuse but within absorptive capacity; no accumulation crisis.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Ayllu reciprocity structures persist; inter-polity warfare reduces trust radius across political boundaries; mutual aid remains robust within communities but attenuates between competing kingdoms.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Chimú nobility sharply differentiated from commoners; highland societies maintain ethnic-group identities; Inca lineage (panaca) system crystallizes in Cusco; boundaries harden under military competition.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Bilateral descent and ayllu structures continue; elite succession increasingly formalized; Inca split inheritance (panaca system) emerges, requiring each ruler to conquer new territory; marriage alliances cement political federations.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Ayllus maintain self-governance for internal affairs; moiety organizations persist; urban quarters at Chan Chan organized by occupation and origin; associational fabric adapts to state demands.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Ritual feasting, chicha consumption, and music/dance continue; Chimú elite sponsor elaborate ceremonies; Inca ritual calendar develops in Cusco; warfare introduces captive sacrifice into some coastal traditions.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Internal disputes mediated by ayllu and kuraka; inter-polity grievances discharged through warfare; ritual combat persists; unmetabolized strain accumulates at polity boundaries and among conquered populations.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Fragmented sovereignty across regional kingdoms; Chimú centralized under hereditary rulers (Chimú Capac) at Chan Chan; highland polities smaller and more numerous; Inca consolidate Cusco valley and begin expansion under Viracocha Inca and Pachacuti by late window.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Chimú succession hereditary within ruling lineage; Inca panaca system develops with split inheritance driving expansionism; transition points generate instability and succession conflicts.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Chimú rulers claim divine descent and ocean associations; Inca develop Inti (sun) cult and origin narratives from Lake Titicaca and Pacaritambo; legitimacy tied to military success and redistributive generosity.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Chimú maintain sophisticated administration with probable khipu use; regional variation in recording systems; Inca khipu standardization begins late in window; capacity adequate for regional control but not yet imperial scale.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Participation channeled through ayllu representation; no popular assemblies; mobilization for warfare and labor drafts organized by lords; commoner voice limited to local disputes.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Competitive multi-polity system with shifting alliances; Chimú-Chincha rivalry and cooperation; highland kingdoms contest valleys; Inca expansion transforms the system by 1400 CE.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Regional armies using maces, slings, spear-throwers, and bronze weapons; fortifications (pukaras) proliferate in contested zones; force distributed among competing polities rather than monopolized.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Internal order maintained within polities; inter-polity warfare is chronic; banditry and raiding in frontier zones; disorder concentrated at boundaries rather than within cores.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Messenger systems within each polity; velocity constrained by terrain and political fragmentation; no unified relay network until Inca expansion; reach limited to polity boundaries.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Khipu likely maintained by regional khipukamayuq without imperial standardization; Chimú administrative records probable but unrecovered; textile encoding continues; persistence depends on specialist lineages.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Regional iconographic programs propagate local narratives; Chimú moon/sea imagery distinct from highland traditions; no inter-polity censorship apparatus; discourse norms embedded in ritual contexts.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Reception concentrated at regional capitals; Chan Chan's compounds focus elite attention; amplification through feasting and ceremony; commoner exposure limited to local ritual.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Regional variation introduces inconsistency; no standardized verification across polities; signal integrity maintained locally through specialist training; noise accumulates across political boundaries.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Reciprocity norms persist; violations punished by community and state; inter-polity conflict operates outside shared moral framework; justice internal to each political unit.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Customary law administered by kuraka and regional lords; Chimú edicts govern Chan Chan; Inca legal traditions crystallize in Cusco; no cross-polity codification until Inca conquest.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Disputes resolved by ayllu elders and regional authorities; enforcement through community sanction and state coercion; collective liability for tribute; inter-polity disputes resolved by warfare.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Rights embedded in ayllu membership; compliance burden includes labor tribute and military service; predictability varies by polity; conquered populations face heavier burdens.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Elite impunity probable; inter-polity violence generates unredressed wrongs among defeated populations; standing gap correlates with military fortune; some wrongs addressed through revenge warfare.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Practical astronomy for agricultural calendars; metallurgical experimentation continues; explanatory paradigms remain embedded in cosmological frameworks; regional variation in specialist knowledge.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Chimú metallurgy reaches peak sophistication (arsenical bronze, gold/silver alloys); irrigation engineering maintains coastal systems; textile techniques continue; reproducible methods transmitted through specialist lineages.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Apprenticeship within craft and administrative lineages; khipu knowledge concentrated among specialists; literacy (if applicable) restricted; skill distribution concentrated but regionally redundant.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Some Middle Horizon techniques lost; regional knowledge pools develop independently; Inca conquest will later absorb and standardize; decay offset by regional innovation.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Regional origin narratives consolidate; Chimú claim emergence from sea; Inca develop Manco Capac origin cycle; huaca devotion anchors local identities; ethnic-group memories persist.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Chimú blackware ceramics, elaborate metalwork, and Chan Chan adobe architecture define coastal style; highland polities maintain distinct traditions; Inca stonework emerges in Cusco; regional diversity characterizes the period.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Competitive atmosphere as polities vie for advantage; confidence among expanding states (late-window Inca); defensive anxiety in polities under pressure; mood varies by regional fortune.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Regional forms vital and evolving; no pan-Andean exhaustion given fragmentation; Chimú forms reach elaboration that Inca conquest will later simplify and standardize; meaning systems localized but charged.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Three-tiered cosmos persists; regional variations on creator deities and ancestor veneration; Inca Inti cult consolidates; fatalism balanced by ritual efficacy; cosmic reciprocity remains central.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Offerings to huacas, ancestors, and regional deities; Chimú sacrifice rituals include humans; Inca capacocha develops; chicha libations and textile offerings universal; calendrical ceremonies mark agricultural cycles.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Regional priesthoods administer cults; oracle sites (Pachacamac) transcend polity boundaries; Inca priesthood formalizes in Cusco; doctrinal authority fragmented across polities.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Regional cults maintain boundaries; Pachacamac oracle attracts pan-regional pilgrimage; Inca Inti cult begins expansion with military conquest; syncretism follows political incorporation.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Sacred frameworks remain vital; no evidence of disenchantment; spiritual needs addressed through intensified regional devotion; competition may heighten rather than erode cosmological commitment.<br><br>
