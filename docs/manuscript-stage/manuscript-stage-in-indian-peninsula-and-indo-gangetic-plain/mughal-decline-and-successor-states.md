# __1707 CE – 1765 CE__ — Mughal Decline and Successor States
__Summary:__ A half-century of imperial dissolution following Aurangzeb's death, during which effective sovereignty fragments among successor states while the Mughal emperor becomes a ceremonial figurehead; binding constraint is the collapsed fiscal-military nexus as provincial governors, Marathas, and Afghans vie for revenue and territory; margin against external shock eliminated by 1739 when Nadir Shah sacks Delhi and by 1757 when Ahmad Shah Durrani repeats the devastation; trajectory moves from contested succession through regional consolidation to European commercial penetration culminating in the Company's Bengal diwani (1765).
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Subcontinent remains arena; Gangetic heartland fought over; Deccan under Maratha expansion; Bengal's riverine terrain favors local powers and European coastal positions; mountain passes admit Afghan invasions; spatial friction now works against any single hegemon.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Monsoon regime unchanged; regional droughts continue to disrupt revenue and provisioning; 1769–70 Bengal famine (just beyond window) prefigured by earlier local scarcities; volatility strains weak successor administrations.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Floods, droughts, localized famines; hazard exposure unchanged but weakened state capacity reduces buffering; armies and populations more vulnerable than under consolidated Mughal rule.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Endowment intact; Bengal's fertility and textile production attract European interest; Deccan's revenue base contested; forests, minerals remain but extraction disrupted by warfare; resources redistributed among competing powers.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Urban warfare damages infrastructure; some cities (Delhi) devastated by invasions; waste absorption capacity unchanged physically but social disruption reduces maintenance; localized degradation around war-damaged cities.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic diseases persist; warfare spreads disease along marching routes; famines and sieges produce mortality spikes; no new epidemics but baseline mortality elevated in war zones.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Agricultural disruption degrades nutrition in contested areas; Bengal remains productive; Awadh, Hyderabad relatively stable; nutritional vitality tracks regional security; uneven across fragmented polity.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Patterns unchanged; war casualties skew sex ratios locally; elite households continue polygyny; no shift in reproductive biology; population growth slowed by warfare mortality.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population stagnant or slightly declining in war-affected regions; Bengal, Awadh maintain density; Delhi and Gangetic plain depopulated by invasions and warfare; age structure stressed by male casualties.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ War widows, orphans, displaced persons accumulate; charitable institutions (Sufi, Sikh, Hindu) absorb some; state incapable of systematic relief; dependency overhang significant in worst-affected areas.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Peasant agriculture continues; revenue demands contested among multiple claimants; subsistence squeezed in war zones; Bengal relatively prosperous; floor adequate where order maintained, precarious elsewhere.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ No change in energy regime; human and animal muscle; agricultural surplus still generated but diverted to military competition rather than accumulation; per-capita work ceiling unchanged.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Peasant labor under multiple revenue claimants; weavers in Bengal increasingly tied to European factors; artisan production continues; coercion shifts from Mughal center to regional powers and Company buyers.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Bengal textiles, Awadh agriculture, Deccan trade continue; overall production maintained in stable regions; surplus contested rather than centrally accumulated; productive capacity intact but not coordinated.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Imperial treasury exhausted; regional states accumulate locally; Nadir Shah and Ahmad Shah Durrani carry off Delhi's remaining treasure; buffering capacity fragmented and inadequate against major shocks.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Internal trade disrupted by warfare and toll extraction; coastal trade flourishes under European protection; Bengal-Europe trade expands; logistics fragmented; Surat declines, Calcutta rises.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Mughal rupee loses uniformity; regional currencies proliferate; shroff networks adapt; European bills of exchange enter; capital flows toward Company-controlled areas; credit system resilient but fragmenting.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Multiple claimants extract from same peasantry; Marathas levy chauth and sardeshmukhi; Awadh and Hyderabad nawabs collect tribute; extraction intensifies, redistribution collapses into warlord competition.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Concentration shifts from Delhi to regional courts and European trading posts; nobles diversify holdings; merchant wealth seeks Company protection; asset defense via mobility, diversification, and alignment with rising powers.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Craft production waste unchanged; war debris and unburied dead in conflict zones; routing informal; sink capacity adequate physically but social disruption prevents maintenance; localized degradation.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Trust radius contracts amid political uncertainty; caste and kin networks remain primary; inter-community trust damaged by succession wars; regional identities (Maratha, Sikh, Jat) strengthen; mutual aid localizes.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Maratha, Rajput, Afghan military identities compete; regional nawabi courts create local elites; caste hierarchy intact; Hindu-Muslim relations variable by region; European presence adds new status gradations.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Patterns unchanged; elite families hedge across factions; marriage alliances realign to new power centers; inheritance customs continue; joint family absorbs war losses.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Caste panchayats, guilds, religious associations persist; Sikh Khalsa emerges as militant brotherhood; Maratha Brahmin administrative networks expand; self-organization fills state vacuum.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Regional courts maintain culture; Lucknow, Hyderabad develop distinctive styles; festivals continue; sociability retreats from war zones to stable courts; nawabi culture flourishes in Awadh.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Petitioning fragmented among multiple courts; religious mediation continues; armed resistance (Maratha, Sikh, Jat) becomes grievance channel; discharge uneven; some grievances accumulate as standing instability.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Mughal emperor nominal sovereign; real power with Marathas (Peshwa at Pune), Nizam of Hyderabad, Nawab of Awadh, Nawab of Bengal; multiple competing centers; authority fragmenting.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Mughal succession wars 1707–19 produce eleven emperors in twelve years; regional dynasties more stable; Peshwa succession orderly; transfer mechanisms vary by polity; Delhi succession a flashpoint.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Mughal farman still legitimates; regional rulers claim autonomy under nominal Mughal sovereignty; Marathas invoke Hindu revival; Sikhs invoke Guru's legacy; multiple competing legitimacy narratives.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Mughal center collapses; regional administrations variable; Awadh and Hyderabad maintain Persian bureaucracy; Maratha administration efficient; Bengal increasingly under Company influence; capacity fragmenting.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Maratha confederacy involves multiple sardars; Sikh confederacy (misl system) disperses authority; elite participation in regional courts; mass mobilization via Maratha light cavalry, Sikh armed community; participation broadens locally.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Afghan invasions (Nadir Shah 1739, Ahmad Shah 1747–61) devastate north; Marathas push to Delhi then destroyed at Panipat (1761); Europeans expand coastal influence; external pressures fragment subcontinent.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Multiple armies; Maratha light cavalry dominant; Afghan heavy cavalry defeats Marathas; Mughal artillery captured by invaders; European-trained sepoy armies emerging; force dispersed among competing powers.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Widespread banditry; warfare endemic; Thuggee active; no single authority maintains order; threat environment high; disorder normalized in contested zones; stable areas under strong regional rulers.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Imperial postal system fragments; regional systems emerge; akhbarat continue but report to multiple courts; velocity unchanged technically but coordination lost; Europeans develop own networks.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Regional courts maintain records; Maratha Peshwa archives extensive; Hyderabad, Awadh continue Persian documentation; manuscript production continues in stable courts; some loss in war-damaged areas.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ No central framing; regional courts control local discourse; multiple narratives compete; Persian remains administrative language; censorship localized; discourse space more varied than under centralized empire.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Elite audiences fragment by region; oral transmission continues; regional literatures (Urdu poetry in Lucknow, Marathi chronicles) flourish; attention localizes; amplification via regional networks.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Multiple conflicting reports from fragmented polity; signal integrity declines; noise increases as coordinating authority collapses; verification difficult across political boundaries.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Regional variation; Maratha courts invoke dharma; Muslim states continue Sharia; sense of justice fragments along political lines; shared moral foundation weakened but not collapsed.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Fatawa-i-Alamgiri continues in Muslim states; Maratha customary law; Sikh codes emerging; no new comprehensive codification; legal evolution localized; regulatory fragmentation.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Qazis in Muslim areas; panchayats and Brahmin judges in Hindu areas; enforcement depends on local power; liability assigned per regional custom; adjudication functions but inconsistently across boundaries.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Variable by region; Hindu subjects better protected under Marathas; Muslims under nawabs; compliance burden depends on local extraction intensity; predictability within regions, chaos across them.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Invaders unpunished; warlords beyond reach; backlog in disrupted courts; impunity widespread in contested zones; redress available in stable regions; overall gap between justice owed and delivered widening.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Persian-Islamic learning continues in Awadh, Hyderabad; Sanskrit scholarship in Maratha areas; no paradigm shift; traditional inquiry maintained in stable courts; investigation disrupted in war zones.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Architecture, textile production, agriculture continue; techniques transmitted via guilds; no new technical methods; European technical influence beginning in coastal areas; military technology borrowed.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Madrasas, pathshalas continue where patronage survives; Lucknow emerges as learning center; literacy stable among traditional elites; knowledge stock maintained in stable regions, threatened in war zones.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Some manuscript collections destroyed (Delhi sack); institutional continuity broken in worst-affected areas; traditional learning maintained in Awadh, Hyderabad, Pune; decay localized rather than systemic.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Maratha identity invokes Shivaji's legacy; Sikh memory of martyred Gurus; Mughal nostalgia among Muslim elites; regional identities crystallize; composite Mughal culture fragments into distinct streams.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Lucknow, Hyderabad develop distinctive styles; Pahari painting flourishes in hills; Deccan painting continues; architecture less monumental; manuscript production continues in nawabi courts; regionalization of artistic expression.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Anxiety in war zones; relative confidence in stable courts; nostalgia for Mughal order; pragmatic adaptation to new powers; mood variable by region; resilience in population accustomed to instability.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Mughal court forms continue but lose power as center collapses; regional adaptations renew some forms (Lucknawi refinement); syncretic symbols contested; meaning decay in former imperial symbols, renewal in regional ones.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Plural cosmologies; Sunni, Shia, Hindu, Sikh coexist; fatalism amid political chaos; devotional traditions provide solace; existential outlook shaped by regional dominance; no unified cosmological shift.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mosque, temple, gurdwara practice continues; pilgrimage disrupted by warfare in some areas; Muharram processions elaborate in Lucknow; devotional intensity high as political order collapses; ritual provides stability amid chaos.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Ulema influential in Muslim courts; Brahmin authority in Maratha domains; Sikh Khalsa consolidates; temple and mosque institutions persist; doctrinal authority localized to regional powers.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Syncretic traditions continue at popular level; political boundaries harden religious identities; Sikh identity sharpens; no displacement but hardening of boundaries between traditions.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ No secularization; sacred remains potent; political chaos interpreted through religious frames; spiritual need met within traditions; no disenchantment; sacred energy channeled into regional and communal identities.<br><br>
