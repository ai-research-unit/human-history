# __1220 CE – 1370 CE__ — Mongol Conquest and Chagatai Khanate Administration

__Summary:__ The Mongol destruction of Transoxiana — Bukhara, Samarkand, Urgench, and Merv sacked, populations massacred or deported, manuscripts burned — followed by gradual recovery under Chagatai Khanate rule, where the binding constraint of Manuscript Stage administration reasserts itself: Mongol chancery practice using Uighur script coexists with and eventually yields to the restoration of Persian bureaucratic tradition, and written records again become the indispensable medium through which authority is exercised over the recovering urban civilization.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The river basins remain fixed — the Amu Darya and Syr Darya, the Zeravshan oases, the Ferghana Valley, the desert corridors — but the Mongol conquest transforms their meaning. Walls that once defended cities lie in rubble. Irrigation infrastructure is damaged and labor to maintain it killed or scattered. The spatial arena must be rebuilt; friction rises as routes that once carried trade now carry armies and administrative emptiness.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The transition from Medieval Warm Period toward Little Ice Age conditions begins during this window, though effects are gradual. Semi-arid continental climate persists. No dramatic climate shift explains the devastation — that is human work. Cooling trends may marginally reduce agricultural potential in later decades, but climate is not the constraining factor.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Natural hazards pale beside the human catastrophe. Earthquakes, floods, and droughts continue at background rates, but the hazard that matters is military — the Mongol destruction of 1219-1221 and subsequent punitive campaigns when populations resist. Urban vulnerability to siege proves total; the region's fortifications offer no protection against Mongol siege craft and numbers.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ The physical endowment remains — irrigable land, water, minerals, pastoral hinterland — but the human capacity to exploit it is shattered. Paper production at Samarkand ceases until recovery begins. Pastoral resources are less affected; the steppe economy survives better than the urban-agricultural complex. Recovery over the window gradually restores productive capacity.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ The catastrophic population decline paradoxically relieves pressure on sinks. Abandoned fields and depopulated cities leave the landscape to recover. Irrigation canals that silted up without maintenance later require labor to restore. The environmental residue of the destruction is abandonment rather than pollution.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ War, massacre, and displacement elevate mortality catastrophically during the conquest phase (1219-1221). Cities lose 75-90% of their populations to killing, flight, and deportation. The survivors face elevated disease risk from disrupted sanitation and famine conditions. Endemic disease resumes its background toll during recovery; no major plague outbreak occurs until the Black Death's passage through Central Asia in the 1340s, which further culls the recovering population.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Famine conditions accompany the conquest as agricultural production collapses and stored grain is seized or destroyed. Recovery over decades restores adequate nutrition for survivors. Pastoral populations fare better; meat and dairy sustain nomads while settled agriculture rebuilds. By mid-window, nutritional status normalizes for the reduced population.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ The massacre of urban populations includes physicians, scholars, and craftsmen. Medical knowledge survives in manuscripts where those survive, and in the training of survivors, but institutional capacity is wrecked. Recovery requires rebuilding the learned class. Healing capacity remains degraded for decades; folk practice fills gaps. By window's end, Persian medical tradition has partially recovered in restored urban centers.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ The conquest disrupts pairing dramatically — men killed, women captured, families scattered. Islamic marriage norms persist among surviving Muslim populations. Mongol pairing practices — different inheritance rules, different status for women — obtain among the ruling stratum. The demographics of recovery require high fertility among survivors; population regrowth is slow but cumulative across the window.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population collapse is the defining demographic fact. Pre-conquest Transoxiana may have held 2-4 million people; the immediate aftermath perhaps one-tenth that in some zones. Gradual recovery across 150 years partially restores population; migration from less-affected regions contributes. By 1370, urban centers have rebuilt to substantial size, though probably not pre-conquest levels. Density remains below the carrying ceiling throughout.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ The conquest creates a massive dependency burden — widows, orphans, the wounded and traumatized. But the scale of killing also removes much of the normal dependent load; the survivors skew toward the young and able. The paradox of catastrophe: fewer dependents relative to the productive cohort among survivors, though the absolute productive base is shattered.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ The floor collapses during the conquest, as agriculture is disrupted, stored grain seized, and irrigation abandoned. Famine conditions prevail in the immediate aftermath. Recovery over decades rebuilds the provisioning base; irrigation is restored, fields are re-cultivated, livestock herds regenerate. By mid-window, the surviving population is adequately provisioned; by window's end, surplus generation resumes.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ The energy base is depopulated rather than destroyed. Muscle power — human and animal — remains the conversion mechanism. The Mongols bring horses in vast numbers; equine energy serves military and pastoral purposes. As population recovers, the human energy base expands. Water mills are rebuilt along restored canals. The per-capita work ceiling is unchanged; the aggregate work capacity gradually recovers.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Mongol conquest imposes new forms of coercion. Artisans are deported to Mongolia and other centers — forced relocation of skilled labor. Corvee obligations for reconstruction add to extraction. The darugha (Mongol governors) oversee extraction for the khanate. As recovery proceeds, labor relations normalize toward pre-conquest patterns: peasant cultivation, guild-organized craft production, various forms of dependent labor. Slave labor remains significant.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Productive capacity is devastated by the conquest — workshops destroyed, artisans killed or deported, commercial networks severed. Recovery is gradual: craft production resumes in rebuilt cities, agricultural surplus grows as irrigation is restored, trade slowly revives. By the mid-14th century, surplus generation supports renewed court culture and religious patronage, though probably below pre-conquest peaks. The window ends with Timur's new conquests beginning, which will push the region into another cycle of destruction and rebuilding.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Mongol seizure of treasuries and granaries eliminates accumulated reserves. Recovery must rebuild from zero. Gradual accumulation across the window restores reserves: treasury stocks under the Chagatai khans, merchant capital in reviving commercial centers, grain stores against famine. By window's end, buffering capacity is functional if not robust.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ The Silk Road is disrupted but not destroyed. Mongol unification of the steppe — the Pax Mongolica — eventually facilitates trans-Eurasian exchange under a single imperial system. Merchants (including the famous Polo family) traverse the routes under Mongol protection. Samarkand and Bukhara gradually resume their entrepot functions. By the 14th century, trade flows have substantially recovered, though the Black Death's transit and political fragmentation of the Mongol Empire disrupt them again late in the window.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Mongol fiscal practice introduces paper currency (chao) in some contexts, though it does not fully displace metallic money in Transoxiana. Silver and gold continue to circulate. Commercial credit resumes as merchant networks rebuild. The debasement and instability of the immediate post-conquest period gives way to more stable monetary conditions as the khanate consolidates.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Mongol extraction is heavy and initially predatory — tribute demands, seizure of goods, forced labor. The yam (postal relay) system, maintained by local populations, exemplifies the extraction burden. As khanate rule stabilizes, extraction systematizes: qubchur (poll tax), tamgha (commercial tax), agricultural levies. The system is extractive but not economy-destroying once the conquest phase ends; it leaves enough for gradual recovery.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ The conquest is a massive wealth-leveling event — urban elites killed or impoverished, their property seized or destroyed. The new wealth concentrators are the Mongol military elite. As recovery proceeds, new urban elites emerge; Persian bureaucrats, major merchants, and religious establishments accumulate. Waqf endowments resume their protective function. By window's end, inequality has reconstituted along familiar patterns.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Reduced population and production mean reduced waste generation. The byproduct of the conquest is ruin — corpses, rubble, abandoned infrastructure — which time, scavengers, and weather gradually clear. As production resumes, normal waste routing resumes with it. No new environmental burden emerges.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Mongol conquest disrupts kinship networks through killing and deportation, but the structures persist among survivors. Islamic inheritance law continues among Muslim populations; Mongol succession practices govern the ruling stratum. The recovery period sees reconstitution of extended households, marriage alliances, and lineage networks. By window's end, kinship structures have normalized.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Craft guilds and neighborhood associations are shattered by urban destruction but reform as cities rebuild. Sufi orders survive and expand — their decentralized structure proves resilient; lodges (khanaqah) provide organization and mutual aid during the recovery. Charitable institutions (waqf) are re-endowed as surplus permits. The associational fabric is thinner mid-window than before or after, but it regenerates.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Religious festivals, poetry gatherings, and communal life resume as recovery permits. Mongol elite culture introduces new forms — the hunt, courtly entertainments in Mongol style — alongside Persian traditions. Friday prayers anchor weekly sociability; bazaars resume their social function as commerce revives. The texture of social life recovers across the window.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ The Mongol conquest imposes a new elite — the Mongol military aristocracy — atop the surviving population. Over time, this elite Islamizes and Turkicizes, intermarrying with local populations and adopting Persian administrative culture. The old hierarchy of Turkic military elite, Persian bureaucrats, ulama, merchants, artisans, and peasants reconstitutes beneath the Mongol overlay. Ethnic boundaries blur as Mongol, Turkic, and Tajik elements intermix in the upper strata.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Mongol administration introduces the yarliq (decree) system, Uighur-script chancery practice, and the darugha (governor) institution. This coexists uneasily with Persian bureaucratic tradition. As Chagatai khans convert to Islam (completed by the early 14th century), Persian administrative practice reasserts dominance. The manuscript-based chancery — tax registers, land documents, correspondence in Persian — becomes again the medium of governance. Administrative capacity rebuilds, though it remains thinner than the pre-conquest Khwarazmian apparatus until Timur's consolidation.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Mongol military supremacy is total during the conquest and immediate aftermath. The decimal organization (tumen, mingghan, etc.), composite bow cavalry, siege engineering, and disciplined command structure that conquered the region remain the dominant military model. As the Chagatai khanate fragments in the 14th century, military capacity diffuses among competing factions. Local strongmen and warlords proliferate; Timur emerges from this milieu to reunify the region by conquest at window's end.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ The conquest is the ultimate discharge failure — total social breakdown, mass death, civilizational collapse in the urban centers. Recovery is the long reconstitution of order from this nadir. Banditry and warlordism persist in the fragmentation period (mid-14th century); disorder rises as central authority weakens. Timur's campaigns at window's end impose order through violence, beginning a new cycle.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Terror, grief, and despair characterize the conquest period. Survivors are traumatized; communal memory of massacre shapes regional consciousness for generations. Recovery brings gradual normalization — the temper stabilizes, trust rebuilds, commerce and daily life resume. By mid-window, urban populations have largely recovered functional public mood, though the memory of catastrophe persists as caution.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Genghis Khan's authority is absolute during the conquest — a force of nature that admits no resistance. The Chagatai khans who follow are lesser figures, their authority diluted by distance, succession struggles, and fragmentation. Islamization of the khans enhances their legitimacy among Muslim subjects. The 14th-century fragmentation disperses authority among competing strongmen; Timur's rise represents the reconcentration of personal authority through military charisma and success.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Mongol rule initially commands obedience through terror, not legitimacy. Gradual Islamization and normalization build actual allegiance over decades. By the 14th century, the Chagatai khans have achieved workable legitimacy among their subjects — they rule as Muslim sovereigns in the Persianate tradition. Fragmentation erodes this; the period before Timur is one of legitimacy crisis, with multiple claimants and no settled authority.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ The Mongol state displays overwhelming political will during the conquest — unified, purposeful, irresistible. The Chagatai successor state is less cohesive; succession struggles and east-west divisions sap collective will. The fragmentation period sees political will diffused among warlords and factions. Timur's rise represents the reconcentration of will in a single figure with the capacity to unify action.

__External Relations & Posture:__ The Chagatai khanate is one node in the Mongol imperial system — subordinate in theory to the Great Khan, in practice increasingly autonomous. Relations with the other khanates (Golden Horde, Ilkhanate, Yuan) shape policy. The Pax Mongolica facilitates trans-Eurasian exchange while Mongol imperial structure holds. Fragmentation turns attention inward; external relations become secondary to internal power struggles until Timur's expansionary campaigns redirect energy outward.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ The conquest breeds helpless submission rather than disaffection — resistance is futile and suicidal. Recovery brings renewed engagement; populations actively rebuild rather than subsiding into apathy. The fragmentation period breeds uncertainty and localized loyalties rather than system-wide disaffection. The will to survive and recover proves resilient.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Multilingual complexity characterizes the period. Persian remains the administrative and literary lingua franca; Arabic the language of religion; Turkic vernaculars serve military and pastoral populations; Mongolian is the prestige language of the conquerors. Uighur script carries Mongolian administrative documents; Persian script carries Persian and Arabic. Literacy is disrupted by the massacre of the learned class but rebuilds as madrasa education resumes. The literate proportion declines then recovers across the window.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ The Mongol yam (postal relay) system provides rapid official communication across the empire — mounted couriers changing horses at stations maintained by local populations. This infrastructure survives into the Chagatai period. Commercial correspondence travels with caravans. The conquest-era disruption of channels gives way to restored connectivity under the Pax Mongolica; fragmentation degrades it again late in the window.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Manuscript culture suffers catastrophic loss during the conquest. Libraries in Bukhara, Samarkand, Urgench, and other cities are destroyed; manuscripts burned as fuel, trampled, dispersed. Scholars who carry texts in memory are killed. The loss is immense — many works survive only in copies that escaped eastward to India or westward to Persia. Recovery requires the copying and importation of texts, the training of new scribes, the rebuilding of libraries. This occurs gradually across the window; by the Timurid period that follows, manuscript culture is flourishing again, but the conquest-era losses are never fully recovered.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Mongol rule imposes no ideological censorship — the conquerors are initially religiously indifferent, later Muslim converts who patronize Islamic learning. Persian court culture and its discourse norms resume as recovery proceeds. Religious heterodoxy remains marginal; Sunni orthodoxy dominates. The permitted band of discourse is similar to the pre-conquest period once recovery is underway.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Sermon, recitation, and oral transmission resume their roles as populations reconstitute. Manuscript copying and circulation rebuild among the literate elite. The audience for written culture is smaller mid-window than before the conquest; it recovers as the learned class regenerates. Popular reception remains mediated through oral channels.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ The conquest introduces massive noise — destruction, displacement, lost records, broken transmission. Recovery reduces noise as institutions stabilize and record-keeping resumes. By window's end, the systems of verification and authentication familiar to Persianate manuscript culture are operational again. Surveillance remains limited to political sensitivity.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ The conquest shatters the sense that the world is just — massacre on this scale defies theodicy. Islamic explanations frame it as divine punishment for sin or inscrutable providence. Recovery requires rebuilding the moral order; the gradual restoration of sharia, settled governance, and predictable rules reconstitutes the sense that justice is possible. By window's end, the moral universe has restabilized around familiar Islamic categories.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Mongol conquest brings the yasa (Genghis Khan's law code) as an overlay on local legal traditions. The yasa governs Mongol subjects; sharia continues for Muslim populations. As the Chagatai khans Islamize, sharia's scope expands and the yasa's application narrows. By the 14th century, sharia is the dominant legal framework in urban centers; Mongol customary law persists in pastoral areas and among the military elite. The plural legal system gradually converges toward sharia dominance.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Mongol justice is initially military and summary — commanders adjudicate, sanctions are harsh. Sharia courts resume operation as urban life recovers; qadis adjudicate, muftis advise. The dual system — Mongol military justice and sharia civil and criminal law — characterizes much of the window. By its end, sharia courts handle most matters affecting settled populations.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Conquest eliminates legal protections; might makes right during the military phase. Recovery restores sharia protections — property rights, contract enforcement, personal security for Muslims, dhimmi status for non-Muslims. Compliance burden is moderate once stability returns; the legal framework is predictable enough for commerce and accumulation to resume.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ The massacre goes unredressed — there is no mechanism to hold the conquerors accountable for mass killing. This is the supreme impunity of conquest. As khanate rule normalizes, more ordinary impunity emerges — powerful figures escape sanction, the connected evade judgment. But the sharia system clears most routine wrongs; impunity is not total once recovery is underway. The historical wrong of the conquest remains a wound in collective memory rather than a redressable grievance.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Intellectual activity collapses during the conquest as scholars are killed and institutions destroyed. Recovery is slow; surviving scholars and manuscripts form the nucleus of renewal. The Mongol period sees no major paradigm shift — the Aristotelian-Ptolemaic-Islamic synthesis persists. Inquiry resumes in astronomy, mathematics, medicine, and religious sciences as madrasas reopen. By window's end, the intellectual tradition is functioning, preparing the ground for the Timurid golden age to follow.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Craft knowledge is severely depleted by the deportation and killing of artisans. Recovery requires the training of new craftsmen; techniques are partially lost, partially reconstructed from surviving practitioners and from imports. Mongol technology transfers — papermaking techniques, certain craft skills brought from China — partially compensate. By window's end, applied knowledge has substantially recovered, though the finest pre-conquest techniques may have been lost.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Madrasa education collapses during the conquest and revives slowly. The knowledge stock suffers catastrophic loss — manuscripts destroyed, scholars killed, transmission chains broken. What survives provides the seed for regrowth; texts are copied from preserved manuscripts, imported from regions that escaped destruction, reconstructed from memory. By window's end, the madrasa system is operational again, training new generations in religious sciences, law, and letters. Literacy remains concentrated in the ulama and bureaucratic classes.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ The conquest causes knowledge decay on a massive scale — not gradual supersession but violent destruction. Texts are lost permanently; skills die with their practitioners. Recovery partially repairs this loss but cannot fully replace what was destroyed. Some pre-conquest achievements — specific scientific treatises, artistic techniques, historical records — are gone forever. The window is one of loss and partial recovery, not advancement.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ The conquest becomes itself foundational — the catastrophe that shapes collective memory. Persian-Islamic identity persists among the surviving population; the Shahnameh's heroes and Islamic sacred history provide continuity with the past. Mongol identity — the legacy of Genghis Khan, the genealogical prestige of Chinggisid descent — shapes the ruling stratum. The two coexist uneasily, gradually fusing as Mongol rulers Islamize and Persianize. By window's end, the composite identity is Turko-Mongol military elite ruling a Persian-Islamic civilization.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Mongol legitimacy initially rests on conquest — the right of the strongest. Genghis Khan's mandate from Heaven (tengri) provides cosmological backing. As Chagatai khans Islamize, they adopt Islamic legitimating narratives — defender of the faith, patron of learning, just ruler. Chinggisid descent becomes prestige marker even within Islamic frameworks. Timur, who is not Chinggisid, rules through a puppet khan and claims legitimacy through Islamic piety and military success.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ The conquest devastates material culture — monuments damaged, artisans dispersed, production halted. Recovery is slow but real. Architectural patronage resumes under the Chagatai khans, producing monuments in the Persianate tradition. Textile arts and metalwork revive. The Mongol period introduces some East Asian influences — certain decorative motifs, technical methods — that blend with the Persianate substrate. Manuscript illumination and calligraphy revive, preparing the ground for the Timurid flowering to follow.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ The problem is not exhaustion but destruction. Cultural forms are interrupted rather than exhausted; when production resumes, Persian poetic forms, architectural vocabulary, and craft traditions prove still vital. The window is one of rupture and reconstruction, not decay. By its end, cultural production is flourishing in forms continuous with the pre-conquest tradition.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ The massacre tests Islamic cosmology — how can God permit such slaughter of the faithful? Explanations range from divine punishment for communal sin to inscrutable providence to the suffering that precedes the final judgment. The cosmos remains ordered; God's purposes are mysterious but ultimately just. Fatalism may deepen — what can human effort avail against such catastrophe? — but piety persists. The sacred provides meaning even amid horror.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Religious practice survives the conquest. Mosques are damaged but rebuilt; prayers continue in ruins and in restored structures. Ramadan and the pilgrimage calendar persist. Sufi practice — dhikr circles, devotion at shrines — provides communal solace and individual discipline. By window's end, the full apparatus of Islamic devotion is restored.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Ulama are killed in large numbers, but enough survive to maintain transmission. Madrasa institutions are destroyed but rebuilt. Waqf endowments are disrupted but eventually re-established. The Chagatai khans' conversion to Islam (complete by the early 14th century) brings state patronage of religious institutions. By window's end, the ulama have recovered their social position; madrasas train new generations; doctrinal authority is consolidated along Sunni lines.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ The Mongol conquest brings shamanistic and Buddhist influences from the east, but Islam prevails. The Chagatai khans' conversion confirms Islamic dominance; the ruling stratum Islamizes. Sufi orders expand their reach, incorporating converts and providing the primary vehicle for Islamization of pastoral populations. Some syncretism occurs — shamanistic elements blend with Sufi practice — but the overall trajectory is Islamic consolidation. No rival cosmology seriously challenges Islamic dominance by window's end.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ The massacre might produce disenchantment — how trust in a God who permits such horror? — but the evidence suggests the opposite: intensified piety, Sufi devotion, and religious reconstruction. The sacred does not lose its grip; it provides the framework for processing catastrophe and rebuilding meaning. The void opened by destruction is filled with renewed religious commitment. No secularizing drift is visible; the cosmology emerges from the ordeal battered but intact.
