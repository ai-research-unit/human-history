# __600,000 BCE – 400,000 BCE__ — Zhoukoudian Locality 1 and Homo erectus pekinensis
__Summary:__ A limestone cave system near modern Beijing hosts repeated occupation by Homo erectus pekinensis across two hundred millennia, leaving the densest hominin fossil accumulation in East Asia. The cave's shelter and proximity to water and game sustain seasonal or multi-generational returns; thick ash-bearing deposits suggest fire use, though natural burning remains possible. Technological investment stays minimal — simple flake tools on local quartz — while imitative transmission alone preserves knowledge of fire-tending and carcass processing across cohorts. The binding constraint is the cave's fixed capacity relative to faunal availability; the system holds moderate margin against climatic oscillation so long as prey herds persist.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Zhoukoudian lies at the piedmont transition between the Taihang foothills and the North China Plain; the limestone karst provides natural shelter, while ridgelines channel movement toward nearby springs and open grassland.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Conditions oscillate across glacial-interglacial cycles; interglacials bring warm-temperate monsoon summers and cold dry winters, while glacials push the zone toward steppe; overall climate within the window is variable but not trending sharply.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Roof-fall and sediment slumping pose localized risks inside the cave; regional seismicity is low; drought and harsh winters present the primary external shocks, with moderate margin given faunal diversity.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Local quartz and vein quartz supply adequate raw material for flake tools; limestone and bone are abundant; fuel wood available within foraging radius; no evidence of resource depletion within the window.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Bone, ash, and lithic debris accumulate on the cave floor but dispersal occurs during periods of non-occupation; the open landscape absorbs organic waste without saturation; sink capacity remains effectively unlimited.<br><br>
