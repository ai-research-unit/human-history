# __170000 BCE – 85000 BCE__ — Late Persistence of the Indian Acheulean
__Summary:__ The Son Valley of north-central India preserves the world's youngest Acheulean occupation — refined small bifaces at Patpara and Bamburi persisting through MIS 6 and into MIS 5e, some 100,000 years after the tradition ended in Africa and Europe; the binding constraint is the conservative fidelity of motor-skill transmission through imitation in isolated populations, with no language to accelerate diffusion of alternative techniques; margin against disruption rests on the adequacy of familiar technology for local subsistence needs; trajectory is stasis followed by gradual displacement as Middle Paleolithic methods spread, with the transition incomplete until ~85 ka.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Middle Son Valley between the Kaimur Range and Baghelkhand Plateau in northeastern Madhya Pradesh; river terraces provide access to quartzite and fine-grained siliceous raw materials; moderate terrain friction with corridors following the Son and its tributaries.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The window opens in MIS 6 glacial conditions (cold, dry, reduced vegetation) and transitions through the Last Interglacial (MIS 5e, ~130–115 ka) with enhanced monsoon and expanded forests, then fluctuates through MIS 5d–5a; secular trend moves from glacial aridity toward interglacial humidity and back.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Moderate flood hazard on river terraces during enhanced monsoon phases; drought exposure during glacial downturns; seismic and volcanic hazard minimal; margin adequate given mobility.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Quartzite cobbles from Himalayan-derived outwash abundant in terrace gravels; siliceous limestone available; no drawdown pressure; perennial river water secure.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Knapping debris dispersed across open terraces; organic refuse biodegraded rapidly; absorptive capacity effectively unlimited at this occupation intensity.<br><br>
