# __250,000 BCE – 160,000 BCE__ — Emergence of Levantine Mousterian Technology
__Summary:__ A technological transition unfolds across the southern Levant as Levallois prepared-core techniques gradually replace Acheulean biface production, marking the shift to the Middle Paleolithic; the binding constraint is the multi-generational transmission of complex knapping sequences through observation alone; absorptive capacity against environmental fluctuation remains adequate; the trend moves toward greater flake standardization and reduced biface prominence.

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Jordan Rift corridor, Mount Carmel caves, and coastal plain remain the principal occupation zones, with limestone karst offering shelter and flint nodules accessible in wadi exposures and chalk outcrops.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Middle Pleistocene glacial-interglacial oscillations drive alternating wet and dry phases; Mediterranean woodland expands during interglacials while steppe advances during colder intervals, shifting foraging radii accordingly.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Seismic activity along the Rift faults poses localized risks; drought cycles during glacial phases compress water availability but cave refugia and spring-fed localities buffer against acute stress.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ High-quality flint remains abundant in Eocene and Senonian chalk formations; the shift to Levallois technique extracts more usable edge per nodule, extending effective raw material yield without approaching depletion.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Knapping debris accumulates at cave mouths and open-air stations but volumes remain negligible relative to landscape absorptive capacity; no measurable sink stress.<br><br>
