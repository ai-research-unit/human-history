# __90,000 BCE – 55,000 BCE__ — Neanderthal Reoccupation and Population Dynamics

__Summary:__ The Levantine corridor during the climate-driven population turnover that replaced early modern humans with Neanderthal populations, examined as a long-term trend spanning the onset of Marine Isotope Stage 4 cooling and the subsequent reestablishment of Neanderthal presence at sites previously occupied by Homo sapiens. The binding constraint is the imitative stage itself: with no language to record history, no symbol to mark territory, and no medium to transmit claims across absence, the population dynamics that determine who occupies the Levant unfold through embodied presence rather than documented succession. Knowledge of the landscape, its resources, and its dangers passes only through showing and copying, hand guiding hand; when one population withdraws or dies out, everything it knew about this place withdraws with it, leaving the landscape to be relearned by whoever arrives next.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Levantine corridor retains its fundamental geographic structure throughout the window: the coastal plain and Mount Carmel caves (Tabun, Kebara, Amud) to the west, the Jordan Rift Valley corridor to the east, and the hill country between them. The key sites of the previous modern human occupation (Skhul, Qafzeh) continue as habitable locales, but occupation shifts to Neanderthal populations. New sites enter the record, particularly Amud Cave in the Galilee, where substantial Neanderthal remains accumulate. The geographic friction that channeled earlier populations through predictable corridors remains unchanged; what changes is which population moves through them. The Sinai land bridge that connected the Levant to Africa becomes increasingly hostile as aridity intensifies, functioning less as a corridor and more as a barrier.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The window spans the transition from Marine Isotope Stage 5 (warm, wet) to MIS 4 (cold, dry), with the boundary at approximately 71,000 BCE marking the onset of severe glacial conditions. MIS 4 brings significant cooling (5-10 degrees C below present) and dramatic reduction in precipitation; Mediterranean woodland contracts to refugia on the coastal slopes and wetter mountain zones, while steppe and semi-desert expand across the interior. The Negev and Sinai become increasingly arid, potentially severing the African connection that enabled modern human dispersal during MIS 5. By 60,000 BCE, conditions have stabilized at a new, colder baseline that persists until the interstadials of MIS 3. This climate shift is the proximate driver of the population turnover: conditions that favored African-adapted modern humans give way to conditions favoring cold-adapted Neanderthals.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ The shift to glacial conditions reduces certain hazards (flash flooding becomes less frequent as precipitation declines) while intensifying others (resource scarcity during harsh winters, increased competition at shrinking resource patches). Seismic activity along the Dead Sea Transform continues at background rates. The primary hazard is the slow-onset scarcity imposed by aridification rather than acute shocks; populations must adapt their subsistence strategies or contract.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Faunal resources shift as woodland contracts: fallow deer, the dominant prey at MIS 5 sites, decline relative to steppe-adapted species including gazelle and wild ass. The contraction of woodland concentrates remaining ungulate populations at water sources and refugial patches, potentially making hunting more predictable but also intensifying competition among predators including both hominin populations and carnivores. Marine shellfish remain accessible along the coast, though their representation in inland sites declines. Flint resources remain abundant and accessible; raw material procurement is not a limiting factor. The energy endowment remains sufficient for forager subsistence but with reduced margin and increased spatial patchiness.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ At the reduced population densities characteristic of glacial conditions, waste absorption capacity exceeds any load imposed. Cave deposits continue to accumulate occupation debris, but external environmental sinks face no saturation pressure. The physical residue is nil.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ The Neanderthal populations reoccupying the Levant are adapted to the pathogen regimes of western Eurasia rather than tropical Africa. Colder, drier conditions reduce certain vector-borne disease pressures while potentially intensifying others (respiratory infections during cold winters, increased crowding at resource patches). The Kebara 2 Neanderthal skeleton (dated to approximately 60,000 BCE) shows no obvious pathology beyond missing lower limbs (a post-depositional artifact); the Amud 1 skeleton displays healed trauma consistent with a physically demanding lifeway. Baseline mortality remains high, with trauma, infection, and nutritional stress as leading causes; the dependent load of injured and aging individuals is absorbed within cooperative band structures.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Neanderthal remains from Levantine sites (Amud, Kebara, Tabun) display the characteristic robust morphology of the taxon: heavy brow ridges, robust long bones, and barrel-shaped torsos adapted to cold stress. Stature is shorter than that of the preceding modern human population (approximately 160-170 cm for males) but with greater muscle mass and bone density. Isotopic analysis of Neanderthal remains from comparable European sites indicates heavily meat-dependent diets; the Levantine populations likely pursued similar subsistence strategies, exploiting large ungulates as primary protein sources. Nutritional status appears adequate to support the energetically expensive Neanderthal body form.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ The Amud 1 Neanderthal displays healed cranial trauma, indicating survival of significant injury with sufficient care to permit recovery. The Shanidar evidence from Iraq (outside the immediate study area but relevant to Neanderthal behavioral patterns) demonstrates sustained care for individuals with severe disabilities. The Levantine Neanderthals can be expected to practice similar levels of conspecific care, supporting injured and dependent individuals within band-level cooperative networks.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Neanderthal reproductive biology differs subtly from that of modern humans: gestation length, interbirth intervals, and age at maturity may have varied, though the evidence remains contested. The small population sizes characteristic of glacial conditions intensify the demographic challenges of maintaining viable breeding populations; any isolated band risks inbreeding depression and demographic stochasticity. Pairing dynamics are archaeologically invisible but consistent with cooperative breeding patterns.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population density during MIS 4 is low, likely lower than during the preceding modern human occupation. The number of Neanderthal individuals recovered from Levantine sites is modest (single individuals at Kebara and Amud, a handful at Tabun), suggesting that total population never exceeded a few hundred individuals distributed across the region. Renewal rates are sufficient to maintain presence across the 35,000-year window but without demographic growth; the population persists in equilibrium with carrying capacity rather than expanding.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Each Neanderthal death represents a loss of embodied knowledge about local landscapes, resource patches, and techniques. The low population density means that knowledge is held by few individuals; the death of a skilled hunter or toolmaker may represent an irrecoverable loss. The dependency load of injured, aged, and juvenile individuals is absorbed within band-level networks; the care shown to disabled individuals indicates that dependent members are not abandoned. The biological residue is the standing attrition of mortality in small populations with limited redundancy.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Neanderthal subsistence in the Levant centers on hunting of medium to large ungulates, with faunal assemblages dominated by gazelle, wild ass, fallow deer (in refugial areas), and aurochs. The Kebara and Amud assemblages show intensive processing of animal carcasses with evidence of marrow extraction and bone fuel use. Plant foods are less visible in the record but were likely exploited where available. The population sits at or near the subsistence edge during harsh periods, with reduced margin compared to the preceding MIS 5 modern human occupation.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human muscle remains the primary energy capture pathway; fire use is ubiquitous at Neanderthal sites, with substantial hearth features at Kebara and Tabun providing warmth, cooking, and social focus. Neanderthal metabolic requirements exceed those of modern humans due to greater body mass and cold-stress physiology, demanding higher caloric intake per individual. The energy budget is tighter than for the preceding population.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Division of labor follows patterns characteristic of forager bands, though the degree of sex-based task differentiation in Neanderthal societies remains debated. Evidence from European sites suggests that both males and females engaged in close-range hunting of large game, a dangerous strategy that may explain the high frequency of trauma in Neanderthal skeletal remains. Labor organization operates through imitation and mutual adjustment without formalized coercion.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Stone tool production follows Levantine Mousterian conventions, with the Tabun B-type industry (elongated Levallois points) characterizing many Neanderthal assemblages. The technological repertoire is sophisticated but stable, showing less innovation than contemporary African Middle Stone Age traditions. Surplus beyond immediate consumption is limited to cached meat and tool blanks; no durable surplus accumulation occurs.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Storage technology is limited to in-body reserves and short-term caching. The cold conditions of MIS 4 may facilitate natural refrigeration of cached meat, extending the window for consumption. Buffering against scarcity relies on mobility, reciprocal sharing, and exploitation of multiple resource patches within the territorial range.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Long-distance transport of materials is less visible in Neanderthal assemblages than in contemporary modern human sites; most raw materials are procured within 20-30 km of occupation sites. Whether this reflects reduced mobility, different territorial organization, or simply the availability of adequate local materials cannot be determined. Intergroup exchange, if any, leaves no clear archaeological trace.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ No medium of deferred exchange exists. Reciprocal obligations persist only in memory and operate within face-to-face communities. The absence of ornaments or symbolic objects in most Levantine Neanderthal assemblages (in contrast to the preceding Skhul-Qafzeh population) suggests reduced investment in material tokens of social relationship.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Redistribution occurs through sharing norms within the band, particularly the obligatory distribution of large-mammal kills. No extraction apparatus exists above the band level.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Personal possessions are limited to tools and portable gear. No defensible wealth accumulation exists; distribution remains egalitarian by default.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Butchery waste, lithic debitage, and organic refuse accumulate in cave deposits. Bone is extensively processed for marrow and may be used as fuel in fire features. The economic residue is nil beyond the archaeological deposit itself.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Kinship organizes the band through bilateral ties, with evidence from European Neanderthal sites (genetic analysis of the El Sidron group) indicating that males may have been philopatric while females moved between groups. The Amud 7 burial of an infant suggests that kinship relations structure social attention to the young dead. Inheritance operates through imitative transmission of technique; without language, genealogical reckoning beyond immediate kin is limited.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ The band functions as a cooperative unit for hunting, food sharing, and care of dependents. The treatment of disabled individuals (Shanidar evidence) and the burial of the dead (Amud, Kebara) imply collective attention that extends beyond immediate utility. Mutual aid is continuous and embedded in daily life.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Leisure time may be reduced compared to the preceding MIS 5 occupation as resource scarcity demands more foraging effort. Cave sites function as gathering places for social activity centered on fire; the elaborate hearth features at Kebara indicate that fire use structures group activity. Sociability centers on the visible presence of co-members within the shelter.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Stratification is minimal and age-graded. No formalized hierarchy exists; prestige accrues through demonstrated competence in hunting, toolmaking, and healing. The small size of Neanderthal bands (likely 10-25 individuals based on European evidence) limits the complexity of internal social differentiation.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ No administrative apparatus exists; coordination emerges through mutual observation and imitation. Leadership is situational and task-specific.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ The band possesses no coercive apparatus beyond individual physical capacity. Interpersonal disputes resolve through avoidance, submission, or violence. Collective defense relies on cooperative vigilance and group size; the robust Neanderthal physique and close-range hunting technology suggest that individuals are formidable in confrontation.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Social friction dissipates through fission, avoidance, and, where attested, mortuary practice. The burial at Kebara 2 (body placed in a shallow pit with removal of the skull, possibly for curation) and the Amud 7 infant burial demonstrate that death occasions collective response. The social residue is the standing friction absorbed by small, isolated populations with limited options for fission.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Leadership operates through demonstrated competence in hunting, toolmaking, and healing. The individual who successfully brings down large game commands attention and deference; the elder who knows the landscape guides group movement. Authority is earned, situational, and non-inheritable.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Allegiance flows to competent practitioners; legitimacy exists as behavioral deference expressed through attention and imitation. Without language to articulate justifications, the basis for authority cannot be verbalized.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Collective will sustains the population through the challenging conditions of MIS 4. The capacity for coordinated hunting, cooperative care, and burial practice demonstrates collective resolve operating through convergent action rather than explicit deliberation. The 35,000-year persistence of Neanderthal presence indicates sufficient collective will to maintain occupation.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Intergroup relations are difficult to assess. The low population density during MIS 4 may reduce intergroup contact frequency. Relations with modern human populations, if any persist in the region, are archaeologically invisible during this window; the population dynamics suggest displacement or extinction of modern humans rather than sustained coexistence. Whether Neanderthal groups cooperated or competed with each other cannot be determined from the available evidence.

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ The emotional tenor of Neanderthal social life is difficult to assess. The care shown to injured individuals, the burial of the dead, and the persistence of cooperative hunting all suggest emotional bonds that motivate altruistic behavior. The behavioral temper must accommodate high-risk activities (close-range hunting of large game) and the stresses of resource scarcity during glacial conditions. Whether the Levantine Neanderthals experienced the same affective range as the preceding modern human population cannot be determined.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ The stresses of glacial conditions and resource scarcity may produce fatigue and depleted will, but the persistence of occupation suggests that collective resolve is maintained. The emotional residue is the standing strain of survival in marginal conditions.


## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Communication operates through demonstration, gesture, and vocalization without syntactic language. The Kebara 2 hyoid bone (a throat structure associated with speech) is anatomically consistent with speech capability, but the cognitive and cultural infrastructure for language may not have been present. Meaning transmission requires co-presence; the Mousterian reduction sequences and burial practices must be learned through observation and guided participation. Mutual intelligibility depends on shared context and demonstrated action.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Information travels only as fast as individuals move and only as far as direct demonstration reaches. The reduced raw material transport distances in Neanderthal assemblages may indicate reduced territorial range and thus reduced information flow compared to contemporary modern human populations. Transmission velocity is bounded by the speed of walking and the geographic range of seasonal mobility.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ No external record exists; knowledge persists only in living memory and embodied habit. The burials at Kebara and Amud mark the presence of death but cannot transmit the practices that created them. Knowledge storage is entirely biological, subject to the mortality of carriers.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Without language, explicit censorship is impossible. Behavioral norms constrain what is demonstrated; enforcement operates through intervention and social pressure.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Reception depends on attentional presence during demonstration. The small size of Neanderthal bands means that any given practice may be held by only a few individuals, limiting amplification and increasing vulnerability to loss through mortality.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Each imitative transmission introduces drift; the Mousterian technological tradition shows remarkable stability across tens of thousands of years and thousands of kilometers, suggesting that copying fidelity is high for lithic technology. The informational residue is the accumulated drift in practices that lack the stability of stone tool reduction sequences.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Moral intuitions operate without articulation. The care shown to injured individuals, the sharing of food, and the burial of the dead all imply felt obligations that structure behavior. The distinction between appropriate and inappropriate treatment of human remains (bodies are buried, not abandoned) represents a proto-moral category operating below the threshold of verbalization.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ No codified law exists; binding constraints take the form of habitual practice enforced through social consequence. The burial conventions represent unwritten expectations; deviation would provoke response without explicit adjudication.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Disputes resolve through direct confrontation, intervention, or avoidance. Liability is personal and immediate; sanction ranges from exclusion to violence. No formal adjudication process exists.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Individual autonomy is constrained by dependence on the group. Rights exist only as behavioral regularities limiting interference. Compliance burden is light.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ The small scale of the band ensures that reputation is common knowledge, limiting impunity. The moral residue is the standing stock of grievances that social structure leaves unaddressed.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ No formal inquiry exists; understanding operates through trial, error, and imitation. The Mousterian reduction sequence represents a sophisticated mental template but not an articulable theory. Whatever Neanderthals understood about their world cannot be recovered.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Stone tool production follows Levantine Mousterian conventions with Levallois prepared-core techniques producing elongated points (Tabun B-type) and broad flakes (Tabun C-type). The technology is effective for its purpose (hunting, butchery, hide working) but shows less innovation than contemporary African assemblages. Fire management is sophisticated, with organized hearth features at Kebara indicating controlled combustion. Technical method exists as embodied skill transmitted through demonstration.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Education occurs through apprenticeship; the novice observes the expert and attempts replication. Knowledge stock exists only in living practitioners; skill distribution is uneven. The small population size means that the total knowledge stock is held by few individuals, creating vulnerability to loss.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ The remarkable stability of Mousterian technology across time and space suggests that transmission fidelity is high and knowledge decay is slow for core techniques. However, the absence of the symbolic and ornamental traditions visible at Skhul-Qafzeh indicates either that Neanderthals never developed these practices or that they were not transmitted to the Levantine populations. The intellectual residue is the unknown extent of knowledge decay in domains that leave no archaeological trace.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Collective memory extends only as far as living recall. Foundational identity rests on immediate kinship and shared experience; without language, no origin narrative can be constructed or transmitted. The Neanderthals may recognize themselves as distinct from other hominins (modern humans, if encountered), but the conceptual content of this recognition is irrecoverable.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Without language, legitimating narrative does not exist in propositional form. Authority rests on demonstrated competence.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ The Levantine Neanderthal assemblages lack the ornamental traditions visible at Skhul-Qafzeh: no perforated shells, no ochre application to personal adornment, no grave goods beyond what may be incidental inclusion. This absence distinguishes the Neanderthal occupation from the preceding modern human population and has been interpreted variously as indicating reduced symbolic capacity, different cultural traditions, or simply archaeological sampling bias. Material culture centers on functional stone and bone technology without evident aesthetic elaboration.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Without the symbolic and ornamental traditions visible in modern human populations, the question of cultural form exhaustion is difficult to apply. The Mousterian technological tradition persists without evident decay; functional forms remain potent. The cultural residue is the absence of symbolic elaboration rather than the exhaustion of once-potent forms.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ No articulated cosmology exists. Whatever sense Neanderthals had of death, the afterlife, or their place in reality cannot be recovered. The burial of the dead at Kebara and Amud indicates that death occasions special treatment, but the conceptual content of this treatment is unknowable.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ The burial sequence constitutes a minimal ritual: stereotyped actions performed on human remains that serve no obvious subsistence purpose. Whether devotion or affect accompanied these actions cannot be determined. The Kebara burial with skull removal may indicate secondary mortuary treatment (curation of the skull elsewhere), but the practice cannot be interpreted with confidence.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ No religious institution exists; no doctrinal authority can assert orthodoxy. The burial practice is held by practitioners whose knowledge dies with them.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ The Neanderthal populations entering the Levant carry mortuary practices that may derive from European or western Asian Neanderthal traditions; the practices observed at Kebara and Amud are consistent with Neanderthal burials elsewhere. The displacement of modern human populations represents a replacement of one cultural-spiritual complex by another, with no evident syncretism.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ The concept of disenchantment presupposes an enchanted state that has been articulated; in the imitative stage, the sacred exists only in action. Whether Neanderthal burial practice carries sacred meaning or represents a different kind of response to death cannot be determined. The spiritual residue remains indeterminate.
