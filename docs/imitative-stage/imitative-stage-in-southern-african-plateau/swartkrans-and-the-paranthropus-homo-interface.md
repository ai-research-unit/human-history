# __700,000 BCE – 500,000 BCE__ — Swartkrans and the Paranthropus-Homo Interface
__Summary:__ A snapshot of the Swartkrans cave system during its later depositional phases, capturing the co-occurrence of Homo erectus and Paranthropus robustus populations within the Cradle of Humankind dolomitic landscape; Homo erectus demonstrates Acheulean technology and bone tool use while Paranthropus persists in declining numbers; the binding constraint is predator pressure within confined cave environments; the system holds moderate margin against environmental fluctuation; demographic balance is shifting toward Homo dominance.

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Swartkrans site occupies a dolomitic cave system within the Bloubank River valley; surrounding terrain comprises rolling grassland and wooded valleys; movement follows watercourses and ridge lines with moderate friction across the undulating plateau.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Seasonal subtropical regime with summer rainfall averaging 600-700mm annually; interglacial conditions support mixed grassland-woodland vegetation; climate oscillates between wetter and drier phases at orbital timescales but remains stable within this window.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Cave collapse poses localized risk; seasonal drought reduces water availability; wildfire periodically sweeps grasslands; predator concentration at cave entrances creates persistent mortality hazard; overall hazard regime is moderate with adequate margin.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Dolomite outcrops provide shelter but poor knapping material; quartzite and quartz cobbles available in river gravels; animal biomass concentrated at waterholes and cave sites; wood fuel abundant in valley woodlands; endowment supports sustained occupation.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Cave chambers accumulate bone debris and organic waste; natural drainage through dolomite fissures clears liquid waste; open landscape absorbs surface refuse without saturation; absorptive capacity far exceeds load from small hominin populations.<br><br>
