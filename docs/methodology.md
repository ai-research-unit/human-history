# Methodology

The project is built in layers, from a fixed sequence of stages down to individual articles. Each layer has explicit rules; this page describes them.

## 1. The Information Sequence

The backbone is an ordered list of information substrates — the media that public life has run on, each in turn the primary way a society stored, sent, copied, and found its shared information. The order is the order in which each substrate first became dominant *somewhere*:

1. Imitative Stage — knowledge in living memory, passed by showing and copying.
2. Oral Stage — spoken language.
3. Manuscript Stage — external written records.
4. Print Stage — mechanically reproduced, identical copies.
5. Broadcast Stage — one source reaching a multitude at once (radio, then television).
6. Digital Stage — all information in one common code, networked many-to-many.
7. AI Stage *(projected)* — retrieval by meaning, asked for in ordinary language.

The sequence carries order only — no dates of its own. Dates attach afterward, per region.

## 2. What defines a stage: dominance

A stage is named for one thing only: the **dominant information substrate** of a region in a given window — the medium its public life actually ran on. It is *not* named for what happened there. This is the single most important thing to understand before reading any article.

An article is a full-spectrum description of a society across ten domains, most of which — wars, harvests, religion, law — have nothing to do with information media. The stage label answers just one question about that society: what was the dominant medium of its public information at the time? So an article filed under the Oral Stage may be almost entirely about world wars, and cash-crop economies; what places it in the Oral Stage is that, in that region and window, speech was still the medium public life ran on — not that the article is "about" speech. The substrate periodizes the article; it does not summarize it.

A substrate is dominant only where two tests hold at once:

- **Centrality** — public life runs on it; it is the load-bearing medium for the society's shared information, not a peripheral convenience.
- **Penetration** — it is the normal, default way the society's public information is conducted, not confined to a specialist or elite enclave.

"Public life" means the shared and institutional life of a society — administration, economy, law, civic coordination, public communication — not the private habits of the average individual. This is why a manuscript period can be dated from the moment institutions ran on written records, even while most people could not read.

Several historically important technologies are deliberately *not* stages, because they fail one test. The telegraph was central infrastructure, but the public's information still ran on print (importance without penetration). The phonograph, film, and tape were widely used but structurally peripheral, and store a signal rather than a symbol (penetration without centrality). Pure computation, authentication, and the material base — power, tools, wealth, schooling — drive the transitions but are not themselves substrates.

## 3. Asynchronous, per-region windows

Dominance is tracked per region and asynchronously. A region enters a stage when the substrate becomes primary there, and leaves when the next substrate takes over, the substrate collapses, or the present is reached. Because regions run on their own schedules, the same stage opens at very different dates in different places, and windows overlap freely across regions — that overlap is simply the lag made visible. Two regions can sit stages apart in the same calendar year; the East African Rift's Oral window reaching 1945 is exactly that. Within a single region only one substrate is primary at a time, so its windows tile in time across the stages it passed through. A region can also step backward — lose a dominant substrate and revert to an earlier stage — and climb again later.

## 4. Regions as physical containers

Within each stage, the regions where the substrate became dominant are named for enduring physiographic features — basins, river systems, rifts, plateaus, ranges, coasts, shelves, peninsulas, archipelagos — chosen so the name reads correctly in deep prehistory and today, independent of any culture, polity, or period. "Mesopotamia" becomes the Tigris and Euphrates Basin; "the Levant" becomes the Eastern Mediterranean Coast and Jordan Rift Valley.

As mass, simultaneous media build large integrated public spheres, the meaningful unit grows: many fine containers of an early stage merge into fewer coarse blocs in a later one. A region can therefore be present in an early stage and absent from a later one because it was absorbed into a larger container that carries its public sphere from then on — a structural change, not an omission.

## 5. The article structure

Each region holds a set of articles, each a Historical Context: a large-scale operational state of a human system. Every article is mapped across the same ten domains, in order:  D01. Physical, D02. Biological, D03. Economic, D04. Social, D05. Emotional, D06. Informational, D07. Moral, D08. Intellectual, D09. Cultural, D10. Spiritual.

Each domain is read dynamically — not as a static inventory but as stocks, flows, pressures, and the ratio of capacity to load — and each closes on a residue line: the discharge it throws off (waste, grievance, noise, disorder, decay) and whether that residue is cleared or left to accumulate. 

The Informational domain records what the society's information actually ran on, consistent with the stage. A late Oral-Stage article may therefore note that external written records *existed* while still identifying speech as the medium public life ran on, because the stage tracks the dominant substrate, not everything merely present.

Subjects and dates are drawn from the historical and archaeological record. Contested or thinly attested points are marked as such; where a function genuinely did not exist in a period — an institution not yet invented — the line records that absence rather than inventing content.

## 6. Generation and checking

The pipeline is run with AI:

1. The Information Sequence fixes the stages.
2. A geographic pass lists, for each stage, the regions where the substrate became dominant, ranked by systemic impact, each with its window and an estimate of how many articles it can support.
3. A cross-stage check coordinates these region lists — confirming that each region's windows form one continuous chain over time, that names stay consistent, and that the stages stay in order.
4. Articles are then written against a shared, source-attributed evidence base, so that no two articles invent conflicting facts.
5. A separate verification pass reads the finished articles and checks them on three independent levels: structure and internal consistency, factual accuracy against external sources, and conformance to the framework's design rules. Structural and source-backed corrections are applied; matters of judgment are flagged rather than silently rewritten.

This separation of generation from verification is deliberate: a model checking its own work inherits its own blind spots.
