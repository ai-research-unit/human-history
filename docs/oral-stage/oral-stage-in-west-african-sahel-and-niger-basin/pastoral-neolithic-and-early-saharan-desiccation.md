# __5000 BCE – 2000 BCE__ — Pastoral Neolithic and Early Saharan Desiccation
__Summary:__ Progressive Saharan desiccation forces a fundamental economic transition from lakeside fishing to mobile cattle pastoralism combined with emergent cultivation; populations shift southward into the Sahel as northern lakes shrink and disappear; the binding constraint is diminishing rainfall and the carrying capacity of shrinking pasture; adaptive flexibility provides margin against shock but system operates closer to environmental limits than the preceding wet phase; trajectory is one of compression, adaptation, and intensification.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Saharan lakes contract and fragment; Niger and Senegal drainages remain viable but reduced; Mega-Chad shrinks dramatically; drying forces populations into narrowing corridors along permanent water; travel across former lake beds and emerging desert becomes more difficult; southward migration routes channeled by terrain.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Monsoon belt retreats southward; rainfall declines by 100–300 mm across the window; interannual volatility increases as climate destabilizes; dry spells lengthen; secular trend unmistakably toward aridity; Green Sahara conditions end by mid-window.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Drought becomes primary hazard — multi-year dry spells threaten herds and crops; flood risk declines as rivers shrink; desertification advances from the north; exposure to environmental failure increases sharply relative to the preceding period.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Aquatic resources collapse as lakes dry; wild game declines on shrinking grasslands; domesticated cattle become primary protein reserve; grazing land contracts southward; lithic and wood resources still available but patchier; endowment declining relative to stable pastoral demand.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Low population density and dispersed pastoral settlement produce minimal waste load; sink capacity ample relative to human output; no saturation.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Cattle introduce new disease ecology (bovine pathogens, associated parasites); waterhole concentration increases transmission risk; overall mortality baseline stable but disease portfolio shifts; endemic conditions persist.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Pastoral diet centers on milk, blood, and occasional meat supplemented by gathered plants and emerging cultivated grains; nutritional status adequate though more precarious than lake-margin abundance; vitality maintained but margin thins.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Pastoral societies develop marriage systems linked to cattle exchange (bride-wealth patterns may originate here); pair-bonding stable; reproductive biology unremarkable.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population contracts in drying north, concentrates in remaining viable zones; density increases locally in Sahel refugia; net replacement positive but growth slows; outmigration to the south continues through the window.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Pastoral mobility challenges elderly and infirm; dependency absorbed within extended kin networks; cattle wealth provides buffer; load manageable but tighter than in sedentary fishing communities.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Cattle pastoralism provides core subsistence — milk the daily staple, meat for special occasions; wild foods supplement; pearl millet cultivation emerges by ~3000 BCE adding storable grain; provisioning floor adequate but closer to edge than preceding aquatic phase.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human muscle dominant; cattle provide traction, transport, and stored energy (meat, milk); fire for cooking and metalworking (late window); per-capita energy capture modestly higher than pure foraging due to livestock.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Household and lineage-based labor; herding tasks divided by age and gender; no coerced labor; cooperative kin labor for large tasks (corralling, milking, planting).<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Cattle herds constitute walking surplus — accumulating animals beyond immediate need; grain cultivation adds storable surplus; productive capacity expanding through diversification; surplus modest but growing.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Cattle themselves serve as reserve — slaughtered or exchanged in crisis; grain stored in pots and bins (Dhar Tichitt shows stone granaries); milk preserved as curds; buffering capacity improved by diversification but drought can wipe reserves rapidly.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Regional exchange of cattle, grain, salt, and lithics between pastoral groups and early cultivators; movement patterns facilitate exchange along seasonal routes; no long-distance trade infrastructure yet; logistics pastoral and foot-based.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Cattle serve as wealth store and medium of exchange; bride-wealth and compensation payments create credit-like obligations; reciprocal sharing persists; deferred exchange now materialized in livestock.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Senior kin and herd-owners accumulate and redistribute cattle; no formalized taxation; redistribution through kinship obligations, feasting, and bride-wealth; extraction remains embedded in social relations.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Cattle ownership introduces portable, accumulable wealth; stratification increases — some lineages amass larger herds; raiding emerges as threat requiring defense; inequality modest but widening relative to egalitarian forager baseline.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Cattle dung used as fuel and building material (recycled); slaughter waste dispersed; agricultural waste minimal; byproduct load low and largely recycled.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Extended kin networks essential for herd management and drought response; trust radius expands to include pastoral allies and exchange partners; mutual aid intensifies as environment becomes harsher.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Status increasingly linked to cattle wealth and herd size; senior herd-owners gain prestige; lineage identity sharpens as competition for shrinking resources increases; ethnic or clan boundaries may harden.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Patrilineal descent likely strengthens with cattle inheritance through male lines; extended patrilocal households manage herds; marriage alliance cemented by cattle exchange; kinship structures elaborate.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Clan and lineage councils coordinate movement, grazing rights, and dispute resolution; age-grade systems possible (attested in later Sahelian pastoralists); self-organization above band level emerging.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Seasonal gatherings at water sources and pastures allow sociability, ceremony, and mate exchange; cattle serve social as well as economic function (prestige display, ritual); leisure integrated with herding rhythm.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Elder councils mediate disputes over cattle, water, and grazing; compensation in cattle institutionalizes dispute resolution; mobility still allows fission; grievance largely absorbed but stakes higher than forager period.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Emerging big-man or chief patterns — senior herd-owners exercise influence but lack institutionalized power; authority personal and contingent; no state formation yet; power diffuse but concentrating.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Influence passes within lineages but not through fixed rules; sons of wealthy herd-owners advantaged; transfer contested and negotiated within kin groups.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Authority legitimated by generosity (redistribution), wisdom, and ritual efficacy; ancestral narratives support lineage prestige; no formal political ideology.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ No formal administration; lineage elders coordinate through oral consensus; memory and oral communication sufficient for pastoral-scale organization; capacity adequate.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Adult male heads of household participate in lineage and clan councils; women's influence exercised through domestic sphere; mobilization for defense or migration collective; participation broad within patriarchal limits.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Relations with neighboring pastoral and cultivating groups governed by exchange, intermarriage, and occasional conflict; territorial claims to grazing and water emerge; external relations tense where resources scarce; no state-level threats.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ No standing military; adult males armed with spears, bows, clubs; raiding parties form ad hoc for livestock theft and defense; force capacity distributed but mobilizable.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Cattle raiding emerges as endemic threat; internal order maintained by lineage authority and compensation systems; violence episodic but real; threat environment elevated relative to forager baseline.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Face-to-face speech remains sole channel; pastoral mobility accelerates information flow across landscape; seasonal gatherings concentrate transmission; velocity still limited to travel pace.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Living memory alone — oral tradition carries genealogies, territorial knowledge, herding lore, and historical memory; no writing or external storage; persistence depends on unbroken human transmission; knowledge vulnerable to demographic loss but lineage depth adds redundancy.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Lineage elders control authoritative speech on genealogy and tradition; no formal censorship; discourse norms govern appropriate speech by age and status.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Audiences remain small-scale (camp, lineage gathering); important information (grazing, water, threats) amplified through pastoral networks; attention focused on survival-critical and social knowledge.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Oral transmission accurate for practical knowledge but genealogies and histories drift over generations; verification through elder consensus; noise load manageable in small-scale society.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Reciprocity, kinship obligation, and respect for elders underpin moral order; cattle theft a serious offense requiring compensation; hospitality obligations strong; justice rooted in balance and restitution.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Customary law transmitted orally; rules governing marriage, inheritance, grazing, and compensation for injury or theft; codes stable but adapted to pastoral conditions; evolution gradual.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Lineage and clan elders adjudicate disputes; compensation in cattle standard remedy; liability collective within lineage; enforcement through social pressure and threat of feud; formal courts absent.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Individual rights embedded in lineage membership and status; autonomy constrained by kinship obligation; compliance burden moderate and internalized through socialization.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Wrongs across lineage or clan boundaries harder to redress; raiding creates uncompensated losses; impunity gap widens relative to tight-knit forager communities but remains limited.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ No formal inquiry; practical knowledge of cattle husbandry, pasture cycles, water sources, and weather signs extensive and refined; explanatory paradigms remain embedded in oral tradition and animist cosmology.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Cattle breeding and management techniques develop; milking, bleeding, and meat preservation methods refined; pearl millet cultivation introduced (~3000 BCE) requiring planting and harvest knowledge; pottery and leather-working techniques continue; all transmitted through apprenticeship.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Youth learn through participation in herding and household tasks; specialized knowledge (healing, ritual, smithing where present) restricted to practitioners; knowledge stock adequate for pastoral-agricultural economy; skill distribution tracks lineage and gender.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Aquatic fishing knowledge fades as lakes disappear — obsolescence driven by environmental change; new pastoral and agricultural knowledge accumulates; decay and accumulation in rough balance.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Pastoral identity crystallizes — cattle become central symbol; ancestral narratives link lineages to founding herds and homeland; origin myths adapt to new landscape; deep memory of the Green Sahara may persist in oral tradition.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Rock art transitions from aquatic to pastoral themes — cattle herds, herders, pastoral scenes dominate later panels at Tassili and Ennedi; pottery continues with regional styles; material culture reflects pastoral priorities (leather, bone, horn).<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Adaptive and resilient but sobering — awareness of environmental decline shapes outlook; mobility and flexibility valued; risk-consciousness heightened; mood pragmatic rather than confident.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Aquatic-era symbols lose relevance; pastoral symbols gain power; transition involves symbolic renewal rather than exhaustion; meaning actively reconstructed around cattle and lineage.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Animist worldview continues — spirits inhabit landscape, cattle, ancestors; cattle may acquire sacred significance (prefiguring later Sahelian cattle cults); human fate bound to herd and kin; outlook balanced between fatalism and active propitiation.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Cattle feature in sacrifice and ritual; life-stage ceremonies (birth, initiation, marriage, death) marked by cattle exchange; rain-calling and fertility rituals probable as drought anxiety rises; sacred practice intensifies.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ No institutional religion; ritual specialists (diviners, rainmakers, healers) hold informal authority tied to efficacy; doctrine oral and flexible; authority personal rather than institutional.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Pastoral spiritual traditions spread with southward migration; local hunter-gatherer traditions absorbed or displaced; gradual diffusion rather than sharp replacement; syncretism probable at contact zones.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ No disenchantment; sacred meaning intensifies around cattle and crisis response; environmental stress increases rather than diminishes spiritual engagement; sacred need fully addressed within adapted cosmology.<br><br>
