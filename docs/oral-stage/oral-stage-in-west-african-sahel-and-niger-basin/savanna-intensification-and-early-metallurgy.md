# __2,000 BCE – 500 BCE__ — Savanna Intensification and Early Metallurgy

__Summary:__ Over fifteen centuries the West African savanna belt between the Niger and Chad basins consolidated a grain-based subsistence regime around pearl millet and sorghum while pioneering indigenous copper and iron metallurgy, a transformation that locked population into sedentary villages, established the technological toolkit for all subsequent Sahelian complexity, and was coordinated entirely through oral transmission of agricultural and metallurgical knowledge across generations.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The savanna belt stretches from the Senegal valley through the Niger Inland Delta and Volta basin to the Chad depression, a corridor of seasonal grasslands, gallery forests, and laterite soils lying between the Sahara margin and the Guinea forests; the Niger River and its tributaries provide water, alluvial soils, and transit routes that concentrate settlement along their courses.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The monsoon delivers 400–1,000 mm of rainfall annually in a single wet season, supporting grass and grain agriculture; interannual variability is high, and multi-year droughts episodically stress crops and herds; the secular trend across this window is toward stabilization after the post-humid collapse, though drier decades recur.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Seasonal floods along the Niger and Senegal bring silt but also destroy granaries; locust swarms and bushfire threaten harvests; disease exposure rises as sedentary populations concentrate near water and livestock.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Laterite soils bear iron ore nodules near the surface; copper deposits in the Air massif of Niger; hardwood forests supply charcoal for smelting; the granitic and Precambrian basement yields grinding stones; the endowment supports both agriculture and metallurgy without long-distance import.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Charcoal production for smelting consumes woodland selectively; agricultural clearing opens vegetation; the landscape absorbs the discharge without measurable saturation in this period, though localized deforestation near smelting sites leaves residual scarring.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic malaria, waterborne intestinal parasites, and trypanosomiasis in the tsetse belt raise background mortality; sedentary life near standing water increases exposure; no evidence of epidemic die-offs, but childhood mortality remains high.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ The shift to grain staples provides carbohydrate stability but narrows the dietary spectrum; skeletal evidence from later periods suggests periodic nutritional stress during drought years; body size and robustness remain adequate for the labor demands of farming and metalworking.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Healing remains embedded in kinship and ritual; herbalism, bonesetting, and midwifery transmitted orally; no specialized institutions; recovery capacity limited to kin-group resources.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Sedentarization and reliable grain stores encourage earlier marriage and higher fertility; polygyny common among herders integrating into farming communities; pairing governed by kinship alliance and bridewealth negotiation conducted orally.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population grows slowly as agriculture raises the carrying ceiling; villages of 50–200 people cluster along rivers and fertile soils; the savanna belt absorbs migrants displaced from the desiccating Sahara, concentrating genetic and cultural diversity.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Childhood mortality and short adult lifespans keep the dependency ratio manageable; the aged and infirm remain within kin compounds; no evidence of institutional dependency management.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Pearl millet, sorghum, fonio, and African rice anchor the diet; cowpea provides protein; cattle, sheep, goats, and fishing supplement grain; the household granary buffers seasonal scarcity; subsistence is secure in normal years but collapses in consecutive drought years.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human muscle powers cultivation, harvest, and grinding; livestock provide transport, traction for some tasks, and manure; fire is the sole non-biological energy source, essential for cooking, clearing, and smelting; per-capita energy remains low but sufficient for village life.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Labor is organized by household and kin group; seasonal work parties mobilize neighbors through reciprocal obligation; no evidence of slavery or coerced labor in this period; metallurgical work is specialized and transmitted within lineages.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Grain surplus supports dry-season reserves and enables craft specialization; ironworking requires surplus food to free smiths from farming; surplus accumulation is modest and vulnerable to drought.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Clay granaries, underground pits, and raised platforms store millet and sorghum for up to two years; livestock serve as wealth on the hoof; no monetary instruments; buffering depends on kin-network reciprocity.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Local exchange of grain, livestock, pots, and metal tools occurs at village scale; longer-distance exchange of copper from the Air and salt from Saharan sources emerges; all conducted face-to-face and orally negotiated; no markets or written accounting.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Cattle and grain function as stored value; bridewealth obligations create deferred reciprocity; no coinage or standardized currency; debt and credit embedded in kin relations and verbal agreement.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Surplus circulates through kinship obligation, bridewealth, and ritual feasting; no centralized extraction or tribute; redistribution governed by oral custom and elder authority.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Wealth differentiates households modestly; cattle ownership marks status; land remains communal or lineage-held; no fortifications or standing defense; wealth is protected by kin solidarity.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Agricultural waste feeds livestock or composts into fields; metallurgical slag accumulates near smelting sites; refuse is minimal and locally absorbed; no evidence of environmental degradation beyond localized woodland clearance.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Patrilineal descent predominates, though matrilineal elements persist in some groups; the extended family compound is the basic social unit; inheritance follows oral custom; elders adjudicate disputes within kin structures.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Age grades, work parties, and ritual sodalities crosscut kin lines; mutual aid in farming, herding, and housebuilding is obligatory; the village community coheres through oral custom and face-to-face negotiation.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Seasonal festivals mark planting, harvest, and initiation; drumming, dance, and oral performance structure communal time; no separate leisure class; sociability is embedded in work and ritual.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Status distinctions emerge between herders and farmers, smiths and cultivators; smiths occupy an ambiguous position, respected for craft but often set apart; no formal class or caste system; boundaries remain fluid.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ No bureaucracy or formal administration; village affairs decided by council of elders; lineage heads coordinate labor and ritual; all governance oral and consensual; no record-keeping.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ No standing military; raiding and feuding occur between groups; bows, spears, and clubs are the weapons; no fortifications; collective defense mobilizes kin and village; iron weapons begin to appear but do not yet transform warfare.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Disputes settled by elder mediation and ritual reconciliation; feuds occasionally persist; no evidence of endemic violence or social breakdown; friction absorbed through kinship and oral custom; residual grievance managed within the fabric.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ The mood is pragmatic and seasonal, attuned to agricultural rhythms; trust extends within kin and village; intergroup relations wary but not hostile; anxiety rises during drought years.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Elders and lineage heads hold authority through seniority, oratorical skill, and ritual knowledge; smiths command ambivalent respect; no paramount chiefs; leadership is situational and personal.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Allegiance attaches to kin, village, and ancestral cult; legitimacy rests on oral tradition, genealogical precedent, and ritual performance; no abstract state loyalty.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Collective action mobilizes for defense, labor, and ritual but does not extend beyond the village; no regional coordination; will is diffuse and locally bounded.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Relations with neighboring villages and pastoral groups are negotiated orally; trade and intermarriage create ties; hostility is episodic rather than chronic; no expansionist drive.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ No evidence of widespread disaffection; the system is small-scale and kin-embedded, absorbing frustration through kinship channels; exhaustion is seasonal, tied to agricultural labor, not political.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Speech is the sole medium; local languages are mutually intelligible within village clusters; no writing; all knowledge transmitted orally; foreigners and distant groups are linguistically opaque.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Information travels at walking speed via messenger and traveler; news diffuses through markets and seasonal gatherings; reach is limited to the regional network of trade and kinship.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Memory is the only archive; genealogies, agricultural calendars, and metallurgical techniques preserved in specialist lineages; loss occurs with death and migration; no durable record.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Elders control ritual speech; taboos govern discussion of the sacred; metallurgical secrets jealously guarded within smith lineages; discourse is shaped by respect protocols and oral etiquette.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Reception occurs in face-to-face settings; amplification through repetition and performance; no broadcast; attention governed by social rank and ritual context.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Oral transmission is vulnerable to drift and loss; correction occurs through redundancy and collective memory; no verification beyond consensus; noise accumulates over generations.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Justice is kinship-based, balancing reciprocity, honor, and revenge; fairness assessed by oral custom and elder judgment; the collective good prioritized over individual autonomy.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ No written law; oral custom governs property, marriage, injury, and inheritance; taboos enforced by ritual sanction; rules evolve slowly through precedent and elder interpretation.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Elders and diviners adjudicate disputes; liability assessed through oral testimony and oath; sanctions include compensation, exile, and ritual purification; no imprisonment.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Individual rights embedded in kin status; protection derives from lineage membership; compliance burden low in normal times; rule-breaking risks ostracism and supernatural sanction.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Powerful lineages may escape sanction; feuds sometimes persist unresolved; the gap between justice owed and delivered is managed through ritual and kinship pressure but never fully closed.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Understanding is empirical and practical, embedded in oral tradition; causation attributed to spirits, ancestors, and observable regularities; no formal theory or inquiry distinct from craft and ritual knowledge.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Metallurgy represents the frontier of technique: smelting iron from laterite nodules using charcoal furnaces, forging tools and weapons, producing copper ornaments; agricultural technique includes seed selection, soil management, and seasonal timing; all transmitted orally within specialist lineages.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Knowledge transmitted through apprenticeship and initiation; metallurgical skill restricted to smith lineages; agricultural knowledge general but variable in depth; literacy nonexistent; the knowledge stock grows through accumulation and is vulnerable to lineage extinction.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Stone tool techniques persist alongside iron but gradually decline; knowledge loss occurs when lineages die out or migrate; no mechanism to preserve technique beyond oral transmission; decay is slow but cumulative.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Identity anchors in lineage, village, and ancestral cult; origin myths explain the first settlers, the discovery of iron, and the covenant with the land; memory is genealogical and oral; no written history.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Authority legitimated by seniority, ancestral precedent, and ritual purity; the smith's power derives from mastery of fire and transformation; no abstract ideology; legitimation is enacted in performance and genealogical recitation.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Pottery, ironwork, and personal ornament display regional styles; no monumental architecture; terracotta figurines appear in the Nok region toward the end of this window; the visual world is functional and ritually charged; dress and scarification mark status and lineage.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Forms are stable and slowly evolving; no evidence of symbolic exhaustion; meaning remains anchored in kin and ritual; the system renews through initiation and seasonal performance.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ The world is animated by spirits, ancestors, and forces immanent in landscape and craft; human life is embedded in a web of obligation to the dead and the land; fate is negotiable through ritual; the outlook is practical and relational.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Ancestor veneration structures daily and seasonal life; sacrifice, libation, and divination maintain cosmic balance; ironworking itself is ritualized, the smith mediating between human and spirit worlds; initiation rites mark life transitions.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ No formal clergy or temple; ritual authority diffused among elders, diviners, and smiths; no orthodoxy; practice varies by lineage and locality; authority is personal and situational.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ No external religious contact in this period; practice is local and internally generated; syncretism occurs within the region as groups mix; no displacement.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ The sacred remains vital and uncontested; no secularizing drift; the cosmology absorbs all experience; disenchantment is absent; the system is spiritually self-sufficient.
