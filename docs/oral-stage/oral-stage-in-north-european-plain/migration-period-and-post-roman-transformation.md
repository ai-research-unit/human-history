# __400 CE – 700 CE__ — Migration Period and Post-Roman Transformation
__Summary:__ The North European Plain undergoes radical political and demographic reordering as the Roman frontier collapses and Germanic peoples — Franks, Saxons, Angles, Lombards — migrate, conquer, and consolidate new polities across former imperial and free territories; the binding constraint is the breakdown of the Roman exchange network that had channeled wealth into the region, forcing elites to compete for diminishing prestige goods and new territorial bases; absorptive capacity for the dislocations is tested by cascading movements, but the low population density and agricultural resilience of the plain provide margin; the direction of change is toward larger, more stratified successor kingdoms, nascent Christianization in Frankish zones, and the depopulation of the plain's western portions as Angles and Saxons depart for Britain.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The North European Plain remains a low-relief corridor of moraines, outwash sands, and river valleys; the Rhine, Elbe, Oder, and Vistula continue to channel movement; forests regenerate in some depopulated areas, slightly increasing friction.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Late Antique Little Ice Age (~536–660 CE) brings cooler, wetter conditions and volcanic dimming events (536, 540 CE); crop yields decline; volatility increases; the system is stressed but not collapsed.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Flood risk persists along rivers; the 536 dust veil event causes widespread crop failure; exposure to acute climate shocks heightened; margin against consecutive bad years thin.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Iron, amber, timber, and furs remain available; some Roman infrastructure (roads, fortifications) decays but rivers stay navigable; endowment intact though access networks disrupted.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Population decline and settlement abandonment reduce waste loads; absorptive capacity ample; no sink stress.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ The Justinianic Plague (541–544 CE and recurrences) reaches northwestern Europe, though impact on the interior plain is poorly attested and likely attenuated; endemic infections persist; mortality baseline elevated during plague pulses.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Cooler climate and disrupted trade reduce dietary diversity in some areas; skeletal evidence shows slight decline in stature during sixth century; nutritional stress moderate, not catastrophic.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Patrilineal exogamy continues; elite polygyny persists; high fertility within surviving households; reproductive patterns stable despite demographic shocks.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population declines regionally due to migration outflows (Anglo-Saxon departure) and plague; density drops in western plain; eastern and northern zones more stable; net renewal uncertain but probably negative in core migration-source areas.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Warfare and migration leave orphans and widows; kin absorption mechanisms strained in worst-affected areas; dependency overhang elevated but not crippling.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Mixed farming continues — cereals, cattle, pigs — but climate stress and labor shortage in depopulated zones push some communities closer to the provisioning edge; household pooling intensifies.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human and animal muscle remain dominant; no technological change in energy capture; per-capita work ceiling unchanged; conversion efficiency stable.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Free farmers and warriors predominate; unfree (thralls) remain a minority; labor reorganization around new elite landholdings emerging in Frankish zones; coercion level rising with proto-manorial structures.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Surplus capacity contracts with population decline and climate stress; elite extraction must compete for a smaller pool; production recovering by late sixth century as climate stabilizes.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Grain storage in pit houses continues; livestock herds provide reserve; centralized storage absent; buffer against multi-year failure thin during worst climate pulses.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Roman trade networks collapse; long-distance exchange shifts to north-south routes (Baltic amber to Mediterranean via Frankish and Lombard intermediaries); local exchange persists; velocity slowed then recovering.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Roman coinage drains away; Frankish gold tremissis and silver denarius circulate in western zones; barter and gift-exchange dominate interior; deferred reciprocity embedded in warband and kin obligations.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Emerging Frankish kings and Lombard dukes extract tribute and military service; redistribution through feasting and gift-giving; extraction formalizing in Merovingian heartland.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Furnished weapon graves show continued elite wealth concentration; land grants to warriors create proto-aristocracy; asset defense via retinue and fortified centers; inequality widening.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Reduced production lowers waste output; settlement dispersal prevents accumulation; no sink stress.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Trust radius contracts during migration chaos, then stabilizes within new political units; kin-based mutual aid persists; guest-friendship (hospitality bonds) continues to bridge groups; trust rebuilding through the window.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Ethnic identities fluid — "Franks," "Saxons," "Lombards" are political coalitions absorbing diverse peoples; warrior aristocracy crystallizes; free-unfree distinction sharpens; boundaries hardening into proto-feudal hierarchy.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Patrilineal descent and partible inheritance continue; royal and noble lineages gain importance; marriage alliances between emerging dynasties; household remains primary unit.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Tribal assemblies decline in areas under strong kingship; warbands evolve into landed retinues; local village communities persist; associational fabric thinning at supra-local level, thickening at elite-retinue level.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Feasting remains central to elite cohesion; gaming, hunting, and drinking bouts continue; Christian feast days added in Frankish zones; sociability adapts to new political forms.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Blood feud persists but increasingly regulated by royal law in Frankish areas (Salic law, Lex Ribuaria); church offers sanctuary; internal grievance partially channeled into external warfare; discharge improving where royal authority consolidates.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Power consolidates into larger kingdoms — Merovingian Franks dominate west, Lombards move south, Anglo-Saxon kingdoms form in Britain; eastern plain remains fragmented among Slavic and residual Germanic groups; authority centralizing in west, fragmenting in east.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Merovingian succession partible among sons, generating civil wars; other kingdoms similarly contested; no routinized transfer; flashpoint potential high.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Royal legitimacy derives from martial success, divine favor, and increasingly from Christian blessing (Clovis's conversion ~496); ancestral charisma remains important; narrative adapting to include Christian elements.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Merovingians inherit Roman administrative fragments — counts, written law codes, tax collection attempts — but capacity remains weak; east of Rhine purely kin-based governance; institutional integrity low, improving slowly in Frankish core.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Free warriors retain assembly rights in theory; in practice, royal courts and aristocratic councils dominate; military obligation universal for freemen; mobilization for campaigns remains effective.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Frankish kingdom expands into Burgundy, Thuringia, Bavaria; Lombards contest Italy; Anglo-Saxons consolidate Britain; eastern plain sees Slavic expansion into depopulated zones; constant frontier warfare and alliance-shifting.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Warbands equipped with iron swords, spears, francisca (throwing axe), shields; some cavalry; no standing armies; force dispersed among royal and aristocratic retinues; fortified centers (burgs) emerging.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Inter-kingdom warfare endemic; banditry elevated during power vacuums; royal law attempts to regulate feud; disorder moderate to high, declining where Frankish authority consolidates.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Oral messengers and face-to-face communication dominate; church networks begin to carry information in Frankish zones; velocity remains at riding speed; reach expanding with Frankish political integration.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Oral memory remains primary storage for law, genealogy, and heroic tradition; Frankish courts produce some Latin documents (charters, law codes) but these serve elites and clergy; interior plain remains non-literate; persistence depends on living memory; loss with memory-keeper death.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Heroic poetry frames elite deeds; Christian discourse enters Frankish zones; no formal censorship; taboo subjects (oathbreaking, cowardice) persist; discourse norms stable but adapting.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Feasts and courts amplify reputation; church sermons add new channel in Christian areas; heroic deeds gain wide attention; mundane information attenuates rapidly.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Oral drift continues; genealogical memory accurate for a few generations; older traditions telescope and merge; noise accumulates but remains tolerable.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Honor, reciprocity, and kin obligation persist; Christian notions of sin and divine judgment enter Frankish zones; sense of justice adapting but core remains Germanic.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Written law codes emerge (Salic law ~500 CE, Lex Ribuaria ~630s) codifying customary practice in Latin; east of Rhine law remains oral; codification represents major shift in Frankish areas.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Royal courts and counts adjudicate major disputes; ordeals and oath-helpers persist; enforcement relies on royal officers backed by threat of force; liability remains collective within kin but individual accountability emerging.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Free warriors retain arms-bearing and assembly rights; church grants sanctuary; unfree bound to land; compliance burden low but royal law adds new obligations.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Royal power checks aristocratic impunity somewhat; church offers some redress for weak; impunity gap narrowing in Frankish core, wide elsewhere.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ No formal inquiry tradition in Germanic areas; church preserves fragments of Roman learning in monasteries; explanatory framework remains mythological for most, theological for Christian converts.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Iron smelting, boat-building, timber construction, and animal husbandry continue; Frankish metalwork and jewelry show technical refinement; Roman craft techniques lost in some areas, preserved by church artisans in others.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Training informal and kin-based for most; monastic schools begin in Frankish zones; literacy confined to clergy and tiny elite; knowledge stock contracting in some crafts, preserved in church contexts.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Roman engineering, road maintenance, and urban crafts decay; some metallurgical techniques lost; monasteries slow decay by copying texts; overall knowledge stock declining outside church preservation.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Tribal origin myths persist; heroic legends (Sigurd, Beowulf traditions forming) anchor identity; Frankish royal genealogy elaborated; Christian salvation narrative added in converted areas; composite identity emerging.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Polychrome jewelry (garnet cloisonne), animal-style metalwork, weapon burials; material culture displays status and ethnic affiliation; Frankish and Anglo-Saxon styles distinctive; church introduces Mediterranean motifs.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Uncertainty and martial alertness during early migration chaos; stabilizing into confident expansion in Frankish zones; mood variable by region and decade; generally recovering by late sixth century.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Traditional pagan forms remain vital in interior; Christian forms newly energetic in Frankish areas; some tension between dying pagan and rising Christian symbolism; form exhaustion minimal, transition ongoing.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Polytheistic worldview persists east of Rhine; Christian cosmology spreading in Frankish and Anglo-Saxon converted zones; fate and divine providence coexist; existential outlook shifting from pagan fatalism toward Christian eschatology in west.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Weapon deposits and bog sacrifices decline; pagan festivals persist in interior; Christian baptism, mass, and saints' feasts spreading in west; mixed practice common in transition zones.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Bishops and monasteries gain power in Frankish kingdom; no centralized hierarchy beyond Rome's distant authority; pagan priesthood declines where Christianity advances; doctrinal authority consolidating in west.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Christianity displacing paganism in Frankish heartland; syncretic blending (pagan sites Christianized, feast days adapted); interior plain remains predominantly pagan; diffusion front moving slowly east and north.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ No secularizing drift; sacred landscape transforms rather than empties; pagan sacred groves replaced by churches; disenchantment absent; spiritual needs re-anchored in Christian practice where conversion occurs.<br><br>
