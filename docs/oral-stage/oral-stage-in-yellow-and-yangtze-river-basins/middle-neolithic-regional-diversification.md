# __5,000 BCE – 3,500 BCE__ — Middle Neolithic Regional Diversification

__Summary:__ Across fifteen centuries the farming villages of the Yellow and Yangtze basins multiply, diverge, and knit themselves into distinct regional traditions—Yangshao painted pottery in the middle Yellow River, Dawenkou jade and elaborate mortuary rites in Shandong, Majiabang and Songze wet-rice communities in the lower Yangtze—while exchange corridors move goods, styles, and probably spoken lore across hundreds of kilometers; the chief causal factor is demographic growth on a now-secure agricultural base, which drives village fission and territorial expansion until regional cultural boundaries crystallize, all governed through spoken tradition alone.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The middle Yellow River cuts through loess terraces that yield easily to digging sticks and hoes, while the lower Yangtze spreads across alluvial plains and lake-margin wetlands suited to paddy rice; corridors along tributary valleys and the Huai River drainage connect these zones, though ridges and marshes impose friction that encourages local clustering rather than continuous settlement.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ The Holocene Climatic Optimum continues: monsoon rainfall is strong and reliable, winters milder than in preceding millennia, and the northern millet zone receives sufficient summer precipitation while the southern rice zone enjoys long, warm growing seasons; interannual variability remains, but no sustained droughts or floods punctuate the archaeological record severely enough to collapse settlement.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Localized flooding along river courses is endemic, prompting villages to occupy terrace edges or elevated ground; earthquake risk exists along fault systems but leaves no catastrophic horizon in the stratigraphy; wildfire threatens the forested margins of expanding farmland, and slash-and-burn clearance is itself a controlled hazard.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Loess soils in the north replenish nutrients through wind-borne deposition, while alluvial silts in the south renew fertility with seasonal floods; timber for construction and fuel is locally abundant though receding as clearance advances; stone for grinding tools is quarried regionally, and jade sources—Liaoning, Jiangsu, and points farther—supply prestige goods through exchange.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Midden accumulation around villages grows with population, but settlements are small enough and turnover frequent enough that abandoned sites revegetate; river systems carry organic waste downstream; no evidence of regional degradation, though localized soil depletion may prompt village relocation after decades of cultivation.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic gastrointestinal and parasitic infections persist wherever humans cluster near stagnant water and animal pens; childhood mortality remains high, but adult skeletal evidence suggests moderate lifespans without epidemic spikes; malaria may be present in the southern wetlands.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Millet in the north and rice in the south now provide staple carbohydrates; supplementary protein comes from pigs, dogs, fish, and hunted game; dental caries appear in rice-dependent populations, but stature is stable or improving relative to Late Pleistocene foragers; vitamin deficiencies remain possible where diets narrow.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Wound care, herbal remedies, and midwifery are transmitted orally within kin groups; shamanic practitioners likely mediate healing rituals; skeletal evidence of healed fractures indicates survival through injury; no specialized medical caste is visible, and care capacity is limited to village-scale reciprocity.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Monogamous or mildly polygynous pair-bonding is inferred from house sizes and burial pairing; exogamous marriage between villages is probable given genetic diversity; fertility is high enough to drive population growth, constrained mainly by infant mortality and resource limits.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population expands from nucleated villages of a few hundred into networks of dozens of settlements per regional culture; birth-to-death ratios favor growth, and migration flows outward as daughter villages hive off from parent sites; density remains low by later standards—perhaps 5–20 persons per square kilometer in core areas.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ The aged and infirm are visible in burials but not in numbers that strain productive capacity; infant mortality absorbs a large fraction of births; communal storage and kinship obligations buffer the dependent load without evidence of crisis.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Millet agriculture in the north and rice paddies in the south secure the caloric floor; gardens provide vegetables; pigs and dogs supply protein; foraging supplements the cultivated base; famine risk is reduced but not eliminated when harvests fail.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human labor remains the primary motive force, supplemented by fire for land clearance, cooking, and pottery kilns; no draft animals yet harness muscle power for plowing; per-capita work output is modest, gating the scale of construction and craft production.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Households organize their own cultivation; communal labor for larger tasks—house-raising, ditch-digging, perhaps kiln-firing—is coordinated through kinship obligation and oral agreement rather than coercion; no evidence of slave-taking or corvée.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Surplus is modest but sufficient to support part-time craft specialists—potters, jade-workers, basket-weavers—and to provision communal feasts; granary pits indicate multi-season storage; the surplus remains village-scale, not aggregated into regional treasuries.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Subterranean storage pits preserve grain against spoilage and rodents; household and communal reserves buffer against single-season shortfalls; multi-year famines would exhaust reserves, but no such collapse is recorded in the archaeology.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Long-distance exchange moves jade from northeastern and southeastern sources, marine shell from the coast, and distinctive pottery styles along river corridors; exchange is embedded in reciprocity and gift-giving, not market trade; no standardized value medium exists.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Jade objects, fine ceramics, and ornaments serve as stores of prestige and potential reciprocity obligations; deferred exchange operates through oral memory of who owes whom; no currency or accounting beyond remembered claims.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Village leaders or lineage heads likely coordinate feasting and redistribution, extracting contributions through social pressure rather than formal taxation; no palatial storehouses or bureaucratic apparatus; redistribution is oral, personal, and bounded by face-to-face relations.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Burial goods show modest differentiation—some graves richer in pottery, jade, or pig mandibles—but no extreme concentration; wealth is defended by kinship solidarity and communal reputation rather than fortification or armed retainers.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Broken pottery, food refuse, and ash accumulate in middens; organic waste decomposes or is scattered on fields; no toxic byproducts; settlement relocation after decades may be partly a response to midden saturation and local soil exhaustion.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Clan and lineage structure, organized through oral genealogy, determines residence, marriage eligibility, and access to land; inheritance likely follows patrilineal or cognatic descent depending on region; household clusters in village layouts suggest extended-family compounds.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Reciprocal labor exchange, communal storage, and collective ritual bind households within villages; inter-village exchange alliances extend mutual aid across the landscape; no formal associations beyond kinship and locality are visible.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Communal feasting, musical instruments (bone flutes, pottery drums), and decorative arts suggest organized sociability; ceramic styles may mark group identity; play and festival punctuate the agricultural calendar, reinforcing bonds through shared pleasure.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Status differentiation is emerging but not rigid—richer burials, larger houses, finer goods—yet no hereditary elite is archaeologically certain; elders, skilled craft-workers, and shamanic figures likely hold prestige; boundaries between villages and regions are cultural, not caste-based.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ No formal administration exists; village affairs are managed through oral deliberation, elder authority, and consensus; inter-village relations are negotiated face-to-face or through kin networks; no bureaucracy, no archive, no permanent office.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Weapons include bows, stone axes, and bone or stone-tipped spears; violence is attested by occasional skeletal trauma, but no standing military or fortification appears in this window; coercion remains interpersonal, not institutionalized.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ Village fission and migration discharge social friction by allowing discontented lineages to depart; no evidence of endemic warfare or widespread violence; the social fabric is loose enough that exit, not rebellion, resolves grievance; disorder residue is low.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ The mood is expansive—population growing, land available, harvests reliable—fostering confidence and willingness to colonize new ground; trust extends through kin and exchange partners but not beyond; intergroup suspicion remains a check on cooperation.

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Leaders are likely lineage heads or charismatic individuals skilled in ritual, oratory, or craft; authority is personal, not official, and must be renewed through persuasion, generosity, and demonstrated competence; no hereditary chieftainship is certain.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Allegiance flows to kin, ancestors, and local spirits rather than to abstract institutions; loyalty is face-to-face, conditional, and reinforced by reciprocity; no concept of sovereignty or state commands obedience.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ Collective action is achievable for village-scale projects—house construction, ritual feasting, perhaps defense—but coordination beyond the village is episodic and negotiated; no permanent polity concentrates will.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Relations among regional cultures are mediated by exchange, intermarriage, and probably oral diplomacy; no evidence of conquest or tributary relations; competition exists but is channeled through prestige rather than warfare.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ No evidence of widespread disaffection; the outlet of migration absorbs malcontents; anomie and exhaustion are not archaeologically visible; will remains localized and renewable.

## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ Spoken language is universal within communities; regional dialects likely diverge as populations spread, but mutual intelligibility persists within cultural zones; no writing; all complex communication is oral.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Information travels at walking pace along river corridors and trade routes; oral messengers, seasonal gatherings, and exchange visits move news and innovation; transmission velocity is slow but sufficient for regional style diffusion over generations.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Memory is the sole archive; genealogies, mythic narratives, and technical know-how are stored in trained minds and transmitted through speech and demonstration; nothing is retrievable once the knower dies without teaching a successor.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Discourse is face-to-face and regulated by kinship etiquette; taboos govern speech about the dead, the sacred, and possibly clan secrets; no formal censorship, but social pressure shapes what may be said and to whom.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Oral performance—storytelling, chanting, oratory—commands attention in communal settings; repetition and rhythm aid memory; innovation spreads through imitation and retelling; no mechanism for mass amplification beyond the gathered audience.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Each retelling introduces drift; no external check verifies accuracy; signal integrity depends on the fidelity of trained memory and the conservatism of oral formulae; noise accumulates across generations, and divergent traditions emerge.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Justice is rooted in reciprocity, kinship obligation, and ancestral precedent; fairness means fulfilling debts, honoring kin, and respecting taboos; transgression injures the community and offends the spirits.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ No codified law exists; norms are transmitted orally as custom, proverb, and ancestral injunction; evolution occurs through reinterpretation and gradual drift, not legislation.

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Elders or shamanic figures adjudicate disputes; liability is collective—kin answer for kin; sanctions include compensation, shaming, exile, and possibly ritual purification; no formal court or written record.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Rights are embedded in kinship and communal membership rather than abstract entitlement; compliance is habitual, enforced by reputation and belonging; the burden is light where norms are internalized.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Wrongs within kin groups may go unredressed if elders decline to act; inter-village wrongs may trigger vendetta cycles; impunity is limited by the small scale of community and the visibility of transgression.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Empirical knowledge of soils, seasons, plant and animal behavior accumulates through observation and is framed within animist and ancestral cosmologies; no distinction between natural and supernatural causation; the paradigm is practical-mythic.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Pottery technology advances—painted wares, controlled firing, regional styles—while jade-working refines drilling and polishing; agricultural technique adapts to local soils; all method is transmitted orally and by demonstration.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Children learn by observation and apprenticeship within kin groups; no formal schooling; specialist knowledge in pottery, jade-work, and ritual is held by practitioners and transmitted to successors; literacy is absent.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ When a skilled practitioner dies without successors, technique is lost; regional traditions diverge partly through this decay; no archive preserves superseded knowledge; innovation and loss proceed together.

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Each regional culture elaborates its own origin narratives, ancestral heroes, and totemic affiliations; painted pottery motifs may encode mythic symbols; identity is local and lineage-bound, not pan-regional.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Authority is legitimated by ancestral sanction, shamanic insight, and demonstrated competence; no formal ideology; the narrative is that the ancestors established the way, and descendants must follow.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Yangshao painted pottery—red and black geometric and zoomorphic designs—is the signature expression of the middle Yellow River; Dawenkou jade, Majiabang pile-dwellings, and regional ceramic styles mark other zones; dress, ornament, and house form express group identity.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Ceramic styles evolve continuously, suggesting renewal rather than exhaustion; forms remain potent carriers of identity; no evidence of anomie or hollowed symbolism; the cultural surface is vital.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ The world is animated by spirits residing in rivers, mountains, ancestors, and animals; human existence is embedded in this spirit-world, and success depends on maintaining correct relations with it; fatalism and piety coexist.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Burial rites, foundation deposits, animal sacrifice, and probably seasonal festivals enact the sacred; shamanic figures mediate between human and spirit realms; ritual is oral, performative, and communal.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ No formal clergy or temple institution; shamanic practitioners hold informal authority; doctrine is oral tradition, not codified dogma; authority is charismatic and localized.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Regional cosmologies likely borrow elements through exchange and intermarriage, but each culture maintains distinct emphases; no missionary expansion or doctrinal conflict is visible; syncretism is incremental.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ No evidence of disenchantment; the sacred pervades daily life; the spiritual need is met by ancestral and animist practice; no residue of emptied cosmology accumulates.
