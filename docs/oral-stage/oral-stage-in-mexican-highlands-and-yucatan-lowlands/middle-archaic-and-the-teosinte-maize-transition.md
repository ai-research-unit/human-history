# __6000 BCE – 4000 BCE__ — Middle Archaic and the Teosinte-Maize Transition
__Summary:__ A two-millennium trend in which deliberate selection pressure transforms wild teosinte into recognizable maize across the Balsas drainage and adjacent highlands, while squash, beans, chili peppers, and bottle gourd enter the cultivated repertoire at Tehuacan, Guilá Naquitz, and related sites. The binding constraint remains seasonal mobility — populations still move between resource patches and cultivation plots supplement rather than replace wild foods — but the trajectory points toward increasing sedentism and caloric reliance on cultigens. Absorptive margin against subsistence failure remains substantial because foraging fallbacks persist; the overall direction is a slow ratchet toward agricultural dependence without yet crossing that threshold.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Highland valleys of the Balsas, Tehuacan, and Oaxaca basins separated by rugged sierras; lowland plains and coastal margins accessible but ecologically distinct; foot travel over mountain passes imposes scheduling friction on seasonal rounds that now incorporate planted plots.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Mid-Holocene thermal maximum brings slightly warmer and variable precipitation; summer monsoon pattern established; interannual drought risk persists but within tolerable bounds; secular trend stable to slightly drier.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Volcanic and seismic risk continues in the Trans-Mexican Belt; localized floods and droughts affect particular valleys; hazard exposure moderate given dispersed settlement and continued mobility; margin adequate.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Wild teosinte, mesquite, agave, cactus fruits, deer, rabbit, and diverse small fauna remain abundant; obsidian, chert, and basalt available; no resource depletion evident; cultivated plots add to rather than replace wild stocks.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Camp refuse and cultivation residue dispersed; no concentration of waste loads; absorptive capacity effectively unlimited at current population density.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic parasites and enteric infections present; population density too low for epidemic amplification; infant and childhood mortality elevated during nutritional stress; overall mortality baseline stable.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Mixed diet of wild and cultivated plants, supplemented by small game, provides adequate nutrition under normal conditions; maize still a minor caloric contributor; physiological vitality moderate with seasonal variation.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Fertility constrained by extended lactation and mobility; pair-bonding stable; selective pressure favoring foraging competence and the capacity to manage cultivation during seasonal rounds.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population densities remain very low but edging upward as cultivation improves returns; slow positive growth; age structure young; migration minimal and localized.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Dependency load small; elderly and disabled absorbed within kin networks; mobility constraints limit accumulation of dependents; overhang cleared within band capacity.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Foraging remains primary with cultivation as supplement; maize, squash, beans, and chili add to seasonal provisioning; household pooling buffers shortfalls; floor adequate but tightening as populations inch upward.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Human muscle and fire remain sole energy sources; fire used for cooking, field clearing, and processing; conversion efficiency unchanged; work ceiling stable.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Task division by age and sex; cultivation introduces new scheduling demands but no coerced labor; cooperation within kin groups for planting and harvesting.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Modest surplus possible through seed storage; production oriented toward immediate consumption with limited carryover; no structural surplus extraction.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Pit storage of dried maize kernels and squash seeds; reserves cover weeks to a few months; reciprocal sharing across bands provides additional buffer.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Obsidian continues to move through down-the-line exchange; marine shell reaches interior sites; seeds and cultivars may diffuse along exchange networks; volume low, embedded in social relations.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Deferred reciprocity obligations persist as the primary credit mechanism; stored seeds serve as value reserve; no monetary instrument; exchange remains personal and memory-based.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Band-level sharing of harvests and large kills; no hierarchical extraction; redistribution by consensus and kinship obligation.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Portable tools and seed caches constitute property; no significant wealth concentration; defense unnecessary given mobility and egalitarian norms.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Cultivation residue left in fields to decompose; camp refuse dispersed; no toxic byproducts; routing ecologically integrated and negligible in impact.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ High trust within bands; extended networks through intermarriage and seasonal aggregation; mutual aid norms reinforced by reciprocity; trust radius stable.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Status achieved through skill in foraging, cultivation knowledge, and ritual expertise; no hereditary rank; group identity tied to territory, dialect, and lineage; boundaries permeable.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Bilateral or mildly patrilineal kinship; exogamous marriage links bands; inheritance limited to knowledge and portable goods; land not individually owned.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Band remains primary unit; seasonal aggregations for ritual, exchange, and mate selection; no supra-band institutions; coordination through kinship and custom.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Fireside storytelling, games, and music; seasonal gatherings intensify sociability; leisure integrated with subsistence rhythms.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Mediation, avoidance, and fission resolve disputes; mobility offers exit; grievance load small and cleared informally.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Egalitarian band organization; leadership situational and based on demonstrated competence; no permanent offices; decisions by adult consensus.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ No formal succession; roles shift with life stage and circumstance; transfer unproblematic given lack of accumulated authority.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Legitimacy derived from skill, generosity, and norm adherence; oral tradition supports egalitarian values; no elaborate justifying ideology.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ No administrative apparatus; coordination face-to-face; no records; integrity question not applicable.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ All adults participate; collective action through persuasion; dissent expressed by departure.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Inter-band relations governed by kinship and alliance; territorial boundaries fluid; occasional low-level conflict; no external polity threats.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Hunting weapons serve for defense; no standing warriors; violence sporadic and personal; command of force not centralized.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Internal disorder minimal; social sanction and ostracism maintain order; banditry absent; enforcement capacity adequate.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Face-to-face speech at walking pace; seasonal aggregations accelerate diffusion; cultivation knowledge spreads through demonstration and intermarriage; reach limited to personal networks.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ All knowledge oral; cultivation techniques, planting calendars, and seed selection criteria stored in memory and transmitted intergenerationally; persistence vulnerable to demographic loss but stable under current conditions.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Discourse shaped by respect for elders and ritual propriety; some knowledge restricted by age or initiation; censorship informal but effective.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Attention on survival information and social news; stories and songs amplify memorable content; audience limited to those present.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Small-group communication maintains integrity; contradictions resolved by discussion; noise negligible; verification through shared experience.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Justice as reciprocity and proportionality; sharing norms deeply held; taboo violation invites supernatural and social sanction.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ No formal law; norms transmitted as custom and taboo; evolution by consensus; new situations resolved by analogy.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Elders mediate disputes; enforcement through compensation, shaming, or exclusion; liability personal.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Individual autonomy high; compliance burden light; predictability through shared norms.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Persistent offenders face ostracism or extreme sanction; impunity gap small in close-knit groups.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Empirical knowledge of plant selection, phenology, and animal behavior accumulating; teosinte-to-maize transition represents active biological manipulation guided by observation; explanatory paradigm animistic; discovery frontier at plant domestication interface.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Selection of larger-kerneled, non-shattering teosinte variants; seed storage and planting techniques; flintknapping, atlatl, fire management; methods transmitted through apprenticeship and demonstration.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Learning by observation and participation; cultivation knowledge becoming specialized asset; skill distribution broad with emerging expertise in seed selection.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Some Pleistocene hunting lore fading; wild-plant knowledge remains vital; oral transmission stable; decay rate low.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Origin stories tied to landscape, ancestors, and founding migrations; emerging significance of maize in oral tradition as cultivation deepens; identity rooted in territory and lineage.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Chipped stone, woven baskets, cordage, gourd containers; body ornamentation; material culture utilitarian with embedded aesthetic; rock art possible but sparsely attested.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Cautious optimism as cultivation experiments succeed; pragmatic and adaptive disposition; confidence in seasonal cycles tempered by awareness of variability.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Traditional forms remain vital; no symbolic exhaustion; ritual and narrative renew meaning annually; form decay absent.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Animistic worldview with spiritual agency attributed to plants, animals, and landscape; human place understood as embedded in reciprocal relations; emerging ritual attention to cultivated species, especially maize; existential outlook accepts cyclical renewal.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Rituals mark planting, harvest, life transitions, and successful hunts; offerings maintain harmony with spirit world; devotional practice woven into subsistence activity.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ No formal institutions; shamans or ritual specialists hold knowledge without coercive authority; doctrine oral and community-validated; orthodoxy maintained by consensus.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Regional variation in belief; diffusion through intermarriage and aggregation; no dominant cosmology displacing others; syncretism organic.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Sacred worldview intact; disenchantment absent; spiritual needs met through ritual coherence; no existential residue.<br><br>
