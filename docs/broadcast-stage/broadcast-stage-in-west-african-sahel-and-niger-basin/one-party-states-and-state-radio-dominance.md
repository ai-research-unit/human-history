# __1970 CE – 1990 CE__ — One-Party States and State Radio Dominance
__Summary:__ The West African Sahel and Niger Basin consolidates under single-party regimes and military governments that monopolize broadcasting for propaganda and development communication. The binding constraint is state capture of all broadcast infrastructure, eliminating alternative information channels and narrowing discourse to regime-approved content. Margins against political shock are thin; coups cycle through Nigeria while Francophone states ossify under aging leaders. The direction of change is stagnation punctuated by crisis as structural adjustment devastates budgets, drought strikes repeatedly, and external services (BBC, RFI, VOA) become the only alternative information source for literate urban populations.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Sahel corridor remains vast and thinly connected; road networks expand marginally; the Niger River provides seasonal transport; broadcast signals extend further as transmitter networks grow but rural coverage gaps persist.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Severe Sahelian droughts strike 1968-1974 and 1983-1985, killing livestock and displacing populations; rainfall variability intensifies; aridification trends become visible at the northern margins.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Drought emerges as the dominant hazard; locust invasions compound agricultural losses; infrastructure margins against these shocks prove inadequate; famine relief depends on international intervention.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Nigerian petroleum output peaks as the primary revenue source; Niger uranium extraction begins; other states remain resource-poor; broadcast equipment continues to depend on imports.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Industrial expansion remains limited; oil flaring in the Niger Delta begins to degrade local environments; overall absorptive capacity is adequate except in extractive zones.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Malaria, measles, and meningitis persist; drought-related malnutrition elevates mortality during crisis years; health infrastructure stagnates under austerity; mortality declines slow.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Famine conditions during droughts devastate pastoral and marginal agricultural populations; urban nutrition deteriorates under structural adjustment; physiological vitality is declining in affected zones.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ High fertility continues; polygyny remains common; reproductive capacity is not biologically limiting; population growth persists despite elevated child mortality.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population growth rates remain among the world's highest; density increases in cities as rural-urban migration accelerates; youth bulge intensifies dependency pressures.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Orphaned children, displaced pastoralists, and drought victims accumulate; the productive cohort struggles to absorb dependency loads; international food aid becomes structurally necessary.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Repeated droughts breach the provisioning floor for millions; structural adjustment removes subsidies; urban populations face rising food prices; the survival margin narrows severely.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Nigerian oil dominates export earnings but generates enclave development; electrification expands slowly in capitals; rural areas remain dependent on firewood and muscle; per-capita energy capture stagnates.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Wage employment contracts under austerity; public sector retrenchment displaces workers; informal labor expands; coercion is political rather than economic as unions are suppressed.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Agricultural output fluctuates with rainfall; manufacturing remains negligible; oil revenues create surplus in Nigeria but others face chronic deficits; productive capacity does not expand.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ State reserves deplete under structural adjustment; foreign exchange constraints bite; grain reserves prove inadequate against multi-year drought; buffering capacity collapses in crisis years.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ Cross-border trade continues informally; official trade channels are disrupted by currency controls and import restrictions; logistics deteriorate as infrastructure maintenance lapses.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ CFA franc maintains parity until 1994 devaluation approaches; Nigerian naira depreciates severely; inflation erodes savings; formal credit contracts; informal tontines persist.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ States extract through marketing boards and petroleum rents; redistribution flows through patronage networks favoring ruling coalitions; structural adjustment demands extraction for debt service.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Inequality widens as political elites capture rents; rural populations lose assets in droughts; wealth defense relies on offshore accounts and ethnic solidarity networks.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Oil extraction waste accumulates in the Niger Delta; urban waste management deteriorates; industrial effluent is negligible elsewhere; waste routing becomes problematic only in extractive zones.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Trust contracts to immediate kin and ethnic networks under scarcity; mutual aid intensifies within groups while inter-group suspicion grows; the trust radius is narrowing.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Ethnic identification sharpens around access to shrinking state resources; military-political elites form distinct strata; class formation overlays ethnic division; boundaries are hardening.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Extended family networks absorb displaced members; household size increases as urban migrants house rural refugees; inheritance patterns remain stable; kinship bears increased load.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ Trade unions and independent associations are suppressed; state-controlled mass organizations replace civil society; religious congregations become alternative associational spaces; fabric is thinning under repression.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Radio listening remains central to urban sociability; football fandom intensifies; bar and market socializing continues; leisure spaces persist despite economic pressure.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Formal grievance channels are closed under single-party rule; ethnic associations absorb some friction; religious movements channel frustration; significant grievance accumulates unmetabolized.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Single-party or military regimes monopolize power; Nigeria cycles through coups (1975, 1983, 1985); Francophone states congeal under long-serving presidents; authority is centralized and personalized.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Transfer is unpredictable; coups replace elections; Francophone leaders cling to power; no routinized succession exists; each transfer becomes a crisis point.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Development and national unity remain official legitimating claims; performance failures erode belief; external broadcasts provide competing narratives; legitimacy is degrading.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Bureaucracies hollow under austerity; ghost workers and corruption proliferate; record-keeping deteriorates; administrative reach contracts to extractive and security functions.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Elections are absent or stage-managed; opposition is suppressed; participation is ritualized through ruling parties; genuine mobilization occurs only through ethnic or religious channels.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ Cold War positioning continues; France maintains Francafrique arrangements; Nigeria claims regional leadership; structural adjustment ties states to IMF/World Bank conditionality.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Military establishments expand with oil revenues (Nigeria) or French support (Francophone); internal security services monitor populations; coercive capacity increases and turns inward.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Ethnic violence flares periodically; student protests are suppressed; banditry increases in drought zones; Tuareg rebellions begin in Mali and Niger; disorder accumulates but is contained by force.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ State radio monopoly is complete; television expands to secondary cities; transmission reaches rural areas as transistor radios spread; velocity remains limited by single-source control.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Broadcast archives grow but maintenance deteriorates; tape degradation begins; written records are sparse; institutional memory depends on aging personnel; persistence is fragile.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Censorship is comprehensive; development and unity messaging dominates; criticism is forbidden; local languages expand on radio but content remains controlled; discourse is severely narrowed.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Transistor radio ownership spreads to villages; communal listening persists; shortwave reception of external services (BBC, RFI, VOA) provides alternative; attention divides between state and external sources.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ State monopoly simplifies signal; rumor and external broadcasts constitute the only counterflow; verification is impossible for official claims; noise accumulates as credibility erodes.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Customary justice norms persist; state law is seen as instrument of elite control; sense of justice diverges sharply from formal system; legitimacy of courts erodes.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Emergency decrees and military orders supersede constitutions; broadcasting remains state monopoly by decree; regulatory evolution is arbitrary and regime-serving.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Courts function under political direction; military tribunals handle dissent; enforcement is selective; liability falls on regime opponents while loyalists enjoy impunity.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Constitutional rights are suspended or ignored; press freedom is absent; citizens navigate arbitrary enforcement; compliance burden is low for the compliant, lethal for dissidents.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Political detention, torture, and extrajudicial killing occur without accountability; judicial redress is unavailable for regime victims; impunity is structural and accumulating.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Dependency theory challenges modernization; African universities struggle under austerity; brain drain accelerates; indigenous paradigms emerge in diaspora; formal inquiry stagnates locally.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Broadcast engineering maintains colonial-era techniques; satellite reception arrives; technical training shifts to local institutions with declining quality; methods ossify.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Primary enrollment plateaus; university quality declines; literacy gains slow; skill distribution remains sharply uneven; the knowledge stock is not growing significantly.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Equipment manuals become outdated; oral transmission of traditional knowledge continues; university libraries deteriorate; knowledge decay is visible in formal institutions.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Independence narratives lose potency as promises remain unfulfilled; ethnic origin stories reassert themselves; national identity weakens relative to ethnic and religious identification.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Afrobeat politicizes music (Fela Kuti, 1970s-80s); jùjú and mbalax spread via radio; architectural modernism stalls; material culture reflects stagnation and selective imitation.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Independence optimism dissipates; cynicism about government spreads; fatalism increases among the poor; risk aversion grows as opportunities narrow; the mood is darkening.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Independence symbols lose vitality; state ceremonies become hollow ritual; official discourse is disbelieved; form exhaustion is evident in political culture while popular culture remains vital.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Islamic reform movements gain adherents; Pentecostalism emerges in Nigeria; syncretic practices persist; fatalism and piety both intensify as material conditions deteriorate.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mosque attendance increases; Pentecostal crusades draw urban crowds; traditional rituals continue in rural areas; devotional activity is intensifying across traditions.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Sufi brotherhoods retain political influence; Izala reformism challenges them in Nigeria and Niger; Pentecostal churches multiply; institutional pluralism is expanding.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Islamic reform spreads southward; Christianity advances in the middle belt; syncretic practices face pressure from both; spiritual landscape is churning rather than stable.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Secular ideologies (socialism, developmentalism) lose credibility; religious revival fills the vacuum; disenchantment with politics does not produce disenchantment with the sacred; spiritual need is redirected rather than abandoned.<br><br>
