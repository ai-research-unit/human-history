# __1960 CE – 1975 CE — Television Expansion and Social Change__

__Summary:__ Television achieves near-universal household penetration across Western and Southern Europe while remaining under public service monopoly, becoming the dominant medium for news, entertainment, and political communication; the binding constraint is the tension between broadcasting's power to shape public opinion and the varying degrees of state control over nominally independent public broadcasters, exposed most dramatically in the 1968 movements and the ORTF strike that revealed the limits of broadcast autonomy under Gaullist direction.

## D01. Physical Domain

__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ Relay networks extend television coverage to rural and mountainous areas; Alpine repeaters, Mediterranean coastal stations, and Scandinavian transmitters close coverage gaps; Eurovision network enables routine pan-European live transmission.

__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Temperate conditions impose no constraint; extreme weather occasionally disrupts transmission but backup systems ensure continuity; no climate-driven changes affect broadcast infrastructure.

__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ No major natural disasters disrupt European broadcasting; Cold War nuclear anxiety underlies civil defense planning but does not materially affect broadcast operations.

__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Oil supplements coal as primary energy source; nuclear power emerges (France especially); energy abundance enables full electrification; 1973 oil shock raises costs but does not interrupt broadcast operations.

__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Electronic waste from obsolete black-and-white receivers begins accumulating as color television diffuses after 1967; volumes remain manageable; no significant environmental feedback.

## D02. Biological Domain

__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Mortality continues declining; infectious disease largely controlled through vaccination and antibiotics; chronic disease becomes primary health concern; life expectancy rises.

__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Affluence delivers abundant nutrition; obesity emerges as concern by 1970s; physical vitality high; television-watching sedentary behavior begins raising health questions.

__Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity:__ Universal healthcare systems mature (NHS, continental social insurance); television health programming expands — documentaries on medicine, campaigns against smoking begin.

__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ Fertility begins declining from baby-boom peaks; contraceptive pill (approved 1960s) transforms reproductive choices; television programming begins reflecting changing family structures.

__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population approaches 400 million; guest worker migration (Turks to Germany, North Africans to France, Southern Europeans northward) creates multicultural audiences not yet reflected in programming.

__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Aging population begins increasing old-age dependency; welfare states expand pension and healthcare provision; broadcasting not yet focused on elderly as distinct audience.

## D03. Economic Domain

__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Affluence universal in Western Europe; consumer society established; television receiver becomes essential household appliance, penetration exceeding 90% by 1975 in most countries.

__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Mixed energy portfolio — coal, oil, hydroelectric, early nuclear; electricity cheap and abundant; per-capita energy consumption doubles between 1960 and 1973; oil shock (1973) inaugurates efficiency concerns.

__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Trade unions at peak strength; broadcast workers strongly organized; ORTF strike (1968) demonstrates union power; labor relations cooperative in northern Europe, more conflictual in Latin countries.

__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Economic golden age — "Thirty Glorious Years" continues; German Wirtschaftswunder, Italian miracle; consumer electronics production expands (Philips, Grundig, Thomson); advertising revenue grows for commercial broadcasters where permitted.

__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Welfare state reserves accumulate; broadcasters' budgets expand with license-fee revenue as receiver penetration rises; some broadcasters accumulate deficits requiring state support.

__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ EEC integration deepens (1967 merger of ECSC, EEC, Euratom); American programming imports increase (Dallas, Bonanza precursors); format trade develops.

__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ Currencies stable until early 1970s; Bretton Woods collapse (1971) and oil shock (1973) introduce inflation; license fees adjusted upward; advertising rates rise.

__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Progressive taxation funds expanded welfare states; license fees rise with inflation; public broadcasting budgets grow substantially.

__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Inequality at historic lows; television reaches across classes equally; receiver ownership nearly universal; some regional/rural lag in coverage persists.

__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Black-and-white receivers discarded as color adoption accelerates after 1967; broadcast production costs rise with color; no significant financial mispricing.

## D04. Social Domain

__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Nuclear family remains normative but begins diversifying; divorce rates rise; television evening viewing remains family ritual but individual viewing emerges.

__Association & Mutual Aid:__ Trade unions, churches, and parties remain strong but membership begins plateauing; pillarization erodes in Netherlands; television provides alternative social participation.

__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Television dominates evening leisure; cinema attendance declines; sports broadcasting (World Cup 1966, 1970, 1974) creates mass shared experiences; Eurovision Song Contest (from 1956) becomes annual pan-European ritual.

__Stratification, Status & Group Structure:__ Class distinctions persist but affluence blurs boundaries; youth emerges as distinct social category with own consumption patterns; television addresses mass audience while beginning to segment.

__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ Public broadcasters mature as institutions — ZDF founded 1963 (Germany's second channel), ORTF reorganized repeatedly, RAI expands to two then three channels. Governing boards reflect political balance (German Proporz, Italian lottizzazione beginnings). Institutional independence contested — ORTF under direct Gaullist control until 1974; RAI influenced by Christian Democrats; BBC and German ARD more autonomous.

__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ NATO security framework stable; Cold War continues; 1968 movements met with police response (Paris, elsewhere); broadcasting covers protests but often with establishment framing.

__Discharge, Disorder & Decay:__ 1968 student movements and worker strikes represent major social disruption; ORTF journalists strike for independence; some radicalization — Red Brigades (Italy), RAF (Germany) emerge by early 1970s; broadcasting becomes contested terrain.

## D05. Emotional Domain

__Leadership & Personal Authority:__ Television creates new political style — de Gaulle masters the medium, dominating ORTF; Adenauer era ends (1963), Brandt's Ostpolitik communicated through television; broadcast journalists (David Frost types emerge) gain authority.

__Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance:__ Democratic legitimacy broadly stable; 1968 challenges establishment but does not overthrow it; Portuguese revolution (1974), Greek junta collapse (1974), Franco's death (1975) end Iberian and Greek authoritarianism, inaugurating democratic transitions where television will play central role.

__Political Will & Collective Resolve:__ European integration advances (1973 UK, Ireland, Denmark accession); collective will to maintain welfare states; 1968 reveals limits of consensus but system absorbs challenge.

__External Relations & Posture:__ Cold War continues; detente emerges (Ostpolitik, Helsinki process); European solidarity strengthens; television covers international affairs extensively — Vietnam War images influential even in Europe.

__Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper:__ Affluent complacency characterizes early 1960s; 1968 movements inject contestation, generational conflict, and critique of authority; oil shock (1973) introduces economic anxiety; television both channels and reflects shifting mood.

__Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will:__ 1968 generation's disaffection partially absorbed, partially channeled into terrorism or alternative lifestyles; broadcasting criticized as establishment tool by left; silent majority remains loyal audience.


## D06. Informational Domain

__Communication Accessibility:__ National languages continue to dominate; minority language broadcasting expands slightly (BBC Wales, some regional German stations); literacy universal; television comprehensible to all.

__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Television achieves dominance — multiple channels in most countries (BBC1/BBC2, ARD/ZDF, RAI 1/2/3, two French channels then three). Color television introduced: BBC2 color 1967, PAL standard adopted continentally 1967, SECAM in France 1967. FM radio expands. Satellite use begins (Telstar 1962) for international transmission; Eurovision network enables routine live pan-European coverage.

__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Videotape recording standard; archives expand; rebroadcasting and international program sales develop; some early material lost due to tape reuse before preservation consciousness.

__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Public broadcasters maintain neutrality norms but political influence varies; ORTF under direct government control until 1974; BBC and German systems more independent; 1968 exposes tensions — ORTF journalists strike for editorial independence; political coverage contested.

__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Television viewing becomes primary information source for majority; evening news bulletins (Tagesschau, Journal de 20 heures, Telegiornale) structure national information consumption; shared simultaneous viewing creates national public.

__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Technical quality high; color adds information density; no significant noise or interference problems; Cold War propaganda directed eastward, not internal.

## D07. Moral Domain

__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Postwar moral consensus begins fracturing; 1968 movements challenge authority, sexual norms, colonialism; television reflects and amplifies moral debates.

__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Broadcasting laws evolve — German Federal Constitutional Court decisions (1961, 1971) protect public broadcasting pluralism; French ORTF reformed repeatedly (1964, 1972, dissolved 1974); Italian Constitutional Court will eventually break RAI monopoly (1976).

__Adjudication, Liability & Sanction:__ Broadcast regulatory bodies mature; political balance in appointments contested; program complaints adjudicated through councils; defamation and obscenity standards applied.

__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Speech freedom formally protected; broadcast monopolies limit access; underground press and pirate radio (Radio Caroline 1964) challenge monopoly; compliance burden for broadcasters modest.

__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ 1968 movement grievances partially addressed; colonial legacies (Algeria, Congo) incompletely reckoned; broadcasting rarely confronts establishment failures directly.

## D08. Intellectual Domain

__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Communication studies mature as academic discipline; Frankfurt School critiques of mass media influential (Adorno, Horkheimer); McLuhan ("the medium is the message") shapes discourse; structuralism and semiotics applied to television.

__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Color television engineering mastered; satellite transmission develops; videotape editing improves; computerization begins affecting broadcast operations by early 1970s.

__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ Mass higher education expands (post-1968 university growth); educational television expands — Open University (UK 1969), Telekolleg (Germany); broadcast training professionalized.

__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Black-and-white techniques superseded; radio skills partially transferred to television; some institutional memory lost in ORTF dissolution (1974).

## D09. Cultural Domain

__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ National broadcasting continues constructing national identity; war memory commemorated; colonial legacies contested (French Algeria); 1968 generation articulates counter-narrative challenging establishment versions.

__Legitimating Narrative & Ideology:__ Public service broadcasting ideology remains dominant but contested; commercial models (American, future European) presented as alternatives; consumerism advances through advertising (where permitted) and American program imports.

__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Television develops mature genres — drama series, variety shows, documentaries; national productions compete with American imports; color changes visual aesthetics; pop culture (music shows, youth programming) expands.

__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ High-culture mandate strains against popular demand; staid public broadcasting criticized as paternalistic; 1968 generation demands relevance; some formats (variety show, radio drama) begin declining.

## D10. Spiritual Domain

__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Secularization accelerates; church attendance declines especially among youth; existentialist and post-Christian worldviews spread; consumer materialism provides practical orientation.

__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Religious broadcasting continues but audience shrinks; Sunday programming less religiously oriented; secular rituals (sports, Eurovision, royal events) gain prominence.

__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Churches lose influence over broadcasting; Christian Democratic parties weaken (except Germany, Italy); Vatican II (1962–1965) represents Catholic adaptation but does not reverse secularization.

__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Eastern spirituality, New Age precursors, and alternative religions attract interest among counterculture; immigrant religions (Islam especially) present but not yet visible in broadcasting.

__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Secularization produces disenchantment among older believers; youth largely indifferent rather than hostile to religion; broadcasting fills some communal-ritual function previously served by churches; existential questions addressed through drama and documentary rather than explicitly religious programming.
