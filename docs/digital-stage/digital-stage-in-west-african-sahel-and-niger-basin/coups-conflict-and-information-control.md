# __2022 CE – 2026 CE__ — Coups, Conflict, and Information Control
__Summary:__ The West African Sahel and Niger Basin experience a cascade of military coups, intensifying jihadist insurgency, and systematic information control across a four-year window marked by French military withdrawal and Russian influence expansion; the binding constraint is the collapse of civilian governance and constitutional order across the Sahel belt, with digital infrastructure weaponized for regime consolidation; the system holds almost no margin against further state fragmentation and external manipulation; the direction is toward authoritarian entrenchment, information closure, and geopolitical realignment away from Western partnerships.
## __P01. Physical Environment__
__Geography & Spatial Friction:__ The Sahel corridor from Mali through Niger to Burkina Faso remains characterized by vast, sparsely governed territory where state reach attenuates rapidly beyond capitals; fiber backbones remain coastal, leaving interior connectivity dependent on satellite and microwave links vulnerable to physical disruption and state control.<br><br>
__Climate Regime & Volatility:__ Rainfall variability intensifies; Lake Chad continues shrinking; drought cycles displace agropastoral populations southward, compounding conflict drivers and straining the resource base of states already at governance limits.<br><br>
__Hazard Regime & Exposure:__ Flooding in the Niger inland delta and Sahel's seasonal watercourses disrupts infrastructure and displaces populations; drought and locust pressure persist; physical hazards compound the conflict-induced humanitarian crisis without themselves being the primary destabilizer.<br><br>
__Resource & Energy Endowment:__ Niger's uranium remains a strategic asset contested between French, Russian, and Chinese interests; Nigeria's oil and gas continue to underwrite regional fiscal capacity; solar potential across the Sahel is largely untapped as instability deters infrastructure investment.<br><br>
__Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity:__ Conflict disrupts waste-management systems in urban centers; e-waste and plastic accumulation in displaced-person camps and peri-urban areas saturates local sinks without formal mitigation; environmental degradation is secondary to survival priorities.<br><br>
## __P02. Biological Environment__
__Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline:__ Endemic malaria, cholera outbreaks in displacement camps, and intermittent Ebola and Lassa fever alerts maintain elevated mortality; conflict zones experience healthcare collapse, raising death rates beyond disease baselines.<br><br>
__Nutrition & Physiological Vitality:__ Acute malnutrition rises in conflict-affected Sahel zones (Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger); WFP and humanitarian access is restricted by insecurity and regime policies; Nigerian and Ghanaian urban populations remain comparatively nourished but face inflation-driven food stress.<br><br>
__Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics:__ High fertility persists; conflict-driven displacement disrupts family formation and increases gender-based violence; early marriage intensifies in insecure areas as a coping mechanism, sustaining demographic pressure.<br><br>
__Demographic Renewal & Density:__ Population growth continues at 2.5–3% annually in Sahelian states; youth-bulge pressures intensify without economic absorption; urban migration accelerates as rural security collapses, straining capital cities' carrying capacity.<br><br>
__Attrition & Dependency Overhang:__ Conflict produces growing orphan, widow, and disabled populations; internally displaced persons exceed five million across the Sahel; dependency burdens outpace social-absorption capacity and humanitarian provision.<br><br>
## __P03. Economic Environment__
__Subsistence & Provisioning Floor:__ Food insecurity reaches crisis levels in conflict zones; markets are disrupted by insecurity and border closures; ECOWAS sanctions on coup states (Mali, Niger) compound economic stress before partial relaxation.<br><br>
__Energy Capture & Conversion:__ Grid electricity remains unreliable; solar-micro-grid projects stall in conflict areas; diesel-generator dependence persists in cities; Nigeria's power crisis continues with less than 5,000 MW available against demand exceeding 20,000 MW.<br><br>
__Labor Organization & Coercion:__ Formal employment contracts in coup states; gig economy persists in Nigeria and Ghana; forced recruitment by armed groups (jihadist and state-aligned militias) constitutes coerced labor in conflict zones.<br><br>
__Production & Surplus Capacity:__ Agricultural output declines in conflict regions; mining (gold in Burkina Faso and Mali, uranium in Niger) continues under shifting foreign partnerships; Nigerian tech sector faces capital outflows and talent emigration amid naira collapse.<br><br>
__Storage, Reserves & Buffering:__ Nigeria's foreign reserves decline below $30 billion under import pressure and capital flight; Sahelian states lack meaningful reserves; household savings erode as inflation accelerates across the region.<br><br>
__Exchange, Trade & Logistics:__ ECOWAS sanctions disrupt Niger's imports via Benin; alternative routes through Togo and Libya expand; illicit gold and contraband flows bypass formal channels; mobile-money transactions provide exchange continuity where banks fail.<br><br>
__Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange:__ The naira collapses to over 1,500/$ on parallel markets by 2024; CFA franc stability is contested as Sahelian states discuss currency exit; cryptocurrency use persists despite restrictions, serving as remittance and store-of-value alternative.<br><br>
__Surplus Extraction & Redistribution:__ Military juntas redirect resource rents (gold, uranium) toward regime consolidation and Russian partnership payments; Nigerian federal revenues decline with oil production drops; redistribution to populations contracts.<br><br>
__Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense:__ Elite capital flight intensifies; real-estate investment in Dubai and London accelerates; middle-class asset erosion through inflation widens inequality; defense of holdings shifts to hard assets and offshore accounts.<br><br>
__Byproducts & Waste Routing:__ Artisanal gold mining expands with mercury pollution unmitigated; conflict debris accumulates; e-waste management collapses in unstable areas; waste is externalized without remediation.<br><br>
## __P04. Social Environment__
__Social Trust & Mutual Aid:__ Trust in state institutions collapses in coup states; ethnic and community networks intensify as survival mechanisms; inter-communal tensions rise as resource competition sharpens under climate and conflict stress.<br><br>
__Stratification, Status & Group Identity:__ Military elites ascend; civilian professional classes emigrate or lose status; ethnic identities (Tuareg, Fulani, Mossi, Bambara) harden as conflict polarizes; youth identity increasingly defined against both Western and domestic elites.<br><br>
__Kinship, Family & Inheritance:__ Displacement disrupts extended-family structures; remittances from diaspora become lifelines; inheritance disputes intensify as death rates rise and property is abandoned or seized.<br><br>
__Civic Association & Self-Organization:__ NGOs face restrictions in coup states; grassroots organizing shifts to encrypted platforms (Signal, Telegram); youth movements persist but operate under surveillance; associational life contracts under authoritarian pressure.<br><br>
__Leisure, Play & Sociability:__ Football and social media remain sociability anchors; cultural events contract under curfews and insecurity; digital sociability persists but is shadowed by surveillance awareness and shutdown risk.<br><br>
__Grievance Channels & Social Discharge:__ Protest is suppressed in coup states; social-media criticism faces prosecution; grievances accumulate without legitimate discharge, building latent instability; diaspora amplification provides external voice but no domestic relief.<br><br>
## __P05. Political Environment__
__Power Structure & Authority:__ Military juntas consolidate in Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, and Guinea; civilian governance survives in Nigeria, Ghana, and Senegal with varying stability; power concentrates in security establishments with constitutional order suspended or constrained.<br><br>
__Succession & Power Transfer:__ Coups replace electoral succession; promised transition timelines extend repeatedly; Nigeria's 2023 election (Tinubu victory) proceeds amid controversy; Senegal's 2024 election installs Faye after detention crisis; transfer mechanisms remain unstable across the region.<br><br>
__Legitimacy & Political Narrative:__ Juntas invoke anti-French sovereignty and security restoration; pan-African and anti-colonial rhetoric legitimates military rule; civilian governments struggle to counter narrative with democratic-governance appeals.<br><br>
__Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity:__ State capacity contracts in coup states as sanctions bite and civil servants emigrate; corruption persists; digital systems (ID, taxation) stagnate or regress; Nigerian government capacity remains low but functional.<br><br>
__Political Participation & Mobilization:__ Opposition suppressed in coup states; social-media activism criminalized; Nigerian and Senegalese civil society remains active but under pressure; youth political energy is channeled toward emigration rather than domestic participation.<br><br>
__Geopolitical Position & External Relations:__ France withdraws military forces; Russia (via Wagner/Africa Corps) expands; China maintains infrastructure investments; ECOWAS fractures over coup responses; regional integration stalls; great-power competition intensifies.<br><br>
__Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force:__ Juntas rely on presidential guards and Russian-backed units; jihadist groups (JNIM, ISWAP) contest territory; vigilante and ethnic militias proliferate; state monopoly on violence fragments across the Sahel.<br><br>
__Internal Order & Threat Environment:__ Jihadist expansion reaches coastal states (Benin, Togo, Ghana's northern border); mass atrocities documented in Burkina Faso and Mali; kidnapping and banditry surge in Nigeria; disorder exceeds coercive absorption capacity throughout the region.<br><br>
## __P06. Informational Environment__
__Channels & Transmission Velocity:__ Mobile networks remain operational but under state control; internet shutdowns accompany coups and protests (Niger 2023, Senegal 2024); Starlink emerges as bypass option for elites and armed groups; velocity is high when channels are open, zero when severed.<br><br>
__Record, Storage & Persistence:__ Social-media documentation of atrocities and protests provides volatile record; cloud storage remains offshore and accessible to those with VPNs; local archives are vulnerable to regime seizure; institutional memory degrades under turnover.<br><br>
__Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms:__ Juntas expel foreign journalists (RFI, France 24 banned in Mali, Burkina, Niger); state media dominates; Russian information operations (RT Afrique, Telegram channels) amplify anti-Western and pro-junta narratives; self-censorship intensifies among domestic journalists.<br><br>
__Reception, Attention & Amplification:__ Audiences fragment between state media consumers and those accessing information via VPN and diaspora sources; TikTok and Telegram grow as platforms; algorithmic amplification favors polarizing and sovereignty-coded content.<br><br>
__Signal Integrity & Noise Load:__ Disinformation campaigns intensify around coups, elections, and atrocity events; Russian-affiliated networks produce coordinated inauthentic behavior; fact-checking capacity is overwhelmed; noise load exceeds verification resources.<br><br>
## __P07. Judicial Environment__
__Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice:__ Popular desire for security and order legitimates strongman rule in some constituencies; human-rights norms retain support among civil society but lose practical force; justice expectations shift toward immediate protection over procedural rights.<br><br>
__Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution:__ Constitutional order suspended by decree in coup states; emergency laws expand executive power; Nigeria's cybersecurity regulations tighten; legal frameworks evolve toward surveillance and control rather than rights protection.<br><br>
__Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability:__ Courts are subordinated to military authority in junta states; ICC and ECOWAS Court jurisdictions are rejected; Nigerian courts function but enforcement remains weak; liability for atrocities is deferred indefinitely.<br><br>
__Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden:__ Detention without trial expands; press freedom contracts; digital-rights protections are nominal; compliance burden shifts to citizens proving loyalty rather than states justifying restrictions.<br><br>
__Impunity & Unredressed Wrong:__ Massacres in Moura (Mali, 2022) and multiple Burkina Faso villages go unprosecuted; security-force impunity is near-total in coup states; the impunity stock grows without any credible mechanism for future redress.<br><br>
## __P08. Intellectual Environment__
__Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm:__ Critical security studies and political-economy analysis of Sahel crisis intensify in academic and think-tank circles; pan-African sovereignty discourse gains intellectual traction; techno-optimism recedes as digital tools are weaponized.<br><br>
__Applied Knowledge & Technical Method:__ Surveillance and shutdown technologies are imported and deployed; drone and counter-IED techniques are transferred via Russian and Turkish partnerships; technical method serves state coercion more than civilian development.<br><br>
__Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution:__ University closures and insecurity disrupt education in conflict zones; brain drain accelerates; digital skills concentrate among those who emigrate; knowledge stock contracts in affected populations.<br><br>
__Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay:__ Institutional knowledge of democratic governance atrophies under military rule; civil-service expertise degrades through purges and emigration; local conflict-resolution traditions are lost as communities disperse.<br><br>
## __P09. Cultural Environment__
__Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity:__ Anti-colonial memory is mobilized to legitimate rupture with France; pan-African solidarity rhetoric anchors junta legitimacy; national identities fragment along ethnic and regional lines under stress; #EndSARS memory persists in Nigeria as generational marker.<br><br>
__Artistic Expression & Material Culture:__ Afrobeats continues global expansion; Sahel cultural production contracts under insecurity; diaspora artists dominate; material culture reflects austerity and improvisation rather than investment.<br><br>
__Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition:__ Fatalism and resignation spread in conflict zones; survivalism dominates; Nigerian urban mood oscillates between cynicism and adaptation; emigration ("japa") becomes aspirational norm for youth.<br><br>
__Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay:__ Revolutionary and sovereignty rhetoric risks emptying through repetition without delivery; hashtag activism loses mobilizing force; cultural forms recycle without renewal; anomie spreads where meaning frameworks collapse.<br><br>
## __P10. Spiritual Environment__
__Cosmology & Existential Outlook:__ Eschatological and apocalyptic themes resonate as crisis deepens; jihadist cosmology frames conflict as sacred struggle; Pentecostal prosperity theology adapts to hardship narratives; secular outlooks retreat under existential pressure.<br><br>
__Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice:__ Mosque and church attendance persists as community anchors; traditional protective rituals revive among displaced populations; digital worship continues but competes with in-person spiritual community as survival networks.<br><br>
__Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority:__ Islamic brotherhoods retain influence in Senegal; Pentecostal networks expand despite crisis; jihadist doctrinal authority competes for allegiance in occupied territories; institutional religion fragments rather than consolidates.<br><br>
__Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement:__ Salafi-jihadist expansion displaces syncretic and Sufi practice in controlled zones; evangelical growth continues in non-conflict areas; traditional religion persists in fragments; the sacred landscape is contested and unstable.<br><br>
__Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge:__ Some survivors report spiritual exhaustion; trauma accumulates without ritual processing; disenchantment emerges among those who expected divine protection; sacred frameworks strain under the weight of unmetabolized suffering.<br><br>
