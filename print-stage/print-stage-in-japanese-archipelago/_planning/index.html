    Planning Document — Print Stage in The Japanese Archipelago (1650 CE – 1935 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1650 CE – 1935 CE

    Region: The Japanese Archipelago — the island chain off East Asia from Hokkaido to Kyushu; Tokugawa shogunate through Meiji, Taisho, and early Showa periods; isolated until 1853, then rapidly modernizing.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes bakufu archives, commercial publishing records, newspapers, government gazettes, and political tracts. Woodblock printing mature by 1600; commercial publishing expands Genroku era; Western typography after 1850s; newspaper press from 1870s; telegraph 1869; radio begins 1925. Window begins with mature Tokugawa print culture; ends as military censorship intensifies and broadcast expands.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Tokugawa Peace and Genroku Culture (1650–1750 CE)

    Tokugawa Stability: Bakufu control; sankin-kotai; peace; urbanization.

    Commercial Publishing: Osaka, Kyoto, Edo centers; woodblock printing.

    Ukiyo-e: Floating world prints; Hishikawa Moronobu; popular culture.

    Kabuki, Bunraku: Theater texts; Chikamatsu; commercial entertainment.

    Literacy Expansion: Terakoya schools; urban literacy; reading public.

    Seclusion Policy: Limited Dutch contact; Nagasaki trade; controlled information.

    2. Late Tokugawa (1750–1830 CE)

    Commercial Expansion: Publishing guilds; lending libraries; mass market.

    Rangaku: Dutch learning; Western science; translation efforts.

    Kokugaku: National learning; Shinto revival; Motoori Norinaga.

    Censorship Tightening: Kansei reforms 1787–1793; political criticism suppressed.

    Economic Strains: Famines; domain debts; merchant wealth; samurai impoverishment.

    Regional Publishing: Osaka commercial; Kyoto cultural; provincial expansion.

    3. Bakumatsu Crisis (1830–1868 CE)

    Perry Arrival: 1853–1854; forced opening; unequal treaties.

    Political Crisis: Bakufu weakness; domain rivalries; sonnō jōi movement.

    Western Knowledge: Translation bureaus; military texts; printing technology.

    Political Pamphlets: Broadsides; current events; public opinion emerges.

    Boshin War: 1868–1869; Meiji victory; shogunate collapse.

    Reform Domains: Satsuma, Choshu; modernizing press; printing imports.

    4. Meiji Restoration (1868–1890 CE)

    Meiji Government: Emperor restored; centralization; modernization.

    Typography Adoption: Western movable type; newspapers; rapid diffusion.

    First Newspapers: Yokohama Mainichi 1871; Yomiuri 1874; Asahi 1879.

    Freedom and People's Rights: Liberal movement; political press; Itagaki.

    Press Laws: 1875 Newspaper Ordinance; censorship; government control.

    Meiji Constitution: 1889; limited press freedom; imperial sovereignty.

    5. Constitutional Japan (1890–1912 CE)

    Diet Politics: Parliamentary system; party development; political press.

    Sino-Japanese War: 1894–1895; Taiwan; press nationalism.

    Russo-Japanese War: 1904–1905; great power status; coverage.

    Korea Annexation: 1910; colonial press; suppression of Korean papers.

    Press Consolidation: Large papers dominate; regional papers persist.

    Meiji Journalism: Serious political press; intellectual journalism.

    6. Taisho Democracy (1912–1926 CE)

    Party Politics: Hara cabinet 1918; democratic hopes; political engagement.

    Labor Movements: Unions; socialist press; Rice Riots 1918.

    Mass Circulation: Million-copy papers; Asahi, Mainichi dominance.

    Magazine Culture: General magazines; literary magazines; women's magazines.

    Universal Male Suffrage: 1925; expanded electorate; mass politics.

    Peace Preservation Law: 1925; targeting leftists; suppression begins.

    7. Early Showa (1926–1935 CE)

    Showa Depression: 1929 crash; rural crisis; social tensions.

    Manchurian Incident: 1931; military expansion; nationalist press.

    Press Control: Tightening censorship; self-censorship; nationalist conformity.

    Leftist Suppression: Communist arrests; socialist press crushed.

    NHK Radio: 1926 established; broadcast competition with print.

    Military Influence: Rising power; press intimidation; February 26 Incident approach.

    8. Woodblock to Typography (1650–1935 CE)

    Woodblock Dominance: Pre-1850; commercial publishing; artistic tradition.

    Early Typography: Mission presses; Dutch contacts; limited adoption.

    Meiji Revolution: Western type; rapid transition; newspapers.

    Printing Technology: Steam presses; linotype; mass production.

    Illustrated Press: Photographs; halftones; visual journalism.

    Coexistence: Art prints continue; typography dominates news.

    9. Censorship and Press Control (1650–1935 CE)

    Tokugawa Controls: Publishing guilds; political restrictions; Christianity banned.

    Meiji Press Laws: 1875, 1909 laws; prepublication censorship; fines.

    Political Suppression: Radical papers; socialist press; Korean press.

    Wartime Censorship: Sino-Japanese, Russo-Japanese wars; patriotic framing.

    1930s Intensification: Peace Preservation Law; military pressure; conformity.

    Self-Censorship: Publisher caution; avoiding prosecution; internalized control.

    10. Regional Publishing (1650–1935 CE)

    Three Cities: Kyoto (cultural), Osaka (commercial), Edo (political).

    Provincial Publishing: Castle towns; domain presses; local markets.

    Meiji Centralization: Tokyo dominance; regional papers persist.

    Dialect Use: Limited in print; standard Japanese dominates.

    Regional Papers: Prefectural papers; local news; political mobilization.

    Colonial Press: Taiwan, Korea; Japanese-language papers; suppression of local press.
