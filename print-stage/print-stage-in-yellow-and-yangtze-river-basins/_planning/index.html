    Planning Document — Print Stage in The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins (1900 CE – 1950 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1900 CE – 1950 CE

    Region: The Yellow and Yangtze River Basins — the great river systems of China; including the North China Plain, Yangtze valley, and southern coastal regions; late Qing empire, Republic, and early People's Republic.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes Qing archives, treaty port newspapers, revolutionary publications, Nationalist and Communist party press, and government documents. Woodblock printing tradition; Western typography introduced treaty ports; vernacular press movement; party newspapers; telegraph from 1880s; radio from 1920s. Window begins with Boxer aftermath; ends as Communist victory transforms press system.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Qing Crisis (1900–1911 CE)

    Boxer Aftermath: 1900 siege; indemnity; reform pressure.

    New Policies: Education reform; constitutional preparation; modernization.

    Revolutionary Press: Overseas Chinese papers; Sun Yat-sen; exile publications.

    Constitutional Movement: Provincial assemblies; reform demands.

    Railway Crisis: 1911; provincial opposition; reform failure.

    Wuchang Uprising: October 1911; Qing collapse; Republic proclaimed.

    2. Early Republic (1911–1919 CE)

    Yuan Shikai: Presidency; dictatorship attempt; death 1916.

    Warlord Era: Regional militarists; political fragmentation.

    Treaty Port Press: Shanghai center; foreign concessions; relative freedom.

    New Culture Movement: Peking University; Chen Duxiu; Hu Shi.

    New Youth Magazine: 1915; vernacular advocacy; cultural critique.

    May Fourth Incident: 1919; student protests; nationalist awakening.

    3. May Fourth Era (1919–1927 CE)

    Vernacular Revolution: Baihua replaces wenyan; literary transformation.

    Communist Party: 1921 founded; labor organizing; revolutionary press.

    GMD Reorganization: Soviet advisors; party press; propaganda.

    First United Front: CCP-GMD alliance; mass mobilization.

    Northern Expedition: 1926–1928; military unification campaign.

    Shanghai Massacre: 1927; GMD-CCP split; Communist suppression.

    4. Nanjing Decade (1927–1937 CE)

    Nationalist Government: Chiang Kai-shek; party state; modernization.

    Press Control: Censorship; party papers; suppression of opposition.

    Rural Reconstruction: Mass education movements; village campaigns.

    Japanese Aggression: Manchuria 1931; Shanghai 1932; encroachment.

    Commercial Press: Major publisher; educational texts; reference works.

    Urban Culture: Shanghai modernity; magazines; popular press.

    5. War of Resistance (1937–1945 CE)

    Full-Scale War: July 1937; Japanese invasion; massive displacement.

    Chongqing: Wartime capital; government press; censorship.

    Yan'an: Communist base; rectification; party press model.

    United Front: Second GMD-CCP cooperation; tensions persist.

    Japanese Occupation: Puppet press; collaboration; resistance networks.

    War's End: 1945; Japanese surrender; civil war looms.

    6. Civil War (1945–1950 CE)

    Postwar Collapse: Inflation; corruption; Nationalist legitimacy crisis.

    Communist Advance: Land reform; military victories; cities fall.

    Press Transformation: Nationalist papers collapse; Communist takeover.

    PRC Founded: October 1949; new press system; party control.

    Nationalists to Taiwan: Retreat; Republic continues offshore.

    Revolutionary Press Model: People's Daily; state-party monopoly.

    7. Vernacular Movement (1900–1950 CE)

    Classical Chinese: Wenyan dominant; elite literacy.

    Vernacular Advocacy: Hu Shi; Chen Duxiu; linguistic democratization.

    Literary Revolution: New fiction; Lu Xun; Lao She; Ba Jin.

    Mass Literacy: Vernacular enables broader readership.

    Political Implications: Language reform as modernization.

    Communist Continuation: Simplified characters; literacy campaigns.

    8. Shanghai Commercial Press (1900–1950 CE)

    Commercial Press Founded: 1897; major publisher; textbooks.

    Zhonghua Book Company: 1912; competitor; reference works.

    Treaty Port Advantages: Foreign concession protection; modern facilities.

    Educational Market: Textbooks; dictionaries; translations.

    1932 Bombing: Japanese attack; reconstruction; resilience.

    Communist Takeover: 1949; nationalization; continued operation.
