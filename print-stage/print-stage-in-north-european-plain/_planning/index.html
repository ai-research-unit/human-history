    Planning Document — Print Stage in The North European Plain (1500 CE – 1930 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1500 CE – 1930 CE

    Region: The North European Plain — the lowland corridor from the Netherlands through Germany to Poland; including the Holy Roman Empire, German states, Austrian Empire, Dutch Republic, and their successor states.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes imperial archives, state papers, newspapers, pamphlets, journals, and government publications. Gutenberg's invention spreads from 1450s; Reformation print explosion; learned journals 17th century; Enlightenment periodicals; censorship regimes; mass press 19th century; telegraph from 1840s; radio begins 1920s. Window begins with mature Reformation print; ends as Weimar democracy struggles and broadcast expands.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Reformation (1500–1555 CE)

    Luther: 95 Theses 1517; printed pamphlets; vernacular Bible.

    Print Explosion: Wittenberg; pamphlet warfare; woodcut propaganda.

    Catholic Response: Eck; papal bulls; censorship attempts.

    Peasants' War: 1525; printed demands; suppression; religious politics.

    Charles V: Emperor; Augsburg 1530; religious division.

    Peace of Augsburg: 1555; cuius regio, eius religio; territorial settlement.

    2. Confessional Age (1555–1648 CE)

    Counter-Reformation: Jesuits; Catholic revival; confessional boundaries.

    Religious Tensions: Calvinist spread; imperial conflicts.

    Thirty Years War: 1618–1648; devastation; population loss.

    Wartime Print: Propaganda; illustrated broadsides; news sheets.

    Dutch Revolt: Independence struggle; Reformed printing.

    Westphalia Approach: War exhaustion; peace negotiations.

    3. Westphalian Order (1648–1740 CE)

    Peace of Westphalia: 1648; sovereignty principle; religious settlement.

    Territorial States: Absolutist courts; bureaucracies; state papers.

    Early Periodicals: Learned journals; commercial gazettes.

    Dutch Republic: Golden Age; publishing center; tolerant.

    Brandenburg-Prussia: Rising power; efficient administration.

    Austrian Habsburgs: Empire; Catholic identity; Vienna center.

    4. Enlightenment (1740–1789 CE)

    Frederick II: Prussia; enlightened despotism; cultural patronage.

    Learned Journals: Scholarly publishing; university presses.

    Reform Debates: Cameralism; education; administration.

    Joseph II: Austrian reforms; toleration; resistance.

    Reading Revolution: Expanding literacy; lending libraries.

    Kant: Critique of Pure Reason; Enlightenment philosophy.

    5. Revolutionary Era (1789–1815 CE)

    French Revolution: 1789; ideas spread; conservative reaction.

    Revolutionary Wars: French invasion; old regime collapse.

    Confederation of Rhine: 1806; Napoleonic reorganization.

    Holy Roman Empire: Dissolved 1806; end of medieval order.

    German Nationalism: Fichte; romantic movement; anti-French.

    Liberation Wars: 1813–1815; Waterloo; Congress of Vienna.

    6. Restoration and Vormarz (1815–1848 CE)

    German Confederation: 1815; Austrian presidency; loose structure.

    Metternich System: Conservative order; censorship; Carlsbad Decrees 1819.

    Liberal Opposition: Student movements; underground press; exile publishing.

    Economic Integration: Zollverein 1834; industrialization begins.

    Press Restrictions: Prior censorship; police surveillance.

    Revolutionary Ferment: 1840s tensions; economic crisis; liberal demands.

    7. 1848 Revolutions (1848–1850 CE)

    March Revolutions: Berlin, Vienna; constitutions demanded.

    Frankfurt Parliament: National assembly; constitutional debates.

    Press Explosion: Censorship lifted; newspapers proliferate.

    Conservative Reaction: Military suppression; constitution rejection.

    Austrian Counterrevolution: Schwarzenberg; centralization.

    Prussian Constitution: 1850; conservative framework; limited liberalism.

    8. Reaction and Unification (1850–1871 CE)

    Prussian Leadership: Bismarck from 1862; Realpolitik.

    War with Denmark: 1864; Schleswig-Holstein.

    Austro-Prussian War: 1866; Königgrätz; North German Confederation.

    Franco-Prussian War: 1870–1871; Sedan; German unification.

    Press and Nationalism: Nationalist papers; war coverage.

    Reich Proclamation: 1871 Versailles; Prussian-led empire.

    9. German Empire (1871–1890 CE)

    Bismarck System: Conservative governance; manipulation.

    Kulturkampf: Anti-Catholic campaign; Center Party; Catholic press.

    Anti-Socialist Laws: 1878–1890; socialist press suppressed.

    Industrial Expansion: Rapid growth; urbanization.

    Colonial Ventures: Africa; Pacific; imperial press.

    Press Diversity: Conservative, liberal, Catholic, socialist papers.

    10. Wilhelmine Germany (1890–1914 CE)

    Wilhelm II: Personal rule; naval ambitions; world policy.

    Mass Press: BZ am Mittag; Berliner Morgenpost; tabloids.

    Socialist Press: Vorwärts; SPD growth; mass readership.

    Naval Race: Britain rivalry; press nationalism.

    Cultural Confidence: Science; universities; scholarly journals.

    July Crisis: 1914; war enthusiasm; mobilization.

    11. Great War (1914–1918 CE)

    World War I: Total war; mass casualties; home front.

    War Propaganda: Official news; enemy atrocities; morale.

    Censorship: Military control; restricted reporting.

    War Weariness: 1917–1918; strikes; discontent.

    Collapse: November 1918; abdication; armistice.

    Revolution: Soviets; republic proclaimed; chaos.

    12. Weimar Republic (1918–1930 CE)

    Weimar Constitution: 1919; democratic framework; press freedom.

    Versailles Treaty: Reparations; territorial losses; resentment.

    Hyperinflation: 1923 crisis; economic devastation.

    Stabilization: 1924–1929; Stresemann; relative prosperity.

    Press Polarization: Hugenberg press; communist papers; fragmentation.

    Radio Begins: 1923 broadcasting; new competition.

    13. Dutch Republic and Press Freedom (1500–1930 CE)

    Revolt and Independence: 1568–1648; Reformed; tolerance.

    Publishing Center: Amsterdam; exile printing; free press.

    Learned Publishing: Elsevier; scientific books; journals.

    European Influence: Dutch papers read across continent.

    19th Century: Kingdom; colonial press; continued freedom.

    Modern Netherlands: Democratic press; maintained traditions.

    14. Polish Partitions and National Press (1500–1930 CE)

    Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth: Early printing; decline.

    Partitions: 1772, 1793, 1795; Poland erased from map.

    Exile Press: Paris; London; nationalist publications.

    Underground Printing: Russian, Prussian, Austrian censorship resistance.

    1918 Independence: Reborn Poland; national press.

    Interwar Poland: Democratic then authoritarian; press conflict.

    15. German Censorship (1500–1930 CE)

    Reformation Attempts: Imperial bans; ineffective.

    Territorial Censorship: State-by-state control; variation.

    Carlsbad Decrees: 1819; press suppression; Metternich.

    Bismarck Era: Press laws; socialist suppression.

    Wilhelm II: Continued controls; lèse-majesté.

    Weimar Freedom: Constitutional guarantee; democratic press.

    16. Scientific and Learned Press (1500–1930 CE)

    University Presses: Lutheran universities; scholarly printing.

    Academies: Berlin; Göttingen; scientific journals.

    Zeitschriften: Specialized periodicals; disciplinary formation.

    German Scholarship: 19th century dominance; research universities.

    Scientific Communication: International networks; German hub.

    Popular Science: Naturwissenschaftliche magazines; public education.
