    1700 CE – 1808 CE — Bourbon Reforms and Enlightened Press

    Summary: The Bourbon dynasty consolidates centralized rule over Spain following the War of Succession, replacing Habsburg composite monarchy with Castilian-model absolutism. Enlightened ministers under Charles III introduce limited reforms, economic societies, and a controlled public sphere through official gazettes, while the Inquisition weakens but persists. The system's margin narrows as colonial tensions mount and Enlightenment ideas circulate among ilustrados despite censorship, trending toward the revolutionary rupture of 1808.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Iberian meseta, Pyrenean barrier, and Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts define a peripheral European position; internal mountain chains fragment overland movement, while coastal ports connect to colonial trade routes and European markets.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Mediterranean and semi-arid continental regimes prevail, with periodic droughts in Castile and Andalusia; the Little Ice Age eases slightly through the century, reducing but not eliminating harvest variability.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: The 1755 Lisbon earthquake devastates Portugal and affects western Spain; periodic flooding along major rivers and drought cycles stress agrarian regions, with infrastructure margin limited by fiscal constraints.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Colonial silver continues flowing though at reduced rates from the Habsburg peak; domestic iron, wool, and agricultural products remain underexploited relative to northern European competitors; coal deposits largely untapped.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban sanitation in Madrid and provincial capitals remains rudimentary; agrarian waste disperses across extensive landscapes with minimal cumulative pressure; absorptive capacity ample for pre-industrial loads.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Endemic typhus, smallpox, and periodic epidemic outbreaks maintain elevated mortality, though the catastrophic plague cycles of the 17th century do not recur; urban death rates exceed rural ones.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Grain-based diets leave much of the population near subsistence; periodic harvest failures produce regional famines; stature data suggest modest improvement over Habsburg-era lows in the later decades.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Church-regulated marriage remains universal; age at first marriage rises slightly in urban areas; fertility constrained by nutritional stress and high infant mortality.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population grows from roughly 7.5 million in 1700 to over 10 million by 1800, recovering Habsburg-era losses; density remains low outside Madrid, Barcelona, and coastal cities.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: High infant mortality and recurrent subsistence crises leave widows and orphans dependent on Church charity and family networks; the productive cohort absorbs the load without systemic strain.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Grain production in Castile and Andalusia supports baseline survival; periodic harvest failures trigger localized dearth; household pooling through extended family and village networks buffers marginal populations.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle power dominates; watermill and windmill use expands modestly; charcoal-fired forges serve artisanal production; per-capita energy ceiling remains low by northwestern European standards.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Seigneurial obligations persist in many regions; wage labor grows in cities and textile workshops; guild structures regulate urban crafts; Bourbon reforms attempt to liberalize internal trade but face resistance.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Agricultural output rises with population; Catalan textile manufactures expand; royal manufactories (tapestries, porcelain) produce for elite consumption; overall surplus generation modest relative to France or Britain.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Crown treasuries depend on colonial remittances; positos (municipal granaries) buffer grain supply; reserves thin during war years (Succession, Seven Years War, Revolutionary Wars).

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Internal customs barriers reduced by Nueva Planta; colonial trade liberalized 1778 (comercio libre); road-building programs under Charles III improve but do not eliminate transit friction; port cities flourish.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Silver peso remains unit of account; Banco de San Carlos founded 1782 to manage royal debt; private credit networks operate through merchant houses; inflationary pressures from colonial silver manageable.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Crown taxation reformed but remains regressive; Church tithes and seigneurial dues extract from peasantry; American remittances fund military and court; fiscal margin tightens during late-century wars.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Land concentrated in nobility, Church, and entailed estates (mayorazgos); limited upward mobility for commercial classes; asset defense through entail and ecclesiastical immunity.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Artisanal and agricultural waste dispersed locally; urban refuse accumulates in streets and rivers; no systemic overload on physical sinks.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Village solidarities, confraternities, and parish networks provide informal safety nets; trust radius extends through kin and locality; Crown reforms do not disrupt traditional reciprocity structures.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Rigid hierarchy persists: grandes, hidalgos, urban bourgeoisie, peasantry; limpieza de sangre (blood purity) norms weaken but endure; regional identities (Catalan, Basque, Galician) persist beneath Castilian overlay.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Partible inheritance in Castile, primogeniture among nobility; extended family households buffer economic shocks; Church-regulated marriage patterns stable.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Economic Societies of Friends of the Country (Sociedades Economicas) spread Enlightenment reform ideas among provincial elites; guilds persist but face Bourbon liberalization; associational life thickens modestly.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Bullfights, religious festivals, and theater (popular and court) structure public leisure; tertulias (salons) provide elite intellectual sociability; coffeehouse culture limited compared to northern Europe.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Petitioning, religious processions, and local patron-client networks absorb grievances; bread riots occur during scarcity but remain localized; no systemic discharge failure.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Bourbon absolutism centralizes power in Madrid; Nueva Planta decrees (1707-1716) abolish Aragonese fueros; intendants replace corregidores; Council of Castile dominates administration; centralizing trend strong.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Bourbon succession stabilizes after War of Succession; Philip V, Ferdinand VI, Charles III, Charles IV transfer power without civil conflict; legitimacy unchallenged until 1808 crisis.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Divine-right monarchy reinforced by Church alliance; Bourbon claim legitimated by Utrecht settlement; Enlightened reform rhetoric under Charles III frames monarchy as agent of progress.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Intendant system improves provincial governance; fiscal administration reformed; corruption persists but within manageable bounds; information-gathering capacity (census of 1787) advances.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: No representative institutions beyond vestigial Cortes; ilustrados advise through councils and societies; popular mobilization absent except localized protest; channels closed.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Family Compact with France defines alliance system; wars against Britain (1739-48, 1761-63, 1779-83) strain treasury; American colonies increasingly restive; European standing secondary to France and Britain.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Standing army expanded and professionalized under Bourbons; navy rebuilt for colonial defense; provincial militias supplement regulars; command concentrated in Crown.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Banditry endemic in Andalusia and Catalonia; Inquisition handles religious deviance; Esquilache Riots (1766) exceptional urban disorder, quickly suppressed; margin adequate.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Printed gazettes (Gaceta de Madrid) distribute official news; postal service connects provincial capitals; information velocity measured in days across Iberia, weeks to colonies; elite reach only.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Royal archives, ecclesiastical registers, notarial records preserve administrative memory; printing expands book production; libraries remain elite institutions; persistence adequate for bureaucratic needs.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Inquisition and Crown jointly censor; Index of Forbidden Books enforced; Enlightenment texts circulate among ilustrados through smuggling and licensed editions; permitted band narrow but widening under Charles III.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Literacy remains below 20%; reading publics concentrated in cities and among clergy; amplification through pulpit, tertulias, and manuscript circulation; no mass audience.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Censorship reduces heterodox noise but also constrains intellectual exchange; competing claims minimal in controlled press; verification capacity adequate for limited information volume.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Catholic natural law frames justice; honor codes persist among elites; communal sense of fair dealing governs village relations; reform sentiment among ilustrados challenges traditional hierarchies.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Multiple legal traditions (Castilian, Aragonese) unified under Bourbon centralization; royal decrees proliferate; codification efforts incomplete; Enlightened penal reform debated but limited.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Royal audiencias and Chancillerias process civil and criminal cases; Inquisition handles religious offenses; seigneurial courts persist locally; throughput slow but functional.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual rights subordinate to corporate privileges and royal prerogative; compliance burden moderate for elites, heavy for peasantry subject to multiple jurisdictions; predictability low in overlapping systems.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Noble and ecclesiastical privilege shields offenders; Inquisition overreach leaves some wrongs unredressed; backlog manageable; impunity concentrated at elite level.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Ilustrados absorb Enlightenment natural philosophy and political economy; Feijoo, Jovellanos, Campomanes apply empirical methods to Spanish problems; paradigm shift partial, constrained by censorship.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Royal manufactories import French and Italian techniques; mining schools (Almaden) train engineers; agricultural improvement schemes disseminate through Economic Societies; application uneven.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Universities stagnant under scholastic curricula; Jesuit expulsion (1767) disrupts secondary education; literacy concentrated among clergy and urban elites; skill distribution narrow.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Scholastic theology and Aristotelian physics persist alongside Enlightenment imports; outdated methods not actively cleared; knowledge stock grows but unevenly distributed; decay from transmission gaps among popular strata.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Catholic Reconquista mythology, Habsburg imperial memory, and Bourbon reformism layer into Spanish identity; regional identities (Catalan, Basque) persist; unity of faith and crown defines collective self-understanding.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Late Baroque gives way to Neoclassicism; royal academies (San Fernando) standardize taste; Goya emerges late in period capturing popular and court life; architecture shifts to Bourbon classicism.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Optimism among reforming elites under Charles III; fatalism and traditionalism among rural populations; late-century war exhaustion and revolutionary anxiety darken the mood under Charles IV.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Baroque religious forms lose vitality among educated classes; Enlightenment rationalism not yet mass phenomenon; traditional symbolism persists in popular devotion; minimal form-exhaustion crisis.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Catholic providential worldview remains hegemonic; Enlightenment deism circulates among ilustrados; fatalism and piety coexist; secularism minimal outside elite circles.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mass attendance, confession, feast-day observance structure daily and annual life; Marian devotions, pilgrimages, and saints' cults flourish; religious orders active despite Jesuit expulsion.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Church retains vast landholdings, tithe income, and educational role; episcopate allied with Crown (regalism); Inquisition weakened but not abolished; doctrinal authority consolidated but contested by regalistas.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Catholicism unchallenged domestically; colonial missions continue in Americas and Philippines; no significant Protestant or heterodox presence; diffusion stable.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Enlightenment rationalism produces limited elite disenchantment; popular piety remains vibrant; no mass secularization; sacred framework intact with narrow cracks among ilustrados.
