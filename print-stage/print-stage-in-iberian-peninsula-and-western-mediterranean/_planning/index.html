    Planning Document — Print Stage in The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean (1500 CE – 1930 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1500 CE – 1930 CE

    Region: The Iberian Peninsula and Western Mediterranean — Spain and Portugal, including their Atlantic and Mediterranean coasts; the core zones of early modern global empires and their 19th–20th century transformations.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes Inquisition archives, royal decrees, colonial correspondence, newspapers, parliamentary proceedings, and political pamphlets. Printing arrives late 15th century; Counter-Reformation censorship; Index of Forbidden Books; Bourbon press reforms; liberal press emerges 19th century; telegraph from 1850s; radio begins 1920s. Window begins with mature Habsburg print; ends as Second Republic and broadcast transform communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Habsburg Zenith (1500–1598 CE)

    Charles V: Emperor; global empire; Reformation challenges; abdication 1556.

    Philip II: 1556–1598; Counter-Reformation; Escorial; centralization; Armada 1588.

    Inquisition: Censorship apparatus; Index of Forbidden Books; Protestant texts banned.

    Golden Age Literature: Cervantes, Lope de Vega; printed works; Madrid center.

    Colonial Print: Limited presses in Americas; controlled production; religious texts.

    Imperial Administration: Correspondence networks; printed ordinances; bureaucratic records.

    2. Habsburg Decline (1598–1700 CE)

    Philip III, IV: Favorites (validos); Lerma, Olivares; reform attempts.

    Revolts: Catalonia, Portugal 1640; Portuguese independence; Westphalia 1648.

    Economic Decline: Silver decline; population loss; plague; deindustrialization.

    Baroque Culture: Calderón, Quevedo, Velázquez; Counter-Reformation aesthetics.

    War of Spanish Succession: 1700–1714; Bourbon victory; Utrecht settlement.

    Print Control: Continued censorship; declining output; provincial stagnation.

    3. Bourbon Reforms (1700–1808 CE)

    Bourbon Dynasty: Philip V; centralization; Nueva Planta decrees; Castilian model.

    Charles III: 1759–1788; Enlightened reforms; ilustrados; economic societies.

    Press Development: Gaceta de Madrid; learned societies; limited public sphere.

    Inquisition Decline: Weakened but persists; Enlightenment pressures.

    Portugal: Pombal reforms; Lisbon earthquake 1755; Jesuit expulsion.

    Colonial Tensions: Bourbon reforms in Americas; creole discontent.

    4. Napoleonic Invasion and Liberal Constitution (1808–1833 CE)

    French Invasion: 1808; Dos de Mayo; Joseph Bonaparte; guerrilla war.

    Cadiz Constitution: 1812; liberal framework; press freedom; sovereignty.

    Ferdinand VII: Restoration 1814; absolutist reaction; liberal triennium 1820–1823.

    American Independence: Latin American independence movements; colonial loss.

    Press Explosion: War and constitutional periods; pamphlets; periodicals.

    Portugal: Liberal revolution 1820; Pedro vs. Miguel; civil conflict.

    5. Liberal Revolution (1833–1874 CE)

    Isabella II: 1833–1868; regency; liberal constitution; instability.

    Carlist Wars: Traditionalist resistance; regional support; three wars.

    Pronunciamientos: Military coups; political instability; liberal factions.

    Disentailment: Church and municipal lands sold; social transformation.

    Sexenio Democrático: 1868–1874; revolution; Amadeo I; First Republic.

    Press Growth: Partisan newspapers; provincial press; limited literacy.

    6. Restoration System (1874–1898 CE)

    Cánovas del Castillo: Constitutional monarchy; Alfonso XII, XIII.

    Turno Pacífico: Alternation of Conservatives and Liberals; controlled elections.

    Caciquismo: Local bosses; electoral manipulation; rural control.

    Regenerationism: Reform movements; intellectual criticism; Costa.

    Cuban War: 1895–1898; Spanish-American War; colonial collapse; 1898 disaster.

    Press Maturity: Commercial papers; El Imparcial; ABC; mass circulation begins.

    7. Crisis of Restoration (1898–1923 CE)

    Generation of '98: Intellectual response to disaster; Unamuno, Baroja.

    Regional Movements: Catalan, Basque nationalism; press in regional languages.

    Tragic Week: Barcelona 1909; anticlerical violence; Ferrer execution.

    Morocco: Rif War; Annual disaster 1921; military crisis.

    Labor Movements: CNT; UGT; strikes; Barcelona violence.

    Mass Politics: Electoral reform 1890; urbanization; socialist, anarchist press.

    8. Primo de Rivera Dictatorship (1923–1930 CE)

    Coup: September 1923; military directory; Alfonso XIII acquiesces.

    Censorship: Press restrictions; opposition suppressed; exile.

    Economic Development: Infrastructure; expositions; brief prosperity.

    Morocco Victory: Alhucemas 1925; Rif pacification.

    Decline: Economic crisis; loss of support; resignation 1930.

    Transition: Fall leads to Second Republic 1931.

    9. Inquisition and Spanish Censorship (1500–1930 CE)

    Inquisition Origins: 1478; religious purity; converso targeting.

    Index of Forbidden Books: 1559 Spanish Index; pre-publication censorship.

    Counter-Reformation: Protestant texts banned; Erasmian influence suppressed.

    Bourbon Continuity: Inquisition weakened; abolished 1834; legacy persists.

    19th Century: Liberal press freedom; conservative reaction; cycles.

    20th Century: Military censorship; Primo de Rivera; continuing controls.

    10. Portuguese Print and Atlantic Networks (1500–1930 CE)

    Early Printing: Late 15th century; Lisbon center; religious texts.

    Colonial Press: Brazil limited until 1808; Goa, Macao; missionary printing.

    Pombaline Reforms: Enlightenment; Real Mesa Censória; education.

    Liberal Era: 1820 revolution; constitutional press; partisan papers.

    Republic: 1910; anticlerical; press freedom; instability.

    Atlantic Networks: Brazil independence 1822; Portuguese diaspora press.
