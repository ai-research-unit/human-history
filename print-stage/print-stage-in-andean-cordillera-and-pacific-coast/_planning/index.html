    Planning Document — Print Stage in The Andean Cordillera and Pacific Coast (1810 CE – 1950 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1810 CE – 1950 CE

    Region: The Andean Cordillera and Pacific Coast — the western spine of South America from Colombia and Ecuador through Peru to Bolivia and Chile; the core zones of independence movements, republican state-building, and export economies.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes independence-era proclamations, government archives, newspapers, diplomatic correspondence, commercial records, and literary publications. Printing presses arrive with independence movements; partisan press develops rapidly; telegraph integration from 1870s. Window begins with independence movements; ends as broadcast radio transforms political communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Independence Wars (1810–1830 CE)

    Simon Bolivar: Venezuelan leader; northern campaigns; Gran Colombia; Peru liberation.

    Jose de San Martin: Argentine leader; Chile campaign; Lima proclamation 1821.

    Liberation Campaigns: Boyaca 1819; Carabobo 1821; Pichincha 1822; Ayacucho 1824.

    Gran Colombia: 1819–1831; Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador; fragmentation.

    Peru Independence: San Martin, then Bolivar; royalist resistance; Ayacucho decisive.

    Print Role: Proclamations, newspapers, propaganda; independence press; literate elite.

    2. Early Republican Fragmentation (1830–1860 CE)

    State Fragmentation: Gran Colombia dissolves; Ecuador, Venezuela, Colombia separate.

    Caudillo Politics: Military strongmen; personalist rule; unstable constitutions.

    Peru-Bolivia Confederation: Santa Cruz 1836–1839; Chilean opposition; dissolution.

    Economic Struggles: Post-independence disruption; mining decline; hacienda continuity.

    Press Development: Partisan newspapers; Lima, Bogota, Santiago centers; elite readership.

    Regional Conflicts: Border disputes; internal rebellions; weak central authority.

    3. Guano Boom and War of the Pacific (1860–1884 CE)

    Guano Wealth: Peruvian exports; fertilizer demand; state revenues; foreign debt.

    Nitrate Deposits: Atacama desert; Chilean, Bolivian, Peruvian claims; tension.

    War of the Pacific: 1879–1884; Chile vs. Peru-Bolivia; Chilean victory; territorial gains.

    Treaty of Ancon: 1883; Peru cedes Tarapaca; Tacna-Arica dispute.

    Bolivian Landlocking: Bolivia loses coast; lasting grievance; trade dependency.

    Telegraph Integration: 1870s; international connections; commercial communication.

    4. Oligarchic Republics (1884–1920 CE)

    Parliamentary Chile: Elite rule; nitrate revenues; civil war 1891; Balmaceda.

    Civilist Peru: Civilian oligarchy; Pierola; guano replaced by cotton, sugar; Lima elite.

    Bolivian Tin: Mining boom; Patino, Hochschild; La Paz oligarchy; indigenous marginalization.

    Export Economies: Primary commodity dependence; British investment; railways.

    Labor Movements: Mining workers; port strikes; anarchist influences; repression.

    Press Expansion: Commercial newspapers; telegraph news; El Mercurio; political debates.

    5. Leguia, Alessandri, and Reformist Currents (1920–1930 CE)

    Leguia Oncenio: Peru 1919–1930; authoritarian modernization; US loans; infrastructure.

    Alessandri Chile: 1920–1925; middle-class support; military intervention; 1925 constitution.

    Indigenismo: Intellectual movement; Mariategui; indigenous rights discourse; print venues.

    Labor Organizations: Unions; strikes; socialist, communist currents; print propaganda.

    University Reform: Student movements; Cordoba influence; political engagement.

    Middle-Class Politics: Challenge to oligarchy; electoral expansion; new constituencies.

    6. Depression and Populism (1930–1950 CE)

    Great Depression: Export collapse; unemployment; political instability; military coups.

    APRA Peru: Haya de la Torre; mass party; exile; partial incorporation.

    Popular Front Chile: 1938–1941; left coalition; Aguirre Cerda; social reforms.

    Military Interventions: Sanchez Cerro Peru; Ibanez Chile; Bolivian instability.

    World War II: Commodity demands; US influence; economic recovery.

    Radio Emergence: Broadcast begins; political communication transforms; print supplemented.

    7. Andean Press and Political Communication (1810–1950 CE)

    Independence Press: Proclamations; Gazeta; elite communication; patriot propaganda.

    Partisan Newspapers: Political factions; editorial positions; limited circulation.

    Telegraph Impact: 1870s onward; news agencies; international connections.

    Commercial Press: El Mercurio (1827); El Comercio Lima; advertising revenue.

    Censorship Cycles: Authoritarian periods; press freedom struggles; exile journalism.

    Literacy Limits: Urban concentration; Spanish language; indigenous exclusion.

    8. Export Economies and Print-Mediated Commerce (1810–1950 CE)

    Colonial Legacy: Mining tradition; hacienda agriculture; merchant networks.

    Guano Boom: 1840s–1870s; Peruvian monopoly; consignment houses; contracts.

    Nitrate Economy: 1880s–1930s; Chilean control; Atacama mines; export documentation.

    Tin Mining: Bolivian highlands; industrial scale; corporate records; labor documentation.

    Commercial Documentation: Bills of lading; contracts; price lists; business correspondence.

    Telegraph Commerce: Price information; market integration; commercial coordination.
