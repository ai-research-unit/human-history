    1810 CE – 1830 CE — Independence Wars and Republican Foundations

    The binding constraint is the spatial friction of Andean geography against military coordination, forcing revolutionary campaigns into sequential coastal and highland phases while printed proclamations, constitutions, and political newspapers become the indispensable medium through which distant patriot elites communicate shared cause, articulate republican ideology, and project legitimacy to literate urban populations scattered across a fragmented terrain.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The Andes divide the theater into three altitude bands — the Pacific costa, the highland sierra, and the eastern selva — each requiring distinct logistical arrangements; the mule-train journey from Lima to Upper Peru takes weeks, while San Martín's 1817 crossing of the cordillera from Mendoza to Chile ranks among the era's most demanding military feats, conditioning the entire war's phasing.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: The cold, thin air of the altiplano and high passes limits campaigning seasons and debilitates unacclimated coastal and foreign troops; the Atacama presents a nearly impassable gap between Peru and Chile, channeling movement through mountain routes; El Niño episodes periodically disrupt coastal agriculture and provisioning.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Earthquakes strike repeatedly — Lima's 1746 devastation remains in living memory — and volcanic activity punctuates highland life; armies march through landslide-prone valleys and cross rivers swollen by seasonal rains.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Silver mines at Cerro de Pasco and Potosí fund whichever side controls them, but output declines from colonial peaks; guano deposits remain unexploited; forests and watercourses provide fuel and power for modest workshop production, while coastal valleys yield the grain and livestock that feed urban centers.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban waste accumulates in Lima, Santiago, and highland cities without systematic removal; battlefield detritus and abandoned camps litter transit corridors; mining debris clogs highland watercourses, though population remains too thin for sink saturation to constrain operations.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Typhus, smallpox, and dysentery circulate in armies and cities, killing more soldiers than battle; highland populations retain some endemic disease resistance, while coastal fevers incapacitate lowland garrisons; infant mortality remains high across all strata.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Highland indigenous populations show chronic nutritional stress from hacienda extraction; coastal populations access marine protein but face periodic drought-induced grain shortages; armies stripped local provisioning, spreading famine in their wake.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Military hospitals are rudimentary — surgeons amputate, priests administer last rites, and most wounded die of infection; urban elite families access trained physicians, while indigenous populations rely on traditional curers; capacity nowhere matches the load war imposes.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Marriage remains sacramental and controlled by Church registry; mestizo populations grow as colonial racial barriers weaken in revolutionary chaos; patriot armies recruit through kinship networks while royalist forces depend on peninsular reinforcement.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Peru holds roughly 1.5 million, Chile one million, Upper Peru somewhat less; Lima's population hovers around 100,000, Santiago at 30,000; war casualties, displacement, and epidemic outbreaks depress demographic growth during the two-decade span.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Widows, orphans, and maimed veterans accumulate; hacienda labor forces fragment as men are conscripted or flee; the productive base contracts under extraction, and the dependency load rises relative to working population.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Highland peasant communities provide their own maize, potatoes, and livestock, while coastal populations depend on irrigated valley production and Pacific fisheries; armies commandeer grain and livestock, collapsing local subsistence margins during campaigns.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle drive agriculture, mining, and transport; waterpower mills process grain and ore; no steam engines operate in the region until after the period; energy surplus is thin, and mobilization exhausts it rapidly.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Indigenous tribute and mita labor obligations survive the colonial-republican transition, though patriot rhetoric promises abolition; debt peonage binds coastal agricultural workers; slave labor persists on plantations until slow abolition processes begin post-independence.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Silver output declines as mines flood and skilled labor disperses; agricultural surplus, already modest, is extracted for war finance; artisan production in Lima and Santiago contracts as capital and labor drain into military supply.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Colonial treasuries are emptied by royalist defense; patriot forces rely on confiscation, foreign loans, and forced contributions; no reserve accumulates during the wars, leaving successor republics debt-burdened from inception.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: British and North American merchants penetrate Pacific commerce once Spanish monopoly collapses; Valparaíso emerges as a free port; overland trade stalls as mule trains are requisitioned for military logistics and routes become unsafe.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Metallic currency circulates, but coinage is debased and counterfeited; British creditors extend loans to patriot governments in exchange for trade concessions; promissory notes and forced loans substitute for functioning credit markets.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Spanish tribute and tithe collection collapse unevenly; patriot governments impose war taxes, requisitions, and confiscations, shifting extraction from indigenous communities to royalist property holders; redistribution is minimal beyond military wages.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Creole landowners and merchants shift allegiance to preserve estates; royalist properties are confiscated and redistributed among patriot officers; Church wealth remains largely intact, though some clerical land is seized.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: War debris — abandoned weapons, dead livestock, camp refuse — litters transit routes; financial waste accumulates as debased currencies and defaulted bonds degrade the value medium; no systematic clearance occurs.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Elite families network through intermarriage, extending political alliance across cities; inheritance passes through patrilineal primogeniture; illegitimate offspring and mestizo kin occupy subordinate positions, though some rise through military service.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Urban artisan guilds persist; indigenous ayllu communities provide mutual aid in the highlands; Masonic lodges — Lautaro Lodge being most famous — coordinate patriot conspiracy across cities; Church confraternities remain channels of sociability.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Public festivals, bullfights, and religious processions continue where security permits; salons and tertulias host elite debate; cockfighting and drinking provide popular recreation; war disrupts but does not eliminate festive life.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Colonial caste distinctions — peninsular Spaniard, creole, mestizo, indigenous, African — persist in practice though patriot ideology proclaims legal equality; military rank becomes a new status ladder, and successful officers enter the post-colonial elite.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Colonial administrative apparatus fractures; patriot governments improvise juntas, congresses, and provisional executives; constitutional drafts multiply, but implementation capacity lags; corruption and patronage pervade successor regimes.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Patriot and royalist armies field infantry with smoothbore muskets, cavalry with lances and sabers, and small artillery trains; naval forces — Lord Cochrane's Chilean squadron notably — contest Pacific control; irregular guerrilla bands harry supply lines.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Banditry proliferates as disbanded soldiers and displaced peasants turn to predation; royalist and patriot terror — executions, confiscations, reprisals — leaves vendetta and grievance; the institutional apparatus inherited from Spain decays faster than republican replacements stabilize.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: San Martín and Bolívar embody charismatic leadership, their personal authority holding together fragile coalitions; O'Higgins in Chile, Sucre in Upper Peru similarly concentrate loyalties; rivalries among these figures shape post-war political fragmentation.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Spanish legitimacy collapses in creole opinion after Ferdinand VII's restoration reveals absolutism untempered; patriot legitimacy rests on constitutional promise and personal charisma, but neither consolidates before the wars end.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Patriot elites sustain will through ideological conviction and material interest; popular will fluctuates with military fortune; royalist will erodes as peninsular reinforcement fails and defeat becomes certain.

    External Relations & Posture: Britain maintains formal neutrality while its merchants and creditors support patriot trade; the United States offers rhetorical support and Monroe Doctrine backing; Spanish reconquest threats persist until Ayacucho settles the military question.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Urban populations oscillate between patriot enthusiasm and war weariness; indigenous highland communities remain largely indifferent to creole factional struggle, their loyalty purchased locally by whichever force occupies; fear, hope, and opportunism coexist.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: War exhaustion is general by 1824; the idealism of 1810 gives way to cynicism and factional self-interest; disaffection among unpaid soldiers and disappointed civilian supporters sets the stage for caudillo politics.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Spanish literacy is confined to urban elites, clergy, and artisans — perhaps 10–15 percent of total population; Quechua and Aymara remain oral, limiting print's reach; proclamations are read aloud in plazas to extend their audience.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Couriers on horseback carry dispatches along colonial post routes; coastal shipping accelerates communication between ports; no telegraph exists; weeks separate Lima and Buenos Aires, days separate Lima and Santiago by sea.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Colonial archives transfer unevenly to successor states; ecclesiastical registers record births, marriages, and deaths; printed decrees, constitutions, and newspaper issues accumulate in elite libraries and government deposits.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Patriot governments suppress royalist press; royalists censor patriot material in areas they control; political discourse is polarized between republican liberty and monarchical loyalty; pamphlet wars escalate rhetorical intensity.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Printed proclamations are posted, read aloud, and discussed in plazas and salons; newspaper circulation is small — hundreds to low thousands of copies per issue — but each copy passes through many hands and is read aloud to others.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Competing claims, forgeries, and rumors circulate alongside official information; battlefield news is delayed and distorted; the signal-to-noise ratio is low, and rumor often outruns print.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Patriot ideology frames independence as restoration of natural rights usurped by Spanish tyranny; royalist counter-ideology invokes divine-right monarchy and social order; popular sense of justice centers on local reciprocity and patron-client obligation rather than abstract principle.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Colonial law persists by default; patriot constitutions — Chile 1818, Peru 1823, Bolivia 1826 — declare liberal principles but lack enforcement capacity; slavery is nominally abolished but freedom laws are hedged with conditions.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Courts continue under improvised jurisdiction; military tribunals handle political crimes; executions for treason occur on both sides; civil dispute resolution breaks down where state authority is contested.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Declared rights — property, person, expression — exceed actual protection; indigenous tribute is abolished in proclamations but often reimposed in practice; compliance burden falls on whoever occupies territory.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: War crimes — massacres, confiscations, forced contributions — accumulate without redress; victors impose justice on losers; the defeated have no appeal; impunity for atrocity is general.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Enlightenment ideas — Rousseau, Montesquieu, the North American founders — circulate among creole elites through printed books and periodicals; natural philosophy lags behind political theory; inquiry is philosophical rather than empirical.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Mining techniques remain colonial; military engineering is improvised; printing technology is imported and operated by small workshops; no indigenous innovation frontier exists.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Universities at Lima (San Marcos) and Santiago continue to train clergy, lawyers, and physicians; Lancasterian schools begin after independence; literacy and technical skill concentrate in urban elites.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Colonial administrative knowledge atrophies as experienced officials emigrate or are purged; library collections are damaged in war; manuscript records are lost or scattered; the knowledge base contracts before republican reconstruction begins.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Creole patriots invent an Andean founding myth — appropriating Inca imagery, condemning Spanish conquest, claiming a continuous American identity — while indigenous populations maintain separate local memories and identities.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Republicanism and popular sovereignty become the legitimating narrative; constitutions invoke natural rights and civic virtue; the narrative requires printing to circulate and does not yet penetrate beyond literate elites.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Neoclassical styles dominate official architecture and portraiture; republican iconography — eagles, liberty caps, allegorical figures — replaces royal heraldry; dress among elites follows European fashion with Andean inflections.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Colonial baroque forms lose official sanction but persist in popular religiosity; revolutionary symbols are fresh, not yet exhausted; meaning decay is minimal in this generative moment.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Catholic Christianity frames the universe for all strata; Providence is invoked by both sides; fatalism coexists with millennial hope; indigenous cosmologies persist beneath Christian overlay.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Mass, confession, feast days, and processions continue; patriot armies carry chaplains and celebrate Te Deums for victories; popular devotion to saints and Marian images is intense and undiminished.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: The Church is divided — lower clergy often patriot, hierarchy often royalist; ecclesiastical property is mostly protected, though some confiscations occur; Rome's recognition of new republics is delayed.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Catholicism remains hegemonic; Protestant presence is negligible; indigenous syncretism continues uninterrupted; no significant religious displacement occurs during the period.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Among anticlerical liberals, Enlightenment skepticism tinges religious observance; for the masses, the sacred remains charged; disenchantment is an elite phenomenon and does not accumulate as a residue during this window.
