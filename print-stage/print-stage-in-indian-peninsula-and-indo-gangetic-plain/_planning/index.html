    Planning Document — Print Stage in The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain (1850 CE – 1960 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1850 CE – 1960 CE

    Region: The Indian Peninsula and Indo-Gangetic Plain — the South Asian subcontinent from the Himalayas to the Indian Ocean; British India and its successor states India and Pakistan; including Bengal, the Gangetic plain, the Deccan, and the southern peninsula.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes colonial archives, vernacular and English-language newspapers, nationalist tracts, government reports, and independence movement publications. Printing expands under Company rule; vernacular press flourishes 19th century; telegraph from 1850s; nationalist press grows through independence; radio from 1920s but limited. Window begins with mature colonial print infrastructure; ends as broadcast and television transform communication in independent India.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Late Company Rule (1850–1857 CE)

    Railways and Telegraph: 1853 railway; 1851 telegraph; communication infrastructure.

    Vernacular Press: Bengali, Hindi, Urdu papers; reformist debates; social issues.

    English Education: Macaulay; English-educated elite; Western ideas.

    Social Reform: Sati abolished; widow remarriage; reform press.

    Company Administration: Dalhousie; annexation policy; Doctrine of Lapse.

    Pre-Mutiny Tensions: Grievances accumulating; sepoy concerns; religious fears.

    2. Mutiny and Crown Rule (1857–1877 CE)

    1857 Rebellion: Sepoy mutiny; widespread revolt; British reconquest.

    Crown Takeover: 1858; Queen's Proclamation; direct rule; viceroy.

    Press Expansion: Vernacular newspapers multiply; colonial suspicion.

    Social Reform Debates: Widow remarriage; education; caste reform.

    Telegraph Networks: Expanded; colonial administration; commercial use.

    Early Nationalism: Educated elite; associations; moderate criticism.

    3. Moderate Nationalism (1877–1905 CE)

    Indian National Congress: Founded 1885; educated elite; loyal opposition.

    English-Language Press: Hindu, Tribune; moderate newspapers.

    Vernacular Growth: Regional papers; literacy slowly expands.

    Social Reform: Age of Consent; women's education; reform movements.

    Economic Critique: Drain theory; Naoroji; nationalist economics.

    Limited Franchise: Councils; token representation; moderate demands.

    4. Swadeshi and Extremism (1905–1919 CE)

    Bengal Partition: 1905; protests; Swadeshi movement; boycott.

    Extremist Press: Tilak's Kesari; sedition trials; imprisonment.

    Revolutionary Movements: Bengal revolutionaries; Ghadar Party abroad.

    World War I: Indian troops; wartime censorship; expectations raised.

    Home Rule Leagues: Tilak; Besant; expanded demands.

    Lucknow Pact: 1916; Congress-League unity; joint demands.

    5. Gandhi and Mass Politics (1919–1930 CE)

    Rowlatt Acts: Repressive legislation; protests; Gandhi's leadership.

    Jallianwala Bagh: 1919 massacre; national outrage; radicalization.

    Non-Cooperation: 1920–1922; mass movement; Congress transformation.

    Khilafat: Hindu-Muslim unity; pan-Islamic cause; Gandhi alliance.

    Swaraj Papers: Young India; Navajivan; Gandhian journalism.

    Salt March: 1930; civil disobedience; mass mobilization.

    Round Table Conferences: 1930–1932; constitutional negotiations; failure.

    Government of India Act: 1935; provincial autonomy; limited franchise.

    Congress Ministries: 1937–1939; provincial governments; experience.

    Muslim League: Jinnah; Pakistan demand; Two-Nation Theory.

    World War II: British declaration; Congress resignation; Muslim cooperation.

    Press Growth: Regional papers; political press; commercial dailies.

    Quit India Movement: 1942; mass arrests; underground networks.

    Famine Coverage: 1943 Bengal famine; press reporting; government failure.

    Post-War Negotiations: Cabinet Mission; communal tensions; breakdown.

    Nehru Era: Nation-building; planning; foreign policy; personality.

    9. Vernacular Press (1850–1960 CE)

    Bengali Press: Earliest; Amrita Bazar Patrika; nationalist fervor.

    Hindi Press: Expanding; Hindu heartland; national language debates.

    Urdu Press: Muslim audiences; literary culture; Pakistan after 1947.

    Tamil, Marathi, Others: Regional identities; political mobilization.

    Circulation Growth: Literacy expansion; mass readership develops.

    Wartime Censorship: Both world wars; press restrictions.

    Underground Press: Revolutionary tracts; smuggled publications.
