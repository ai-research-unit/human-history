    1919 CE – 1930 CE — Gandhi, Non-Cooperation, and Mass Politics

    Summary: The subcontinent transitions from elite petition politics to mass nationalist mobilization under Gandhi's leadership; the Rowlatt Acts and Jallianwala Bagh massacre delegitimize British rule among wider strata; non-cooperation (1920–1922) and civil disobedience campaigns bring millions into direct confrontation with colonial authority; the binding constraint is the British monopoly on coercive force countered by mass non-violent resistance and print-mediated solidarity; margin against repression narrows as each crackdown swells recruitment; direction is toward intensifying popular challenge to colonial legitimacy.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: Railways and telegraph knit the subcontinent into a single mobilizable political space; Gandhian campaigns exploit this connectivity for coordinated hartals and marches; the Salt March traverses 240 miles of Gujarat coast, leveraging terrain for symbolic impact.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Monsoon variability persists; periodic droughts in the Deccan and western India stress agrarian populations, amplifying grievances channeled into nationalist agitation; no major secular shift within the window.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Floods along the Gangetic plain and cyclones on the coasts impose episodic shocks; the 1927 Bihar flood and 1928 Godavari flood strain colonial relief capacity and provide openings for Congress-led relief work building organizational depth.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Coal in Bengal-Bihar; cotton in Gujarat and Maharashtra; jute in Bengal; salt along the entire coastline becomes the symbolic target of civil disobedience; colonial extraction continues with surplus flowing to metropolitan Britain.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban sanitation in Calcutta, Bombay, and mill towns remains inadequate; industrial effluent from jute and cotton mills contaminates waterways; absorptive capacity strained but not collapsed; rural areas retain dispersed capacity.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Malaria, cholera, and plague remain endemic; the 1918–1919 influenza pandemic killed an estimated 12–17 million in India; mortality baseline high but post-pandemic improvement gradual; sanitary reform slow.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Chronic undernutrition widespread among peasant and laboring classes; famines less catastrophic than nineteenth century but food insecurity persists; average stature and vitality constrained by poverty and disease load.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Early marriage norms persist; fertility high but checked by high infant and maternal mortality; no significant shift in reproductive patterns within the window.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population grows from approximately 305 million (1921) to 338 million (1931); growth rate around one percent annually; density rising in irrigated plains and urban centers; age structure young with large dependent cohorts.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Widows, orphans, and the chronically ill accumulate in a system lacking institutional safety nets; joint family absorbs much load; nationalist rhetoric invokes suffering masses but organizational relief remains episodic.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Peasant agriculture dominates; landlessness and tenancy widespread; subsistence precarious for millions; household pooling through joint family structures buffers but does not eliminate risk.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Human and animal muscle remain primary; coal-fired railways and mills expand but per-capita energy consumption remains among the world's lowest; Gandhi's charkha (spinning wheel) symbolically reasserts human-muscle production.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Plantation labor coerced through indenture and debt-bondage; mill workers in Bombay and Ahmedabad organize strikes; Congress cultivates labor support; wage labor expanding but largely unprotected.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Agricultural output fluctuates with monsoon; cottage industry (especially handloom) revived by Swadeshi; jute and cotton mills produce for domestic and export markets; surplus modest and unevenly distributed.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Colonial grain reserves minimal; no effective famine code enforcement; merchant hoarding destabilizes local markets; peasant household reserves thin; vulnerability to supply shocks high.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Railways move grain, cotton, and jute to port cities; internal markets integrate but colonial tariff policy favors British manufactures; boycott campaigns disrupt British cloth imports temporarily.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Rupee linked to sterling; colonial banking serves European commerce; indigenous moneylenders dominate rural credit at usurious rates; Congress promotes cooperative credit but penetration limited.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Land revenue, salt tax, and excise extract surplus for colonial state; drain to Britain continues; redistribution minimal; nationalist critique of drain intensifies mobilization.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Zamindars, princes, and British commercial firms hold concentrated wealth; peasant landlessness rising; Congress draws support from both urban middle class and dispossessed agrarian populations.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Mill towns discharge effluent into rivers; urban sanitation primitive; waste routing decisions left to municipal bodies with limited capacity; disposal burden falls on lowest castes.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Caste and community networks provide mutual aid; Khilafat movement (1919–1924) temporarily bridges Hindu-Muslim divides; Gandhi's ashram model promotes cross-caste solidarity; trust radius expanding under nationalist umbrella but fragile.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Caste hierarchy persists; untouchables begin organizing (Ambedkar rises); Hindu-Muslim-Sikh identities politicized; Congress claims secular nationalism but communal tensions simmer beneath.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Joint family remains normative; Hindu succession laws codified but reform limited; Muslim personal law separate; widow remarriage slowly accepted among reformist groups.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Congress transforms into mass organization; Khilafat committees, Hindu Mahasabha, Muslim League, and caste associations proliferate; volunteer corps (Seva Dal) organize; associational density thickens.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Cricket, wrestling, and folk festivals continue; nationalist songs, theater, and public meetings become sites of political sociability; Congress mass meetings double as festive gatherings.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Hartals, marches, and civil disobedience provide structured outlets; communal riots (Moplah rebellion 1921, communal clashes 1924–1927) indicate unmetabolized grievance; discharge capacity expands but not fully.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: British colonial authority remains sovereign but faces escalating challenge; Government of India Act 1919 (Montagu-Chelmsford reforms) introduces dyarchy with limited provincial autonomy; real power retained by viceroy and ICS.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Viceroys appointed from London; Indian participation in governance token; Congress develops internal succession mechanisms; Gandhi's moral authority transcends formal office.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: British legitimacy collapses after Jallianwala Bagh; Congress narrative of Swaraj (self-rule) gains mass traction; Gandhi frames struggle as moral and spiritual, broadening appeal beyond educated elite.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Colonial bureaucracy efficient at extraction and repression but incapable of winning consent; Congress builds parallel administrative capacity through volunteer networks and panchayats.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Mass participation explodes; non-cooperation brings peasants, students, women, and workers into politics; Congress membership grows from tens of thousands to millions; mobilization channels open dramatically.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: India remains British dependency; World War I aftermath shapes expectations; League of Nations discussions of self-determination resonate; diaspora (South Africa, East Africa, Southeast Asia) connects struggles.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: British Indian Army and police maintain order; Rowlatt Acts permit detention without trial; lathi charges, arrests, and shootings deployed against satyagrahis; force concentrated but legitimacy cost rising.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Mass civil disobedience strains enforcement capacity; Chauri Chaura violence (1922) leads Gandhi to suspend non-cooperation; communal riots flare; revolutionary terrorism persists at margins; disorder manageable but system stressed.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Vernacular press reaches millions; Gandhi's Young India and Navajivan distribute weekly; telegraph carries news rapidly; public meetings and word-of-mouth amplify print; radio introduced (1927) but reach limited.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Colonial archives, newspaper morgues, Congress records, and police files accumulate; pamphlet literature proliferates; ephemeral leaflets complement durable records; documentation dense for the period.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Colonial censorship under Press Acts targets seditious content; editors imprisoned (Gandhi jailed 1922); self-censorship coexists with defiant publication; discourse norms favor nationalist framing among Indian readership.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Mass meetings amplify print messages; Gandhi's persona commands attention; boycott campaigns and Salt March become media events covered internationally; attention concentrated on nationalist leadership.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Rumor circulates alongside print; communal tensions amplified by partisan press; colonial propaganda competes with nationalist narrative; verification capacity limited outside educated readership; noise load moderate.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Jallianwala Bagh crystallizes sense of colonial injustice; Gandhian ethics of non-violence and suffering redefine justice as moral witness; mass opinion shifts against colonial legal order.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Rowlatt Acts (1919) extend wartime emergency powers; Indian Penal Code enforces sedition; Press Acts regulate publication; legal framework repressive; reforms piecemeal.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Colonial courts process sedition cases; Gandhi's trials become political theater; mass arrests overwhelm judicial capacity; summary punishments common; enforcement biased toward regime preservation.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Civil liberties nominal under emergency laws; preventive detention, press seizures, and assembly bans routine; compliance burden on nationalists extreme; colonial subjects lack effective rights against state.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Dyer (Jallianwala perpetrator) censured but not criminally punished; communal violence often unprosecuted; police excesses unredressed; impunity for colonial agents normalized; justice deficit accumulates.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Nationalist historiography (R.C. Majumdar, Jadunath Sarkar) constructs Indian past; economic critique of colonialism (drain theory) systematized; Gandhi's moral philosophy synthesizes Indian and Western thought.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Gandhian constructive program applies techniques of self-reliance (khadi, village sanitation); modern medicine and engineering remain colonial monopoly; intermediate technology promoted symbolically.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Literacy around eight percent; English education creates nationalist leadership; national education experiments (Jamia Millia, Gujarat Vidyapith) challenge colonial curriculum; knowledge concentrated in urban educated strata.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Traditional crafts revived by Swadeshi but economically marginal; colonial education displaces indigenous learning; synthesis attempted but traditional knowledge base eroding; decay not reversed.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Ram Rajya, ancient Indian civilization, and Mughal-Maratha resistance invoked; Gandhi as mahatma embodies purified tradition; Jallianwala Bagh enters traumatic national memory; foundational identity consolidating around anti-colonial narrative.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Khadi becomes nationalist uniform; folk songs and theater deployed for mobilization; Bengal School painting asserts Indian aesthetics; material culture politicized as marker of loyalty.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Oscillates between mass enthusiasm (non-cooperation, Salt March) and demoralization (post-Chauri Chaura suspension); hope and sacrifice coexist; mood trending toward confident defiance by 1930.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Moderate petition politics exhausted; new forms (satyagraha, constructive program) inject meaning; nationalist symbols fresh and potent; form exhaustion minimal within the window.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Gandhi synthesizes Hindu, Jain, and Christian ethics into ahimsa-centered worldview; karma and dharma frame political action as spiritual duty; fatalism countered by activist faith; secularism coexists with pervasive religiosity.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Prayer meetings integral to Gandhian practice; Ram Dhun sung at gatherings; fasting as political-spiritual tool; pilgrimage continues; communal worship both unifying and divisive along Hindu-Muslim lines.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Hindu reform movements (Arya Samaj) and Muslim organizations (Khilafat committees, Jamiat Ulema) mobilize; institutional authority fragmented; Gandhi's personal authority transcends institutional religion.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Gandhian synthesis attempts Hindu-Muslim unity; Khilafat alliance temporary; communalism rising beneath surface; syncretism aspirational but displacement of cosmopolitan possibility by communal hardening underway.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Western-educated elite partially secularized; Gandhi re-enchants politics with spiritual vocabulary; mass participation channels spiritual energy into national cause; disenchantment checked but not eliminated among modernizers.
