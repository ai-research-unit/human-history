    1914 CE – 1921 CE — War, Revolution, and Civil War

    Summary: The Russian Empire collapses under the strain of World War I, triggering revolution, regime change, and civil war across the East European Plain; the binding constraint is state disintegration — political authority fragments, coercive capacity scatters, and no successor regime can consolidate power until 1921; the system holds zero margin against further shock; the direction of change is violent rupture followed by Bolshevik consolidation.

    P01. Physical Environment

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The vast lowland plain from Poland to the Urals imposes immense logistical friction on armies; poor roads, few railroads, and vast distances favor defensive attrition; the western borderlands become contested zones of occupation and retreat.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Continental climate with severe winters disrupts military operations; the 1917–1921 period sees harsh winters compounding supply breakdowns; no secular drift within the window.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: No major natural disasters; the hazard is anthropogenic — war, revolution, and famine; infrastructure destruction removes all margin against environmental stress.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Grain-producing regions of Ukraine and south Russia become contested territories; Donbas coal and Baku oil change hands repeatedly; access to resources fluctuates with military control.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Battlefield destruction, abandoned equipment, and unburied dead litter the landscape; urban sanitation collapses; absorptive capacity overwhelmed in combat zones and cities.

    P02. Biological Environment

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Typhus, cholera, and influenza epidemics sweep through armies and civilian populations; the 1918–1919 influenza pandemic compounds war losses; mortality rate spikes catastrophically above peacetime baseline.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Famine conditions in cities and contested regions; grain requisitioning depletes rural stocks; widespread malnutrition and starvation; physical vitality collapses especially among urban and displaced populations.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Mass mobilization separates families; wartime and revolutionary chaos disrupts marriage and fertility; birth rates decline sharply.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Massive population losses — military deaths, civilian casualties, famine, epidemic, emigration — produce demographic collapse; age structure skews toward the very young and old; replacement ratios collapse.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: War widows, orphans, disabled veterans, and refugees constitute an enormous dependency burden; the productive cohort is depleted and cannot absorb the load.

    P03. Economic Environment

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Urban provisioning collapses; grain requisitioning under War Communism strips the countryside; barter replaces money exchange; the population sits at the edge of starvation.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Industrial production collapses to a fraction of prewar levels; coal mines and oil fields change hands or cease operation; the energy conversion regime effectively disintegrates.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: Conscription drains labor from agriculture and industry; War Communism introduces labor armies and compulsory mobilization; the coercion level rises to near-total.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Industrial output falls to perhaps 15–20% of 1913 levels by 1920; agricultural output collapses; surplus production is effectively zero.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Reserves exhausted by 1917; grain requisitioning depletes rural stockpiles; no buffer remains against further shock; the 1921 famine demonstrates the total absence of reserves.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Market exchange suppressed under War Communism; rail network disrupted by combat and fuel shortages; black markets and barter replace formal trade; logistics network fragmented.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Hyperinflation destroys the ruble's purchasing power; credit system collapses; barter and in-kind requisitioning replace monetary exchange; value storage impossible.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: War Communism extracts grain by armed requisition squads; "surplus" is defined administratively and taken by force; redistribution serves the Red Army and urban workers; extraction load approaches confiscation.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Bolshevik nationalization eliminates private property in land, industry, and finance; former elites stripped of assets; wealth concentration collapses through confiscation rather than redistribution.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Industrial waste negligible given production collapse; agricultural and military waste dispersed across the landscape; routing decisions irrelevant when production barely exists.

    P04. Social Environment

    Social Trust & Mutual Aid: Trust radius contracts to immediate family and locality; class war rhetoric and denunciations fracture social bonds; mutual aid persists within villages and worker collectives but not across factional lines.

    Stratification, Status & Group Identity: Class becomes the dominant identity marker; former nobles, bourgeoisie, and clergy targeted as class enemies; workers and poor peasants elevated rhetorically; ethnic and national identities mobilized by competing factions.

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Family structures disrupted by mobilization, flight, and death; inheritance abolished for significant property; household formation delayed by chaos.

    Civic Association & Self-Organization: Soviets emerge as organs of self-organization; workers' committees, soldiers' committees, and peasant communes briefly flourish; independent associations suppressed as Bolshevik control consolidates.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Revolutionary festivals and mass mobilization replace traditional leisure; theater and agitprop serve political purposes; genuine leisure disappears in the struggle for survival.

    Grievance Channels & Social Discharge: Revolution provides catastrophic discharge of accumulated grievances; land seizures, factory takeovers, and score-settling release decades of suppressed tension; new grievances accumulate immediately under War Communism.

    P05. Political Environment

    Power Structure & Authority: Imperial autocracy collapses in February 1917; Provisional Government never consolidates; Bolsheviks seize power in October; civil war fragments authority until Red victory in 1921; power centralizes violently toward the Communist Party.

    Succession & Power Transfer: Tsar abdicates; Provisional Government lacks legitimacy; Bolshevik seizure is a revolutionary rupture; Lenin's authority rests on party control and revolutionary prestige; transfer mechanisms remain improvised.

    Legitimacy & Political Narrative: Bolsheviks claim legitimacy through socialist revolution and worker-peasant alliance; opponents invoke democracy, national independence, or restoration; no narrative achieves broad acceptance until Red victory.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Imperial bureaucracy disintegrates; Provisional Government cannot build replacement; Bolsheviks construct new apparatus from party cadres, Red Guards, and co-opted specialists; capacity minimal but growing by 1921.

    Political Participation & Mobilization: Mass mobilization unprecedented — soviets, parties, trade unions, Red Army, White armies; participation coerced and voluntary; opposition increasingly suppressed as one-party rule consolidates.

    Geopolitical Position & External Relations: Russia exits World War I through Brest-Litovsk (March 1918) at enormous territorial cost; Allied intervention supports Whites; new state emerges diplomatically isolated but surviving.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: Imperial army dissolves; Red Army built from 1918; Cheka established for internal security; White armies, national movements, and foreign interventions create multi-sided conflict; coercive capacity fragmentary until Red consolidation.

    Internal Order & Threat Environment: Civil war, banditry, peasant uprisings, urban crime, and political terror render internal order nonexistent; the Kronstadt rebellion (1921) marks the final major challenge before consolidation.

    P06. Informational Environment

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Telegraph and rail disrupted by combat; newspapers remain primary channel but distribution erratic; oral networks and rumor fill gaps; velocity slows outside party-controlled zones.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Imperial archives preserved but access restricted; new Soviet record-keeping begins; much documentation lost in chaos; persistence uncertain for non-party records.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Press freedom briefly expands after February; Bolsheviks close opposition newspapers by mid-1918; party press becomes monopoly channel; framing shifts to revolutionary Marxist vocabulary; permitted discourse narrows rapidly.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Literacy remains limited; agitprop trains and posters reach non-readers; amplification serves Bolshevik messaging; attention focused on survival.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Rumor, propaganda, and disinformation create high noise load; verification impossible for most; signal integrity collapses outside immediate experience.

    P07. Judicial Environment

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Revolutionary justice overrides formal law; class origin determines guilt or innocence; popular sense of justice satisfied by land redistribution and attacks on elites; terror justified as class war.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Imperial legal system abolished; revolutionary decrees replace law; codification minimal; regulation by decree and party directive.

    Adjudication, Enforcement & Liability: Revolutionary tribunals and Cheka replace courts; summary justice predominates; enforcement arbitrary and politicized; liability determined by class position.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Individual rights suspended under revolutionary emergency; protection depends on class standing and party connection; compliance burden extreme — survival requires navigating arbitrary power.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Red and White terror produce mass impunity; atrocities committed by all sides go unredressed; judicial deficit essentially infinite.

    P08. Intellectual Environment

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Marxism-Leninism becomes official theory; bourgeois science initially tolerated for practical utility; revolutionary ideology displaces alternative paradigms in social thought.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Technical expertise desperately needed; former specialists co-opted despite class suspicion; military and industrial methods improvised under crisis conditions.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: Schools disrupted; literacy campaigns begin; university enrollment shifts toward workers; knowledge stock preserved unevenly; skill distribution chaotic.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Imperial administrative and legal knowledge deliberately discarded; practical skills preserved for necessity; ideological purification removes "bourgeois" content; decay and deliberate erasure proceed together.

    P09. Cultural Environment

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: Revolution becomes foundational myth; October 1917 rewritten as heroic origin; Imperial past recast as oppression; competing national myths emerge in borderlands.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Revolutionary avant-garde flourishes briefly — Constructivism, Futurism, agitprop art; material culture impoverished by production collapse; artistic energy directed toward revolutionary themes.

    Collective Mood & Behavioral Disposition: Mood oscillates between revolutionary enthusiasm and exhaustion; fear and violence normalize; survival orientation dominates by 1920–1921; mood lifting only with NEP announcement.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Imperial forms — religious, monarchical, aristocratic — deliberately destroyed; revolutionary forms not yet exhausted; meaning creation active through revolutionary culture.

    P10. Spiritual Environment

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Marxist materialism promoted as official worldview; religious cosmology attacked but persists among peasantry; fatalism and millenarian hope coexist; secularization accelerates in urban areas.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Church services continue but face hostility; revolutionary rituals — red funerals, revolutionary holidays — compete with Orthodox practice; devotional infrastructure under attack.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Orthodox Church stripped of property and legal status; clergy targeted as class enemies; Patriarch Tikhon resists then accommodates; institutional authority collapses; doctrinal unity fractures under pressure.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Revolutionary faith partially displaces religious belief among youth and urban workers; traditional Orthodoxy persists in villages; no syncretism but sharp competition.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Religious cosmology loses public sanction; revolutionary enthusiasm provides substitute meaning for some; sacred need incompletely absorbed by political faith; disenchantment and belief coexist uneasily.
