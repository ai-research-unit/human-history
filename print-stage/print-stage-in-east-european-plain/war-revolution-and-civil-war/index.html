    1914 CE – 1921 CE — War, Revolution, and Civil War

    The binding constraint is institutional collapse under compound shock: four years of industrial war drain the empire's reserves, shatter the army's cohesion, and delegitimize the autocracy; the February Revolution unseats the tsar but the Provisional Government cannot stabilize authority or end the war; the October Revolution installs a Bolshevik dictatorship that imposes press monopoly, expropriates property, and fights a civil war whose violence, blockade, and requisitioning push the population to the edge of subsistence. Print — newspapers, decrees, broadsides, pamphlets — saturates the revolutionary rupture as the primary channel for mobilization, agitation, and command, even as paper shortages and censorship constrict all other publishing.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The East European Plain extends from the Baltic to the Black Sea, traversed by rivers and, since the 1860s, by a rail network radiating from Moscow and Petrograd. The front line runs through western borderlands — Poland, the Baltic, Galicia, then Ukraine and the Caucasus — while interior distances complicate supply. Rail is the critical artery; its seizure determines which faction controls provisioning, troop movement, and communications.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Harsh winters impose logistical constraints on campaigns; the 1916–1917 winter sees fuel and food shortages compound urban misery. Harvests in 1916 and 1920 are adequate, but procurement failures and blockade turn sufficiency into famine.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: Typhus, cholera, and influenza (1918–1919 pandemic) sweep through populations weakened by malnutrition, crowded refugee columns, and collapsed sanitation. The Civil War zone experiences epidemic mortality exceeding combat deaths.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: The empire possesses coal (Donbas), oil (Baku), iron (Urals), and vast grain-producing regions, but war and civil conflict sever access. Baku changes hands repeatedly; the Donbas lies in contested territory; grain regions fall to Whites, Greens, and foreign intervention before Red reconquest. The energy base is present but inaccessible.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Urban sanitation collapses; corpses accumulate unburied during worst epidemics; industrial effluent ceases as factories close. Environmental pressure is low because production itself has stopped.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: Typhus spreads explosively, killing millions during 1918–1921. The 1918 influenza pandemic adds to mortality. Endemic tuberculosis worsens with overcrowding and malnutrition. Military and civilian death rates spike far above the prewar baseline.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Urban populations starve as grain requisitioning and transport breakdown cut rations. Petrograd's population falls from 2.4 million (1917) to under 700,000 (1920) through death, flight, and mobilization. Peasants hoard grain against requisition squads. Caloric intake falls well below subsistence in cities and contested zones.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Military medical services strain beyond capacity; civilian hospitals lose staff and supplies. Doctors and nurses flee, emigrate, or are conscripted. The Red Army prioritizes its own wounded; civilian care collapses outside party channels.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Wartime separation and mortality depress fertility; marriages postpone; births fall sharply. The demographic gap persists into the 1920s.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: The empire enters the war with 170 million; by 1921, the Soviet territory holds perhaps 135 million — losses from combat, disease, famine, emigration, and territorial cession (Poland, Finland, Baltic states). Cities empty; rural areas absorb refugees.

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: War orphans, widows, crippled veterans, and displaced refugees overwhelm relief capacity. The 1921 famine in the Volga region adds five million deaths and millions more orphaned children.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: By 1918, the provisioning floor collapses in cities. War Communism (1918–1921) requisitions grain at gunpoint, leaving peasants with seed grain or less. Urban workers subsist on rations distributed through workplace canteens. Barter displaces money; the ruble hyperinflates into worthlessness. Survival depends on access to party channels or the black market.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Coal production falls by 80%; oil output plummets as Baku is contested. Factories close for lack of fuel. Petrograd's electrical supply intermittently fails. Wood replaces coal where possible; furniture and books are burned for heat.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: The Bolshevik state militarizes labor; Trotsky proposes labor armies. Workers are tied to enterprises by ration cards. Peasants are subject to grain requisitioning enforced by armed detachments. Coercion replaces wages as the primary labor mobilization mechanism.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Industrial output falls to perhaps 10–20% of prewar levels. Agricultural production declines as peasants plant only for subsistence, anticipating confiscation. There is no surplus; the system consumes its capital stock.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Reserves exhaust by 1918. The treasury is empty; gold reserves are seized and spent. There is no buffer against the 1921 drought.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: The market economy collapses under War Communism's prohibitions. Internal trade is illegal (though pervasive on the black market). External trade is blockaded by the Allies until 1920. Rail transport, the only efficient logistics, is monopolized by military needs.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: The ruble hyperinflates; by 1921 it has lost essentially all purchasing power. Barter, in-kind payment, and ration distribution replace monetary exchange. Credit ceases to exist.

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: The state extracts through prodrazvyorstka (grain requisitioning), seizing peasant output. Redistribution occurs through ration tiers — workers and soldiers prioritized, former bourgeois ("lishentsy") last. The extraction regime fuels peasant revolts.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: The October Revolution expropriates property — land, factories, banks, buildings. Former owners lose all. Concealment of valuables, emigration with portable wealth, and barter hoarding are the only asset-defense strategies. By 1921, wealth is nearly leveled — at subsistence.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Industrial waste ceases with production. Urban refuse accumulates uncollected. Financial debris — worthless bonds, canceled contracts, unredeemable deposits — is simply voided by revolutionary decree.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: Revolutionary legislation abolishes estate-based inheritance and introduces civil marriage and easy divorce. Family structure strains under separation, death, and displacement. Orphanages fill.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Pre-revolutionary associations — zemstvos, cooperatives, professional societies — are dissolved or absorbed into Soviet structures. The party, soviets, and trade unions monopolize associational life. Informal mutual aid persists in villages and among neighbors.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Organized leisure collapses; fuel shortages close theaters and cinemas. The party sponsors agitprop performances and mass festivals. Street life diminishes under curfew and fear.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Revolutionary upheaval inverts the old hierarchy. Former nobles, officials, and bourgeois become disenfranchised "former people" (byvshie), subject to discrimination in rations, housing, and employment. Workers and poor peasants are rhetorically valorized, though party cadres form a new elite. Class origin becomes the primary status marker.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: The tsarist bureaucracy disintegrates; the Provisional Government fails to build a replacement. The Bolsheviks construct a new party-state apparatus — the Cheka (secret police), the Red Army, local soviets — but capacity is low, staffed by inexperienced cadres and forced to coerce compliance. Corruption, arbitrariness, and incompetence are endemic.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: The old army dissolves in 1917; the Provisional Government cannot rebuild it. The Bolsheviks create the Red Army (1918) under Trotsky, using conscription, military specialists (former tsarist officers), and commissar supervision. The Cheka conducts Red Terror — summary executions, hostage-taking, concentration camps — against class enemies and opponents. White armies, foreign interventionists, and peasant insurgents (Greens) contest Bolshevik control. The Civil War is won by 1921 through superior organization, central position, and ruthlessness.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: The period is maximal disorder — revolution, civil war, banditry, pogroms (especially in Ukraine, 1919–1920), peasant uprisings (Tambov, 1920–1921), worker strikes (Kronstadt, 1921). The state responds with terror; order is imposed by force, not consent.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: The initial revolutionary moment produces euphoria and utopian expectation; by 1920, exhaustion, fear, and disillusionment predominate. Peasants resent requisitioning; workers strike against hunger; soldiers desert. Trust is confined to immediate circles.

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Lenin commands Bolshevik allegiance through ideological authority and tactical acumen. Trotsky builds the Red Army and inspires through oratory. Local commanders and commissars exercise personal authority in the chaos. Kerensky fails to command allegiance; White generals lack unified leadership.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: The tsarist regime loses legitimacy by February 1917; the Provisional Government never acquires it. The Bolsheviks claim legitimacy through soviets and class mission, but rely on coercion. Peasants accept Bolshevik land decrees but resist requisitioning.

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: The Bolshevik party exhibits concentrated will — disciplined, centralized, willing to use unlimited force. Opponents are divided: Whites lack political vision; Greens are local and defensive. The Red victory reflects superior political will as much as material advantage.

    External Relations & Posture: The regime signs the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk (March 1918), ceding vast territory to exit the war. Allied intervention supports the Whites but commits insufficiently. The Comintern (1919) promotes world revolution, but no revolution succeeds abroad. By 1921, the regime is isolated but surviving.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: By 1921, all factions are exhausted. The Kronstadt rebellion expresses disillusionment even among revolutionary sailors. The shift to NEP acknowledges that coercion has reached its limit.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Russian is the dominant language; literacy is perhaps 40% by 1920, rising through Bolshevik campaigns. Ukrainian, Polish, and other languages persist in borderlands. The party communicates through Russian; local agitation uses vernaculars.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Telegraph and rail-borne press remain the primary channels. The party controls telegraph traffic. Newspapers are printed in Petrograd and Moscow and distributed by rail to provincial centers. Oral agitation supplements print where literacy is low.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Archives are seized, reorganized, and partially destroyed in revolutionary transitions. The party builds new record-keeping systems — personnel files, denunciations, surveillance reports — but institutional memory is fragmented. Many pre-revolutionary records survive by accident.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: The Provisional Government briefly permits press freedom; hundreds of newspapers appear across the political spectrum. The Bolsheviks close opposition newspapers (November 1917), establish Glavlit (1922), and impose party control on all publishing. By 1921, only party-approved publications circulate legally. Agitprop dominates; dissent is silenced.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Newspapers are read aloud in factories and barracks; posters, broadsides, and illustrated placards (ROSTA windows) reach the illiterate. Attention is captured by crisis; amplification is controlled by the party.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Rumor, misinformation, and propaganda saturate the information environment. Competing factions spread contradictory claims. The party monopoly reduces noise by silencing alternatives, not by establishing truth.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Revolutionary justice replaces tsarist law: class origin determines guilt. "Bourgeois legality" is rejected; revolutionary necessity justifies any action. Peasant moral economy — the right to land and harvest — conflicts with requisitioning.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Tsarist law is abolished; the Bolsheviks govern by decree. Legal codes are drafted but not yet consolidated. The Cheka operates outside any legal constraint. Revolutionary tribunals dispense summary justice.

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Courts are replaced by tribunals; procedure is minimal. The Cheka conducts extrajudicial executions. Hostage-taking and collective punishment are routine. Liability is determined by class, not act.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Pre-revolutionary rights are void. The 1918 Soviet Constitution proclaims worker rights but provides no enforcement against the state. Compliance with party directives is absolute or fatal.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Impunity is total for party and Cheka agents; wrong is unredressable for victims. Pogroms in Ukraine kill tens of thousands of Jews with no sanction. The moral residue is vast: mass death, dispossession, and injustice accumulate without reckoning.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Marxism-Leninism becomes the official paradigm, displacing liberal and conservative thought. Scientific inquiry is disrupted by emigration, closure of institutions, and subordination to ideology. Some scientists remain and adapt.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Technical knowledge is critical for the Red Army and remaining industry. Former specialists (spetsy) are employed under political supervision. Practical method continues; theoretical advance pauses.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: The Bolsheviks prioritize literacy campaigns (likbez) to create a literate proletariat. Schools reopen under Soviet control with new curricula. Universities are disrupted; many professors emigrate. The knowledge stock survives but transmission is interrupted.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Pre-revolutionary humanistic and religious education is declared obsolete and suppressed. Technical knowledge is preserved; classical learning atrophies. The break in transmission creates gaps that persist.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: The Revolution becomes instant myth — October as the decisive rupture, the proletariat as world-historical agent, Lenin as prophet. Tsarist memory is suppressed; revolutionary martyrology replaces it. National identities (Ukrainian, Georgian) complicate the Russian-centered narrative.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Marxism-Leninism provides the legitimating narrative: the dictatorship of the proletariat, the transition to socialism, the withering of the state. The narrative justifies terror as historical necessity.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Revolutionary art flourishes briefly — Constructivism, avant-garde poster design, agitprop theater. Mayakovsky, Meyerhold, Rodchenko. The material culture is impoverished — ragged clothing, repurposed buildings, requisitioned goods — but revolutionary aesthetics embrace the austere.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Traditional forms are declared exhausted and bourgeois. New revolutionary forms are created but not yet canonized. The transition is generative rather than exhausted; meaning is actively contested.

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Official atheism replaces Orthodox Christianity. Dialectical materialism provides the cosmology: history has direction, class struggle is its engine, communism its terminus. For many, the revolutionary moment carries quasi-religious intensity.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Revolutionary rituals replace religious ones — red funerals, revolutionary holidays, oath-taking ceremonies. Church services continue but under pressure; clergy are persecuted.

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: The Orthodox Church is disestablished, its property confiscated, its hierarchy fractured. The Patriarch Tikhon resists, then accommodates. Sectarians and Old Believers face similar pressure. Religious authority collapses under state assault.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Atheism is promoted; religious practice recedes in public and persists in private. The revolutionary cause absorbs spiritual energy for many participants.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: The sacred is officially discharged — secularization by decree. For believers, the loss is traumatic. For revolutionaries, the cause provides transcendence. The spiritual residue accumulates: the void is filled by ideology, or left empty.
