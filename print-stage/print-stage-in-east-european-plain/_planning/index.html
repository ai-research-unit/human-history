    Planning Document — Print Stage in The East European Plain (1700 CE – 1930 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1700 CE – 1930 CE

    Region: The East European Plain — the vast lowland stretching from Poland through Russia to the Urals; centered on the Russian Empire and its successor states; including Ukraine, Belarus, and the Baltic regions.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes imperial archives, censorship records, newspapers, thick journals, revolutionary pamphlets, and Soviet publications. Church Slavonic printing from 16th century; civil script reform under Peter I; censorship dominant until 1905; revolutionary underground press; Soviet state publishing. Telegraph from 1850s; radio emerges 1920s. Window begins with Petrine reforms; ends as Soviet system consolidates and broadcast begins.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Petrine Revolution (1700–1762 CE)

    Peter I: Westernization; St. Petersburg founded 1703; military modernization.

    Civil Script Reform: 1708; simplified Cyrillic; secular texts; newspapers.

    First Newspaper: Vedomosti 1702; state-controlled; elite readership.

    Academy of Sciences: 1725; scholarly publishing; Western knowledge.

    Elite Westernization: Nobility education; French language; cultural gap.

    Church Subordination: Patriarchate abolished; Holy Synod; state control.

    2. Catherine to Alexander (1762–1825 CE)

    Catherine II: Enlightened absolutism; Nakaz; limited reforms; Pugachev.

    Novikov, Radishchev: Early independent publishing; censorship response; exile.

    Napoleonic Wars: 1812 invasion; patriotic mobilization; veteran politicization.

    Alexander I: Early liberal hopes; Holy Alliance; later reaction.

    Decembrist Revolt: 1825; officer conspiracy; failure; repression.

    Thick Journals Begin: Literary-political magazines; Messenger of Europe.

    3. Nicholas I Reaction (1825–1861 CE)

    Nicholas I: "Orthodoxy, Autocracy, Nationality"; censorship intensified.

    Third Section: Secret police; surveillance; political control.

    Thick Journals: Sovremennik; intellectual debates; Belinsky.

    Herzen Abroad: Free Russian Press London 1853; Kolokol (Bell).

    Westernizers vs. Slavophiles: Intellectual debates; print venues.

    Crimean War: 1853–1856; defeat; reform pressure.

    4. Great Reforms (1861–1881 CE)

    Emancipation 1861: Serfs freed; land settlement; social transformation.

    Zemstva: Local government; schools; public sphere expansion.

    Revolutionary Populism: Narodniks; "going to the people"; terrorism.

    Underground Press: Illegal publications; revolutionary networks.

    Tolstoy, Dostoevsky: Literary giants; thick journal serialization.

    Alexander II Assassination: 1881; People's Will; reaction follows.

    5. Reaction and Industrialization (1881–1905 CE)

    Alexander III: Reaction; counter-reforms; Russification; pogroms.

    Witte System: Industrialization; railways; foreign investment.

    Marxist Circles: Plekhanov; Lenin; Iskra; underground organizing.

    Legal Press Growth: Commercial newspapers; literacy expansion.

    Russo-Japanese War: 1904–1905; defeat; revolutionary crisis.

    Zemstvo Constitutionalism: Liberal movement; press campaigns.

    6. 1905 Revolution and Constitutional Experiment (1905–1914 CE)

    1905 Revolution: Strikes; Bloody Sunday; October Manifesto.

    October Manifesto: Civil liberties; Duma; constitutional framework.

    Press Freedom: Censorship relaxed; legal parties; open debate.

    Stolypin: Agrarian reform; repression; "necktie" executions.

    Duma Politics: Kadets; Octobrists; Social Democrats; debates.

    World War I Approach: Balkan crises; alliance system; mobilization.

    7. War and Revolution (1914–1921 CE)

    World War I: 1914–1918; massive casualties; economic strain; collapse.

    February Revolution: 1917; Tsar abdicates; Provisional Government.

    October Revolution: Bolshevik seizure; Lenin; Soviet power.

    Civil War: 1918–1921; Reds vs. Whites; foreign intervention.

    War Communism: Nationalization; grain requisition; terror.

    Revolutionary Press: Pravda; Izvestia; propaganda; censorship.

    8. NEP and Stalin Rise (1921–1930 CE)

    New Economic Policy: 1921; partial market; cultural relaxation.

    Party Press Dominance: Pravda; regional papers; agitprop.

    Lenin Death: 1924; succession struggle; Stalin consolidation.

    Cultural Revolution: 1928–1931; proletarian culture; specialist persecution.

    First Five-Year Plan: 1928; industrialization; collectivization; famine.

    Radio Begins: 1920s; broadcast supplements print; state control.

    9. Censorship and Underground Press (1700–1930 CE)

    Petrine Control: State monopoly; limited circulation; elite access.

    Catherine's Experiment: Brief relaxation; then reaction.

    Nicholas I System: Prior censorship; Third Section; self-censorship.

    Underground Networks: Herzen; revolutionary printing; smuggling.

    1905 Opening: Press freedom; then partial restoration of controls.

    Soviet System: Party monopoly; no independent press; state publishing.

    10. Thick Journals and Intelligentsia (1700–1930 CE)

    Origins: Late 18th century; European models; literary-political.

    Golden Age: 1840s–1860s; Sovremennik; Otechestvennye Zapiski.

    Intelligentsia Formation: Educated stratum; alienated; oppositional.

    Literary Serialization: Novels serialized; Dostoevsky, Tolstoy.

    Political Debates: Westernizers, Slavophiles, radicals; print venues.

    Soviet Transformation: Journals continue; party control; ideological conformity.
