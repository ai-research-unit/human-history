    1917 CE – 1930 CE — World War, Red Scare, and Jazz Age Press

    Summary: The United States from wartime mobilization through the Roaring Twenties, when mass-circulation print reaches its commercial zenith even as the war-spawned apparatus of censorship and propaganda establishes precedents for government-press entanglement, the binding constraint being the contradiction between print's unprecedented commercial reach and the political restrictions that war, red scare, and immigration closure impose on its discursive scope.

    D01. Physical Domain

    Geography & Spatial Friction: The continental infrastructure matures: 265,000 miles of railway, expanded highway networks for automobiles, and airmail service (1918) compress spatial friction; national magazines and syndicated features create uniform content from coast to coast.

    Climate Regime & Volatility: Climate stability prevails; the period's agricultural crises (postwar deflation, early Dust Bowl precursors in the 1920s) derive from economic rather than climatic factors.

    Hazard Regime & Exposure: The influenza pandemic (1918-1919) kills over 675,000 Americans, though press coverage is muted under wartime censorship; urban hazards continue as industrial accidents and tenement fires fill local news columns.

    Resource & Energy Endowment: Petroleum surpasses coal in strategic importance as the automobile transforms American life; coal still powers industry and urban heating; electrification reaches 35 percent of homes by 1920, enabling evening newspaper reading.

    Waste Absorption & Sink Capacity: Industrial pollution intensifies; urban air quality degrades further with automobile exhaust joining coal smoke; these conditions receive less press attention than in the muckraking era as reform enthusiasm wanes.

    D02. Biological Domain

    Pathogen Load & Mortality Baseline: The influenza pandemic proves catastrophic, killing more Americans than the war; mortality otherwise continues declining with sanitation improvements and medical advances; infant mortality falls below 80 per 1,000 by 1925.

    Nutrition & Physiological Vitality: Nutritional standards improve with refrigeration, canning, and national food distribution; vitamin science emerges; Prohibition drives alcohol consumption underground without eliminating it.

    Care, Healing & Recovery Capacity: Hospitals proliferate; nursing professionalizes; veterans' healthcare becomes federal responsibility; medical costs rise as professionalization advances.

    Reproductive Biology & Pairing Dynamics: Birth rates continue declining; birth control advocacy expands (Margaret Sanger) though legal restrictions persist; the "flapper" represents changing sexual mores that newspapers both celebrate and deplore.

    Demographic Renewal & Density: Population grows from 103 million (1917) to 123 million (1930); immigration restriction (1921 Emergency Quota Act, 1924 National Origins Act) sharply reduces European inflow; the Great Migration accelerates, bringing over one million African Americans north by 1930; urbanization crosses the 50 percent threshold (1920 census).

    Attrition & Dependency Overhang: Wounded veterans (over 200,000) create new dependency burdens; child labor finally declines under state laws and changing economic conditions; the productive population carries relatively light dependent loads as restricted immigration reduces elderly returns and birth rates fall.

    D03. Economic Domain

    Subsistence & Provisioning Floor: Postwar deflation (1920-1921) devastates farmers; urban workers experience the prosperity of the 1920s unevenly; consumer credit expands access to durable goods.

    Energy Capture & Conversion: Electrification transforms industry and household life; petroleum powers the automobile boom; press production benefits from electric motors and improved paper-making.

    Labor Organization & Coercion: The Red Scare crushes radical unionism; the 1919 steel strike fails; AFL membership declines from 5 million (1920) to 3.4 million (1929); "welfare capitalism" substitutes corporate paternalism for collective bargaining; labor press shrinks.

    Production & Surplus Capacity: Industrial production soars, doubling between 1921 and 1929; automobile production reaches 5.4 million annually by 1929; advertising expenditure quadruples, reaching $3 billion annually, much of it in newspapers and magazines.

    Storage, Reserves & Buffering: Federal Reserve management produces relative monetary stability; corporate profits accumulate; agricultural sector carries chronic overproduction and debt burden.

    Exchange, Trade & Logistics: Mass retail (chain stores, mail order) reaches national scale; Sears and Montgomery Ward catalogs bring urban goods to rural homes; newspapers serve as advertising vehicles for this consumer economy.

    Value Storage, Credit & Deferred Exchange: Consumer credit (installment plans) enables automobile and appliance purchases; stock market speculation escalates through the 1920s; real estate speculation produces Florida land boom and bust (1926).

    Surplus Extraction & Redistribution: Federal income taxes cut under Coolidge; tariffs remain high; wealth concentration increases; the top one percent captures over 20 percent of income by 1929.

    Wealth Distribution & Asset Defense: Inequality reaches new peaks; stock ownership spreads to middle class but remains concentrated; agricultural distress contrasts with urban prosperity; racial income gaps persist.

    Byproducts & Waste Routing: Industrial waste continues accumulating; consumer society generates new waste streams; financial speculation creates malinvestment that will collapse in 1929.

    D04. Social Domain

    Kinship, Family & Inheritance: The nuclear family ideal intensifies; divorce rates rise; women's domestic role shifts as appliances reduce household labor; youth culture emerges as distinct social category.

    Association & Mutual Aid: Voluntary associations flourish (Rotary, Kiwanis, American Legion); ethnic mutual aid societies serve immigrant communities; African American fraternal organizations expand; the Ku Klux Klan revives, claiming 4-5 million members by 1924.

    Leisure, Play & Sociability: Spectator sports (baseball, boxing, football) dominate sports pages; movies and radio compete with print for leisure time; nightclubs, dance halls, and speakeasies create new social spaces; the automobile enables personal mobility.

    Stratification, Status & Group Structure: Class lines remain sharp despite rhetoric of mass prosperity; ethnic hierarchies hardening into law through immigration restriction; racial segregation tightens in South and expands in North with Great Migration.

    Administrative Capacity & Institutional Integrity: Federal government expands (Prohibition enforcement, immigration control, veterans' affairs) but reforms stall; corruption persists (Teapot Dome scandal, 1923-1924, broken by press investigation); municipal reform movements achieve mixed results.

    Coercive Apparatus & Means of Force: National Guard deploys against strikes; Palmer Raids (1919-1920) demonstrate federal coercive capacity against radicals; Prohibition creates new enforcement apparatus (Bureau of Prohibition) and organized crime; lynching continues with near-total impunity.

    Discharge, Disorder & Decay: Major race riots (Chicago, 1919; Tulsa, 1921) reflect postwar tensions; Prohibition generates organized crime; labor unrest is suppressed rather than accommodated; Klan violence targets African Americans, Catholics, Jews, and immigrants.

    D05. Emotional Domain

    Leadership & Personal Authority: Wilson's collapse discredits idealism; Harding, Coolidge, and Hoover embody business-oriented normalcy; charismatic figures emerge in other domains (Lindbergh, Ruth, popular entertainers) whose celebrity newspapers amplify.

    Legitimacy as Lived Allegiance: Business legitimacy peaks; government legitimacy stable but not actively embraced; immigrant communities maintain divided loyalties; African Americans increasingly contest the legitimacy of segregation through the press (Crisis, Defender).

    Political Will & Collective Resolve: Reform will exhausted; business confidence dominant; labor militancy crushed; nationalist mobilization for war proves the period's high-water mark of collective resolve.

    External Relations & Posture: Wartime alliance yields to isolationist withdrawal; Senate rejects League of Nations; Washington Naval Conference (1921-1922) represents limited engagement; tariff walls rise.

    Public Sentiment & Behavioral Temper: Wartime nationalism yields to postwar disillusionment among intellectuals but popular patriotism persists; the 1920s mood combines consumer optimism with nativist anxiety; the "return to normalcy" represents exhaustion with reform.

    Disaffection, Apathy & Spent Will: Intellectuals express disillusionment (Lost Generation); working-class radicalism suppressed but not extinguished; African American militancy grows (New Negro movement, Garvey); voter turnout declines through the 1920s, reaching lows in 1924.

    D06. Informational Domain

    Communication Accessibility: Literacy exceeds 94 percent; English language dominance increases as immigration restriction and wartime suppression reduce foreign-language press; African American literacy continues rising.

    Channels & Transmission Velocity: Wire services achieve instantaneous national reach; telephone penetration reaches 35 percent of households; radio emerges (KDKA, 1920) and spreads rapidly to 40 percent of households by 1930, beginning print's displacement for breaking news.

    Record, Storage & Persistence: Newspaper archives expand; news reels create visual record; government records proliferate with wartime bureaucracy; FBI begins systematic surveillance files.

    Framing, Censorship & Discourse Norms: Wartime censorship under Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918) suppresses dissent, with over 2,000 prosecutions; radical and foreign-language press targeted; postwar, censorship recedes but leaves chilling effect; objectivity norms consolidate in mainstream press.

    Reception, Attention & Amplification: Tabloids (New York Daily News, 1919) pioneer visual journalism for urban masses; syndicated columnists (Walter Lippmann, H.L. Mencken) achieve national readership; celebrity culture intensifies press attention on personalities.

    Signal Integrity & Noise Load: Tabloid sensationalism and publicity-driven coverage increase noise; advertising-editorial boundaries blur; public relations emerges as profession (Ivy Lee, Edward Bernays), systematically manipulating press coverage; wartime propaganda demonstrates information's weaponization.

    D07. Moral Domain

    Moral Foundations & Sense of Justice: Protestant moral consensus fragments; Prohibition represents its last major legislative triumph; cultural conflict between "wets" and "drys," modernists and fundamentalists, divides the nation.

    Law, Codification & Regulatory Evolution: Prohibition (18th Amendment, 1920) criminalizes alcohol; immigration restriction codifies ethnic hierarchy; labor law retreats from Progressive gains; women gain suffrage (19th Amendment, 1920).

    Adjudication, Liability & Sanction: Courts uphold wartime speech restrictions (Schenck, 1919; Debs imprisoned); later cases begin protecting speech (Holmes's dissents); Prohibition enforcement overwhelms courts; Sacco-Vanzetti case (1921-1927) exposes judicial bias.

    Rights, Protections & Compliance Burden: Free speech doctrine begins developing; women's suffrage achieved; civil liberties organizations emerge (ACLU, 1920) in response to wartime suppression; African American rights remain systematically violated.

    Impunity & Unredressed Wrong: Lynching continues without federal intervention (anti-lynching legislation fails); race riots produce no accountability; Prohibition corruption pervades enforcement; Teapot Dome produces convictions but system remains unchanged.

    D08. Intellectual Domain

    Inquiry, Theory & Paradigm: Social science matures; behaviorism (Watson) influences advertising and propaganda; public opinion research begins (Lippmann's Public Opinion, 1922); fundamentalist-modernist controversy frames intellectual conflict.

    Applied Knowledge & Technical Method: Mass production (Fordism) reaches maturity; advertising develops psychological techniques; journalism education expands; public relations systematizes opinion management.

    Education, Knowledge Stock & Skill Distribution: High school enrollment doubles; college attendance grows but remains elite; journalism schools proliferate; technical education serves industrial needs.

    Obsolescence & Knowledge Decay: Wartime propaganda discredits idealistic rhetoric; Progressive reform methods appear naive; craft knowledge continues eroding under industrial standardization; the Scopes trial (1925) dramatizes knowledge conflicts.

    D09. Cultural Domain

    Mythos, Memory & Foundational Identity: The war is remembered ambiguously: sacrifice honored but purpose questioned; "100 percent Americanism" demands conformity; the "melting pot" ideal coexists with nativist exclusion; African Americans assert "New Negro" identity.

    Legitimating Narrative & Ideology: Business civilization celebrates corporate capitalism as American achievement; "American Dream" narratives proliferate; immigration restriction justified through scientific racism; white supremacy maintains its grip on Southern and increasingly Northern discourse.

    Artistic Expression & Material Culture: Jazz Age culture explodes through print and record; Harlem Renaissance achieves national visibility through magazines and books; modernist literature (Fitzgerald, Hemingway) reaches wide audience; Art Deco defines visual style; automobile, radio, and movie reshape material culture.

    Form Exhaustion & Meaning Decay: Victorian gentility exhausted; war poetry and fiction mark disillusionment; traditional newspaper formats challenged by tabloid innovation; muckraking reform mode hollows out though investigative journalism continues (Teapot Dome).

    D10. Spiritual Domain

    Cosmology & Existential Outlook: Protestant hegemony challenged by Catholic and Jewish immigration, secularism, and internal division; fundamentalism crystallizes in opposition to modernism; existential doubt pervades intellectual culture.

    Ritual, Devotion & Sacred Practice: Church attendance remains high; immigrant religious practices persist; African American churches flourish in northern cities; civic religion intensifies around war remembrance (Armistice Day, Tomb of Unknown Soldier).

    Religious Institutions & Doctrinal Authority: Fundamentalist-modernist controversy splits Protestant denominations; Catholic hierarchy consolidates institutional power; Jewish communal institutions mature.

    Spiritual Diffusion, Syncretism & Displacement: Religious diversity increases despite immigration restriction; Pentecostalism spreads; African American churches blend Southern traditions with urban forms; secularism advances among educated elite.

    Disenchantment & Sacred Discharge: Intellectuals embrace secularism; Prohibition represents religious moralism's political climax and eventual discrediting; Scopes trial dramatizes science-religion conflict; the sacred retreats from public life among educated classes while remaining vital in popular religion.
