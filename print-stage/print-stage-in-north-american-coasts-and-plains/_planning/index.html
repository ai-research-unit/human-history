    Planning Document — Print Stage in The North American Eastern Woodlands and Mississippi Basin (1750 CE – 1930 CE)

    Age Definition

    Print Stage: Mechanically reproduced, identical copies become the substrate of the public sphere. A text is reproduced into many exact copies without a scribe, so the cost of the hundredth collapses toward the cost of the first; the public sphere, news, and opinion come to be carried by printed matter — books, then the periodical press distributing the same text on a schedule to a scattered readership. Dominant until broadcast addresses the mass audience at once.

    Window: 1750 CE – 1930 CE

    Region: The North American Eastern Woodlands and Mississippi Basin — the Atlantic seaboard, Appalachian region, and Mississippi-Ohio river systems; British colonies becoming the United States; expanding westward through continental conquest.

    Note on Evidence: Documentary record includes colonial archives, revolutionary pamphlets, constitutional debates, newspapers, magazines, and government publications. Colonial presses from 17th century; revolutionary press; partisan press under early republic; penny press 1830s; telegraph 1844; mass circulation late 19th century; radio begins 1920s. Window begins with colonial crisis; ends as broadcast transforms communication.

    Shared Evidence Base

    1. Colonial Crisis and Revolution (1750–1783 CE)

    Seven Years War: 1756–1763; British victory; colonial taxation.

    Stamp Act Crisis: 1765; press resistance; Sons of Liberty; repeal.

    Revolutionary Pamphlets: Common Sense 1776; Declaration; propaganda.

    Committees of Correspondence: Print networks; colonial coordination.

    Revolutionary Press: Patriot and Loyalist papers; partisan mobilization.

    Independence: 1776; war; French alliance; Treaty of Paris 1783.

    2. New Republic (1783–1800 CE)

    Constitutional Convention: 1787; Federalist Papers; ratification debates.

    First Amendment: 1791; press freedom; foundational principle.

    Party Press: Federalist vs. Republican papers; partisan warfare.

    Alien and Sedition Acts: 1798; press suppression; controversy.

    Hamilton vs. Jefferson: Political rivalry; newspaper proxies.

    Limited Circulation: Elite readership; high costs; subscription papers.

    3. Jeffersonian Republic (1800–1828 CE)

    Revolution of 1800: Peaceful transfer; Republican dominance.

    Louisiana Purchase: 1803; territorial expansion; western press.

    War of 1812: British conflict; nationalism; Hartford Convention.

    Missouri Compromise: 1820; sectional tensions; slavery debates.

    Era of Good Feelings: Monroe; limited party competition.

    Press Expansion: More papers; postal subsidies; spreading literacy.

    4. Jacksonian Democracy (1828–1848 CE)

    Jackson Election: 1828; mass democracy; party organization.

    Penny Press: 1833 New York Sun; cheap papers; advertising model.

    Reform Movements: Temperance; abolition; women's rights; print campaigns.

    Bank War: Political controversy; press partisanship.

    Mexican-American War: 1846–1848; manifest destiny; territorial conquest.

    Telegraph Arrives: 1844; Morse; news transmission transformed.

    5. Sectional Crisis (1848–1861 CE)

    Compromise of 1850: Fugitive Slave Act; sectional tension.

    Kansas-Nebraska: 1854; popular sovereignty; violence.

    Abolitionist Press: Liberator; North Star; Frederick Douglass.

    Lincoln-Douglas Debates: 1858; printed verbatim; national attention.

    John Brown: Harpers Ferry 1859; media sensation; polarization.

    Secession: 1860–1861; Confederate formation; war approaches.

    6. Civil War (1861–1865 CE)

    Union Press: Northern papers; war coverage; Lincoln management.

    Confederate Press: Southern papers; paper shortages; morale.

    Censorship: Military restrictions; telegraph control; correspondents.

    Photography: Brady; visual documentation; home front impact.

    Emancipation: 1863 Proclamation; printed throughout Union.

    War's End: 1865; Lincoln assassination; press coverage.

    7. Reconstruction (1865–1877 CE)

    Constitutional Amendments: 13th, 14th, 15th; printed, debated.

    Freedpeople's Press: Black newspapers; church publications.

    Republican Papers: Reconstruction advocacy; Black political participation.

    Redemption: White supremacist violence; Democratic restoration.

    Compromise of 1877: Federal withdrawal; press debate.

    Lost Cause: Southern narrative construction through print.

    8. Gilded Age (1877–1900 CE)

    Industrial Expansion: Railroads; steel; corporate power.

    Immigration: Mass European immigration; ethnic press explosion.

    Labor Conflict: Strikes; Haymarket; Pullman; press coverage.

    Yellow Journalism: Pulitzer; Hearst; sensationalism; circulation wars.

    Spanish-American War: 1898; press role; imperialism.

    Jim Crow: Southern segregation; Black press resistance.

    9. Progressive Era (1900–1917 CE)

    Muckrakers: McClure's; Sinclair; Steffens; Tarbell; reform journalism.

    Roosevelt, Wilson: Progressive politics; regulatory state.

    Women's Suffrage: Movement press; state victories; federal amendment.

    NAACP: Crisis magazine 1910; Du Bois; anti-lynching campaigns.

    Immigration Restriction: Nativist publications; quota movement.

    World War I Approach: Neutrality debates; preparedness.

    10. World War and 1920s (1917–1930 CE)

    World War I: 1917–1918; propaganda; Committee on Public Information.

    Espionage and Sedition Acts: Press restrictions; socialist papers suppressed.

    Red Scare: 1919–1920; Palmer raids; radical press targeted.

    1920s Prosperity: Consumer advertising; mass magazines; tabloids.

    Harlem Renaissance: Black cultural flourishing; literary magazines.

    Radio Begins: 1920 KDKA; broadcast competition emerges.

    11. African American Press (1750–1930 CE)

    Colonial Era: Limited; slave literacy suppressed.

    Freedom's Journal: 1827; first Black newspaper; abolitionism.

    Frederick Douglass: North Star; most influential antebellum.

    Reconstruction Papers: Political mobilization; church publications.

    Ida B. Wells: Anti-lynching journalism; 1890s.

    Crisis, Defender: NAACP; Chicago Defender; Great Migration.

    12. Religious and Reform Press (1750–1930 CE)

    Colonial Religion: Sermons printed; Great Awakening.

    Revivalism: Second Great Awakening; tract societies.

    Abolition: Liberator 1831; moral suasion; political action.

    Temperance: Reform publications; WCTU; prohibition campaign.

    Women's Suffrage: Movement papers; state and national campaigns.

    Social Gospel: Reform Christianity; progressive publications.

    13. Immigrant and Ethnic Press (1750–1930 CE)

    German Press: Largest non-English; Pennsylvania Germans; later immigrants.

    Irish Press: Boston Pilot; Fenian publications; political engagement.

    Jewish Press: Yiddish papers; Forward; cultural preservation.

    Italian Press: La Gazzetta; community organization; assimilation tensions.

    Restriction Era: 1920s; ethnic press decline; Americanization.

    Native Language Persistence: Generational shift to English.

    14. Party Press to Mass Circulation (1750–1930 CE)

    Colonial Model: Printer-publishers; limited circulation; elite audience.

    Party Press: Subsidized partisan papers; political patronage.

    Penny Press Revolution: 1830s; advertising model; mass readership.

    Telegraph Impact: Wire services; AP; standardized news.

    Industrial Production: Steam press; linotype; cheap newsprint.

    Corporate Newspapers: Chains; Hearst; Scripps; business model.
